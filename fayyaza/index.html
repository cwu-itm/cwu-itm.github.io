Module 1.1 Blog Post: Model-Based Systems Thinking in Digital Environments

Introduction

The digital landscape is no longer simple. From cloud infrastructure and microservices to artificial intelligence and user experience design, modern digital systems are composed of layers of interconnected components. These components are dynamic and evolve rapidly. Yet despite this complexity, many organizations still rely on outdated, linear models of thinking when attempting to design or solve problems within these systems. These traditional approaches are often reductionist, focusing on parts in isolation and missing the interdependencies that define real-world behavior.

Systems thinking presents an alternative—a more adaptive and holistic method for understanding and managing complexity. This approach emphasizes how the parts of a system interact, rather than simply analyzing them individually. Even more powerful is model-based systems thinking, which uses structured models—visual, conceptual, or computational—to explore, simulate, and improve system behavior over time. In digital environments where outcomes are often nonlinear, unpredictable, and interdependent, model-based systems thinking offers designers, engineers, and strategists a toolkit to develop more robust, responsive, and human-centered solutions.

Understanding Systems Thinking

At its core, systems thinking is about seeing the whole picture. Rather than breaking things into parts and examining each in isolation, systems thinking focuses on the relationships between components and the emergent behavior that arises from their interaction. As Donella Meadows (2008) explained in Thinking in Systems, "A system is more than the sum of its parts; it is the product of their interactions."

Systems thinking originated in the mid-20th century and has since influenced disciplines such as ecology, organizational behavior, education, engineering, and information systems. The key principles include interconnectedness, feedback loops, emergence, delay and nonlinearity, and understanding boundaries and perspectives. These principles help practitioners address root causes and long-term consequences rather than symptoms.

The Pitfalls of Linear Thinking in Digital Design

Traditional linear thinking assumes problems are best addressed step-by-step. However, digital systems involve many interrelated parts. A change in one component can impact many others. Linear thinking can lead to oversights, such as fixing a UI element without considering how it affects server load or user behavior. In contrast, systems thinking encourages considering all interacting parts, long-term feedback, and user experience holistically.

The Value of Models in Systems Thinking

Model-based systems thinking translates abstract systems concepts into concrete visualizations or simulations. These models enable communication among teams, allow scenario testing, and support learning. Models do not need to be perfect, only useful and iterative. They provide structure to complex decisions, help identify feedback loops, and clarify assumptions.

Model-Based Thinking in Action: A UX Perspective

Jeff Johnson (2020) connects system design and cognitive psychology. Mental models—internal maps users form—must align with interface logic. External models, such as mockups or diagrams, bridge the gap between system functionality and user understanding. For example, wireframes in app design model how users interact with systems and how features meet user expectations.

Real-World Case Study: Smart Cities

Smart cities integrate infrastructure, user behavior, and data. A city bike-sharing system is not just hardware and software; it involves data modeling, maintenance cycles, real-time analytics, and feedback systems. Cities like Amsterdam and Singapore use digital twins to simulate urban environments, reflecting true model-based systems thinking in action.

The Role of Feedback Loops and Delays

Feedback loops and delays are essential features in systems thinking. Reinforcing loops amplify behavior, while balancing loops stabilize systems. Delays can mask cause-effect relationships. Ignoring these dynamics can lead to poor design decisions. Models help make these invisible mechanisms visible.

Organizational Systems and Team Collaboration

Systems thinking applies to human systems too. In software teams, roles, feedback, and communication patterns create a system. Agile, CI/CD, and retrospectives are ways to improve not just what is built, but how it’s built. Models can reflect workflows, improve efficiency, and reduce miscommunication.

Critical Reflection and Limitations

Model-based systems thinking is powerful but not perfect. Models rely on assumptions, and poor inputs can lead to poor outputs. They are simplifications—not reality. Organizational culture, siloed thinking, and lack of training can also limit their effectiveness. Ethical considerations must be integrated to ensure models align with values, not just efficiency.

Ethical and Human-Centered Design

Systems thinking supports ethical reflection. For instance, platform design must consider misinformation, addiction, and user data security. Human-centered models help ensure systems support user well-being. Jeff Johnson (2020) emphasizes designing interfaces that support cognitive usability, which scales to broader systems design.

Recommendations for Practice

  • Map your systems early using tools like causal loop diagrams or user journeys.
  • Model only what matters—focus on high-leverage points.
  • Use iterative prototyping aligned with user mental models.
  • Include diverse perspectives in modeling and system discussions.
  • Validate models regularly and revise based on outcomes.

Conclusion

Model-based systems thinking provides a lens and toolkit for engaging with complex digital systems. It allows for better understanding, communication, decision-making, and design. It emphasizes sustainability, feedback, and inclusivity in a way that traditional thinking cannot. As systems grow in complexity and influence, adopting a systems mindset will be essential for designers, developers, and leaders alike.

Designing for the Long Haul: Life Cycle Thinking in Systems Engineering

In today’s digital and interconnected world, engineering systems demand more than just technical expertise or the creation of functional products. Successful systems engineering is about managing complexity, anticipating system evolution, and designing with long-term value and adaptability in mind. At the core of this philosophy lies the Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC)—a structured methodology that enables engineers to manage each stage of a system’s journey, from initial planning to eventual decommissioning.

This blog post explores the SDLC and takes a deeper look into three core life cycle models: linear, iterative, and incremental. These models shape how engineers approach development and decision-making over time. The post also integrates systems thinking, Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE), and human-centered design principles—drawing on Jeff Johnson’s Designing with the Mind in Mind—to show how user needs and system longevity are both critical to effective engineering.

Understanding the Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC)

The SDLC provides a framework for developing and managing systems through distinct phases: concept development, requirements gathering, system design, implementation, testing, deployment, operation, maintenance, and retirement (INCOSE, 2015). These stages ensure a holistic view—incorporating not just the technical aspects of a system, but also human, financial, and operational considerations throughout its lifespan.

Systems Thinking: A Holistic Engineering Perspective

Systems thinking encourages engineers to understand the dynamic relationships between components within a system. It highlights how local decisions can influence broader system behavior. According to Systems Engineering Demystified (n.d.), this mindset shifts focus from isolated problem-solving to viewing systems as interconnected wholes that interact with their users and environments.

Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE)

MBSE replaces static documents with interactive, digital models that improve communication, traceability, and early validation (INCOSE, 2015). These models are particularly effective in life cycle models like iterative and incremental, where requirements and solutions evolve through feedback loops and stakeholder engagement.

Human-Centered Design: Learning from Cognitive Psychology

Jeff Johnson (2020) stresses that many system usability failures occur when design ignores how people perceive, think, and behave. Human-centered design, grounded in cognitive psychology, aims to align system behavior with mental models, reduce cognitive load, and create intuitive experiences. Involving users early and consistently helps produce systems that are both efficient and user-friendly.

Comparing Life Cycle Models

Choosing the right development model can significantly affect system success. Each life cycle model offers unique strengths and limitations depending on the project context, complexity, and level of requirement uncertainty.

  • Linear (Waterfall Model): This model follows a strict, sequential order of phases. It is best suited for projects with well-defined, stable requirements—such as government systems or infrastructure projects—where major changes during development are not expected. However, the linear model is inflexible and offers little opportunity to revisit earlier phases once they’re completed, which can lead to inefficiencies if problems emerge late in the process.
  • Iterative Model: The iterative model embraces uncertainty by cycling through repeated phases of development and refinement. Each iteration produces a prototype that can be evaluated and improved. This approach is useful when requirements are unclear or evolving, such as in machine learning systems or UX-intensive applications. It enables continuous stakeholder feedback and aligns well with MBSE, which supports visual modeling and refinement throughout iterations.
  • Incremental Model: In this model, the system is built and delivered in smaller components, or increments. Each part adds to the previous one, offering partial functionality early and allowing for ongoing testing and user validation. It's ideal for modular systems, such as enterprise software or mobile apps, where risk can be minimized by validating key features before the full system is delivered.

In today’s practice, many engineering teams use hybrid models that combine elements of iterative and incremental approaches—like Agile frameworks. These allow for adaptability, continuous delivery, and rapid response to change. Ultimately, life cycle thinking requires choosing or tailoring a model not just based on technical goals, but also on user needs, stakeholder engagement, and long-term maintainability.

Conclusion

By understanding and applying principles from systems thinking, MBSE, and user-centered design, engineers can design systems that are robust, sustainable, and adaptable. Whether applying a linear, iterative, or incremental model, the key is to select the approach that best supports flexibility, system longevity, and stakeholder alignment. A thoughtful life cycle strategy transforms engineering from short-term implementation to long-term system stewardship.

Designing to Meet Human Needs: The Importance of Perception, Empathy, and Usability

What does it take to design in a way that serves people? This question—while seemingly simple—is the foundation of human-centered design (HCD). HCD places people, not technology, at the center of the design process. It emphasizes empathy, iteration, and deep attention to how people see, think, and act in the real world. As digital systems increasingly shape our lives, designers bear a responsibility to create solutions that align with human perception and experience—not just business goals (IDEO.org, 2015).

Design goes far beyond appearance. It is about solving problems in ways that resonate with users. This post explores how HCD frameworks like ISO 9241-210 and IDEO’s Field Guide intersect with concepts of perception, attention, and key UX laws. When used together, they create more inclusive, intuitive, and human-driven experiences.

Human-Centered Design: Building Empathy into Solutions

Human-centered design is a structured yet flexible approach to solving design challenges by focusing on users’ real contexts, behaviors, and feedback. It doesn’t just involve users after a product is developed—it includes them at every phase. The goal is to create systems that not only work, but feel natural and trustworthy (DIN, 2019).

The IDEO Field Guide to Human-Centered Design champions creativity, curiosity, and empathy. It outlines a five-phase process: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test. Each stage builds on user understanding, promoting co-creation over assumptions (IDEO.org, 2015).

On the other hand, ISO 9241-210 provides a formal framework for applying HCD in technical systems. It emphasizes six key activities: understanding context, specifying user requirements, producing design solutions, evaluating those solutions, iterating based on findings, and involving users throughout (DIN, 2019). The structure ensures usability principles are embedded from the beginning—not treated as an afterthought.

Seeing and Thinking: Perception, Attention, and the Interface

How users visually perceive information determines how they interact with interfaces. Perception shapes what users notice first, while attention filters which elements are processed. When designers understand these processes, they can guide attention toward what matters most (Wolfe & Horowitz, 2004).

Visual hierarchy is one of the most important tools for this. It organizes interface elements by their importance. A brightly colored call-to-action button placed in a central location draws attention instantly. According to Wolfe and Horowitz (2004), visual features such as color, contrast, and motion attract attention through “pre-attentive processing.” This means users may notice these elements before consciously thinking about them.

But perception has limits. Our brains can’t process every detail at once. Poor visual hierarchy causes cognitive friction, forcing users to stop, think, and search—rather than flow smoothly through tasks. Designs that match natural patterns of vision and cognition are easier to use and more enjoyable (Johnson, 2014).

Key UX Laws: Jakob's, Fitts's, Hick's, and Miller's

Designers can anticipate and address usability issues by applying foundational UX laws. These psychological principles help make systems feel intuitive. Here’s how each applies in practice:

  • Jakob’s Law: People expect digital experiences to be similar to those they've used before. When websites place the shopping cart in the top-right corner or use standard icons for navigation, they reduce friction. As Nielsen (2000) explains, consistency breeds usability and comfort.
  • Fitts’s Law: This law suggests that the time to reach a target depends on its size and distance. Small or far-away buttons slow users down. On mobile interfaces, making buttons larger and closer to natural touch zones improves interaction speed and accuracy (DIN, 2019).
  • Hick’s Law: More choices increase decision time. This principle underlies why designers break down complex tasks into smaller steps. For example, online forms that reveal only one question at a time reduce cognitive overload and improve completion rates (Sharma, 2021).
  • Miller’s Law: People can hold 7±2 items in working memory. Designers use this law to group related content and avoid showing too many elements at once. Interfaces that respect this cognitive limit feel manageable and friendly (Johnson, 2014).

Used together, these laws ensure interfaces align with how people naturally think and act. They turn user behavior from a mystery into a roadmap.

Empathy in Design: A Subtle Force

While cognitive science guides structure, empathy gives design its soul. Empathy allows designers to understand not only what users do, but why they do it. The IDEO Field Guide emphasizes the power of observation, deep interviews, and storytelling. Through these techniques, designers see beyond functionality and into emotional needs (IDEO.org, 2015).

ISO 9241-210 formalizes empathy by requiring designers to “specify the context of use.” This involves studying physical settings, social dynamics, and limitations such as literacy or access (DIN, 2019). When done well, empathy ensures the solution respects the whole human—not just the interface user.

Empathy also helps designers avoid one-size-fits-all assumptions. For example, building tools for people with visual impairments, low bandwidth access, or language barriers requires stepping into unfamiliar shoes. Great design doesn’t assume—it asks, listens, and adapts.

Example: A Public Health App

Let’s consider a mobile app created to help parents in underserved communities track child vaccinations. The design team begins by applying ISO 9241-210: they study the environment—shared phones, limited digital literacy, and inconsistent internet access (DIN, 2019).

Following IDEO’s process, they host community workshops to co-create solutions. Early sketches are tested with paper prototypes. Through empathy-driven research, they discover that users prefer icons over long text and want reminders tied to local health centers (IDEO.org, 2015).

Applying UX laws strengthens the interface. Fitts’s Law leads to large, tappable buttons. Hick’s Law ensures each screen has no more than three choices (Sharma, 2021). Miller’s Law encourages chunked flows (Johnson, 2014). Jakob’s Law guides layout decisions—mirroring common health apps to reduce learning time (Nielsen, 2000).

The result is more than a usable product—it’s a trusted tool that reflects local needs and values. Through perception, empathy, and iteration, the team builds something useful and respectful.

Final Thought: Design With People, Not Just for Them

Human-centered design is not just a method—it’s a mindset. It challenges us to listen, to see, and to care. The ISO 9241-210 framework provides structure and rigor, while IDEO’s Field Guide invites imagination and openness. Together, they help us design with—not just for—people (DIN, 2019; IDEO.org, 2015).

By grounding our choices in how users perceive and process information, and by applying UX laws rooted in psychology, we reduce friction and increase clarity. But above all, it is empathy that transforms good design into meaningful design. When we truly understand users, we create not just usable systems—but experiences that matter.

The future of design isn’t about screens or systems. It’s about people. The most enduring products are those that make people feel seen, supported, and empowered.

Thinking with the Interface

Good design doesn’t just look appealing—it supports the way users think, act, and remember. Interfaces aren’t just tools; they are cognitive environments that either help or hinder users in completing tasks efficiently. With increasing complexity in digital systems, understanding the relationship between human cognition and interface design is crucial. This blog post will explore how memory, mental models, feedback, and consistency contribute to usability, and how poor designs can impose unnecessary cognitive load.

Mental Models and Memory

When users interact with an interface, they rely on what are known as mental models—internal representations of how they believe a system should work. These models are formed from prior experiences with other systems and inform expectations. For example, when using a new email client, users expect to find a 'compose' button somewhere at the top left or right, because that’s where it is in Gmail, Outlook, and Yahoo. Breaking from this expectation confuses users.

Memory also plays a vital role. According to George Miller’s law, our working memory can only retain about 7±2 items at once. Designers must therefore avoid interfaces that require users to remember information across multiple steps. In Designing with the Mind in Mind, Johnson (2020) highlights how recognition-based interfaces outperform recall-based ones. Recognizing icons, options, or paths significantly lowers mental strain compared to remembering exact commands or steps.

Amazon’s 1-click ordering exemplifies this principle. Once users associate the button with a successful transaction, they mentally model that action as trustworthy. No lengthy forms or repeated confirmations are required—just a single, predictable action. This reduces memory burden and streamlines the decision-making process.

Consistency and Feedback

One of the most effective ways to reduce cognitive load is through consistency. Jakob’s Law, discussed in The Laws of UX (Babich, 2019), states that users prefer your site to function like other familiar platforms. When layouts, colors, or labels change arbitrarily, users are forced to relearn patterns, increasing the likelihood of errors and abandonment.

Feedback, on the other hand, provides immediate reassurance. Systems that respond to user actions—through animations, messages, or sound—help users feel in control. Consider Google Docs: when you type, a “Saving…” message appears, followed by “All changes saved.” This micro-interaction prevents user anxiety about data loss and improves overall satisfaction.

Compare that to a multi-step web form that, upon pressing "Submit", erases all data and provides no error message if something was filled incorrectly. Users feel frustrated, not only because their time was wasted, but because the system failed to communicate.

Recognition vs. Recall

Another foundational cognitive principle in UX is the distinction between recognition and recall. Recognition is passive and triggered by external cues, whereas recall requires active memory retrieval. Interfaces should rely more on the former.

Consider the difference between typing a command into a terminal versus clicking an icon on a GUI. The latter is more user-friendly for the average person. Spotify, for example, uses clearly labeled icons and playlist thumbnails. If users had to recall exact artist names or song titles without suggestions or images, the app would be far less usable.

Well-designed interfaces also offer visual continuity. Netflix, for instance, displays previously watched episodes, recommendations, and progress bars. Users are guided through their experience based on what they see—not what they have to remember.

Cognitive Load and Tesler’s Law

Cognitive load is the mental effort required to use a system. Designs that reduce unnecessary choices or ambiguity are more effective. Tesler’s Law tells us that "every application has an inherent amount of complexity. The job of the designer is to manage it."

Unfortunately, many systems shift this complexity onto users. Airline booking forms often ask for seating preferences, fare class, loyalty numbers, and insurance details—before showing available flights. This overloads users at the wrong time. A better approach would sequence these steps and reduce the visible fields at each stage, like what Hopper or Skyscanner do.

Duolingo illustrates good practice by letting learners make one choice at a time. Each lesson builds on past lessons, reinforcing learning without overwhelming. The interface uses bright icons, progress indicators, and encouraging messages. These combine to reduce mental strain while reinforcing motivation.

Good Design in Practice

The CarPoolin app interface below is a model of minimal cognitive load. First, the home screen uses friendly imagery that immediately communicates its purpose: travel and savings. Next, users are guided step-by-step—from selecting destination, date, to passenger count. Each action is visual, intuitive, and consistent with mobile norms.

This design aligns perfectly with users’ mental models of ride-sharing and travel booking apps. It avoids redundant fields, reduces the need for typing, and provides immediate feedback through button states and confirmations. More importantly, it respects the user's time and attention—two of the most valuable digital currencies today.

Compare this to a cluttered interface with dropdowns hidden under tabs, unclear icons, and no error recovery. Every misstep costs attention and patience. A well-designed interface is silent—it simply works.

Case Study: Poor Design and High Cognitive Load

Consider the user experience of an older tax filing system used in many countries before modernization efforts. The interface was filled with jargon, no tooltips, no inline validation, and mandatory multi-page forms that didn’t save progress. Users were expected to calculate deductions manually, understand legal tax language, and format values precisely to avoid rejection.

This system not only violated best practices but ignored Tesler’s Law entirely. Complexity was dumped onto the user. The resulting experience was one of anxiety, confusion, and frequent errors. Redesigns of these systems now include guided flows, help text, autocomplete, and live chat support—features that recognize user limitations and ease mental effort.

Designing for Accessibility and Cognitive Differences

Designing with cognition in mind is especially vital for users with ADHD, dyslexia, or age-related cognitive decline. A design that minimizes distractions, uses predictable layouts, and offers progressive disclosure (showing information as needed) can dramatically improve usability for these groups.

For example, Apple's iOS “Focus” mode and interface simplifications benefit not only neurodivergent users but everyone. Similarly, readable fonts, ample spacing, and avoidance of scrolling text improve comprehension. These aren’t just ethical considerations—they enhance performance and satisfaction across all user types.

Inclusive design respects the diversity of human cognition and acknowledges that everyone benefits when systems are clear, predictable, and accommodating. As Norman (2013) argued, design should be for how people actually behave—not how we wish they behaved.

Future Directions: Cognitive Design in the Age of AI

As artificial intelligence becomes increasingly embedded in everyday interfaces—from voice assistants to recommendation engines—understanding user cognition is more important than ever. Systems now adapt to users in real time, but poor cognitive alignment can lead to confusion and loss of trust.

Consider AI chatbots: if their responses lack transparency or fail to reflect users' mental models of a helpful assistant, users may abandon them quickly. Similarly, algorithmic recommendation systems (like those on YouTube or Netflix) need to strike a balance between novelty and familiarity. Too much change disrupts consistency; too little feels stale.

Emerging trends in UX include adaptive interfaces, context-aware simplification, and emotion-sensing feedback loops. These aim to align system behavior even more closely with how humans perceive, decide, and remember. But the fundamentals remain the same—respect memory limits, reduce load, and build around real mental models.

Conclusion

Design is not just about aesthetics—it's about empathy. It’s about knowing that your user is a human with limited time, limited memory, and a desire for clarity. By embracing cognitive principles like recognition over recall, consistent structure, immediate feedback, and minimizing unnecessary load, designers can create systems that feel intuitive and delightful.

Tesler’s Law reminds us: complexity exists, but it’s our job to manage it—not pass it on to the user. From tax systems to mobile apps, and from accessibility to AI, design should meet users where they are—supporting, guiding, and never overwhelming. Cognitive-based design is not just smart UX—it’s human-centered thinking at its best.

Designing for Flow: A UX Analysis of LinkedIn’s Interface

By Aroona Fayyaz | Module 3.1 Blog Post

Introduction

LinkedIn, with more than 900 million users globally as of 2025, stands as the world’s leading platform for professional networking. It offers a range of features designed to help users manage their professional identities, build connections, search and apply for jobs, share content, and follow companies and influencers. Despite its scale, the success of LinkedIn depends on how well its interface facilitates intuitive, goal-directed user interaction. Poor design could turn opportunity into confusion, while effective UX logic helps people navigate their careers with clarity and confidence.

This blog post presents a comprehensive analysis of LinkedIn’s interface structure and logic. Drawing upon cognitive psychology, usability heuristics, and principles from user experience (UX) design, the evaluation will highlight both positive and frustrating experiences using LinkedIn. It also applies three foundational UX laws—the Aesthetic–Usability Effect, the Von Restorff Effect, and Postel’s Law—along with references from core course readings such as Johnson’s Designing with the Mind in Mind and Lidwell’s Universal Principles of Design. The final goal is to demonstrate how thoughtful interface decisions shape perception, guide behavior, and build trust.

Positive Experience: Clear Navigation and Predictable Layout

One of LinkedIn’s greatest strengths lies in its interface consistency and intuitive navigation design. From the moment a user logs in, the interface offers a streamlined and familiar top navigation bar that includes key sections like “Home,” “My Network,” “Jobs,” “Messaging,” and “Notifications.” These are supported by universal icons and meaningful labels, ensuring quick recognition. This structure reflects Jakob’s Law, which states that users prefer interfaces that behave similarly to ones they have already experienced. The layout closely mirrors other high-use platforms such as Facebook or Google, making it immediately familiar—even for new users.

According to Chapter 10 of Johnson’s Designing with the Mind in Mind, good UX minimizes memory load and promotes the recognition of patterns. LinkedIn applies this principle by keeping its main functions accessible via top-level navigation while relegating secondary options—such as settings, privacy, or help—to drop-down menus. This spatial hierarchy reduces visual clutter and focuses the user’s attention on frequent tasks. Consistency in positioning, design, and language supports rapid orientation, especially among users who access the site multiple times per week.

The structure of user profiles on LinkedIn also enhances learnability and clarity. Each profile features a large banner, followed by the user’s name, headline, contact info, and experience, laid out in a clear vertical hierarchy. This layout supports the principle of progressive disclosure: more detailed information appears lower on the page, after the most critical data. The organization is not only visually coherent but also supports scanning—a key factor for recruiters and hiring managers.

Additionally, interaction feedback is strong. When hovering over buttons such as “Connect” or “Message,” users receive immediate visual changes, often supported by tooltips. These microinteractions reinforce that the controls are functional, clickable, and safe to explore. This aligns with what Johnson (2014) refers to as “perceptible affordances”—elements that naturally indicate their function without the need for instructions. Visual feedback through color changes and icon animation keeps users oriented and confident in their actions.

On a visual level, LinkedIn’s design benefits from the Aesthetic–Usability Effect, a UX law that suggests users perceive more attractive interfaces as easier to use (Lidwell et al., 2010). The site’s use of a professional blue-and-white palette, sans-serif fonts, and liberal use of whitespace cultivates a sense of reliability and order. Users are more tolerant of minor usability flaws when the overall experience “feels” polished. For example, even if a search query produces limited results, the clean layout and responsive controls reduce frustration.

Another beneficial design choice is the dashboard area on user homepages, which provides contextual feedback about profile views, search appearances, and post engagements. These summary cards present data in a simple, digestible format that supports user learning. As noted in usability studies, feedback should be relevant, timely, and clearly presented to support task efficiency. LinkedIn succeeds in this aspect by not overwhelming the user with raw data, but instead offering top-level summaries with optional drill-down links.

In short, LinkedIn’s navigation and visual design work well because they harmonize usability principles with polished aesthetics. The interface minimizes friction, supports exploration, and encourages engagement without overwhelming the user. These are the signs of a mature, experience-centered design philosophy that puts user needs front and center.

UX Analysis: Interface Design, Laws, and User Experience

Impact of Interface Layout and Control Design on User Behavior

Interface layout and control design are foundational to shaping how users interact with digital platforms. Layout determines how information is organized visually on the screen, affecting what users notice first, how they scan content, and how easily they can complete tasks. Controls—such as buttons, search bars, toggles, and filters—serve as interaction points that empower users to manipulate the system and achieve their goals efficiently.

Taking LinkedIn as an example, the homepage feed uses a vertical scrolling layout that mixes multiple content types, including posts, ads, job suggestions, and notifications. This layout influences user behavior by encouraging continuous exploration but also risks overwhelming users due to the high volume of information. The placement of control elements, such as the “Easy Apply” button positioned directly beneath job descriptions, facilitates quick action but may inadvertently promote hasty decisions if users do not fully read job requirements.

Moreover, LinkedIn’s search bar employs autocomplete and error-tolerant input processing, guiding users toward relevant results even when their queries are incomplete or misspelled. This control design reduces cognitive load by minimizing the effort required to find information, which in turn increases user satisfaction and efficiency. The ability to apply filters or toggle feed sorting enables users to customize their experience, although the limited filtering options sometimes leave users feeling out of control.

Such interface design decisions directly impact user emotions and behaviors. A clear, consistent layout with intuitive controls fosters confidence and reduces frustration, promoting prolonged engagement. Conversely, cluttered layouts and ambiguous controls can cause confusion, increase cognitive friction, and lead to abandonment of tasks or even the platform itself.

Reflection on UX Laws: Aesthetic–Usability Effect, Von Restorff Effect, and Postel’s Law

UX laws provide essential principles that inform effective interface design. Three particularly relevant laws are the Aesthetic–Usability Effect, the Von Restorff Effect, and Postel’s Law, all of which are evident in modern platforms like LinkedIn.

Aesthetic–Usability Effect

The Aesthetic–Usability Effect, described by Johnson (2014), states that users perceive visually attractive designs as easier to use. This perception influences users’ willingness to engage with an interface and their overall satisfaction, independent of actual usability. LinkedIn’s clean, minimalist design—characterized by muted colors, consistent typography, and ample white space—leverages this effect by conveying professionalism and clarity. The polished aesthetic lowers users’ initial anxiety about navigating complex features, encouraging exploration and repeated use.

Von Restorff Effect

The Von Restorff Effect posits that items that visually stand out from their surroundings are more likely to be noticed and remembered (Lidwell, Holden, & Butler, 2010). LinkedIn effectively uses this principle through profile banners like the green “Open to Work” and purple “Hiring” badges, which contrast sharply with the platform’s subdued color palette. These badges immediately draw attention to user status or intent, increasing visibility among recruiters and network contacts.

Additionally, brightly colored callouts such as “Top Applicant” or “Remote” tags on job postings utilize this effect to guide scanning behavior. By highlighting critical information in a visually distinctive manner, LinkedIn reduces the time users spend searching for relevant details, enhancing efficiency and perceived value.

Postel’s Law (Robustness Principle)

Postel’s Law advises designers to “be liberal in what you accept from others, and conservative in what you send” (Norman, 2013). This principle promotes tolerance for user errors and inconsistencies, which enhances usability and accessibility. LinkedIn applies this law in its search functionality by accepting misspelled or incomplete keywords and still providing relevant suggestions. For example, typing “marketng manager” or “data anlyst” still yields appropriate results, reducing frustration for users who may make typographical errors or use non-native English.

Similarly, the platform’s messaging and connection workflows demonstrate error tolerance. Users can send messages without strict adherence to punctuation or capitalization rules, and LinkedIn generates polite default connection requests if the user opts not to customize. These design choices align with Norman’s concept of “error tolerance,” ensuring users can succeed even when inputs are imperfect.

Personal Experience: Frustration with “Easy Apply” Feedback

A particularly frustrating experience I encountered involved LinkedIn’s “Easy Apply” job application feature. After completing and submitting an application, the interface displayed only a brief, fleeting confirmation message with no further details. I was left wondering whether my application had been successfully received, who would review it, and what the next steps would be. This lack of clear, persistent feedback increased my anxiety, especially given the high stakes of job searching.

This experience highlights a failure to adhere to one of the fundamental usability heuristics described by Johnson (2014): making system status visible at all times. Effective interfaces provide users with clear indications of what is happening in response to their actions to build trust and reduce uncertainty. LinkedIn’s minimal feedback after applying contrasts with its otherwise polished design and interrupts the user’s sense of flow.

Furthermore, this design oversight contradicts Hick’s Law, which states that decision time increases with complexity and uncertainty (Nielsen, 2020). When users receive ambiguous feedback, cognitive load rises, and their trust in the system diminishes. By failing to provide meaningful post-application information, LinkedIn inadvertently discourages repeat use and undermines emotional confidence during critical moments.

Supporting Concepts from Designing with the Mind in Mind and The Laws of UX

Johnson (2014) emphasizes the importance of microinteractions and feedback in sustaining user engagement. He notes, “Clear, perceptible feedback enhances user trust and task fluency” (p. 120). LinkedIn’s subtle hover previews and loading indicators serve this function well, reassuring users that the system is responsive. However, these small signals are insufficient in moments where users require explicit confirmation, such as after submitting job applications.

Lidwell, Holden, and Butler (2010) describe the Von Restorff Effect as a critical tool in creating effective visual hierarchy. LinkedIn’s use of vibrant badges and tags leverages this principle expertly to direct attention and reduce the cognitive effort required for scanning dense information.

Norman (2013) advocates for design that anticipates user errors and accommodates variability. LinkedIn’s forgiving search and messaging interfaces embody this “error tolerance,” promoting inclusive usability across diverse user skills and contexts.

Finally, Nielsen (2020) highlights Hick’s Law as essential to reducing cognitive friction by limiting choices and simplifying decision paths. LinkedIn’s overloaded homepage feed often contradicts this law by presenting excessive and repetitive content, leading to user frustration and disengagement.

Frustrating Interface Logic: Feed Overload and Content Redundancy

Beyond the “Easy Apply” issue, LinkedIn’s homepage feed often suffers from clutter and content overload. Users are presented with a mix of updates, ads, job postings, and suggestions, many of which feel algorithmically prioritized but lack relevance. This clutter introduces significant cognitive friction and undermines the platform’s professional focus.

Hick’s Law states that decision time increases as the number of choices grows (Nielsen, 2020). When faced with excessive content, users find it difficult to discern which posts or job suggestions warrant attention. The result is cognitive overload, where users may skim superficially or disengage entirely.

Additionally, content redundancy—such as repeated job postings or duplicate updates—exacerbates this problem by making the interface feel repetitive and uncurated. Users report encountering the same posts multiple times or irrelevant jobs based on their profile, indicating shortcomings in personalization algorithms and filtering heuristics.

This interface clutter violates key usability principles by reducing the clarity and focus essential for effective information architecture (Morville & Rosenfeld, 2007). Without control over what content appears first or stronger filtering options, users feel more like passive consumers of algorithmic noise than active participants in a tailored professional network.

Mobile UX Challenges: Inconsistencies and Cognitive Disruption

LinkedIn’s mobile app, while visually aligned with its desktop counterpart, introduces unexpected cognitive friction due to inconsistencies in navigation and interaction. Menu items can shift position, gestures may behave differently across platforms, and the feed occasionally reloads unexpectedly, causing users to lose their place.

These inconsistencies undermine the “internal model” that users build to predict system behavior, as described by Norman (2013). When users’ expectations are violated, confusion and frustration increase. Tasks like profile editing or viewing analytics become more cumbersome due to buried controls and additional navigation steps.

The lack of persistent scroll memory also disrupts flow. For example, after viewing a job and tapping into the company page, returning users are placed back at the top of the feed instead of their previous scroll position. This design lapse forces repeated scanning and increases memory load, counteracting usability heuristics advocating minimal cognitive effort.

To maintain user engagement across devices, LinkedIn must enhance mobile predictability by standardizing gestures, supporting deep-linking, and preserving scroll positions. Users expect seamless, fluid experiences whether on desktop or mobile, particularly for a career-focused platform used on the go.

Design Recommendations for Clarity, Flow, and Empathy

Based on the analysis above, the following recommendations could improve LinkedIn’s user experience substantially:

  • Streamline the feed: Introduce granular personalization options that allow users to prioritize content types such as industry news, career milestones, or connections’ posts. Implement smarter content filters to reduce redundancy and increase relevance.
  • Enhance application feedback: Provide a dedicated post-application confirmation screen displaying visual confirmation, recruiter contact information (if available), and clear next steps to alleviate user anxiety.
  • Improve mobile consistency: Standardize navigation layout and gesture interactions across platforms. Preserve scroll memory and support deep-linking to ensure seamless transitions.
  • Humanize notifications: Revise system-generated alerts to adopt a more personal tone, avoiding robotic or generic language to foster emotional connection.
  • Increase algorithmic transparency: Offer users explanations for content prioritization and job suggestions, building trust through informed engagement and reducing perceived randomness.

Implementing these recommendations will not only reduce cognitive friction but also strengthen user trust, emotional connection, and loyalty, enhancing LinkedIn’s role as a professional networking platform.

Interaction That Matters: Designing with Feeling, Timing, and Ethics

By Aroona Fayyaz | Module 3.2 Blog Post

UX Laws, Motion, and Interface Intuition

Interaction design is a multidisciplinary practice that blends psychology, technology, and art to create digital experiences that are intuitive, efficient, and emotionally satisfying. While it might seem straightforward on the surface, the craft of interaction design involves deep understanding of human cognitive limits and behaviors. This is where foundational UX laws come in—these laws are distilled from research and decades of design experience and offer essential guidelines that help designers shape digital products people love to use.

Fitts’s Law is one of the most cited principles in UX. It articulates a clear relationship between the size of a target and the distance to it, indicating how quickly a user can reach a target. This has significant implications on the design of clickable or tappable elements in user interfaces. For example, small icons in the corner of a mobile app can slow down interaction and increase frustration, especially for users with motor impairments or those using their device in unstable environments, like walking or commuting. On the other hand, large touch targets placed within easy thumb reach can reduce errors and make navigation feel natural.

In a practical sense, Fitts’s Law informs common mobile app designs where key actions—like submitting a form or playing a video—are given large, distinct buttons placed at the bottom of the screen. This placement aligns with ergonomic studies showing that thumbs naturally rest near the bottom half of a device, making interactions faster and more comfortable. Apps like Instagram and TikTok leverage this by placing their main navigation controls along the bottom bar, making switching between home, search, notifications, and profile both seamless and fast. Ignoring this law can cause interface elements to feel "unreachable" or cumbersome, ultimately diminishing user satisfaction.

Jakob’s Law, coined by usability expert Jakob Nielsen, teaches that users spend the majority of their time on other websites and expect your site or app to work similarly. This principle underscores the importance of familiarity in design—people bring mental models shaped by commonly accepted layouts, controls, and interaction patterns. For example, placing the main navigation menu in the top or left side of the screen is an expected norm, and deviating from this without clear benefit risks confusing users. Similarly, using standard icons like a magnifying glass for search or a hamburger icon for a menu ensures users immediately recognize and understand how to interact.

In one of my roles working with a university's student portal redesign team, Jakob’s Law was a critical consideration. The existing portal had a navigation structure that was unique to the university but inconsistent with other widely used platforms. This unfamiliar design caused significant user frustration, leading to frequent support tickets. We restructured the portal navigation to follow common web conventions, grouped related features logically, and applied standard iconography. Post-launch usability tests showed a 30% reduction in task completion time and positive user feedback highlighting the ease of finding information. This experience reinforced the power of meeting users where they are instead of forcing them to learn new interaction paradigms.

Tesler’s Law, or the Law of Conservation of Complexity, states that every application has an inherent amount of complexity that cannot be reduced, only shifted between the user and the system. This law forces designers to think critically about what complexity to expose and what to hide. Great design removes unnecessary cognitive load from users by automating repetitive tasks, simplifying decision-making, and revealing complexity only when needed.

For example, in a project where I collaborated with a nonprofit focused on community health data collection, we faced the challenge of complex data entry forms required by regulatory standards. Our initial forms presented all fields upfront, overwhelming users. By applying Tesler’s Law, we restructured the form into progressive disclosure, showing only essential fields initially and allowing users to expand sections for additional detail if necessary. We also integrated real-time validation and smart defaults based on previous inputs, reducing user errors and cognitive load. The end result was a more approachable interface that respected user time and attention without compromising data integrity.

Beyond static design principles, motion plays a vital role in user experience. Motion design isn’t simply decorative—it provides feedback, guides attention, and makes interactions feel alive. For instance, when a button visually "presses down" on tap, the user receives confirmation that their input was registered. These microinteractions create an emotional connection, signaling that the system is responsive and trustworthy.

Microinteractions are small, contained moments that revolve around a single use case—such as liking a post, toggling a setting, or receiving a notification. Well-designed microinteractions have four parts: a trigger (user action or system event), rules (what happens in response), feedback (visual or audio cues), and loops/modes (how long the interaction lasts or if it can be undone). For example, in an app I helped develop for task management at a local nonprofit, we introduced animated checkmarks when users completed tasks. The subtle bounce effect was not just fun—it gave users a sense of accomplishment and encouraged continued engagement.

However, motion must be carefully balanced. Excessive or poorly timed animations can overwhelm or annoy users, especially those with cognitive or vestibular sensitivities. Accessibility guidelines recommend respecting users’ preferences for reduced motion, which many operating systems now support. During our nonprofit project, we ensured our app checked for this setting and adapted animations accordingly—providing a smooth experience for all users.

In summary, effective interaction design is grounded in a blend of science and empathy. Laws like Fitts’s Law, Jakob’s Law, and Tesler’s Law guide us in designing interfaces that are not only usable but intuitive and satisfying. Thoughtful use of motion and microinteractions enhances user understanding and emotional connection. From my experiences working with university teams and nonprofits, the best results come when we listen to users, test assumptions, and use these laws as a foundation rather than a constraint. After all, at the heart of interaction design is the desire to create digital experiences that feel natural, human, and empowering.

Persuasive Design, Ethics, and Emotional Responsibility

Persuasive design is an influential subset of UX focused on shaping user behavior to benefit both users and organizations. While it offers powerful techniques to motivate users toward positive actions—like adopting healthier habits or improving financial literacy—it also carries significant ethical considerations. Responsible designers must balance the effectiveness of persuasion with respect for autonomy, transparency, and emotional well-being.

One foundational concept in this field is the identification and avoidance of dark patterns, a term coined by Harry Brignull to describe design choices that trick or coerce users into unintended actions (Brignull, 2010). Examples include hidden subscription fees, disguised ads, and forced continuity, where users find it difficult to cancel a service. Dark patterns exploit cognitive biases and attentional limits, damaging user trust and potentially exposing companies to legal risks.

Recognizing the risks, many organizations now emphasize ethical design frameworks that promote user empowerment rather than manipulation. The Nielsen Norman Group’s Ethical Heuristics advocate principles such as full disclosure of persuasive intent, user control over decisions, and providing easy opt-out options (Nielsen Norman Group, 2023). These heuristics serve as guiding beacons to ensure that persuasive design supports user interests without undermining informed consent.

Gamification is one of the most common persuasive techniques. By integrating game-like elements such as points, badges, and streaks, apps aim to increase user engagement and motivation. For example, Duolingo uses daily streaks to encourage consistent language practice. While this can boost habit formation, it can also trigger anxiety or guilt when users break their streaks. Recognizing this, Duolingo added the option to "freeze" streaks, allowing users to pause progress without penalty, which reflects a commitment to balancing motivation with user well-being (Duolingo, 2023).

In my personal project designing a mindfulness app for university students, we initially implemented streaks and frequent reminders to encourage daily meditation. However, feedback revealed that many users felt pressured and stressed by the rigid tracking system, which was counterproductive for a mindfulness tool. After redesigning the app to emphasize flexibility, with optional reminders and a focus on user choice, we observed improved user satisfaction and retention. This experience highlighted how ethical persuasive design requires ongoing user research, empathy, and willingness to iterate based on emotional impact.

Persuasive design is closely tied to emotional responsibility. Each interaction can shape a user’s mood, motivation, and trust. Ethical designers must consider the emotional states that features induce—avoiding fear, guilt, or frustration as motivators. Instead, designers should aim to foster feelings of competence, autonomy, and relatedness, which psychological research shows support long-term engagement and wellbeing.

Another example comes from a digital fundraising tool I worked on with a local nonprofit. Early versions used urgency messaging like "Only 1 spot left!" to drive quick donations. Although this tactic temporarily increased clicks, many users expressed skepticism and a sense of manipulation. After switching to transparent progress indicators that showed real impact ("You’ve helped fund 3 meals"), we saw donations become more consistent and user feedback became more positive. This reinforced the idea that ethical persuasion grounded in honesty and respect outperforms pressure tactics.

Persuasive technology expert Johnson (2020) emphasizes supporting user agency rather than overriding it. This means designing systems that scaffold progress without exploiting vulnerabilities. For example, offering meaningful choices, clear explanations, and undo options respects users' autonomy and builds trust.

In conclusion, persuasive design is a double-edged sword. It can improve lives when done thoughtfully, but can cause harm if misused. As UX professionals, we must approach persuasion with humility, transparency, and empathy. Designing with ethics means designing for human dignity. When we align persuasion with user values and emotions, we create digital experiences that not only achieve goals but also enrich lives.
