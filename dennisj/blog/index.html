Module 1.1 Blog Post

Systems thinking is a foundational methodology in systems engineering and digital environments. It focuses on understanding how individual parts interact within a system. Rather than analyzing problems in isolation, systems thinking demonstrates a holistic perspective that considers component relationships and dependencies. In modern digital systems, this approach is important for effective design and problem solving.

Define Systems Thinking

To define system thinking, we first look at what a system is. A system can be described as a group of components that work together as an interconnected network to achieve a specific purpose. While the exact structure of a system depends on its context, the main idea is that all parts are connected and influence one another.

According to Peter Checkland, systems can be categorized into five types: natural systems, designed physical systems, designed abstract systems, human activity systems, and transcendental systems. Natural systems are not created by humans, this can include weather patterns, solar, nature, ecosystems, and time. Designed physical systems are human-made and consist of tangible components such as smartphones, cars, and household appliances. In contrast, designed abstract systems do not have a physical component but are used to represent ideas or concepts, such as math equations or theoretical frameworks (Holt, 2021).

Human activity systems involve people working together toward a shared goal. These include social groups, political systems, and organizations. Transcendental systems refer to systems that are beyond human understanding, including spiritual or philosophical concepts and unsolvable problems.

Each system is made up of elements that interact with each other. These elements do not function alone, rather, they depend on each other to keep the system operating effectively. An important aspect of understanding systems is recognizing the difference between a system of interest and the supporting or enabling systems that help it function. This shows how systems are often part of a larger, more complex network (Crawley, 2022).

Systems also exist for a purpose, which can be understood through their requirements, features, and goals. Requirements are the essential functions a system must have. For example, a smartphone must be able to make phone calls. Features are additional capabilities, such as a flashlight or camera. Goals represent the overall purpose of the system, such as enabling communication and access to information (Holt, 2021).

Because systems are complex, problems are inevitable. A network may develop security vulnerabilities, or a vehicle may experience mechanical issues. This is where systems engineering and systems thinking become important, rather than focusing on a single issue, systems thinking encourages a broader perspective.

In simple terms, systems thinking is the practice of viewing a system as a whole rather than individual parts. It involves understanding how components interact and how changes in one area can affect the entire system. This approach is important when dealing with complex systems, as it allows for better problem-solving and a deeper understanding of how interconnected elements work together.

How it differs from more traditional, linear ways of thinking

Traditionally, problems are approached using linear thinking. Linear thinking follows a step-by-step process where one action leads directly to another, also described as moving from point A to point B. This approach focuses on a clear cause-and-effect relationship and works well for simple, predictable problems that have an established resolution. For example, when trouble shooting a computer issue, a linear approach might involve checking each step one at a time, such as restarting the device, inspecting cables, or testing individual components.

While this method can be effective in simple situations, it has limitations when applied to complex digital systems. Linear thinking does not fully consider things like feedback loops, interdependencies, or the broader environment in which a system operates. As a result, it may not consider how one change can impact multiple parts of a system or create unintended consequences. In fast-paced and interconnected environments, this narrow focus can lead to temporary fixes rather than long-term solutions.

As we previously learned, systems thinking takes a more holistic approach. Instead of viewing problems individually, it considers how different components interact within a larger system. Systems thinking emphasizes relationships, patterns, and the way elements influence one another over time. This perspective is especially useful in digital environments, where systems often involve multiple layers, including software, hardware, networks, and users.

Comparing the two approaches demonstrates their main differences. Linear thinking focuses on individual components and relies on step-by-step solutions, making it best for problems with clear and predictable outcomes. For complex designs, systems thinking is more suitable. It recognizes that problems often involve multiple interconnected factors and encourages more flexible and adaptive strategies. Rather than aiming for a quick fix, systems thinking seeks to improve the overall system by addressing root causes and long-term impacts.

Each approach also has its strengths and weaknesses. Linear thinking is efficient and easy to apply in simple situations, but it can lack when dealing with complex systems. Systems thinking requires more analysis, and a broader perspective. It provides a deeper understanding of how systems function, and how to improve them effectively. In today’s digital environments, where complexity is in almost every system, this way of thinking offers a more comprehensive and sustainable way to solve problems.

Considering all of this, one important takeaway from comparing systems thinking and the traditional linear thinking is that systems thinking is not meant to replace linear thinking, but rather to expand on it in order to support more complex situations. For example, if an IT specialist is troubleshooting a computer, they might begin with a linear approach of following a step-by-step process to eliminate potential issues. If that does not resolve the problem, they can then shift to a systems thinking approach and analyze which components are not functioning and how they are connected. This combination of approaches allows for more efficient and effective problem solving. In digital environments where systems are constantly evolving, this flexibility is especially important. Using both approaches together provides a balanced and practical way to design, analyze, and improve systems over time.

Why a model-based approach is applicable in the design of complex digital systems

As complex systems continue to increase over time, model-based systems engineering (MBSE) has become applicable as it helps engineers and designers understand and organize system components more effectively. A model-based approach is a form of systems engineering that supports the design, analysis, and development of complex systems by using structured models, such as diagrams, rather than purely text-based documentation.

Complex digital systems are made up of many interacting components such as software, hardware, networks, and data flows. While the system itself may change over time, its main purpose remains the same, giving each component a clear function within the larger system. However, these systems will sometimes contain accidental complexity, including unplanned difficulties that arise from interactions between components. This is where systems thinking and model-based approaches become essential (Shevchenko, 2020).

A model-based approach represents a system visually or structurally, often through diagrams that show how components are connected. For example, arrows may represent data flow between software applications, servers, and users. This abstraction allows the system to be broken down into different views. Each view focuses on a specific aspect of the system. For instance, a desktop computer system could be represented through separate views for hardware, software, networking, and user interaction. Each view allows for a deeper understanding and lens into how that part contributes to the overall system.

In practice, a model-based approach can be organized into three key components: approach, goal, and visualization. The approach defines what information is included in the model and how system elements are structured, often using frameworks or ontologies. The goal connects system requirements to model views, making sure that each part of the system aligns with its intended purpose. Visualization refers to the graphical representation of the system, using standardized notation (language) to clearly communicate relationships and interactions between components (Shevchenko, 2020).

An important part of model-based systems engineering is compliance. This refers to ensuring that the system follows relevant standards and best practices when in use. This might include process-based standards, framework-based standards, or application-based standards, depending on the industry and organization. Compliance ensures consistency, reliability, and scalability across design systems (Holt, 2021).

Compared to traditional paper-based documentation, a model-based approach offers significant advantages. Paper-based systems can be unclear, inconsistent, and difficult to update. They may also change depending on the author and are typically not digital making collaboration challenging. In contrast, digital models provide standardized, editable, and multi-view representations of a system. This improves communication between teams and stakeholders and allows for easier updates and validation of system design.

A model-based approach integrates systems thinking and systems engineering by providing a structured way to understand complex systems. By modeling relationships, dependencies, and interactions, it allows engineers to predict system behavior and improve design outcomes (Shevchenko, 2020).

Ultimately, systems thinking provides a shift away from traditional, step by step problem solving, toward a more intertwined and holistic understanding of how systems operate. By recognizing relationships between components and the importance of feedback loops, it becomes possible to better understand and manage complex digital environments. When combined with model-based systems engineering, this approach becomes even more applicable. It allows systems to be visualized, tested, and improved in a safe and structured way. Together, these methods support more effective design, clearer communication, and better long-term system performance in the digital environment.

Module 1.2 Blog Post

As we previously learned, systems are everywhere, from political and education systems to technology and infrastructure. Expanding on systems engineering, which is a holistic approach of looking at the system as a whole rather than individual parts, one important concept within systems engineering is the Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC). The SDLC provides a structured way to design, build, and maintain systems over time. In today’s world, where systems are constantly evolving, having a clear process in place is important for efficiency, reliability, and success.

What is the Systems Development Life Cycle?

The Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC) is a framework that is used to create and manage information technology systems. It is a structured process that guides how a system is planned, built, used, and eventually retired. Instead of treating a system as something unchangeable, the SDLC recognizes that systems evolve over time.

An important concept within the SDLC is the idea of a life cycle. A life cycle represents the stages a system goes through from beginning to end, with each stage marking a specific point in the system’s cycle. Another important concept is the life cycle model, which defines how those stages are organized and carried out. In other words, the life cycle shows what happens, while the life cycle model shows how it happens. This distinction is important because different models can approach the same stages in different ways depending on the needs of the system (Holt, 2021).

SDLC Stages

The SDLC includes several main stages: conception, development, production, utilization, support, and retirement. The conception stage focuses on identifying the need for a system and defining its purpose. The development stage explores solutions to meet those needs and selects the most efficient approach. The production stage takes that solution and turns it into a functioning system, including important processes such as verification and validation to make sure it works correctly (Holt, 2021).

Once the system is complete, it moves into the utilization stage, where it is used by end users and stakeholders. This stage can also include training users so they can properly interact with the system. Working in tandem with utilization is the support stage, which focuses on maintaining the system, addressing user issues, and making necessary updates. Lastly, the retirement stage describes how the system is safely and securely dismantled when it is no longer needed, this includes properly disposing of and handling any data to avoid security risks.

It is important to note that these stages are not always sequential or required in every situation. Depending on the system and the life cycle model being used, stages may be skipped, repeated, or adjusted. The ability to adapt to a system allows the SDLC to be applied to a variety of systems with different levels of complexity.

SDLC Phases

The stages of the SDLC can be segmented into more detailed phases that act as a step-by-step guide for developing systems. These phases include planning, analysis, design, development, testing, implementation, and maintenance. The planning phase involves defining the scope of the project, identifying needs, setting timelines, and allocating resources. This helps make sure that the project is well organized from the beginning.

The analysis phase focuses on understanding user needs and identifying potential challenges. This may include gathering input from users, reviewing existing processes, and establishing requirements. The design phase uses the gathered information to create essentially a blueprint of the system’s structure, including its architecture, databases, interfaces, and components.

During the development phase, the system is built according to the design. This includes coding, implementing features, and creating the necessary components. The testing phase then follows, which is a vital step where errors and issues can be identified and fixed before implementation. The implementation phase is when the system is deployed and made available to users. This often includes installation, data migration, and user training.

Lastly, the maintenance phase ensures that the system continues to operate effectively as time goes on. This includes updates, performance improvements, security patches, and ongoing support. This phase is important in today’s reliance on the digital environment and systems (GeeksforGeeks, 2023).

Breaking down the SDLC, we can see that it provides a structured but flexible approach when it comes to developing systems. It helps reduce risk, improve system quality, and allows systems to remain functional throughout their life cycle.

Life Cycle Models

Linear Life Cycle Model

The linear Life Cycle Model is a straightforward, sequential framework, also known as the Waterfall Model. It follows an order of stages: concept, development, production, utilization, and retirement. Each stage begins after the previous stage is completed, meaning there is no going back once a stage is completed.

This model is typically used for projects where the needs and objectives are clearly defined and unlikely to change, along with the technology being used. Because of its structure, the linear model is easier to manage, especially for smaller or short-term projects. It is also predictable, since each step follows a direct path.

The main characteristic of this model is its simplicity. The process is easy to follow and understand, which can be a big advantage for teams that need clear direction and strong documentation. However, this structure also has limitations. With the lack of flexibility, it is difficult to adapt if the needs change later in the process. Also, problems may not be discovered until later stages, or even after deployment, which can make them harder and could be more costly to fix (Holt, 2021).

In short, the linear Life Cycle Model works well for simple projects, but is not well suited for complex systems where change is likely. This limitation leads to the need for more flexible approaches, such as iterative and incremental models.

Iterative Life Cycle Model

In contrast to the linear Life Cycle Model’s singular path, the iterative Life Cycle Model is based on repeated cycles, or iterations. Instead of moving through each stage once, the system can move back and forth between stages such as concept, development, and production. This allows the system to be improved over time.

The iterative Life Cycle Model breaks the system into smaller parts, or iterations, that are developed and improved individually. Each iteration goes through similar stages, but focuses on improving specific parts of the system. This approach allows for better flexibility, since errors can be identified and fixed within a specific part of the system without restarting the entire process (Radigan, 2026).

An advantage of this model is that it allows for continuous improvement. Since each iteration is small parts of the whole, updates and fixes can be found and made quickly compared to the linear model. This approach is commonly used in the software industry because it supports the constant evolution in the tech world.

However, there are also disadvantages. Because the system is done in parts, the systems may be released before it is fully complete or debugged, which can lead to user or stakeholder frustration. Since changes can be made frequently, the overall scope of the project may grow over time (Holt, 2021). Regardless, the flexibility of the iterative model makes it a great option for projects where requirements and needs may change.

Incremental Life Cycle Model

The incremental Life Cycle Model is similar to the iterative Life Cycle Model in that it does not follow a strict linear path. However, it differs in that the system is developed and released in multiple parts, otherwise known as increments. Instead of delivering the entire system at once, smaller pieces of the system are built and deployed over time.

In other models, the concept stage defines the system needs and defines the purpose, whereas for this model, the concept stage defines the overall system requirements. The development and production stages focus on creating partial solutions that support the system as a whole. Each increment adds new functions, allowing users to start interacting with the system before it is fully complete. This would be useful with projects like e-commerce websites, mobile apps, or cloud-based platforms (Radigan, 2026).

An advantage of this approach is that developers can test and evaluate each part of the system as it is released. This makes it easier to identify errors early and make sure that each component is functioning correctly. It is especially useful for larger or long-term projects where flexibility is required, or where the system must be used before it is fully finished.

Like the other models, there are still disadvantages such as the incremental model is not suitable for every project. Some systems cannot easily be broken into smaller parts, which makes this approach difficult to apply. Integrating different increments can also be challenging, and may lead to inconsistencies in the overall system design if developers are not careful (Holt, 2021).

Thinking in System Life Cycles

Thinking in system life cycles supports more effective and innovative decision-making. Technology systems are constantly evolving, and in today’s digital world, they require routine maintenance and updates to remain functional and secure. This is important when thinking of cybersecurity, where threats are evolving alongside the creation of systems, and systems must constantly adapt.

System life cycle thinking also provides a perspective on how different components of a system relate to one another. Instead of focusing on siloed parts, it is looking at the system as a whole. This helps improve communication, supports better problem solving, and allows teams to identify patterns and anticipate challenges before they arise. Organizations can plan more effectively and respond to issues proactively instead of reactively.

Another important part of life cycle thinking is sustainability. As we learned, systems are not limited to technology, they also include ecosystems, natural systems, and other complex environments. When applying this mindset to digital systems, sustainability means designing systems that can last and evolve over time rather than constantly replacing them. Rebuilding systems from scratch is not an efficient use of time or resources. Instead, using flexible life cycle models such as iterative or incremental approaches allows systems to be updated and improved without needing to start over.

This approach also supports long-term planning and cost efficiency. Developing new technology systems can be expensive, so maintaining and improving existing systems is often more practical. By thinking in terms of a life cycle, organizations can reduce costs over time while still meeting ever changing needs. This is reflected in real-world examples such as applications and websites that receive regular updates, as well as systems that require continuous security improvements.

Altogether, thinking in system life cycles focuses on long-term success rather than short-term solutions. This is why many modern digital systems prefer the flexible models, such as iterative and incremental, to support today’s rapidly changing digital environment.

Module 2.1 Blog Post

In a world heavily reliant on ever-evolving technology, human empathy is the characteristic that we cannot afford to forget. When designing systems, products, or services, it is not enough to simply function, they need to work well for everyone using them. Human-centered design focuses on understanding users’ perception, attention, and experiences to create systems that are usable, accessible, and meaningful.

Designing for people means considering how users think, what they are familiar with, and how they interact with the technology. Perception is how users interpret what they see, while attention determines what they will focus on. Empathy ties everything together by helping designers understand what users need rather than what they think they need. Without these elements, systems can fail because they are frustrating or confusing to use.

One widely recognized approach to human-centered design is the ISO 9241-210, which is a structured process. This process includes planning, understanding the context of use, defining user requirements, producing design solutions, evaluating designs, and improving them. While this framework is a standard approach, other perspectives such as the IDEO’s Field Guide, are a more flexible and creative option to designing for people.

ISO 9241-210 vs. IDEO’s Field Guide

ISO 9241-210 Perspective

ISO 9241-210 is a globally used standard for human-centered design in interactive systems. It was first introduced in 1999, and focuses on creating systems that are usable, efficient, and accessible by considering ergonomics, human factors, and user needs (Franz & Kauer-Franz, 2024). The goal of this standard is to have systems that are designed based on real data rather than assumptions.

The ISO approach is structured and has principles to follow. It begins with planning the design process, where teams consider usability, goals, risks, and the overarching purpose of the system. It then moves into understanding the context of use, which includes identifying users, stakeholders, and the environment in which the system will be used. Then, user requirements are defined and translated into testable design solutions, such as prototypes or simulations.

An important part of the ISO 9241-210 is the evaluation step. Designs are tested with real users to determine if they meet usability requirements, and where improvements are needed. This process is iterative, meaning designers continuously refine the system based on feedback from users. This reflects the principles of human-centered design, as mentioned in Mentel's blog on building solutions to meet real user needs (Mentel, 2026).

The ISO approach takes a data-driven approach, prioritizing efficiency, consistency, and measurable usability. This approach is most seen for large systems or organizations where standardization is the best path forward.

IDEO’s Field Guide Perspective

The IDEO's Field Guide offers a different approach when it comes to human-centered design. Instead of focusing on principles or a standardized process, it emphasizes creativity, empathy, and innovation. The guide, launched in 2015, includes a toolkit of methods designed to help teams understand users and develop meaningful solutions (IDEO, 2015).

IDEO structures its approach into three main phases: inspiration, ideation, and implementation. The inspiration phase focuses on learning about users by observing their behaviors, needs, and challenges. The ideation phase involves generating ideas, testing concepts, and refining their solutions. The implementation phase brings those ideas into the real world to evaluate their impact, which defines their success.

An important note in the IDEO’s approach is the adoption of creative confidence, which encourages designers to experiment, take risks, and learn from failure. Unlike ISO, which relies on a structured process and metrics, IDEO embraces ambiguity and flexibility. It heavily focuses on empathy, making sure designs reflect the needs of the people it is created for.

Although the IDEO’s Field Guide may be less structured, I think it allows for more innovation and human perspective than the ISO approach. With modern digital products and services, this flexibility allows designers to respond to changing user needs, which is useful in fast paced environments or where user expectations vary person to person.

Comparison

The ISO 9241-210 and the IDEO’s Field Guide both are created to support designing systems that are for the people. They both rely on user research, testing, and iteration to improve designs. However, their approaches differ in a few ways.

ISO is structured, data driven, and focused on usability and performance. It looks for consistency and reliability, which is useful for large systems. IDEO is flexible, creative, and driven by empathy and innovation. It allows designers to think freely, explore new ideas, and adapt to user needs in a creative way.

This stark difference is important because design decisions often depend on user context. For example, government or enterprise systems may benefit from ISO’s structured approach to ensure accessibility and compliance standards like Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act (Section508, 2022). At the same time, more creative or consumer focused products may benefit from IDEO’s emphasis on creative innovation and user experience.

In my opinion, neither approach works best on its own. While ISO provides the structure needed for reliability and accessibility, it can sometimes not be enough for what designers need. IDEO’s approach provides room for more creativity and empathy, but could result in a lack of consistency if not used properly. In the end, the best designs often combine both approaches. Using a structured approach like ISO requires certain benchmarks that ensure usability, while using IDEO’s flexible approach incorporates empathy and creativity to better serve all users.

UX Laws: Perception, Attention, and Usability in Design

To understand how users interact with systems or products, it is important for designers to analyze and apply the foundation of UX Laws where visual hierarchy and perception are integrated with usability, as they influence how users process information and achieve their goals.

Jakob's Law

Jakob’s Law focuses on the familiarity users develop when browsing different websites across the internet. Users expect designs to work in similar ways, which shows why designers should follow common layout patterns instead of reinventing the wheel. When systems follow familiar structures, users are able to navigate them more easily and reach their goals faster.

For example, in 2018 Snapchat launched a drastic update, which significantly changed its layout. Many users found it confusing because it did not match their expectation, rather it prioritized advertisements. This led them to other platforms such as Instagram. In another case, when platforms like YouTube change how content is organized, it can frustrate users who are used to a certain experience (Yablonski et al., 2020).

In my experience, when a website or app changes too much, it can feel frustrating enough to stop using it altogether. Consistent designs, such as standard layouts for shopping websites, make it easier for users to achieve their goals quickly. This law shows how designing with user expectations in mind improves usability.

Fitt's Law

Fitt’s Law explains how the time it takes to interact with an element depends on its size and distance. In other words, larger and closer elements are easier to identify and locate.

This is most important for touch interfaces. Common buttons like “search” or “add to cart” must be large enough, iconized correctly, and spaced properly so users can select them easily. If elements are too small or close together, users may click the wrong thing, again leading to frustration.

Designing with Fitt’s Law in mind improves accessibility and overall user experience. It shows how small designs decisions like symbols, size, and location can have a big impact on usability (Yablonski et al., 2020).

Miller's Law

Miller’s Law theorizes that people can only hold about seven pieces of information in their short-term memory at once. Because of this, designers must be careful to not overload users with information.

A suggested strategy Miller’s Law recommends is chunking, which involves breaking content into smaller, organized sections. For example, phone numbers are grouped into chunks, and websites use headings, spacings, and visuals to make content easier to scan and find what a user is looking for (Yablonski et al., 2020).

When information is presented as one large chunk of text, users are less apt to read it, especially in today’s digital world where users are constantly exposed to large amount of information at once. By organizing content clearly, designers help users process information more efficiently.

Hick's Law

Hick’s Law states that the more choices a user has, the longer it takes to make a decision. Because of this, simple and clean designs are more effective.

For a digital example, a busy website with too many options can overwhelm users, increase cognitive load and make it harder to complete tasks. In comparison, streamlined designs guide users toward key actions and improve usability.

A common physical example can be seen with smart TVs. As they became more popular and advanced, their remotes also became more complex, often including too many buttons in a small space. This led to the creation of simplified smart remotes that only include essential functions like volume, making them much easier to use (Yablonski et al., 2020).

This law demonstrates the importance of reducing unnecessary complexities in services and products that have a human-centered design.

Why It All Matters

Designing for people means including more than the functionality, but focusing on how users think, feel, and interact with systems. Human-centered design emphasizes perception, attention, and empathy, making sure that systems are not only usable but also meaningful.

Frameworks like ISO 9241-210 and IDEO’s Field Guide provide different but valuable approaches to achieving the goal of having a well-designed system. While ISO offers structure and predictability, IDEO encourages creativity and deeper human understanding. When combined, they create a balanced approach to a human-centered design.

UX Laws such as Jakob’s, Fitt’s, Hick’s, and Miller’s further support this by explaining how users interact with systems. These principles show that even small design decisions can have major impacts on usability.

In the real world, designing for people leads to better experiences, increased accessibility, and more successful systems. Furthermore, the best designs are those that truly understand and prioritize the people who use them. As technology continues to evolve, designing for people will only become more important.

Module 2.2 Blog Post

As technology becomes more intertwined with daily life, users expect more from digital systems. People want faster access to information, improved efficiency, and interfaces that are simple to learn and navigate. As more daily tasks move online, usability has become one of the most important aspects of digital design. Usability refers to how effectively users can interact with a system to accomplish their goal.

Human behavior plays an important role in usability because people rely on their memory, habits, and mental shortcuts when interacting with technology, especially for those of us who have grown up in the digital age. These mental shortcuts, also known as cognitive biases, help users make quick decisions based on past experiences and familiar patterns. When systems are designed around these behaviors, users are able to navigate them better. However, poorly designed systems can increase cognitive load, making users spend more time and mental effort completing simple tasks.

Memory and Mental Models

Memory and mental models impact how users interact with digital systems. People naturally rely on past experiences and familiar patterns when learning how to navigate websites, applications, and devices. Instead of having to learn every new interface, users expect systems to work similarly to ones they are used to. This is why familiar layouts and recognizable icons and symbols are important in user experience design.

In Designing with the Mind in Mind, the fovea in human vision is discussed. In many customer service jobs, employees often say how people ignore signs directly in front of them. Even a bright red “pull” sign on a door may go unnoticed. According to Johnson (2014), humans only focus clearly on a small portion of what they see at one time, while peripheral vision is better at detecting movement than processing detail. In digital systems, this means users may overlook important information if interfaces are too cluttered or visually overwhelming. Designers often use warning colors, bold text, or symbols to direct attention toward important actions, such as red “required field” notifications on forms.

Short-Term Memory

Short-term memory also affects usability. Humans can only retain a limited amount of information at one time, which means systems should not force users to remember excessive details when completing tasks. For example, when I am working, I might open Outlook to respond to an email, receive a Teams notification, switch applications to answer the message, then forget what I was originally doing. Digital systems that rely too heavily on users remembering information create frustration and can reduce efficiency.

User interface designers must understand these limitations and design systems that reduce memory burden. Features such as visible navigation menus, bookmarks, and highlighted links help users remember what the task is. This is considered recognition over recall discussed in Designing with the Mind in Mind. Users should be able to recognize options instead of being forced to remember them from memory alone (Johnson, 2014).

Long-Term Memory

Long-term memory also shapes user interaction through mental models. A mental model is a user’s expectation of how a system should work based on previous examples. As we previously saw in Jakob’s Law, which states that users spend most of their time on websites and therefore expect similar systems to function similarly (Yablonski et al., 2020). For instance, retail websites follow familiar layouts with sections of “tops”, “bottoms”, “clearance”, etc. Because users are already familiar with these patterns, they can quickly navigate the site without learning a new format.

Authentication systems are another area where usability and memory intersect. With many companies requiring logins now, users often have dozens of online accounts with different password requirements and remembering every password becomes unrealistic. Security questions can also create confusion because users may not remember the exact variation of an answer they originally used. For example, a user may not remember the exact way they wrote out their favorite teacher’s name. Since humans are naturally prone to error, developers have introduced tools such as password managers, browser passkeys, and authentication apps to reduce the cognitive burden and memory limitation.

Interface Consistency and System Feedback

Consistency is sought after in many aspects of life, and in the context of usability users prefer the consistency of familiarity. Most people interact with systems to complete a task as quickly as possible, not to learn a new process. Familiar layouts and behaviors allow users to operate on autopilot, which reduces the mental effort it takes for their task.

Humans naturally prefer familiar paths, even when an alternative option may technically be faster. People often choose the method they already know because it is comfortable and less stressful. For example, if I am driving somewhere unfamiliar, I would rather take the familiar backroads than use the freeway, even if the freeway would save time. Familiarity reduces uncertainty and allows people to focus less mental energy on their task.

This same behavior appears in digital systems, for instance, I still prefer using simple online PDF merge websites even though my workplace now has Adobe Pro. The free website feels faster and easier to use because it is straightforward and familiar. Unlike Adobe, there are no extra pop-ups or menus to navigate. Although these shortcuts can be viewed as inefficient, they initially place more burden on the user because they require learning new habits.

Consistency improves usability because the human brain constantly searches for patterns and structure. Users expect buttons, menus, and navigations tools to function similarly across systems. This principle relates to Jakob’s Law because users develop expectations based on previous experiences with other interfaces (Yablonski et al., 2020). When systems behave inconsistently, users have to stop and think more deeply about their actions.

System Feedback

System feedback is a growing response from usability. Humans often focus on completing a task, that they forget the “loose end” actions. For example, people may forget to save a document before closing an application or leaving their key in the ignition after turning their car off. To help users avoid mistakes, some systems now provide feedback through reminders, warnings, and confirmations.

Modern systems use feedback constantly, cars beep when keys are left inside the vehicle, Microsoft applications ask users whether they want to save a document before exiting, websites log users out after periods of inactivity to protect security. All of these notifications reduce the amount of information users must remember on their own.

Graphical user interface (GUI) design also supports usability through recognition. According to Johnson (2014), it is easier for users to recognize and choose options than it is to recall and type information from memory. Icons and symbols simplify interactions by visually communicating what they mean, for example, the universal icon for email, the magnifying glass for search, or the shopping cart for purchase. These visual shortcuts reduce the mental effort it takes to read descriptions of what you are looking for.

Cognitive Load and Poor Design

Cognitive load refers to the mental effort required for people to process information, solve problems, and make decisions. Every task consumes a certain amount of mental capacity, especially with unfamiliar systems or a lot of information. In digital design, poor usability often increases cognitive load by making users think harder than necessary.

According to the Cognitive Load Theory, there are three types of cognitive load: intrinsic load, extraneous load, and germane load. Intrinsic load refers to the natural difficulty of the task itself. Extraneous load is the unnecessary mental effort caused by poor design, and germane load relates to building long-term understanding and skills (Werkmeister, 2024). Developers primarily focus on reducing extraneous cognitive load because it is the one they can best control.

Poor design creates unnecessary cognitive load in many ways. Cluttered layouts, excessive pop-ups, confusing navigation, tiny menus, and overall inconsistencies force users to expend more mental effort understanding how a system works instead of completing their actual task. Even small frustrations from confusion can negatively impact usability when repeated. A spreading example of unnecessary cognitive load is the growing expectation that users download apps to scan QR codes for once simple tasks. Many restaurants have customers scan QR codes to read a menu, adding extra steps that some users may find inconvenient. Instead of simplifying the experience, these systems sometimes make simple tasks more complicated.

At my workplace, we are currently redesigning our SharePoint page based on user feedback and analytics data. One major issue is that the site contains too much information on a single page, making it difficult for users to quickly locate what they need. Since users can only focus clearly on a limited amount of visual information at once, long drop-down lists increase cognitive strain. Furthermore, each department page is designed differently because each department has an editor maintaining their page, creating inconsistency throughout the system.

Research also suggests the importance of reducing cognitive load in design. Clemen Lavrsen (2024) explains that lower cognitive load positively impacts user performance and efficiency. When systems reduce unnecessary mental effort, users are more productive and experience less frustration. Systems should help users focus on accomplishing goals rather than struggling to understand the interface itself.

Tesler's Law

Tesler’s Law states that every system contains a certain amount of unavoidable complexity, and designers should work to manage it as much as possible to limit the burden on users (Yablonski et al., 2020). We see Tesler’s Law in many technology practices today, for example, logging into accounts is unavoidable part of digital security, but systems now include tools such as password managers, passkeys, facial recognition, and autofill features to simplify the process. Instead of forcing users to manually remember dozens of complex passwords, systems have adapted to manage that complexity for them.

We also now have predictive technology that reduces cognitive efforts. Smartphones predict where users may be traveling through maps applications, messaging apps suggest words before users finish typing. Retail technology has also evolved toward simplified check out experiences. Some corporations, the ShoWare Center for example, now allow customers to tap their phone as they enter, pick out items, and leave without having to do a checkout process but are charged correctly.

Another concept related to usability is progressive disclosure, which involves displaying only the most important actions initially while still making advanced features accessible when needed. This approach works well with Tesler’s law because it prevents users from becoming overwhelmed by too many options at once, reducing the cognitive load by simplifying what users see.

Effective digital systems are designed around the way humans naturally think, remember information, and form habits. Memory limitations, mental models, interface consistency, and system feedback all influence how users interact with technology. When systems reduce unnecessary cognitive effort, users are able to complete tasks more efficiently. However, poorly designed systems increase cognitive load, forcing users to spend more time and mental energy navigating interfaces and focusing on their goals.

Concepts such as recognition over recall, Jakob’s Law, and Tesler’s Law all illustrate how important designing systems around human behavior are, rather than expecting users to constantly adapt to new interfaces. As technology continues to evolve, usability will remain important for creating digital systems that are efficient and accessible for users.

The Influence of Visual Design Choices

The average American adult spends approximately 5 hours and 4 minutes per day looking at a screen, not including their workday (Duarte, 2024). This means, if interactive systems want to be successful, they need to consider how their interface influences user behavior and interaction with technology. Every design decision, from visual hierarchy to button placement and color, affects how easily users can complete their tasks. When interfaces are familiar and predictable, users can navigate systems without straining their mental load. Since technology is now integrated into everyday life, interfaces and interaction design have become important components in usability, accessibility, and user satisfaction. UX Laws and principles can help designers understand why users respond the way they do, and how to give the user an overall positive experience with digital systems.

How Interface Layout Influences User Behavior

The interface layout of a system influences how users experience and interact with it, thus affecting their behavior. User recognition makes people feel more comfortable because they are familiar with the environment. Humans learn from experience and apply that knowledge to similar situations. As users become familiar with certain layouts and navigation patterns, systems become predictable and easy to use. According to Designing with the Mind in Mind, successful interface layouts should follow familiar patterns and maintain consistency to support usability and reduce cognitive load (Johnson, 2014).

An example from Designing with the Mind in Mind discussed a user attempting to purchase airline tickets who kept getting prompted for the password. The user later discovered the website assumed he was her husband, who has recently purchased tickets on the same device. Although the system was attempting to improve efficiency by remembering user information, it instead created confusion and frustration for the user (Johnson, 2014). This shows how systems that attempt to simplify tasks can unintentionally create usability problems when assumptions about user behavior are incorrect.

When interactive systems require user attention, such as login systems or password recovery, users can become frustrated if the interface is not predictable. For example, requiring users to guess if a login is asking for an email or username, can slow tasks and increase cognitive load. Most users prefer interfaces that are predictable and easy to navigate because familiarity is comfortable, and improves efficiency while reducing mistakes.

Consistent terminology is another important concept, when systems use multiple terms to describe the same concept, it can create confusion and ambiguity. Inconsistent wording can make interfaces feel less intuitive and increase the chance of user errors. Consistency across navigation, labels, and controls help users build mental models that improve usability.

Interface layouts also influence behavior through system feedback and responsiveness. Responsive systems help users understand the status of their actions and reduce uncertainty. For example, clear error messages or password prompts provide users with guidance rather than leaving them confused on why a system is not working. Delayed or unclear feedback can decrease productivity, frustrate users, and in some cases discourage them from continuing to use the system altogether.

UX Laws

Postel's Law

Postel’s Law focuses on making systems more flexible and forgiving for users. In digital interfaces, people are going to type things differently or make mistakes, therefore systems should be able to work with users. This allows systems to be easier to use for people with different accessibility and usability needs.

As mentioned in the Laws of UX, systems should be designed to recognize the intended input, process it correctly, and give proper feedback. For example, if a user were to misspell a word in Google’s search engine, Google will recognize the intended meaning and suggest corrections or similar search results. This improves usability and reduces frustration if a user were to make a small mistake. Because of this, users are still able to find the information they need without having to be perfect (Yablonski et al., 2020).

This principle is important to adhere to because overly strict systems can make users feel limited or overwhelmed by small mistakes. Manny systems today can automatically make corrections because developers understand that users are not always going to interact with interfaces the same. These types of systems are easier to use and make the user feel like they understand the interface, which reduces cognitive load.

Aesthetic-Usability Effect

Another UX principle is the Aesthetic-Usability Effect. This principle explains how users often perceive visually appealing systems as easier to use, even when usability problems may exist. Aesthetic design choices like color palettes, clean layouts, spacing, and typography all influence how uses feel about an interface. The common phrase “don’t judge a book by its cover” does not apply to interface design because users naturally form opinions based on appearance and ease of use. For example, if users compared two websites that contain similar information, but look different, they are more likely to choose the website that appears more modern, organized, and visually appealing. Bright colors, clear spacing, and minimal clutter can make interfaces easier to process and navigate. When something appears visually overwhelming or cluttered, users may feel frustrated or less likely to continue using the interface (Yablonski et al., 2020). We saw this in the earlier reference to Apple’s recent updates, specifically the Photos application. Because of its new cluttered design, users took the time to learn how to revert it to its original format as much as possible.

Von Restorff Effect

Similar to the Aesthetic-Usability Effect, the Von Restorff Effect focuses on how visual elements stand out are more likely to be remembered. Every part of a design is hoping for user’s attention, especially in today’s digital world of people constantly scrolling through online platforms filled with information. Since users are exposed to large amounts of information every day, designers use visual hierarchy and emphasis to guide attention toward important actions or information.

This principle is most noticeable in online shopping and advertising. When users browse online stores with thousands of products, bright colors, pop-ups, and motion effects are used to direct attention to sales or important information. Since these platforms often contain large amounts of information at once, users tend to scan pages quickly rather than read carefully. A good interface design recognizes this behavior, and intentionally uses visual emphasis to influence what users notice first. This helps direct users toward actions such as clicking products or completing purchases.

Another important aspect of the Von Restorff Effect is accessibility. Even though visual emphasis can improve usability, designers also need to consider users with visual impairments or color blindness. Some users may not perceive contrast, color, or highlighted elements the same way others do, which can make navigation difficult if information relies too much on visual cues. This is why accessibility and moderation are important in interface design, too much of anything can overwhelm users and make it difficult to process information (Yablonski et al., 2020).

Experience with Interface Logic: Apple

An unfortunate and seemingly ongoing frustration I have experience with interface logic involves Apple’s recent updates to the iPhone operating system. With the emergence of iOS 18, many users were surprised by the new configuration of the Photos application. Apple completely changed the layout, reorganizing and renaming folders while moving commonly used albums, such as Favorites and Videos, deeper into subcategories. Although users were provided the option to customize album placement, the updated layout still felt extremely difficult to navigate. Instead of improving usability, the redesign increased confusion and made it harder for users to locate content. As stated in Jakob’s Law, users want websites to be familiar and follow patterns to reduce cognitive load, from Apple’s update, they did not consider this principle.

One of the most frustrating aspects of the redesign was how older photos were automatically placed at the bottom of the camera roll by default. This disrupted familiar navigation patterns that many people who are familiar with digital photo albums are used to. Users often rely on predictable layouts and repeated behaviors when interacting with systems such as a smart phone, sudden changes to interface logic can create frustration and decrease efficiency. (Add source about Apple’s positive rep for UX). In addition to this change, Apple also introduced a large “Collections” section filled with categories such as media types, utilities, pinned items, and other albums you cannot opt out of. The amount of information displayed at once made the interface feel cluttered and overwhelming. Users were unable to disable these categories, even if they do not use them, which reduces the control over the application.

The redesign also created confusion through inconsistent organization and terminology. For example, the Favorites album is placed under the “Utilities” section, even though the term utility is generally associated with system settings. This required users to spend more time searching through categories and relearning where content was located. Instead of the update feeling familiar, it increased the effort required to complete simple tasks. This experience shows how interface changes that disrupt user expectations can negatively impact usability and increase cognitive load (Shvaya, 2025).

Following the iOS 18 update, Apple introduced another update that included a “Liquid Glass” visual design update. This update focused on the icons, which have not been significantly changed in over ten years (Rogers, 2025). While the intention was to modernize the interface, many users were again upset due to the significant visual change of the icons. After the update, they appeared transparent, shadowed, and at times blurry. The redesign reduced readability and made icons more difficult to differentiate quickly, which negatively affects navigation and usability. Visual design experts that attended the Worldwide Developers Conference expressed concerns about usability and unnecessary distractions from this update (Rogers, 2025). This type of issue is an example as to why the Aesthetic-Usability Effect should be considered when revamping designs.

The update also required users to spend additional time researching settings and customization options in order to make the interface feel usable again. I personally had to search online for ways to reorganize settings and reduce some of the visual effects brought on by the update. The experience increased frustration and cognitive effort because users were forced to relearn navigation patterns that had previously felt automatic. Even though Apple later responded to user feedback and provided customization changes, the experience demonstrates how visual and interaction design decisions directly influence user behavior and satisfaction of a digital interface.

The design of an interface influences how users experience digital systems, users rely on familiarity, consistency, and feedback to navigate the digital world comfortably. When systems are designed with usability and human behavior in mind, users are more likely to complete their task seamlessly. However, poor interface logic, cluttered layouts, or unnecessary visual changes can increase cognitive load and negatively affect usability. UX principles such as Postel’s Law, the Aesthetic-Usability Effect, and the Von Restorff Effect help explain why certain design choices feel overwhelming to users. As technology continues to evolve and increase in complexity, it is imperative that designers consider accessibility, predictability, and user expectations.

Human Interaction with Digital Systems

Digital interactions are part of everyday life, whether they are voluntary or unavoidable. The interactions users have with digital systems can affect emotions and shape how people respond to technology. Small design choices such as responsiveness, feedback timing, and motion can influence whether users feel supported, frustrated, overwhelmed, or confident while using an interactive system. As technology continues to evolve and users develop a higher expectation for speed and usability, designers must consider UX laws such as the Peak-End Rule, Doherty Threshold, and Zeigarnik Effect to better understand how users perceive and remember their experiences with digital systems to create an ethical and accessible system.

Interaction Patterns

Feedback timing, responsiveness, and motion shape how users feel while interacting with digital systems. These patterns can reduce uncertainty, support task completion, and help users feel more confident.

Ethical Design

UX laws and persuasive techniques can improve usability, but designers must use them carefully. Ethical design should support user wellbeing, accessibility, and informed decision-making.

User Emotions with Digital Interaction

All users respond differently to digital interactions such as feedback timing, responsiveness, and motion, and it is important for a designer to consider all users. Some users naturally have more patience, while others may become frustrated quickly depending on how the system responds.

Feedback timing is the idea that users should not have to wonder whether a system is working or not. Systems that do not meet users’ timing expectations are generally considered less effective and can make users feel uncertain or ignored (Johnoson, 2014). For example, if a user tries to log into something but accidently enters the wrong password and receives no indication that the password is incorrect, they are left waiting and wondering if the website is functioning properly. In this case, a simple message or visual indicator can reduce confusion and reassure the user that the system heard and is responding to their actions.

Responsiveness is the most important part of digital interaction because interactive systems need to react to user actions in a timely manner. This can include icon changes, hover states, loading indicators, or buttons visually responding when clicked. Responsiveness refers to how quickly a system reacts to an action, while feedback is the confirmation that the action was received (Johnson, 2014). Systems that fail to provide responsiveness often leave users feeling frustrated and unsure if their input was acknowledged.

Recently, I have experienced an ongoing issue with the signature feature not responding in Adobe Pro. When I try to click the “X” to change my signature, it does not respond. Normally, it would take less than ten seconds to switch my signature to my supervisor’s signature, but instead it takes several minutes of repeated clicking, exiting the application, and sometimes even restarting my computer just to get it to function correctly. The lack of responsiveness makes it difficult to complete my work efficiently because the system gave no indication that it received my input. According to Johnson (2014), feedback that does not meet users’ deadlines or expectations can negatively affect usability because users begin to feel uncertain about their tasks. Situations like this can also increase cognitive load because users must spend their time trying to troubleshoot or find workarounds instead of focusing on their actual task.

Motion also plays an important role in how users emotionally respond to a system. The motion in an interactive system should be smooth, intentional, and easy for all users to follow. Motion such as animation can help guide attention, communicate progress, and improve usability. If motion is too fast, overwhelming, or excessive, it can frustrate users and make interfaces harder to navigate. For example, if a menu or sale animation moves too quickly, users may not have enough time to read or process the information before it disappears. This negatively impacts accessibility, cognitive load, and the overall user experience.

To better support users’ emotional needs, interactive systems should acknowledge user actions when possible. Even if a task takes time to process, users should still receive indicators that the system is working, such as using loading wheels, progress bars, or confirmation messages. These small interaction patterns can help reassure users and create a more positive experience.

UX Laws

Peak-End Rule

The Peak-End Rule explains how people tend to remember an experience based on its emotional peak and its ending, rather than the small moments in between. People are more likely to remember highly emotional moments, positive or negative, instead of the mundane details. This happens because memory bias can sometimes override smaller details with stronger emotional reactions.

Humans naturally remember emotional experiences more vividly than neutral ones, and this also applies to digital interactions. According to Yablonski (2020), examples of the Peak-End Rule can often be seen in digital spaces such as Spotify wrapped and Apple Music Replay. These features leave users with a strong emotional impression that is connected to impactful memories.

A way designers can apply this rule is through journey mapping, where emotional peaks are identified throughout the user experience, typically though analyzing lens, experience, and insights (Yablonski et al., 2020). My experience using Adobe Pro at work illustrates an example of this rule in practice because the frustrating ending of the interaction shaped my overall perception of the software. Even if the program worked properly most of the time, the repeated lagging and lack of responsiveness during important tasks left me with a negative lasting impression and loss of trust.

Doherty Threshold

The Doherty Threshold intertwines with feedback timing and responsiveness since it focuses on keeping users engaged with an interactive system. System should provide feedback as fast as 400 milliseconds to maintain user attention. In today’s digital world, users expect fast and reliable performance, and delays can negatively affect the overall experience.

The speed at which a system responds is extremely important because slow response times can increase frustration. When users have to stop what they are doing and wait for a system to process a task, they tend to lose focus. This can also increase cognitive load because users shift their attention away from their original task, then have to try to refocus once the system is working. Modern technology has conditioned many users to expect almost instant responses from websites and applications. According to Verint (2025), 56% of consumers prioritize speed over empathy during digital interactions, illustrating how responsiveness impacts users’ expectations.

Interactive systems can maintain engagement when loading by using visual feedback such as loading pages, skeleton pages, or progress indicators. These interaction patterns help users see that a system is functioning, even if the content takes a moment to load. For instance, Reddit is a platform that uses loading animations to create the perception of responsiveness while content is processing. Instead of leaving users starting at a blank screen, the application provides visual feedback that communicates the system is working.

Zeigarnik Effect

The Zeigarnik Effect explains how unfinished tasks take up space in the mind, increasing cognitive load. It suggests that users tend to remember incomplete tasks better than completed ones because the brain continues to focus on unresolved actions. When an interactive system takes too long to respond and users move on to pass the time, they may still continue to think about their unfinished task. This can negatively affect focus, productivity, and the user experience.

For example, when people create mental to-do lists and begin a task but do not finish it, the unfinish task often remains in their thoughts because they are aware it still needs to be completed. This creates mental tension and can sometimes trigger emotional fatigue or frustration. In digital environments, systems that fail to provide fast responsiveness or clear feedback can contribute to this feeling by leaving users uncertain on whether an action was completed successfully.

The Zeigarnik Effect is also used in design to encourage users to continue interacting with a platform. Features such as progress bars, completion trackers, shopping cart notifications, and unread messages indicators all encourage users to return to the incomplete task. These interaction patterns can help users stay organized and remember important tasks (Dash, 2023).

Ethics in Design

As technology continues to rapidly evolve, it is the responsibility of designers to create interactive systems ethically while considering usability, accessibility, and user wellbeing. Although people technically have the option not to use smartphones or digital platforms, modern society is increasingly becoming dependent on technology for communication, information, work, and everyday tasks. Because of this, inaccessible design choices can create barriers for users.

For example, many restaurants, information boards, and other services require scanning of QR codes in order to view the information. However, not all users’ own smartphones, have reliable internet access, or feel comfortable navigating digital systems for tasks that were once analog. Some websites also assume users already understand technology, forcing them to create accounts just to navigate a complicated interface before accessing the information they seek. Designers must recognize that users have different level of knowledge, abilities, and access to technology.

Although designers may not have bad intentions, persuasive technologies can create ethical concerns depending on how they are used. Common features such as the Facebook like button, Instagram hearts, view count, and others all are part of a variable reward system to keep users engaged by enticing them to continuously check the applications. While these interactions can increase system usage, they can also negatively affect mental health, attention spans, and self-esteem when used too much.

Designers must thoughtfully implement persuasive features and prioritize supporting users, rather than application engagement. Giving users the ability to change their notification settings, personalization options, set screen time reminders, adjust accessibility settings allow for an option of control for users and support them making their own decisions when interacting with a system.

Ethical design also requires transparency and accountability. Users should understand when information is being collected, how systems can influence behavior, and why interaction patterns are being used. Designers can apply UX principles ethically by focusing on human-centered design rather than manipulation. IDEO’s design principles also support this by emphasizing empathy and designing around what is desirable and beneficial for users. All in all, designers have the responsibility to create systems that consider all types of users.

There are many factors in a digital system that affects users’ emotions, some include responsiveness, feedback timing, motion, and persuasive design. UX laws such as Peak-End Rule, Doherty Threshold, and Zeigarnik Effect help designers understand why users remember certain interactions, expect fast responses, and continue thinking about unfinished tasks. As technology continues to become more integrated into daily life, ethical design is imperative because designers have the capability to influence users’ emotions, attention, and behavior. Designers should create systems that not only function well, but also support usability, accessibility, and user wellbeing.
