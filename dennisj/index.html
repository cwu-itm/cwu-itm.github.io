Module 1.1 Blog Post

Systems thinking is a foundational methodology in systems engineering and digital environments. It focuses on understanding how individual parts interact within a system. Rather than analyzing problems in isolation, systems thinking demonstrates a holistic perspective that considers component relationships and dependencies. In modern digital systems, this approach is important for effective design and problem solving.

Define Systems Thinking

To define system thinking, we first look at what a system is. A system can be described as a group of components that work together as an interconnected network to achieve a specific purpose. While the exact structure of a system depends on its context, the main idea is that all parts are connected and influence one another.

According to Peter Checkland, systems can be categorized into five types: natural systems, designed physical systems, designed abstract systems, human activity systems, and transcendental systems. Natural systems are not created by humans, this can include weather patterns, solar, nature, ecosystems, and time. Designed physical systems are human-made and consist of tangible components such as smartphones, cars, and household appliances. In contrast, designed abstract systems do not have a physical component but are used to represent ideas or concepts, such as math equations or theoretical frameworks (Holt, 2021).

Human activity systems involve people working together toward a shared goal. These include social groups, political systems, and organizations. Transcendental systems refer to systems that are beyond human understanding, including spiritual or philosophical concepts and unsolvable problems.

Each system is made up of elements that interact with each other. These elements do not function alone, rather, they depend on each other to keep the system operating effectively. An important aspect of understanding systems is recognizing the difference between a system of interest and the supporting or enabling systems that help it function. This shows how systems are often part of a larger, more complex network (Crawley, 2022).

Systems also exist for a purpose, which can be understood through their requirements, features, and goals. Requirements are the essential functions a system must have. For example, a smartphone must be able to make phone calls. Features are additional capabilities, such as a flashlight or camera. Goals represent the overall purpose of the system, such as enabling communication and access to information (Holt, 2021).

Because systems are complex, problems are inevitable. A network may develop security vulnerabilities, or a vehicle may experience mechanical issues. This is where systems engineering and systems thinking become important, rather than focusing on a single issue, systems thinking encourages a broader perspective.

In simple terms, systems thinking is the practice of viewing a system as a whole rather than individual parts. It involves understanding how components interact and how changes in one area can affect the entire system. This approach is important when dealing with complex systems, as it allows for better problem-solving and a deeper understanding of how interconnected elements work together.

How it differs from more traditional, linear ways of thinking

Traditionally, problems are approached using linear thinking. Linear thinking follows a step-by-step process where one action leads directly to another, also described as moving from point A to point B. This approach focuses on a clear cause-and-effect relationship and works well for simple, predictable problems that have an established resolution. For example, when trouble shooting a computer issue, a linear approach might involve checking each step one at a time, such as restarting the device, inspecting cables, or testing individual components.

While this method can be effective in simple situations, it has limitations when applied to complex digital systems. Linear thinking does not fully consider things like feedback loops, interdependencies, or the broader environment in which a system operates. As a result, it may not consider how one change can impact multiple parts of a system or create unintended consequences. In fast-paced and interconnected environments, this narrow focus can lead to temporary fixes rather than long-term solutions.

As we previously learned, systems thinking takes a more holistic approach. Instead of viewing problems individually, it considers how different components interact within a larger system. Systems thinking emphasizes relationships, patterns, and the way elements influence one another over time. This perspective is especially useful in digital environments, where systems often involve multiple layers, including software, hardware, networks, and users.

Comparing the two approaches demonstrates their main differences. Linear thinking focuses on individual components and relies on step-by-step solutions, making it best for problems with clear and predictable outcomes. For complex designs, systems thinking is more suitable. It recognizes that problems often involve multiple interconnected factors and encourages more flexible and adaptive strategies. Rather than aiming for a quick fix, systems thinking seeks to improve the overall system by addressing root causes and long-term impacts.

Each approach also has its strengths and weaknesses. Linear thinking is efficient and easy to apply in simple situations, but it can lack when dealing with complex systems. Systems thinking requires more analysis, and a broader perspective. It provides a deeper understanding of how systems function, and how to improve them effectively. In today’s digital environments, where complexity is in almost every system, this way of thinking offers a more comprehensive and sustainable way to solve problems.

Considering all of this, one important takeaway from comparing systems thinking and the traditional linear thinking is that systems thinking is not meant to replace linear thinking, but rather to expand on it in order to support more complex situations. For example, if an IT specialist is troubleshooting a computer, they might begin with a linear approach of following a step-by-step process to eliminate potential issues. If that does not resolve the problem, they can then shift to a systems thinking approach and analyze which components are not functioning and how they are connected. This combination of approaches allows for more efficient and effective problem solving. In digital environments where systems are constantly evolving, this flexibility is especially important. Using both approaches together provides a balanced and practical way to design, analyze, and improve systems over time.

Why a model-based approach is applicable in the design of complex digital systems

As complex systems continue to increase over time, model-based systems engineering (MBSE) has become applicable as it helps engineers and designers understand and organize system components more effectively. A model-based approach is a form of systems engineering that supports the design, analysis, and development of complex systems by using structured models, such as diagrams, rather than purely text-based documentation.

Complex digital systems are made up of many interacting components such as software, hardware, networks, and data flows. While the system itself may change over time, its main purpose remains the same, giving each component a clear function within the larger system. However, these systems will sometimes contain accidental complexity, including unplanned difficulties that arise from interactions between components. This is where systems thinking and model-based approaches become essential (Shevchenko, 2020).

A model-based approach represents a system visually or structurally, often through diagrams that show how components are connected. For example, arrows may represent data flow between software applications, servers, and users. This abstraction allows the system to be broken down into different views. Each view focuses on a specific aspect of the system. For instance, a desktop computer system could be represented through separate views for hardware, software, networking, and user interaction. Each view allows for a deeper understanding and lens into how that part contributes to the overall system.

In practice, a model-based approach can be organized into three key components: approach, goal, and visualization. The approach defines what information is included in the model and how system elements are structured, often using frameworks or ontologies. The goal connects system requirements to model views, making sure that each part of the system aligns with its intended purpose. Visualization refers to the graphical representation of the system, using standardized notation (language) to clearly communicate relationships and interactions between components (Shevchenko, 2020).

An important part of model-based systems engineering is compliance. This refers to ensuring that the system follows relevant standards and best practices when in use. This might include process-based standards, framework-based standards, or application-based standards, depending on the industry and organization. Compliance ensures consistency, reliability, and scalability across design systems (Holt, 2021).

Compared to traditional paper-based documentation, a model-based approach offers significant advantages. Paper-based systems can be unclear, inconsistent, and difficult to update. They may also change depending on the author and are typically not digital making collaboration challenging. In contrast, digital models provide standardized, editable, and multi-view representations of a system. This improves communication between teams and stakeholders and allows for easier updates and validation of system design.

A model-based approach integrates systems thinking and systems engineering by providing a structured way to understand complex systems. By modeling relationships, dependencies, and interactions, it allows engineers to predict system behavior and improve design outcomes (Shevchenko, 2020).

Ultimately, systems thinking provides a shift away from traditional, step by step problem solving, toward a more intertwined and holistic understanding of how systems operate. By recognizing relationships between components and the importance of feedback loops, it becomes possible to better understand and manage complex digital environments. When combined with model-based systems engineering, this approach becomes even more applicable. It allows systems to be visualized, tested, and improved in a safe and structured way. Together, these methods support more effective design, clearer communication, and better long-term system performance in the digital environment.

Module 1.2 Blog Post

As we previously learned, systems are everywhere, from political and education systems to technology and infrastructure. Expanding on systems engineering, which is a holistic approach of looking at the system as a whole rather than individual parts, one important concept within systems engineering is the Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC). The SDLC provides a structured way to design, build, and maintain systems over time. In today’s world, where systems are constantly evolving, having a clear process in place is important for efficiency, reliability, and success.

What is the Systems Development Life Cycle?

The Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC) is a framework that is used to create and manage information technology systems. It is a structured process that guides how a system is planned, built, used, and eventually retired. Instead of treating a system as something unchangeable, the SDLC recognizes that systems evolve over time.

An important concept within the SDLC is the idea of a life cycle. A life cycle represents the stages a system goes through from beginning to end, with each stage marking a specific point in the system’s cycle. Another important concept is the life cycle model, which defines how those stages are organized and carried out. In other words, the life cycle shows what happens, while the life cycle model shows how it happens. This distinction is important because different models can approach the same stages in different ways depending on the needs of the system (Holt, 2021).

SDLC Stages

The SDLC includes several main stages: conception, development, production, utilization, support, and retirement. The conception stage focuses on identifying the need for a system and defining its purpose. The development stage explores solutions to meet those needs and selects the most efficient approach. The production stage takes that solution and turns it into a functioning system, including important processes such as verification and validation to make sure it works correctly (Holt, 2021).

Once the system is complete, it moves into the utilization stage, where it is used by end users and stakeholders. This stage can also include training users so they can properly interact with the system. Working in tandem with utilization is the support stage, which focuses on maintaining the system, addressing user issues, and making necessary updates. Lastly, the retirement stage describes how the system is safely and securely dismantled when it is no longer needed, this includes properly disposing of and handling any data to avoid security risks.

It is important to note that these stages are not always sequential or required in every situation. Depending on the system and the life cycle model being used, stages may be skipped, repeated, or adjusted. The ability to adapt to a system allows the SDLC to be applied to a variety of systems with different levels of complexity.

SDLC Phases

The stages of the SDLC can be segmented into more detailed phases that act as a step-by-step guide for developing systems. These phases include planning, analysis, design, development, testing, implementation, and maintenance. The planning phase involves defining the scope of the project, identifying needs, setting timelines, and allocating resources. This helps make sure that the project is well organized from the beginning.

The analysis phase focuses on understanding user needs and identifying potential challenges. This may include gathering input from users, reviewing existing processes, and establishing requirements. The design phase uses the gathered information to create essentially a blueprint of the system’s structure, including its architecture, databases, interfaces, and components.

During the development phase, the system is built according to the design. This includes coding, implementing features, and creating the necessary components. The testing phase then follows, which is a vital step where errors and issues can be identified and fixed before implementation. The implementation phase is when the system is deployed and made available to users. This often includes installation, data migration, and user training.

Lastly, the maintenance phase ensures that the system continues to operate effectively as time goes on. This includes updates, performance improvements, security patches, and ongoing support. This phase is important in today’s reliance on the digital environment and systems (GeeksforGeeks, 2023).

Breaking down the SDLC, we can see that it provides a structured but flexible approach when it comes to developing systems. It helps reduce risk, improve system quality, and allows systems to remain functional throughout their life cycle.

Life Cycle Models

Linear Life Cycle Model

The linear Life Cycle Model is a straightforward, sequential framework, also known as the Waterfall Model. It follows an order of stages: concept, development, production, utilization, and retirement. Each stage begins after the previous stage is completed, meaning there is no going back once a stage is completed.

This model is typically used for projects where the needs and objectives are clearly defined and unlikely to change, along with the technology being used. Because of its structure, the linear model is easier to manage, especially for smaller or short-term projects. It is also predictable, since each step follows a direct path.

The main characteristic of this model is its simplicity. The process is easy to follow and understand, which can be a big advantage for teams that need clear direction and strong documentation. However, this structure also has limitations. With the lack of flexibility, it is difficult to adapt if the needs change later in the process. Also, problems may not be discovered until later stages, or even after deployment, which can make them harder and could be more costly to fix (Holt, 2021).

In short, the linear Life Cycle Model works well for simple projects, but is not well suited for complex systems where change is likely. This limitation leads to the need for more flexible approaches, such as iterative and incremental models.

Iterative Life Cycle Model

In contrast to the linear Life Cycle Model’s singular path, the iterative Life Cycle Model is based on repeated cycles, or iterations. Instead of moving through each stage once, the system can move back and forth between stages such as concept, development, and production. This allows the system to be improved over time.

The iterative Life Cycle Model breaks the system into smaller parts, or iterations, that are developed and improved individually. Each iteration goes through similar stages, but focuses on improving specific parts of the system. This approach allows for better flexibility, since errors can be identified and fixed within a specific part of the system without restarting the entire process (Radigan, 2026).

An advantage of this model is that it allows for continuous improvement. Since each iteration is small parts of the whole, updates and fixes can be found and made quickly compared to the linear model. This approach is commonly used in the software industry because it supports the constant evolution in the tech world.

However, there are also disadvantages. Because the system is done in parts, the systems may be released before it is fully complete or debugged, which can lead to user or stakeholder frustration. Since changes can be made frequently, the overall scope of the project may grow over time (Holt, 2021). Regardless, the flexibility of the iterative model makes it a great option for projects where requirements and needs may change.

Incremental Life Cycle Model

The incremental Life Cycle Model is similar to the iterative Life Cycle Model in that it does not follow a strict linear path. However, it differs in that the system is developed and released in multiple parts, otherwise known as increments. Instead of delivering the entire system at once, smaller pieces of the system are built and deployed over time.

In other models, the concept stage defines the system needs and defines the purpose, whereas for this model, the concept stage defines the overall system requirements. The development and production stages focus on creating partial solutions that support the system as a whole. Each increment adds new functions, allowing users to start interacting with the system before it is fully complete. This would be useful with projects like e-commerce websites, mobile apps, or cloud-based platforms (Radigan, 2026).

An advantage of this approach is that developers can test and evaluate each part of the system as it is released. This makes it easier to identify errors early and make sure that each component is functioning correctly. It is especially useful for larger or long-term projects where flexibility is required, or where the system must be used before it is fully finished.

Like the other models, there are still disadvantages such as the incremental model is not suitable for every project. Some systems cannot easily be broken into smaller parts, which makes this approach difficult to apply. Integrating different increments can also be challenging, and may lead to inconsistencies in the overall system design if developers are not careful (Holt, 2021).

Thinking in System Life Cycles

Thinking in system life cycles supports more effective and innovative decision-making. Technology systems are constantly evolving, and in today’s digital world, they require routine maintenance and updates to remain functional and secure. This is important when thinking of cybersecurity, where threats are evolving alongside the creation of systems, and systems must constantly adapt.

System life cycle thinking also provides a perspective on how different components of a system relate to one another. Instead of focusing on siloed parts, it is looking at the system as a whole. This helps improve communication, supports better problem solving, and allows teams to identify patterns and anticipate challenges before they arise. Organizations can plan more effectively and respond to issues proactively instead of reactively.

Another important part of life cycle thinking is sustainability. As we learned, systems are not limited to technology, they also include ecosystems, natural systems, and other complex environments. When applying this mindset to digital systems, sustainability means designing systems that can last and evolve over time rather than constantly replacing them. Rebuilding systems from scratch is not an efficient use of time or resources. Instead, using flexible life cycle models such as iterative or incremental approaches allows systems to be updated and improved without needing to start over.

This approach also supports long-term planning and cost efficiency. Developing new technology systems can be expensive, so maintaining and improving existing systems is often more practical. By thinking in terms of a life cycle, organizations can reduce costs over time while still meeting ever changing needs. This is reflected in real-world examples such as applications and websites that receive regular updates, as well as systems that require continuous security improvements.

Altogether, thinking in system life cycles focuses on long-term success rather than short-term solutions. This is why many modern digital systems prefer the flexible models, such as iterative and incremental, to support today’s rapidly changing digital environment.

Module 2.1 Blog Post

In a world heavily reliant on ever-evolving technology, human empathy is the characteristic that we cannot afford to forget. When designing systems, products, or services, it is not enough to simply function, they need to work well for everyone using them. Human-centered design focuses on understanding users’ perception, attention, and experiences to create systems that are usable, accessible, and meaningful.

Designing for people means considering how users think, what they are familiar with, and how they interact with the technology. Perception is how users interpret what they see, while attention determines what they will focus on. Empathy ties everything together by helping designers understand what users need rather than what they think they need. Without these elements, systems can fail because they are frustrating or confusing to use.

One widely recognized approach to human-centered design is the ISO 9241-210, which is a structured process. This process includes planning, understanding the context of use, defining user requirements, producing design solutions, evaluating designs, and improving them. While this framework is a standard approach, other perspectives such as the IDEO’s Field Guide, are a more flexible and creative option to designing for people.

ISO 9241-210 vs. IDEO’s Field Guide

ISO 9241-210 Perspective

ISO 9241-210 is a globally used standard for human-centered design in interactive systems. It was first introduced in 1999, and focuses on creating systems that are usable, efficient, and accessible by considering ergonomics, human factors, and user needs (Franz & Kauer-Franz, 2024). The goal of this standard is to have systems that are designed based on real data rather than assumptions.

The ISO approach is structured and has principles to follow. It begins with planning the design process, where teams consider usability, goals, risks, and the overarching purpose of the system. It then moves into understanding the context of use, which includes identifying users, stakeholders, and the environment in which the system will be used. Then, user requirements are defined and translated into testable design solutions, such as prototypes or simulations.

An important part of the ISO 9241-210 is the evaluation step. Designs are tested with real users to determine if they meet usability requirements, and where improvements are needed. This process is iterative, meaning designers continuously refine the system based on feedback from users. This reflects the principles of human-centered design, as mentioned in Mentel's blog on building solutions to meet real user needs (Mentel, 2026).

The ISO approach takes a data-driven approach, prioritizing efficiency, consistency, and measurable usability. This approach is most seen for large systems or organizations where standardization is the best path forward.

IDEO’s Field Guide Perspective

The IDEO's Field Guide offers a different approach when it comes to human-centered design. Instead of focusing on principles or a standardized process, it emphasizes creativity, empathy, and innovation. The guide, launched in 2015, includes a toolkit of methods designed to help teams understand users and develop meaningful solutions (IDEO, 2015).

IDEO structures its approach into three main phases: inspiration, ideation, and implementation. The inspiration phase focuses on learning about users by observing their behaviors, needs, and challenges. The ideation phase involves generating ideas, testing concepts, and refining their solutions. The implementation phase brings those ideas into the real world to evaluate their impact, which defines their success.

An important note in the IDEO’s approach is the adoption of creative confidence, which encourages designers to experiment, take risks, and learn from failure. Unlike ISO, which relies on a structured process and metrics, IDEO embraces ambiguity and flexibility. It heavily focuses on empathy, making sure designs reflect the needs of the people it is created for.

Although the IDEO’s Field Guide may be less structured, I think it allows for more innovation and human perspective than the ISO approach. With modern digital products and services, this flexibility allows designers to respond to changing user needs, which is useful in fast paced environments or where user expectations vary person to person.

Comparison

The ISO 9241-210 and the IDEO’s Field Guide both are created to support designing systems that are for the people. They both rely on user research, testing, and iteration to improve designs. However, their approaches differ in a few ways.

ISO is structured, data driven, and focused on usability and performance. It looks for consistency and reliability, which is useful for large systems. IDEO is flexible, creative, and driven by empathy and innovation. It allows designers to think freely, explore new ideas, and adapt to user needs in a creative way.

This stark difference is important because design decisions often depend on user context. For example, government or enterprise systems may benefit from ISO’s structured approach to ensure accessibility and compliance standards like Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act (Section508, 2022). At the same time, more creative or consumer focused products may benefit from IDEO’s emphasis on creative innovation and user experience.

In my opinion, neither approach works best on its own. While ISO provides the structure needed for reliability and accessibility, it can sometimes not be enough for what designers need. IDEO’s approach provides room for more creativity and empathy, but could result in a lack of consistency if not used properly. In the end, the best designs often combine both approaches. Using a structured approach like ISO requires certain benchmarks that ensure usability, while using IDEO’s flexible approach incorporates empathy and creativity to better serve all users.

UX Laws: Perception, Attention, and Usability in Design

To understand how users interact with systems or products, it is important for designers to analyze and apply the foundation of UX Laws where visual hierarchy and perception are integrated with usability, as they influence how users process information and achieve their goals.

Jakob's Law

Jakob’s Law focuses on the familiarity users develop when browsing different websites across the internet. Users expect designs to work in similar ways, which shows why designers should follow common layout patterns instead of reinventing the wheel. When systems follow familiar structures, users are able to navigate them more easily and reach their goals faster.

For example, in 2018 Snapchat launched a drastic update, which significantly changed its layout. Many users found it confusing because it did not match their expectation, rather it prioritized advertisements. This led them to other platforms such as Instagram. In another case, when platforms like YouTube change how content is organized, it can frustrate users who are used to a certain experience (Yablonski et al., 2020).

In my experience, when a website or app changes too much, it can feel frustrating enough to stop using it altogether. Consistent designs, such as standard layouts for shopping websites, make it easier for users to achieve their goals quickly. This law shows how designing with user expectations in mind improves usability.

Fitt's Law

Fitt’s Law explains how the time it takes to interact with an element depends on its size and distance. In other words, larger and closer elements are easier to identify and locate.

This is most important for touch interfaces. Common buttons like “search” or “add to cart” must be large enough, iconized correctly, and spaced properly so users can select them easily. If elements are too small or close together, users may click the wrong thing, again leading to frustration.

Designing with Fitt’s Law in mind improves accessibility and overall user experience. It shows how small designs decisions like symbols, size, and location can have a big impact on usability (Yablonski et al., 2020).

Miller's Law

Miller’s Law theorizes that people can only hold about seven pieces of information in their short-term memory at once. Because of this, designers must be careful to not overload users with information.

A suggested strategy Miller’s Law recommends is chunking, which involves breaking content into smaller, organized sections. For example, phone numbers are grouped into chunks, and websites use headings, spacings, and visuals to make content easier to scan and find what a user is looking for (Yablonski et al., 2020).

When information is presented as one large chunk of text, users are less apt to read it, especially in today’s digital world where users are constantly exposed to large amount of information at once. By organizing content clearly, designers help users process information more efficiently.

Hick's Law

Hick’s Law states that the more choices a user has, the longer it takes to make a decision. Because of this, simple and clean designs are more effective.

For a digital example, a busy website with too many options can overwhelm users, increase cognitive load and make it harder to complete tasks. In comparison, streamlined designs guide users toward key actions and improve usability.

A common physical example can be seen with smart TVs. As they became more popular and advanced, their remotes also became more complex, often including too many buttons in a small space. This led to the creation of simplified smart remotes that only include essential functions like volume, making them much easier to use (Yablonski et al., 2020).

This law demonstrates the importance of reducing unnecessary complexities in services and products that have a human-centered design.

Why It All Matters

Designing for people means including more than the functionality, but focusing on how users think, feel, and interact with systems. Human-centered design emphasizes perception, attention, and empathy, making sure that systems are not only usable but also meaningful.

Frameworks like ISO 9241-210 and IDEO’s Field Guide provide different but valuable approaches to achieving the goal of having a well-designed system. While ISO offers structure and predictability, IDEO encourages creativity and deeper human understanding. When combined, they create a balanced approach to a human-centered design.

UX Laws such as Jakob’s, Fitt’s, Hick’s, and Miller’s further support this by explaining how users interact with systems. These principles show that even small design decisions can have major impacts on usability.

In the real world, designing for people leads to better experiences, increased accessibility, and more successful systems. Furthermore, the best designs are those that truly understand and prioritize the people who use them. As technology continues to evolve, designing for people will only become more important.
