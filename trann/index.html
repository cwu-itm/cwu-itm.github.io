Module 1.1 Blog Post

Understanding Systems Thinking in Everyday Situations

Systems thinking is basically a way of looking at problems by paying attention to how everything connects instead of looking at things one piece at a time. Instead of asking “what is wrong with this one part,” it pushes you to ask “how is this part connected to everything else, and what happens if something changes?” To me, it feels like zooming out and seeing the full picture instead of focusing on one small detail.

This idea shows up a lot in systems engineering. Holt (2019) explains that systems should not be understood in isolation but through the relationships between their elements and other systems. That idea stood out to me because it means even a small change can affect a lot more than we expect. Nothing really works alone, especially in technology today.

How It Differs from Linear Thinking

Linear thinking is more straightforward. You follow steps in order and expect a clear result at the end. It works well for simple tasks, like solving a math problem or following instructions. But once things get more complicated, that approach starts to fall short.

Systems thinking doesn’t assume everything works in a straight line. Instead, it takes into account feedback, delays, and unexpected outcomes. For example, if a company tries to push employees to work faster, it might seem like productivity will go up. But over time, people might get tired, make more mistakes, or even quit. So the result ends up being the opposite of what was expected.

Another big difference is how each approach deals with complexity. Linear thinking tries to simplify things by breaking them apart. Systems thinking accepts that complexity is part of the situation and focuses on managing it instead. Holt (2019) mentions that complexity often comes from the relationships between system elements, not just the elements themselves. That explains why problems don’t always have simple solutions.

Because of that, systems thinking feels more realistic. It helps explain why quick fixes sometimes create bigger problems later.

Why Systems Thinking Matters Today

In today’s world, almost everything is connected in some way. Apps connect to servers, servers connect to databases, and everything depends on networks. Even something simple like ordering food online involves multiple systems working together behind the scenes.

Systems thinking helps make sense of that. Instead of focusing only on one part, it helps you understand how everything fits together. For example, if a website crashes, the problem might not actually be the website itself. It could be the server, the database, or even a third-party service. Without looking at the whole system, it’s easy to misdiagnose the problem.

It also helps with communication. In systems engineering, different people look at the same system in different ways. Developers, users, and managers all care about different things. Holt (2019) points out that poor communication and lack of understanding are major causes of system failure. Systems thinking helps bring those perspectives together so people can understand how their role fits into the bigger picture.

Why a Model-Based Approach Makes Sense

A model-based approach is basically a way of representing a system using models instead of just written descriptions. These models can show how parts of the system connect, how data flows, and how the system behaves over time.

This is really helpful because it makes things easier to understand. When systems get complex, reading long documents is not always enough. People can interpret things differently or miss important details. A model gives a clearer picture that everyone can look at and agree on.

According to Friedenthal, Moore, and Steiner (2015), model-based systems engineering helps improve system design by making relationships and interactions more visible. Instead of guessing how parts connect, you can actually see it. That makes it easier to find problems early before they become bigger issues.

Another advantage is that models can be updated as the system changes. Technology is always evolving, so having something flexible is important. Instead of rewriting everything, you can adjust the model and see how those changes affect the rest of the system.

Handling Complexity in Real Systems

One thing that stood out from the reading is how much complexity has increased over time. Holt (2019) explains that modern systems are more complex not just because they have more parts, but because those parts interact in more complicated ways.

A good example is comparing older technology to newer systems. Older systems were more mechanical and had fewer connections. Today’s systems rely heavily on software, networks, and real-time data. That adds more layers and more chances for things to go wrong.

Because of this, it is not enough to just understand individual components. You have to understand how everything works together. That is where systems thinking and model-based approaches really help. They give a way to manage complexity instead of being overwhelmed by it.

Final Thoughts

Systems thinking changes how problems are approached. Instead of jumping straight to a solution, it encourages taking a step back and looking at the bigger picture. That alone can make a big difference, especially when dealing with complex systems.

Compared to linear thinking, it feels less simple but more accurate. Real-world problems are rarely straightforward, and this approach reflects that. When combined with model-based methods, it becomes even more useful because it turns abstract ideas into something you can actually see and work with.

Overall, systems thinking is not just useful for engineering. It can apply to everyday situations too, like managing time, understanding group dynamics, or solving problems at work. It just takes practice to start thinking that way.

References:

Holt, J. (2019). Systems Engineering Demystified (2nd ed.). Packt Publishing.

Fiedenthal, S., Moore, A., & Steiner, R. (2015). A Practical Guide to SysML: The Systems Modeling Language. Morgan Kaufmann.

INCOSE. (2018). Systems Engineering Handbook: A Guide for System Life Cycle Processes and Activities. Wiley.

Module 1.2 Blog Post

Thinking in Life Cycles

Before getting into this topic, I mostly thought system development meant building something, testing it, and releasing it. That was basically the whole picture in my head. But after reading more about systems engineering, I started realizing that this way of thinking is too narrow. A system does not just appear, do its job, and stay the same forever. It begins as an idea, moves through planning and development, gets used in the real world, needs maintenance and updates, and eventually reaches a point where it has to be replaced or retired. That full journey is what the systems development life cycle is really about. It is not just a technical process. It is a way of thinking that forces people to consider the entire life of a system instead of only the moment when it is launched.

In my own words, the systems development life cycle is the full path a system follows from the moment someone identifies a need all the way to the point where the system is no longer useful or is officially retired. That includes planning, gathering requirements, designing, building, testing, deploying, operating, supporting, improving, and eventually replacing it. What makes this idea important in systems engineering is that the stages are connected. The early stages do not just “come before” the later stages. They shape them. If the requirements are vague, the design may be weak. If the design is weak, maintenance becomes harder. If maintainability is ignored, the system may still work at launch but become frustrating and expensive later.

One thing that helped me understand this topic better was learning the difference between a life cycle and a life cycle model. At first those sounded like the same thing. They are related, but not identical. A life cycle is the overall path from beginning to end. A life cycle model is the way that path is organized and carried out. So the life cycle is the big picture, while the model is the approach used to move through the stages. That difference matters because systems can have similar life cycle stages but still be developed in very different ways depending on uncertainty, risk, stakeholder needs, and the kind of system being built.

A linear life cycle model is probably the easiest one to picture. It moves in a step-by-step sequence. First comes requirements, then design, then implementation, then testing, then deployment. Each stage is supposed to lead into the next in a mostly orderly way. This model is often associated with the waterfall style of development. There is something appealing about that structure because it feels clean and manageable. Everyone knows what stage they are in. Teams can create documents, approvals, and deadlines around each phase. In settings where changes are expensive or where projects are heavily regulated, that level of structure can be useful.

The biggest strength of a linear model is clarity. It gives people a roadmap. It can also make accountability easier, because each phase has expected deliverables and decisions. If a project has stable requirements from the start, a linear model may actually work well. For example, if an organization is building a system with very specific contractual requirements and not much room for change, a sequential approach can help keep work controlled and predictable. There is also less danger of endless revisions because the model encourages teams to settle key decisions before moving on.

At the same time, the drawbacks of a linear model are pretty clear once uncertainty enters the picture. The model assumes that people can understand the problem well enough at the beginning to make strong long-term decisions. In real life, that often is not true. Users may not fully understand what they want until they see an early version. Stakeholders may change priorities. Technical limitations may show up later than expected. When that happens, a strict linear process can become frustrating because changes made late in the project are harder and more expensive to handle. That is one reason linear models can feel too rigid for many digital systems, especially systems that have to respond to changing user needs or shifting technologies.

I think one of the most important weaknesses of the linear model is that it can make teams focus too much on completion of phases instead of actual learning. A team might say, “We finished requirements,” but later discover that those requirements did not reflect real use. In that situation, the problem is not just a minor mistake. It can affect every stage that follows. So even though a linear model can provide order, it also carries the risk of locking in weak assumptions too early.

The iterative life cycle model takes a different view. Instead of expecting the system to be fully understood from the start, it assumes that knowledge develops over time. Teams build a version, review it, test ideas, gather feedback, and then improve the system in another cycle. That means development is treated as a process of refinement rather than a one-time pass. This seems especially realistic for complex systems, because complex systems usually involve things people do not fully understand at the beginning.

The biggest advantage of iteration is that it supports learning. A team can admit that the first version may not be complete or perfect. That sounds simple, but it actually changes the whole mindset of development. Instead of pretending uncertainty is a problem to hide, the model treats uncertainty as something to work through. Iteration can improve quality because problems are more likely to be discovered earlier. A prototype, early interface, or partial model may reveal design flaws before the full system is finished. It can also improve communication with stakeholders. Rather than relying only on written plans, teams can show a working version and get reactions to something real.

Still, iterative development has its own problems. It sounds flexible, but if it is poorly managed, it can become messy. Teams can end up revisiting the same decisions too many times. Deadlines can slip. Scope can expand because new ideas keep being added. In other words, iteration works best when the project still has direction and discipline. It cannot just be “keep changing things until everyone is happy,” because that can go on forever. So while the iterative model is more adaptable than the linear one, it still needs strong boundaries and priorities.

The incremental life cycle model is related to iteration, but it is not exactly the same. In an incremental model, the system is built and delivered in parts. Instead of waiting for the entire system to be complete, teams release useful pieces over time, with each increment adding new features or capabilities. This model fits a lot of digital systems because it reflects the reality that users often do not need everything at once. A first release can provide core value, and later releases can build on it.

One major strength of incremental development is that it delivers value sooner. Instead of waiting until everything is done, users can start benefiting from part of the system earlier. That matters because early use creates feedback. Teams get to see how people actually interact with the system, which can guide future increments. Incremental delivery also spreads out risk. If one part runs into trouble, the whole project does not necessarily collapse. Work can continue on other areas, and priorities can be adjusted based on what is learned.

I think the incremental model also feels realistic because most digital systems today are never really “finished.” Apps, websites, enterprise platforms, and online services are constantly updated. New functions are added. Old ones are improved or removed. Security issues are patched. Interfaces are redesigned. But incremental development can create problems too. If the system is delivered in pieces without a strong overall architecture, it may start to feel disconnected. One part may be polished while another seems unfinished. Teams might focus too much on short-term delivery and not enough on long-term coherence. That can create technical debt, where quick decisions made for one release make future development harder.

When I compare linear, iterative, and incremental models, I do not think one of them is always the right answer. Each one has situations where it makes sense. The linear model is useful when requirements are stable and control matters. The iterative model is better when understanding grows through testing and revision. The incremental model is strong when early delivery and gradual expansion are important. In real projects, I think organizations often combine these models instead of following one in a pure way. That makes sense because real systems are rarely simple enough to fit perfectly into a single pattern.

What really stands out to me, though, is not just the differences between the models. It is the bigger reason life cycle thinking matters at all. It matters for design because it forces people to think ahead. If engineers only focus on getting the system to work right now, they may ignore maintenance, training, scalability, support costs, and future upgrades. That can create a system that technically works but becomes a burden later. Thinking in life cycles pushes teams to ask better questions early: Will this be easy to maintain? Can this be updated later? Will new people understand how it works? Those questions are just as important as whether the system can launch on time.

It also matters for sustainability. In this context, sustainability is not just about energy use or environmental impact, even though those can matter too. It also means whether the system can remain useful and manageable over time. A system that requires constant emergency fixes, expensive workarounds, or major redesign every year is not very sustainable. A system that depends too heavily on one team member’s knowledge is also not sustainable. Life cycle thinking helps reveal those problems early because it reminds people that systems have to survive beyond the build stage.

Another big reason this matters is evolution. Digital systems operate in environments that keep changing. User expectations change. Organizations change. Policies change. Security threats change. Technology itself changes. A system that cannot evolve eventually falls behind, even if it looked successful at the beginning. That is why flexibility, modular design, documentation, and maintainability matter so much. They are not extra features. They are part of what allows a system to keep being useful.

I also think life cycle thinking changes how success and failure should be judged. A system does not fail only when it crashes or stops functioning. It can also fail by becoming too hard to maintain, too expensive to support, too insecure to trust, or too outdated to meet current needs. In the same way, success is not just about whether a system works on release day. Success also includes whether it can continue working well, adapt to change, and remain valuable over time. That is a more demanding way to look at systems, but it also feels much more honest.

Overall, thinking in life cycles makes systems engineering feel more practical and more responsible. It reminds us that building the system is only one part of the job. After that, the system still has to exist in the real world, where people use it, depend on it, update it, and eventually replace it. Linear, iterative, and incremental models each offer different ways to move through that broader journey. Each one has strengths and real limits. What matters most is choosing an approach that fits the system, the level of uncertainty, the needs of stakeholders, and the long-term goals. For me, the biggest takeaway is that good systems engineering is not just about creating something that works once. It is about creating something that can keep working, keep improving, and keep providing value throughout its life.

Designing for Real People, Not Just Screens

When people hear the word “design,” they often think about how something looks first. They may think about colors, fonts, buttons, icons, images, or whether a website looks modern. Those details matter, but they are only part of design. A website or app can look polished and still be confusing. It can look professional and still make people feel lost. To me, designing for people means thinking beyond the screen and paying attention to the person who has to use it. Human-centered design is about asking what people need, how they think, what frustrates them, and what would make their task easier.

This matters because most people do not use technology in perfect situations. They may be tired, rushed, distracted, stressed, or unfamiliar with the system. A student may be trying to submit an assignment before a deadline. Someone may be using a banking app while worrying about money. A patient may be checking test results while already feeling anxious. In those moments, design is not just decoration. It can either reduce stress or add more stress. A human-first design helps people complete tasks with more confidence and less confusion.

The two course readings explain human-centered design from different angles. The ISO 9241-210 reading presents human-centered design as a structured process for designing interactive systems. It focuses on understanding users, their tasks, their environment, and their requirements before creating and evaluating design solutions (Tullis & Albert, 2022). IDEO.org’s Field Guide to Human-Centered Design describes it in a more creative and people-focused way. It emphasizes empathy, testing ideas, learning from failure, and designing with the people who are affected by the problem (IDEO.org, 2015). Together, the two readings show that human-centered design is both a process and a mindset.

Understanding Human-Centered Design

Human-centered design begins with the idea that users should not be treated as an afterthought. They should be part of the design process from the beginning. In the ISO 9241-210 approach, designers first plan the human-centered design process, then understand the context of use, specify user requirements, produce design solutions, and evaluate those solutions against the requirements (Tullis & Albert, 2022). This structure is helpful because it stops teams from jumping straight into building something based only on assumptions.

For example, imagine a team is designing a college advising website. If they only think from the school’s point of view, they may organize the website around departments, policies, and office structures. That might make sense internally, but it may not match how students think. A student may just want to know which classes to take next quarter, how to contact an advisor, or whether a course counts toward graduation. A human-centered process would push the team to study how students actually use advising information, what questions they ask most often, and where they usually get stuck.

IDEO’s approach feels more flexible and creative. The Field Guide explains three main phases: Inspiration, Ideation, and Implementation (IDEO.org, 2015). In the Inspiration phase, designers learn from people and observe real experiences. In Ideation, they turn those observations into ideas and prototypes. In Implementation, they think about how the solution can work in the real world. I like this approach because it accepts that designers may not know the answer at first. Instead of pretending to have the perfect solution right away, they keep learning from people.

The main difference I see between ISO 9241-210 and IDEO is the tone and focus. ISO feels more formal and organized. It gives a clear structure for making sure user needs are included in the design process. IDEO feels more hands-on and human. It encourages designers to empathize, experiment, make things tangible, and learn from mistakes. In a real project, both are useful. ISO helps a team stay organized, while IDEO helps the team stay open-minded and connected to people.

Why Perception and Attention Matter

A big part of designing for people is understanding that users do not look at a screen the way designers sometimes imagine they do. Users usually do not read every word carefully from top to bottom. They scan. They notice headings, buttons, images, spacing, color, and anything that looks different from the rest of the page. This is why perception and visual hierarchy are so important.

Visual hierarchy is how a design shows users what to notice first, second, and third. If everything on a page looks equally important, then nothing feels important. Users have to work harder to figure out where to begin. A strong visual hierarchy helps guide attention. For example, on a checkout page, the order total, shipping address, payment information, and final confirmation button should be easy to find. If the “Place Order” button is small, hidden, or surrounded by too many other buttons, the user may hesitate or make a mistake.

Perception also affects trust. A cluttered website can make people feel unsure, even if the system technically works. For example, a healthcare portal should not overwhelm users with too many alerts, links, and blocks of text at once. People using a healthcare portal may already feel anxious about test results, appointments, or insurance. A more human-centered design would use clear headings, readable spacing, plain language, and obvious next steps. These choices do not just make the page look better. They make the experience feel safer and easier to handle.

IBM describes user experience as the overall experience a person has when interacting with a product, system, or service, including ease of use, accessibility, visual design, interface function, and emotional impact (Finn & Downie, n.d.). This definition is useful because it shows that UX is not only about whether a screen works. It is also about how the user feels while using it. A system can be technically correct and still create a poor experience if it makes users feel confused, rushed, or unsupported.

Jakob’s Law: Familiar Design Helps Users Feel Comfortable

Jakob’s Law says that users bring expectations from other websites and apps they already use. This means a new design is usually easier to understand when it follows familiar patterns. For example, most people expect a logo at the top of a website to lead back to the homepage. They expect a shopping cart icon on an online store. They expect search bars, menus, and buttons to behave in familiar ways.

This does not mean every website has to look the same. It means designers should be careful before changing common patterns just to be original. If a designer makes navigation unusual without a good reason, the design may feel creative to the designer but confusing to the user. Human-centered design requires designers to care more about the user’s comfort than their own desire to be different.

This law connects closely to the ISO reading because understanding the “context of use” includes understanding what users already know from other systems (Tullis & Albert, 2022). A person does not come to a website with an empty mind. They bring habits and expectations from past experiences. Good design uses those expectations instead of fighting against them.

Fitts’s Law: Important Actions Should Be Easy to Reach

Fitts’s Law explains that the time it takes to move to a target depends on the size of the target and how far away it is. In UX design, this means buttons and clickable areas should be large enough and placed where users can reach them easily (Interaction Design Foundation, n.d.). This is especially important on mobile devices, where people use their fingers instead of a mouse.

A small button may not seem like a big problem until someone keeps tapping the wrong thing. For users with larger fingers, shaky hands, low vision, or limited mobility, tiny buttons can become a real barrier. Even for users without accessibility challenges, small touch targets create frustration. A human-centered design would make important actions clear, visible, and easy to select.

For example, in a food delivery app, the “Add to Cart” or “Checkout” button should not be tiny or placed too close to another action like “Remove.” If someone is hungry, busy, or ordering quickly, the design should help them complete the task without mistakes. Fitts’s Law shows that usability is not only about thinking. It is also physical. People interact with technology through their eyes, hands, and bodies, so design has to support that reality.

Hick’s Law: Too Many Choices Can Overwhelm Users

Hick’s Law explains that the more choices people have, the longer it can take them to make a decision (Interaction Design Foundation, 2020). This is easy to see in everyday life. A menu with five good options can feel simple. A menu with fifty options can make people stop and second-guess themselves. The same thing happens on websites and apps.

This does not mean designers should remove every option. It means they should organize choices carefully. A school website, for example, may need to include information about admissions, tuition, advising, financial aid, housing, and registration. But if all of those links are thrown onto one page without structure, students may not know where to start. Grouping related information, using clear labels, and showing the most common actions first can make the experience much easier.

Hick’s Law also connects to empathy. A designer should think about how a user feels when facing too many choices. Confusion is not always the user’s fault. Sometimes the system is asking the user to do too much mental work. A human-centered design reduces that burden by breaking decisions into smaller steps and making the next action clear.

Miller’s Law: People Need Information in Manageable Groups

Miller’s Law is often connected to the idea that people can only hold a limited amount of information in working memory at one time. In design, the useful lesson is that information should be grouped into meaningful chunks. Users should not have to remember too much while moving through a task.

A good example is a long online form. If the form asks for personal information, address details, payment information, and account setup all on one long page, it can feel tiring. A better design might divide the form into sections with clear headings. Users can focus on one part at a time. This makes the task feel more manageable, even if the same information is still required.

Miller’s Law also supports visual hierarchy. Grouped information is easier to scan and remember. Phone numbers are easier to read when they are broken into sections. Navigation menus are easier to understand when similar links are grouped together. Dashboards are easier to use when information is divided into cards or categories. These are small design choices, but they can make a major difference in how usable something feels.

Designing With Empathy Instead of Assumptions

One of the strongest ideas from IDEO is that the people who experience a problem often understand it in ways outsiders do not (IDEO.org, 2015). This is important because designers, developers, and organizations can easily make assumptions. A designer may think a feature is obvious because they helped create it. A developer may think a label is clear because they understand the system behind it. A business team may want to add more features because it sounds good in a presentation. But users may only want the task to be faster, clearer, and less stressful.

This is why testing and feedback matter. The ISO reading explains that design solutions should be evaluated with users, not only by experts (Tullis & Albert, 2022). Expert reviews can be helpful, but they cannot fully replace real user feedback. Users may notice problems that the team missed. They may misunderstand a button label, skip an important instruction, or use the system in a way the team did not expect. These discoveries are not embarrassing. They are part of making the design better.

IDEO also talks about learning from failure and testing ideas early (IDEO.org, 2015). I think this is a realistic way to approach design. No team gets everything right on the first try. A rough prototype can be useful because it gives people something to react to. It is better to find problems early, when they are easier to fix, than after the final product has already been built.

My Reflection: Human-First Design Reduces Unnecessary Stress

My biggest takeaway is that human-centered design is really about reducing unnecessary stress. A good design does not make users feel stupid. It does not force them to guess what a button means. It does not hide important information or overload them with too many choices. It respects that people have limited time, attention, memory, and patience.

This matters because digital systems are now connected to important parts of life. People use websites and apps for school, banking, healthcare, jobs, transportation, shopping, and government services. When these systems are confusing, the consequences can be more serious than just annoyance. A student might miss a deadline. A patient might misunderstand medical information. A job applicant might fail to complete an application. Human-centered design helps prevent these problems by treating usability as a real responsibility.

I also think human-centered design requires humility. Designers have to admit that their first idea may not be the best idea. They have to listen to people who have different experiences from their own. They have to balance creativity with evidence. ISO 9241-210 supports this by giving a process for understanding users, creating requirements, producing solutions, and evaluating them. IDEO supports it by encouraging empathy, making, testing, and learning. Together, they show that good design is not just about having talent. It is about paying attention, asking better questions, and being willing to revise.

Conclusion

Designing for people means more than making a screen look nice. It means understanding how people see information, how they make decisions, what they expect, and where they may struggle. ISO 9241-210 gives human-centered design a clear structure, while IDEO’s Field Guide brings in empathy, creativity, experimentation, and learning from failure. The four UX laws also support this human-first approach. Jakob’s Law reminds designers to use familiar patterns. Fitts’s Law shows that important actions should be easy to reach. Hick’s Law warns that too many choices can slow people down. Miller’s Law shows why information should be grouped in manageable ways.

A human-first design is successful because people can use it with less confusion and more confidence. It helps users complete their tasks without feeling overwhelmed. It respects people’s attention, memory, emotions, and time. In that sense, human-centered design is not only a design method. It is a way of showing respect for the people who have to live with the systems we create.

Why “Easy to Use” Is Not as Simple as It Looks

A digital system is usable when people can move through it without feeling confused, stressed, or forced to fight with the screen. Usability does not mean a system has to be simple or have fewer features. A system can still be powerful and detailed while feeling easy from the user’s side. What matters is whether the design fits how people actually think, remember, build habits, and make decisions.

When an app, website, or online tool feels natural, it is usually because the design reduces unnecessary mental effort. The user can focus on the task instead of trying to figure out how the interface works. This is why usability is not only a technical issue. It is also a human issue. People bring memory limits, expectations, habits, and mental shortcuts into every digital interaction.

A student using Canvas, a customer ordering food online, or a patient scheduling an appointment is not starting with a blank mind. They already have ideas about where buttons should be, what certain icons mean, and what should happen after they click something. Good design works with those expectations. Poor design ignores them and makes users carry extra cognitive load.

Memory Shapes How People Use Interfaces

Memory has a big impact on usability because users can only hold a limited amount of information in their minds at once. If a system forces users to remember too many steps, rules, passwords, codes, or menu locations, the task becomes tiring. Jeff Johnson’s Designing with the Mind in Mind connects interface design to human psychology, especially the limits of attention, memory, and perception (Johnson, 2020). A strong interface should reduce memory pressure instead of adding to it.

One useful idea is that recognition is easier than recall. Recognition means the user can see options and choose the right one. Recall means the user has to remember something without much help. For most people, recognition is much easier. A menu with clear options is easier than a blank search box that requires the user to know the exact term. A calendar picker is easier than typing a date in a specific format. A saved address list is easier than retyping the same address every time.

I notice this often when using school platforms. If an assignment page clearly shows the due date, instructions, rubric, and submission button in one place, I do not have to spend extra time searching. The interface supports my memory. But when the instructions are in one area, the required file is somewhere else, and the submission link is in another place, I have to remember where everything is before I can even start the assignment. That creates extra mental work.

Password forms are another example. Some websites only show the password rules after the user submits the form and fails. That is poor design because the user has to guess first, make a mistake, and then try again. A better design shows the rules from the beginning and checks them as the user types. For example, the form could show “8 characters,” “one number,” and “one symbol,” then mark each requirement as complete. This lowers cognitive load because the user does not have to remember or guess the rules.

Mental models also influence how people use digital systems. A mental model is the user’s idea of how something works. People build mental models from past experiences. If someone has used many shopping websites, they expect a cart icon to hold items, a checkout button to start payment, and a confirmation page to appear after purchase. If someone has used maps before, they expect a pin to show a location and a blue dot to show where they are.

Problems happen when the interface does not match the user’s mental model. For example, if a website uses a trash can icon for “archive” instead of delete, users may feel unsure. If a button says “Continue” but actually submits a final order, the user may feel tricked. If a school website uses “modules,” “pages,” “assignments,” and “tasks” in inconsistent ways, students may not know where to go.

Nielsen’s usability heuristics include “match between the system and the real world,” which means a design should use familiar language and concepts instead of internal jargon (Nielsen, 2024). This matters because users should not have to translate the system’s language into their own. A student should not have to guess that “learning artifact submission portal” means “submit assignment.” A patient should not need to understand medical department names before scheduling a basic appointment. A usable system speaks in terms the user understands.

Interface Consistency and Feedback Improve Usability

Interface consistency improves usability because it reduces the amount of new thinking users have to do on each screen. When the same action uses the same label, style, and location throughout a system, users can learn the pattern and reuse it. Nielsen’s heuristics also describe consistency and standards as an important usability guideline because users should not have to wonder whether different words or actions mean the same thing (Nielsen, 2024).

A common example is online checkout. Many shopping websites follow a familiar order: cart, shipping, payment, review, and confirmation. Because users have seen this pattern before, they understand where they are in the process. If a website suddenly asks for payment before showing shipping costs, users may feel uncertain. The system may still function, but it breaks the expected pattern.

Consistency is also important in school systems like Canvas. The platform itself may have a consistent structure, but each class can be organized very differently. One instructor may put everything in weekly modules. Another may place instructions in announcements, files, or pages. Another may include important links only inside assignment descriptions. Even though the tool is the same, the student experience changes from class to class. This adds cognitive load because students have to learn a new organization style for every course.

This shows that usability is not only about software design. Content organization also affects usability. A system can have a clean interface, but if the information inside it is inconsistent, users still struggle. Clear headings, repeated structure, and predictable locations make a system easier to use.

System feedback is another important part of usability. Feedback tells users what happened after they took an action. Did the file upload? Did the payment go through? Is the page loading? Did the password save? Without feedback, users are left guessing. Nielsen Norman Group explains that visibility of system status helps users understand what is happening and feel more in control (Harley, 2018).

This is especially important when a task takes time. A loading spinner, progress bar, confirmation message, or checkmark can reduce anxiety. For example, when I submit an assignment online, I want to see a clear message that says the submission was successful. Even better, I want the system to show the file name and the time submitted. Without that confirmation, I may keep refreshing the page or worrying that the assignment did not go through.

Feedback also helps prevent repeated actions. If a user clicks “Place Order” and nothing changes, they may click again. That can lead to duplicate orders or payments. A simple message like “Processing payment” or “Please wait” can prevent that problem. The same idea applies to file uploads, appointment bookings, account changes, and online forms.

Error messages are also a form of feedback. A weak error message says something like “Invalid input.” That does not help much because the user still has to figure out what went wrong. A stronger message explains the problem and the fix: “Please enter a 10-digit phone number” or “This file is too large. Maximum size is 10 MB.” Good feedback does not just point out failure. It helps the user recover.

Habits and Mental Shortcuts Affect Interaction

People do not read every word on every screen. Most users scan, guess, and rely on habits. This is not because they do not care. It is because everyday digital life is full of repeated patterns. People click “next,” close pop-ups, tap back arrows, search with magnifying glass icons, and expect blue text to be clickable. These habits and shortcuts save time.

Good design uses these habits carefully. It places common actions where people expect them and uses familiar patterns when they make sense. For example, a “Save” button should not move around randomly from page to page. A dangerous action like deleting an account should not look exactly like a normal “Continue” button. The design should support the user’s habits while also slowing them down when the action is serious.

Poor design can take advantage of habits. Some websites make the “accept” button large and clear while hiding the “decline” option in small text. Some subscription services make signing up easy but make cancellation difficult. These patterns may help a business in the short term, but they hurt trust. A usable system should not depend on confusing the user. It should help people make choices they actually understand.

This connects to cognitive load because users are often balancing multiple things at once. A person may be using an app while walking, cooking, working, or studying. If the interface requires careful reading for every small action, it becomes exhausting. A better system uses clear labels, predictable layouts, and helpful feedback so the user does not have to slow down more than necessary.

Poor Design Creates Unnecessary Cognitive Load

Cognitive load is the mental effort needed to complete a task. Some cognitive load is necessary, especially for complex tasks. The problem is unnecessary cognitive load. This happens when users spend their energy figuring out the interface instead of completing the actual goal.

One example is a parking payment app. If a person is standing outside trying to pay quickly, the app should not require too many steps. It should not force the user to create an account, verify an email, manually search for a parking zone, and guess which payment option applies. The environment already adds pressure. The person may be cold, late, or worried about getting a ticket. A usable design would allow guest checkout, scan a QR code, detect the location, show the time clearly, and confirm payment.

Another example is healthcare scheduling. A user may know they need help, but not know which department name to choose. If the website asks the user to pick from medical categories without explanation, it creates confusion. A better design could ask simple questions first, such as “What do you need help with?” and then guide the user to the correct appointment type.

This is where Tesler’s Law connects to usability. Tesler’s Law says that every system has a certain amount of complexity that cannot be completely removed (Yablonski, 2024). The important question is where that complexity goes. Does the system handle it, or does the user have to handle it?

Tax software is a good example. Taxes are complicated no matter what. A tax app cannot remove the laws, forms, or calculations. But it can ask questions in plain language, save progress, explain unfamiliar terms, and warn users when something is missing. The complexity still exists, but the system carries more of the burden.

The same idea applies to travel booking. Flights involve dates, times, layovers, baggage rules, seat choices, cancellation policies, and prices. A poor interface dumps all of that information on the user at once. A better interface provides filters, comparisons, summaries, and warnings. It helps users make decisions without needing to manually track every detail.

Tesler’s Law also shows why making a screen look simple is not always the same as making it usable. If a designer hides too much information, the user may have to search harder. If an app removes labels and only uses icons, the interface may look clean but become confusing. True usability is not about removing everything. It is about deciding what should be visible, what should be guided, and what the system should handle quietly in the background.

Poor design affects more than convenience. It can affect trust, stress, access, and confidence. When a system is confusing, users may blame themselves. They may think they are not good with technology. But many times, the real problem is that the design is asking them to do extra mental work that could have been avoided.

Conclusion

A usable digital system works with the way people think. It supports memory by showing options instead of forcing users to remember everything. It respects mental models by using familiar language and patterns. It uses consistency so users can build habits and move with confidence. It gives feedback so users know what is happening and how to fix mistakes.

The course ideas about usability show that good design starts with real users, not just features. Tesler’s Law adds another important point: complexity has to go somewhere. If designers do not handle it carefully, users end up carrying it. Good usability does not mean removing every challenge. It means removing unnecessary struggle between the user and the task.

In the end, the best digital systems feel easy because they respect human limits. They do not expect users to remember too much, translate confusing language, or guess what happened after every click. They guide people clearly, respond to their actions, and make the next step feel reasonable. That is what makes a system not just functional, but genuinely usable.

Module 3.1 Blog Post

Designing for Flow: How Apple Maps Guides Users Through an Interface

A good digital interface does not only look nice. It helps people know where they are, what they can do next, and what will happen after they take action. This is what interface logic means to me. The design should guide the user through the task without making them stop and think too much. For this blog post, I am focusing on Apple Maps because it is an app that depends heavily on clear layout, controls, feedback, and user flow. When someone opens a map app, they are usually trying to do something quickly, such as search for a place, compare routes, check traffic, or start navigation. The interface has to support that behavior without adding confusion.

Layout and User Behavior

Layout has a major impact on how people behave in an app. In Apple Maps, the search area is usually one of the first things users notice because searching is the main starting point for many tasks. A person might type an address, a business name, or a general phrase like “coffee near me.” Apple’s Human Interface Guidelines explain that maps should use location-related cues to help users understand places and open place cards when needed (Apple, n.d.). This matters because users are not just looking at a picture of a map. They are trying to connect the map to real-world decisions.

The interface also supports user behavior by keeping important controls close to the task. For example, the location button helps users return to their current position, route cards help users compare driving or walking options, and the “Go” button gives a clear next step. This structure reduces the amount of searching the user has to do inside the interface. Nielsen Norman Group’s usability heuristics also support this idea because good interfaces should keep users informed, make actions visible, and reduce memory load (Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.). In a navigation app, that is especially important because the user may be outside, walking, driving, or dealing with time pressure.

Control Design and Feedback

Controls are the parts of the interface that users click, tap, drag, or select. In Apple Maps, controls are not just decoration. They directly affect the user’s sense of control. Buttons for directions, route choices, current location, and zoom all help the user move through the app. When these controls are placed logically, users can build confidence because they can predict what each action will do.

Feedback is also important. When Apple Maps shows an estimated arrival time, highlights a selected route, or updates the blue location marker, it tells the user that the system is responding. That connects to the visibility of system status heuristic, which says users should be kept informed about what is happening through timely feedback (Nielsen Norman Group, n.d.). Without that feedback, a map app would feel unreliable. For example, if I tap “Go” and nothing changes, I might wonder whether the app froze, whether I tapped the wrong button, or whether the route failed to load.

Aesthetic–Usability Effect

One UX law that applies strongly to Apple Maps is the Aesthetic–Usability Effect. This law explains that users often see visually pleasing designs as easier to use, even when the design still has flaws (Yablonski, 2024). Apple Maps uses a clean visual style with soft colors, simple icons, and organized route information. This makes the app feel calm and trustworthy. A map interface can easily become overwhelming because there are roads, labels, traffic colors, business names, and navigation instructions all competing for attention. If the design looks messy, the user may feel lost before even starting.

The clean look of Apple Maps helps reduce that feeling. The user can focus on the route instead of fighting the interface. However, the Aesthetic–Usability Effect can also be a weakness. A beautiful interface does not automatically mean the app is always easier to use. For example, if a route option is hidden too far down or if a place card covers too much of the map, the clean design might still create frustration. This is why visual design should support usability, not replace it.

Von Restorff Effect

Another useful law is the Von Restorff Effect. This law says that when several similar items are shown, the item that looks different is more likely to be noticed and remembered (Yablonski, 2024). Apple Maps uses this idea when it highlights the selected route or makes a main action button more noticeable. The “Go” button needs to stand out because it is the action that begins navigation. If every button had the same size, color, and visual weight, users would need more time to decide what to tap.

This law also affects how route options are shown. A recommended route may appear more visually important than the other routes. That helps users make a faster decision, especially when the difference between routes is small. At the same time, designers have to be careful. If too many items are highlighted, then nothing really stands out anymore. The Von Restorff Effect works best when the interface uses contrast only for the most important action or information.

Postel’s Law

Postel’s Law is also important in map design. In simple terms, the system should be flexible with what users enter, but clear and reliable with what it returns. Apple Maps supports this when users search in different ways. Someone can type a full street address, a restaurant name, a category like “gas station,” or a broad search like “coffee near me.” The app then turns that messy human input into structured results.

This matters because real users do not always search perfectly. They may misspell a business name, forget the exact address, or use casual language. A strict system would reject those searches too easily. A more helpful system tries to understand the user’s intent and gives useful options. This connects with Johnson’s explanation that people are better at recognizing options than recalling exact details from memory (Johnson, 2020). When Apple Maps shows a list of possible places, it helps the user recognize the right result instead of forcing them to remember the perfect search term.

Connection to Designing with the Mind in Mind

Jeff Johnson’s Designing with the Mind in Mind connects well to map interfaces because the book focuses on how people perceive, learn, remember, and act in digital environments. One idea that fits Apple Maps is that users should not have to solve unnecessary problems while using an interface. In navigation, the user’s real problem is already hard enough: they are trying to get somewhere. The interface should not add extra mental work.

This is why recognizable controls matter. A search bar, current location icon, route line, and direction steps are all familiar patterns. Users do not need a long explanation because they have seen similar patterns before. Johnson explains that learned actions are easier for people than constant problem-solving (Johnson, 2020). Apple Maps benefits from this because it uses familiar mobile map patterns. Even if someone has not used Apple Maps much, they can often guess how to search, zoom, or start a route because the controls match common expectations.

My Experience with Interface Logic

A positive experience I have had with Apple Maps is using it when I need to get somewhere unfamiliar. The interface works well when the route is clear, the ETA is visible, and the next turn appears at the top. I do not have to keep checking the whole map because the app gives me the most important information at the right time. That is good interface logic because the design understands the user’s situation. During navigation, I do not need every possible feature. I mainly need to know where I am, what direction I should go, and how long it will take.

A frustrating experience can happen when the interface gives several route options and I am not sure which one is best. Sometimes the fastest route, shortest route, and route with less traffic can look similar. If the difference is only a few minutes, I may spend extra time deciding. This is where the interface could do more to explain the reason behind a recommendation. For example, a short note like “less traffic” or “fewer turns” can make the decision easier. The design already shows information, but the logic could be stronger if it explained why one route is recommended.

Why Interface Flow Matters

Interface flow matters because users usually do not want to think about the interface itself. They want to complete a task. In Apple Maps, that task might be finding a place, choosing a route, or following directions. Good layout helps users know where to begin. Good controls help users act with confidence. Good feedback helps users trust that the system is working. UX laws like the Aesthetic–Usability Effect, Von Restorff Effect, and Postel’s Law explain why these choices matter from a human perspective.

Apple Maps is not successful only because it has map data. It is useful because the interface turns complex information into steps that feel manageable. Roads, businesses, traffic, distances, and directions are all complicated pieces of information. The interface organizes those pieces so users can make decisions quickly. That is the main lesson I take from this topic: good interface logic is not about adding more features. It is about arranging the right features in a way that matches how people think, choose, and move.
