Seeing the Bigger Picture: Systems Thinking and MBSE in Action

Modern problems are rarely isolated. When designing software, managing infrastructure, or building global systems. We a are increasingly working in interconnected environments. We have begun exploring how systems thinking helps us better understand and manage complexity. This post reflects on that concept, its differences from traditional thinking, and how a model-based approach like MBSE (Model-Based Systems Engineering) helps us better navigate the digital world.

What is Systems Thinking?

Systems thinking is really a way of understanding problems by looking at the whole system, not just a singular individual part. Rather than focusing only on the immediate issue, it encourages us to explore how different components interact and influence one another over time. According to Holt (2021), systems thinking requires shifting our perspective from “what is broken” to “how do the parts interact to create this result?” This change in mindset helps engineers and problem-solvers recognize hidden feedback loops, unintended consequences, and long-term patterns.

For example, imagine a university IT helpdesk struggling with slow ticket response times. A traditional fix might involve hiring more staff. Systems thinking, however might uncover a deeper issue that students are receiving inadequate technical training which in turn leads to more support requests. Or perhaps even outdated tools cause delays across multiple departments. Systems thinking looks at the entire network of causes and relationships instead of treating symptoms.

Linear Thinking vs. Systems Thinking

Traditional also known as linear thinking typically focuses on one-directional cause and effect scenarios such as “A causes B.” It works well for straightforward problems but falls short when complexity enters the equation. In contrast, systems thinking embraces circular cause-and-effect and recognizies that “A influences B, which may also influence A.” This feedback loop concept is critical in systems engineering.

Holt (2021) contrasts these two mindsets by showing that linear thinking leads to reactive decision-making, while systems thinking enables proactive design. For instance, in digital systems, a linear approach might optimize a single component like speed, which could unintentionally make other parts unstable. Systems thinking helps anticipate those ripple effects.

This shift from breaking problems into parts to viewing the entire system is what makes systems thinking so relevant in today’s interconnected environments. According to the International Council on Systems Engineering, systems engineering depends on understanding lifecycle impacts and how interconnected elements influence one another across complex architectures (INCOSE, 2015).

Why Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) Matters

As complexity grows, engineers need more than intuition to manage it they need a clear visual model of how everything fits together. That’s where MBSE comes in. MBSE replaces traditional document-based engineering with digital models that represent systems and their relationships. These models allow engineers to visualize how parts interact, simulate outcomes, and ensure that the design stays aligned with the system’s goals throughout the lifecycle.

For instance, when designing a smart home ecosystem, MBSE allows you to model devices, software, user interactions, and data flows. Instead of building the system first and fixing problems later, MBSE helps teams build a blueprint beforehand identifying potential bottlenecks and inefficiencies early.

Personal Reflection

Before IT 248, I often approached problems by jumping straight into solutions. But systems thinking has challenged me to take a step back and consider the broader environment. I’ve learned that sometimes, what looks like a technical failure is actually a process issue or even a cultural one. For example, in a past group project, our slow progress wasn’t because of a bad workflow, but because team roles were unclear. A systems mindset would have helped me identify that sooner.

What surprised me most is how useful systems thinking is outside of engineering. Whether managing a team, solving a customer service problem, or even planning personal goals, thinking in terms of relationships and patterns can lead to better decisions. Meadows (2008) notes that systems thinking gives us the ability to find leverage points — places in a system where a small shift can produce big change.

Conclusion

Systems thinking gives us the tools to tackle complexity by shifting our focus from isolated parts to interacting systems. It’s a mindset that helps uncover the root causes of problems and anticipate the consequences of decisions. When combined with Model-Based Systems Engineering, this approach becomes even more powerful — allowing engineers to create clear, adaptable models of the systems they’re building. As we’ve explored in IT 248, this integrated perspective isn’t just useful — it’s essential in the design and management of complex digital environments.

Understanding Life Cycle Thinking in Systems Engineering

In systems engineering, the goal is not just to build something and be done. Systems are dynamic and go through a full journey. This journey is called the systems development life cycle, which guides a system from concept to retirement. As explained in Systems Engineering Demystified (Holt, 2023), it helps engineers plan for every phase of a system’s life, not just the design stage.

Thinking in terms of a life cycle allows teams to consider the long-term impacts of their decisions. It promotes smarter planning, reduces risk, and helps make systems that are sustainable, adaptable, and easier to manage throughout their use.

Life Cycle Models in Systems Engineering

There are several ways to approach the development of a system. The three most common models are the linear model, the iterative model, and the incremental model. Each offers its own advantages and drawbacks, depending on the goals and flexibility of the project.

Linear Model

The linear model, sometimes called the waterfall model, moves through each phase in order. You start with requirements, move to design, then build, test, and maintain.

  • Easy to manage with clear steps
  • Works well when requirements will not change
  • Hard to go back if issues are found later

This model works best in environments where everything needs to be locked in from the start. It is predictable, but not very flexible.

Iterative Model

The iterative model focuses on repeating the development cycle. Each cycle produces a working version that improves based on feedback.

  • Encourages learning as you build
  • Responds better to changing requirements
  • Can waste time without clear direction

This is common in software development and is useful when the end goal is evolving. It supports refinement and exploration, which makes it ideal for innovative work (Estefan, 2007).

Incremental Model

The incremental model delivers the system in pieces. Each piece adds functionality until the full system is complete.

  • Delivers working parts faster
  • Supports shifting priorities
  • Can create integration challenges

This approach helps users see progress early and give feedback. It balances structure with flexibility and is often used in product rollouts and digital platforms (Walden et al., 2015).

Why Systems Life Cycle Thinking Matters

Thinking across the entire life of a system helps avoid surprises. For example, some large system failures happened not because of bad design, but because the long-term use was not considered. Life cycle thinking helps engineers ask: how will this system be maintained, updated, or retired?

This thinking also improves collaboration. Everyone on the team sees the big picture and knows how their part fits in. Combined with model-based systems engineering (MBSE), this makes the whole development process more transparent and effective (Holt, 2023).

MBSE and the Future of Systems Development

MBSE is changing how we build systems. Instead of relying only on documents, engineers use models to manage complexity. These models show how parts connect, how behavior changes, and how everything fits together.

MBSE tools help teams avoid errors and spot issues early. This is especially important in large systems where changes in one area can affect the whole. MBSE supports life cycle thinking by making information more visible and easier to manage across all phases (Estefan, 2007).

Final Thoughts

Systems engineering is not just about solving today’s problems. It is about planning for tomorrow. Life cycle models give structure, while MBSE helps teams stay connected. Together, they help engineers build systems that are useful, sustainable, and ready for whatever comes next.

Designing for People: Perception, Empathy, and the Power of Human-Centered Design

Design is not just about function. It is about people. Whether creating a digital interface, a service, or a product, human-centered design begins by understanding how people perceive, think, and feel. When we design for people, we focus on empathy, observation, and how humans interact with technology. Principles like perception, attention, and cognitive limits are essential.

What is Human-Centered Design?

Human-centered design is a way of solving problems that begins with the people who will use the solution. According to the ISO 9241-210 standard, human-centered design is an approach to interactive systems development that aims to make systems usable and useful by focusing on the users, their needs and requirements (International Organization for Standardization, 2019). The ISO model includes clear steps like understanding the context, setting user needs, and testing solutions.

The IDEO Field Guide offers a more flexible approach. It supports open thinking, observation, and creative idea generation. Where ISO gives structure, IDEO focuses on storytelling and empathy. Both methods keep users at the center and value real human insight.

Perception and Visual Hierarchy in Usability

Human perception affects how we understand and use digital interfaces. A good layout guides attention. A bad one makes users feel confused. Visual hierarchy means organizing elements to show importance. Using size, contrast, and spacing to highlight buttons and headings helps users know where to go next.

Johnson (2020) explains that early choices like layout and button size can help or hurt usability. A site with equal text sizes for all content may hide important actions. People often rely on quick judgments. Design should support those quick moments of decision.

UX Laws That Guide Design

  • Jakob’s Law says people prefer websites that feel familiar. If most sites use a top-right menu, moving it can confuse users (Weinschenk, 2011).
  • Fitts’s Law shows that the closer and larger a button is, the faster users can click it. Making buttons easy to reach is especially helpful on mobile (Wickens et al., 2015).
  • Hick’s Law tells us that more choices take more time. Designers should limit options or show them gradually to keep users focused (Johnson, 2020).
  • Miller’s Law says people can only hold about seven items in memory. Grouping or chunking information makes it easier to understand (Miller, 1956).

Empathy and Early Design Choices

Design begins with empathy. Research like interviews and observation reveals problems users may not mention. For example, an audit might show that people with low vision struggle with color contrast. Fixing this early can lead to a more accessible and inclusive design (W3C, 2023).

Skipping early research often leads to designs that are hard to use. But when designers listen and observe from the start, they can create products that feel easy and natural. Both ISO and IDEO remind us that understanding people is the key to better design.

Conclusion

Designing for people means caring about how they think and feel. Whether using the structure of ISO 9241-210 or the creativity of IDEO, the goal is the same. Good design supports attention, avoids overload, and respects human limits. By applying UX laws and staying focused on real people, we can create tools that are helpful, clear, and human.

Making Digital Systems Usable: Memory, Habits, and the Power of Good Design

What makes a digital system usable? At the heart of usability lies the ability for a system to match the way people think and behave. This means understanding how users remember, react, and adapt. Usability is not just about clean interfaces; it’s about reducing confusion and helping people complete tasks with confidence and minimal effort. To do that, designers must consider how memory, mental models, and feedback shape every interaction.

Memory and Mental Models

People interact with digital systems based on their mental models—internal representations of how things work. These models are shaped by past experiences and influence how users expect an interface to behave. If a system’s actual structure aligns with a user’s mental model, it feels intuitive. If it doesn’t, frustration builds quickly. For example, someone familiar with physical file cabinets might expect digital files to be organized in nested folders. When software veers from these expectations, users may struggle to find what they need.

Short-term memory, also known as working memory, is limited. According to Miller’s Law, people can hold roughly seven items (plus or minus two) in working memory at one time (Miller, 1956). Designing with this limitation in mind means simplifying interfaces and avoiding information overload. For instance, dropdown menus that list 20+ options without grouping overwhelm users and make navigation harder. Interfaces should support recognition rather than recall—helping users see their options rather than forcing them to remember commands or paths.

Habits and Design Consistency

Once users learn how to perform tasks in a system, they develop habits. Consistency helps reinforce these patterns. When similar functions are placed in different locations across screens, users waste time re-learning instead of focusing on their goals. Designing consistent interfaces lowers cognitive effort and builds user confidence (Johnson, 2020).

For example, placing navigation bars in the same location across an app ensures users don’t have to search for controls on each screen. Icons should behave the same across similar contexts—clicking a trash can icon should always delete something. Familiarity speeds up learning, especially for complex systems.

Image Source: WordPress (Primary Rules for Designing Consistent User Interfaces)

Feedback and Interaction

Feedback is one of the most critical elements of usability. Systems should always communicate with users about what is happening. Whether it’s a loading spinner, an error message, or a green checkmark confirming a completed task, feedback reduces uncertainty. Without it, users feel lost or assume a system is broken (Norman, 2013).

For example, a form that refreshes with no message after submission causes confusion. Did it work? Did it fail? A simple “Thanks for submitting!” message or progress bar can make a huge difference. Real-time feedback helps users stay oriented and in control.

When Design Adds to Cognitive Load

We’ve all encountered poorly designed interfaces. Think of a cluttered website with flashing banners, auto-playing videos, and ten overlapping navigation menus. These design choices create cognitive overload, forcing users to spend extra effort just figuring out what to click. This goes against Tesler’s Law, which states that every application has an inherent complexity. The goal is to shift that complexity from the user to the system (Tesler, 1984).

For example, the IRS tax website often overwhelms users with dense jargon, complicated navigation, and unclear form structure. In contrast, TurboTax simplifies the experience by guiding users step-by-step, using plain language and real-time checks. The same task—filing taxes—feels easier not because it's inherently simpler, but because of better design.

Image Source: Teach-ICT (System Feedback)

Principles from Designing with the Mind in Mind

Two key principles from Jeff Johnson’s Designing with the Mind in Mind illustrate why some systems are easier to use than others. First, “Users perceive what they expect.” This means designers must be careful about changing standard patterns or visual cues. Users may overlook even important elements if they’re not where expected. Second, “Users focus attention on tasks, not the interface.” This principle reminds us that interfaces should stay out of the way—good design enables task completion, not distraction (Johnson, 2020).

When systems fail to follow these principles, users get stuck. For instance, moving the ‘Submit’ button to the top of a form (rather than the bottom) violates expectations. Users may miss it entirely or waste time scrolling. Interface decisions that contradict mental shortcuts slow everyone down.

Conclusion

Usable digital systems are those that work with our memory, support our habits, and align with our mental models. Consistency and feedback reduce confusion. Good design shifts complexity from the user to the system, creating smoother, more efficient experiences. By applying cognitive psychology and UX principles thoughtfully, we can build systems that feel natural and helpful instead of confusing or burdensome.

Designing Flow: How Interface Logic Shapes User Behavior

Have you ever clicked the wrong button on a website because it looked more important than the one you actually needed? Or abandoned a form halfway through because it didn’t give you any feedback? These aren’t just annoyances—they’re examples of how interface logic can either support or sabotage user experience. The way designers organize content, style buttons, provide feedback, and structure layouts has a massive impact on how people move through digital spaces. In this post, I’ll explore how these choices influence behavior, referencing three key UX laws and personal experiences—both positive and painful—to show why interface design matters more than ever.

Learning Made Easy: Duolingo’s Friendly Flow

Duolingo is a language learning app that excels in intuitive interaction and visual clarity. It’s colorful, friendly, and incredibly easy to navigate, even for first-time users. What makes it so effective is the way it gently guides the user through lessons using layout, feedback, and gamified elements. Each lesson appears as a large, tappable circle that progresses vertically, helping users intuitively move from one concept to the next. The interface never demands cognitive strain—everything feels like a natural step forward.

What’s brilliant here is the use of the Aesthetic–Usability Effect (Lidwell, Holden, & Butler, 2010). Duolingo’s bright color palette and minimalist shapes make it look simple and welcoming, even when the tasks involve complex grammar. This aesthetic friendliness makes users more forgiving when the app falters. For instance, if you accidentally choose the wrong answer, the app immediately responds with a subtle animation, color change, and a cheerful tone. Instead of discouraging you, the app motivates you to try again.

The app also uses the Von Restorff Effect to guide behavior. Key actions, such as “Start,” “Check,” or “Next,” are always brightly colored—typically green—making them stand out against muted backgrounds. This contrast ensures that users never struggle to find the next step. Even icons like hearts for lives or stars for bonuses are visually prioritized, reinforcing goals and rewards.

Duolingo excels in feedback as well. If you answer correctly, you get a green highlight and a soft confirmation chime. If incorrect, you’re told why and shown the correct answer with a clear, friendly tone. These feedback loops are immediate and informative, which reduces uncertainty and keeps the experience flowing. This kind of micro-interaction design transforms an educational app into something playful and rewarding (Budiu, 2022).

When Interfaces Fail: A DMV Horror Story

On the opposite end of the spectrum is my recent experience with a state DMV website while trying to renew my driver’s license. The homepage was a cluttered mess—dozens of menu items, confusing categories, banner announcements, and even a scrolling news ticker (in 2025!). It was like stepping into a time machine that forgot what usability meant. I wasn’t sure where to click or how many steps the process would take.

Key functions like “License Renewal” weren’t even labeled clearly. Instead, they were buried under “Driver Services,” which opened a submenu of unrelated links. I had to scan everything line-by-line to find the correct action. This completely ignored the Von Restorff Effect, as nothing stood out. Every button looked the same. Important and unimportant actions were presented with equal visual weight. I felt overwhelmed before I even started.

To make matters worse, when I finally located the form and entered my ID number—with dashes—the page just refreshed without explanation. No error message, no feedback. I tried again without the dashes, and it worked. This failure to be flexible violates Postel’s Law, which says systems should be liberal in what they accept from users and strict in what they output (Babich, 2020). A forgiving interface would have either stripped the dashes automatically or informed me they weren’t allowed. Instead, I had to guess—and that eroded my trust in the site.

There was also no feedback after submitting the form. The page just went blank for a moment before returning to the homepage. Did it go through? Was there an error? Without a success message or visual cue, I wasn’t sure whether to refresh, try again, or wait for an email. This absence of feedback creates uncertainty and frustration—two things no user should ever feel when trying to complete a basic online task.

Key UX Laws at Work

The contrast between these two experiences helps highlight just how essential good interface logic is. Duolingo follows principles that ease cognitive load, reward user input, and emphasize what matters. The DMV site ignores these principles, creating a maze of ambiguity and unnecessary complexity.

The Aesthetic–Usability Effect shows us that users are more likely to trust and tolerate visually pleasing systems (Lidwell et al., 2010). A cheerful, inviting interface softens negative moments, while a cluttered or outdated one increases friction—even if it’s technically functional.

The Von Restorff Effect reminds designers to use contrast intentionally. Without visual hierarchy, users don’t know where to look or what to do next. In digital spaces, time is precious and confusion costs clicks.

Postel’s Law is a technical principle with major UX implications. A forgiving interface that interprets user input flexibly—whether it’s dashes in a number or mixed capital letters—creates confidence. A rigid system without clear messages creates anxiety and failure.

Conclusion: Design as Experience

Interface logic isn’t just about pretty buttons or trendy layouts—it’s about guiding users with care and purpose. Every choice, from padding around a button to how errors are displayed, contributes to the overall experience. Good design feels invisible—it supports our behavior, builds confidence, and makes digital tools feel natural to use. Bad design gets in the way, slows us down, and makes us feel dumb or lost.

Whether you’re creating an app like Duolingo or maintaining a government website, you’re shaping a journey. That journey should be clear, forgiving, and rewarding. And with the help of UX principles like the Aesthetic–Usability Effect, the Von Restorff Effect, and Postel’s Law, designers can ensure that users not only succeed—but enjoy the process too.

Designing Ethical and Meaningful UX Through Motion, Timing, and Prevention

Introduction

Thoughtful interaction design doesn't just look good—it feels good. The subtleties of timing, feedback, and motion can shape whether an experience delights or frustrates. But with the growing power of persuasive design, ethical considerations are more important than ever. In this post, we’ll explore how motion and timing affect emotion, reference key UX principles, and reflect on the designer’s ethical responsibility.

Timing and Responsiveness: The Core of Feeling "Understood"

Timing is more than speed—it's about how quickly feedback occurs after user input. Research shows that when users receive instant or near-instant responses, satisfaction increases. According to the Doherty Threshold, productivity soars when system response time is under 400 milliseconds (Yablonski, 2020). Apps like Instagram and Google Docs thrive because they meet this threshold, creating a flow state for users.

When users feel that their actions are acknowledged swiftly, they experience a sense of control and assurance. This aligns with principles of cognitive fluency: the brain prefers interactions that feel easy and fluid. Long waits without visual feedback, such as a blank screen or unresponsive button, break that fluency. Instead of flow, users feel friction, frustration, and even anxiety. Designers can counter this by ensuring that loading indicators or visual cues immediately acknowledge input, even if a process takes a few seconds to complete.

Johnson (2020) explains that when feedback is delayed without acknowledgment, users experience cognitive dissonance and feel ignored. Something as simple as a loading spinner or a "saving..." message can reestablish trust during moments of waiting. Microinteractions play a vital role here—subtle animations or changes in color, icon state, or label can signify that a task is underway. These microinteractions act as silent communicators between user and system.

Motion with Meaning

Motion should clarify, not distract. When users hover over a button and it animates subtly, they feel acknowledged. But unnecessary movement can be overwhelming. According to Johnson (2020), our brains are wired to notice motion—it’s a survival instinct. That means gratuitous animation pulls focus, wasting cognitive energy. It's also a potential accessibility concern for users with vestibular disorders.

Instead, motion should enhance orientation. Good examples include Apple’s UI transitions or Google Material Design’s motion guidelines. When opening an app, the animation mimics physical movement. This anchors users in a spatial model of navigation and supports continuity between screens. It helps maintain mental models, reducing cognitive strain.

This also supports the Peak-End Rule, a UX law that suggests users judge experiences based on how they felt at the most intense moment and at the end. Smooth, meaningful transitions create positive peaks. Avoiding jarring shifts or crashes preserves a positive end, leading to better overall satisfaction.

Error Prevention and the Psychology of Choices

Users don’t like making mistakes—and they hate being blamed for them. Error prevention involves anticipating mistakes before they happen. One powerful tool here is Hick’s Law, which states that decision time increases with the number of options (Yablonski, 2020). Designers can use this to reduce complexity. For instance, presenting options progressively instead of all at once can reduce overwhelm.

Chunking information and clearly labeling actions reduces user error. According to Miller’s Law (Yablonski, 2020), people can only hold about seven pieces of information in working memory. That’s why multi-step forms or progressive disclosure are effective. For example, Amazon’s one-click purchase simplifies a typically error-prone task—online checkout—to a single button.

Additionally, designing for error recovery is just as important. Providing undo options, confirmation dialogues, and informative error messages allows users to feel in control rather than helpless. Google Docs’ version history is a stellar example—it offers reassurance that no mistake is permanent. Designing with forgiveness in mind leads to a more humane and usable experience.

Designing Persuasively—But Ethically

Persuasive design walks a fine line. Techniques like infinite scroll or streaks (in apps like Duolingo) keep users coming back—but can easily become manipulative. Eyal (2014) argues that habits are formed through a trigger-action-reward loop. While this builds engagement, it can exploit users’ attention if not handled ethically.

Designers must ask: Is this keeping users engaged—or trapping them? Are we empowering choice or reducing it? Ethics in UX means understanding behavioral psychology while avoiding coercion. Nudges—subtle design cues that guide users—should be used with transparency, not deceit.

Cooper et al. (2014) emphasize that ethical design empowers users without deception. Dark patterns—like hiding unsubscribe buttons or sneakily pre-checking boxes—might boost short-term engagement but destroy long-term trust. Ethical design respects autonomy and promotes informed consent.

Transparency is key. If you're using personalization algorithms, inform users. If a choice has consequences, surface that information clearly. Empowerment, not manipulation, should be the ultimate goal of persuasive design.

Conclusion

Great UX is invisible—it feels intuitive, supportive, and empowering. When we design with attention to timing, motion, and error prevention, we don’t just make beautiful apps—we make meaningful ones. And when we balance persuasion with ethics, we build trust that lasts.

UX is ultimately about people. Every decision, from how fast a button responds to how an error message is worded, contributes to emotional resonance. Designers have a responsibility to consider the psychological and ethical implications of their work. The tools we create influence behavior, emotions, and even societal norms.

As designers, our responsibility goes beyond UI. It reaches into how people feel, behave, and trust the tools they use. Let’s make those feelings intentional—and ethical. By embracing UX laws and practicing ethical empathy, we not only enhance usability—we contribute to a more respectful and user-centered digital world.
