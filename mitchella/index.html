Complexity Is Never Gone — Only Relocated: Systems Thinking and MBSE

A critical reflection on mental models, feedback, and the human dimensions of model-based engineering

The Problem We Keep Rediscovering

Engineering failures are rarely caused by a single broken component. Bridges resonate apart. Hospital records systems, built to spec, cause dangerous delays because no one mapped the human workflow they would disrupt. Satellite software verifies perfectly on the ground and misbehaves in orbit. The failure is almost never in a component — it lives in the space between things: the interface, the assumption that traveled unexamined from one team’s document to another’s.

Addressing this gap — genuinely, not just naming it — is what systems thinking and model-based systems engineering (MBSE) are fundamentally about. But there is a dimension that engineering practice often underemphasizes: the cognitive one. Complexity does not only threaten our designs; it threatens our ability to understand our own designs. That distinction matters enormously for how we practice MBSE.

This blog post explores three interconnected ideas: the principle that complexity is always conserved and never truly eliminated, the systems thinking tradition that helps us locate where complexity lives, and the MBSE approach that attempts to make that complexity governable. Each idea sharpens the others, and together they describe what rigorous engineering practice actually requires — not just technically, but organizationally and cognitively.

Tesler’s Law and the Conservation of Complexity

Jon Yablonski, in Laws of UX, articulates a principle with striking relevance beyond interface design. Tesler’s Law states that every application has an inherent amount of complexity that cannot be reduced, only relocated (Yablonski, 2023). Simplifying a user interface does not eliminate complexity — it shifts it onto the developer, the system, or a different part of the workflow. The question is never whether complexity exists, but where it lands and who bears it.

“Every application has an inherent amount of complexity that cannot be removed or hidden. Instead, it must be dealt with, either in product development or in the user’s interaction with the product.”

— Yablonski, 2023, p. 61

This is a systems thinking insight in UX clothing, and it reframes MBSE precisely. Traditional document-centric engineering relocated complexity into human review: engineers manually traced change implications across hundreds of documents. MBSE does not eliminate this complexity — it moves it into a formal model where it can be computed and checked automatically. New complexity emerges around model governance and tool discipline, but it becomes manageable in ways that cognitive labor alone cannot match.

Recognizing complexity as a conserved quantity changes how we evaluate engineering decisions. We stop asking whether a choice is “simpler” and start asking where the displaced complexity is going and whether that destination can handle it. This is a harder question, and a more honest one. It also reveals why so many “simplification” efforts in engineering fail: they move complexity somewhere invisible rather than somewhere manageable, and the invisible rarely stays that way (Yablonski, 2023).

Systems Thinking: What It Actually Demands

The tradition of systems thinking insists on a shift in perception: from seeing the world in terms of things, to seeing it in terms of relationships, flows, and feedback loops. Donella Meadows identified three elements present in every system — components, interconnections, and purpose — and argued that the least visible of these are also the most important (Meadows, 2008). We specify components with great precision: dimensions, voltages, interfaces. But we treat interconnections as afterthoughts and purpose as a given. Systems thinking demands we invert these priorities.

Feedback loops are where system behavior actually lives. Balancing loops stabilize — a project manager tracking variance against budget is operating one. Reinforcing loops amplify — a schedule slip that causes overtime that causes defects that cause more slips is a reinforcing loop driving toward collapse. These dynamics are almost always invisible in a document-centric environment, where requirements capture static snapshots but say nothing about the causal chains connecting them.

Meadows (2008) also emphasized that the purpose of a system — what it actually does, as opposed to what we intend it to do — is revealed by its behavior over time, not by its design documentation. A hospital records system designed to improve care coordination may in practice slow down nurses who must navigate extra steps before accessing patient information. The system’s actual purpose, as expressed through its behavior, is different from its stated purpose. Only by tracing the feedback loops that shape daily use can that gap be diagnosed and corrected. This is systems thinking at work: looking beyond the component to the dynamic pattern.

  • Components are the most visible part of any system, yet often the least decisive (Meadows, 2008).
  • Interconnections and purpose shape behavior more than any individual part.
  • Balancing loops resist change and keep systems stable — for better or worse.
  • Reinforcing loops amplify change and can accelerate failure if left undetected.
  • A system’s actual purpose is shown by what it does, not what its documentation says it does (Meadows, 2008).

MBSE: Modeling the Whole

MBSE replaces — or more precisely, augments — documents with formal, structured models from which analyses, simulations, and traceability matrices can all be derived. The model becomes the single authoritative source of truth across the engineering lifecycle (Holt, 2021). The primary formal language is SysML, which provides standardized diagrams for structure, behavior, requirements, and parametrics in a single interconnected representation.

“MBSE enables a more integrated approach where models serve as the central artifacts, promoting a common understanding of the system across all stakeholders.”

— Holt, 2021

A change to an interface definition propagates through a SysML model in ways that surface conflicts automatically. Interface contradictions that might sit undetected for months in separate documents appear at the point of modeling. This is not merely an efficiency gain — it is a qualitative change in the system’s ability to learn about itself.

The value of a centralized model is especially clear when we consider traceability. In a document-based environment, tracing a requirement from its origin through design decisions, implementation choices, and test results requires manual cross-referencing across files that may not even share a consistent format. In an MBSE environment, that same traceability is embedded in the model structure and can be queried automatically. When a requirement changes, the model immediately reveals which design elements, behaviors, and tests depend on it. This is the systems thinking goal applied at the engineering level: making relationships — the interconnections that matter most — explicit, navigable, and computable (Holt, 2021).

What MBSE Cannot Do

A model that stakeholders do not understand, trust, or consult is functionally no different from the document silos it was meant to replace. Yablonski’s (2023) insight about mental model gaps applies directly: the gap between the model in the tool and the models in engineers’ heads is where MBSE programs fail. Every model is a simplification. The decisions about what to include are not technical — they are judgments about what matters. A SysML model capturing physical interfaces with great precision may say nothing about the organizational dynamics governing their implementation.

Large models develop their own inertia, becoming expensive to update and resistant to late-cycle learning. Engineers can end up managing the model rather than using it to manage the system. The deeper limitation is cultural. Meadows (2008) observed that the hardest interventions in any system are the ones requiring people to change their paradigms. MBSE is a paradigm shift — from documents to models as the authoritative artifact. Purchasing licenses does not accomplish it.

Organizations that skip the organizational and human investment end up with elaborate diagrams no one trusts: MBSE theater, where the forms of the practice are adopted without its substance. The signals are recognizable: models maintained by one specialist while the rest of the team continues to use documents, models updated after decisions rather than before them, models that grow large and expensive to navigate and are quietly abandoned when schedules tighten. Each of these failure modes is a systems failure, not a technology failure. The interconnection between the modeling tool and the human processes that should drive it was never designed.

Toward a Mature Practice

A mature MBSE practice treats the human stakeholder as part of the system, not a user of an external tool. It asks where complexity should land to serve engineers best — and recognizes that not everything that can be modeled should be. It invests in model governance as seriously as in model construction, because a model without an owner and a review cadence is not a shared artifact; it is a private one with broad access granted. Governance means deciding who can change what, how changes are reviewed, how the model is versioned, and how its relationship to reality is periodically audited (Holt, 2021).

Tesler’s Law, Meadows’ feedback loops, and MBSE’s model-centric paradigm all express the same truth: complexity in an engineered system is conserved, not eliminated. The right question is not “can we capture this in the model?” but “where should this complexity live, and who is best equipped to bear it?” That is simultaneously a systems engineering question, a cognitive design question, and an organizational one (Yablonski, 2023; Meadows, 2008).

Systems thinking teaches us that leverage lives in feedback loops and purpose — not components. MBSE, practiced well, is how that lesson gets put to work: making invisible dynamics visible, traceable, and partially governable. The challenge is ensuring the humans who build and consult the model are equipped to do so honestly — alert to the gap between the model and the system it represents, and resistant to mistaking precision for accuracy. A precise model of the wrong thing is still wrong. That is not a failure of tools; it is a failure of the systems thinking that should have guided their use. Getting both right, together, is the actual work of systems engineering.

Not Just Built — Guided: What Three Life Cycle Models Show About How We Think About Systems

Systems Engineering · Life Cycle Models

Introduction

Picture a program that fails, not because its engineers lacked skill, not because the requirements were wrong, and not because the technology was unproven. It fails because the team imposed a linear model on a problem whose requirements could only be discovered through building. Or it fails because leadership adopted the vocabulary of iterative development without granting engineers the authority to revise the scope when a cycle revealed something important. In systems engineering, this inconsistency between the life cycle model and the problem structure is not an edge case. It is one of the most common and least discussed sources of program failure.

Holt (2021) frames the entire practice of systems engineering around three linked challenges: the intricacy of modern systems, the ambiguity pervading communication between stakeholders and engineers, and the fundamental difficulty of achieving a common understanding across diverse perspectives. The life cycle model, as Holt (2021) describes it, is the main mechanism by which a systems engineer imposes structure on these problems. It controls the evolution of a system from conception through retirement. But a model can only do that work if it is deliberately chosen, carefully matched to the problem at hand, and then honored in practice. The three models most used in systems engineering are linear, iterative, and incremental. Each represents a distinct stance on how complexity is best managed, and understanding that stance is what separates engineers who can execute a plan from those who can choose the right one.

The Linear Model: Structure as a Response to Complexity

The linear model, also known as the waterfall or sequential model, is built on a premise that is both simple and demanding: if you can fully understand a problem before you begin solving it, then the safest path is to do so. Requirements flow into design, design flows into implementation, implementation flows into verification, and the system flows into operation and eventual retirement. Each phase completes before the next begins. Progress moves in one direction. The system is delivered at the end of the arc.

This structure is best understood not as rigidity for its own sake, but as a deliberate response to one of the three evils Holt (2021) identifies: complexity. When the engineering challenge is sufficiently complex, when hundreds of interdependent subsystems must be specified, built, and verified in precise relationship to one another, uncontrolled change becomes catastrophic. A requirement revision late in development for a nuclear power plant, a commercial aircraft, or a deep-space probe does not affect only one component; it permeates an elaborate network of dependencies, invalidating analyses, rework cycles, and verification records. The linear model’s insistence on phase completion is, in this light, a risk management strategy. By demanding completeness at each gate, it surfaces gaps and ambiguities before they metastasize into embedded defects.

The criticism of the linear model is familiar: real requirements are rarely stable, stakeholders often cannot articulate their needs until they see a working system, and the cost of identifying a fundamental design flaw during verification can exceed the cost of the entire prior development effort. These criticisms are legitimate. But they have produced a discourse that too often dismisses the linear model categorically, when the real lesson is one of fit. Bridge construction, satellite deployment, and nuclear infrastructure are situations where requirements are genuinely stable, the cost of change is prohibitive, and regulatory accountability demands exhaustive documentation at every stage. In these contexts, the linear model’s traceability and structural clarity continue not to be limitations; they are the features. The failure mode of linear thinking is not the model itself, but its misapplication to problems whose complexity originates from ambiguity rather than scale.

The Iterative Model: Building as a Form of Thinking

The iterative model begins with a fundamentally different assumption: that the most important knowledge about a system cannot be fully articulated before development starts, because it can only be discovered through the act of building. Requirements, design, and implementation are treated not as sequential layers but as overlapping surfaces, each guiding and reshaping the others across successive cycles. The system spirals toward completeness rather than marching in a straight line.

Unlike sequential approaches, iterative development allows constant refinement of both requirements and solutions based on feedback and learning from each cycle, and it is especially powerful in complex, high-uncertainty projects where stakeholder needs are poorly defined, or technical risk warrants early prototyping (SEBoK Editorial Board, 2025). This is where the model directly confronts the second of Holt’s (2021) three evils: ambiguous communication. When stakeholders cannot yet articulate what they need, asking them to sign off on a requirements document before development begins guarantees that the document will be wrong. The iterative model creates structured opportunities for the system to speak for itself — for a working prototype or operational increment to surface requirements that no amount of upfront analysis could have produced.

The most important and underappreciated point about iterative development, however, is that iteration is a form of thinking, not simply a form of scheduling. Terry (2023) makes this vivid by observing that even linear-looking lifecycle diagrams hide the iterative, incremental, and recurrent nature of the underlying process, and that the formal Vee model, despite its sequential appearance, depends on ongoing iteration to function in the real world. Modern systems that must stay operational for decades cannot be adequately governed by a single forward pass through a development model. They require continual involvement from systems engineering, which the standard lifecycle diagram does not depict. This means that iteration is not a concession to disorder; it is a recognition of how complex systems evolve.

The iterative model is also, unfortunately, the most frequently misapplied. Organizations adopt their vocabulary — sprints, cycles, feedback loops, and retrospectives — without adopting their underlying discipline: the genuine authority and willingness to revise requirements, architecture, and scope in response to what each cycle reveals. The result is what might be called pseudo-iteration: a calendar-based ritual that cycles through predetermined phases while leaving all basic assumptions untouched. Teams call it iterative because they operate in two-week sprints. They behave linearly because no program manager will authorize a scope change based on what those sprints demonstrated. The overhead of iteration is incurred; the benefit is not. Closing this gap demands organizational commitment that goes far deeper than process adoption, and it is the defining difference between a team that is genuinely adaptive and one that is merely performing adaptability.

The Incremental Model: Staged Value Without Staged Thinking

The incremental model is often mistaken for the iterative model, as both involve delivering capabilities in stages and revisiting development activities. However, the distinction is critical: the iterative model allows requirements to evolve with each cycle, adapting as new insights emerge. In contrast, the incremental model requires that requirements be fully defined at the outset; the complete system is planned and specified at the outset, then delivered in a series of increments. Each increment adds to the operational capability of the already-fielded system, but the plan does not change based on what is learned during development (Federal Highway Administration, n.d.).

This model excels when the organization is certain of its requirements but must phase delivery over time. For example, infrastructure rollouts, metropolitan transit expansions, and phased defense deployments fit this model. Unlike iteration, where requirements are shaped as work progresses, the incremental approach retains the original plan, segmenting the system into workable deliveries. A city might define its entire intelligent transportation system up front, covering network architecture, sensors, communication, and management systems, and then implement it in increments, district by district. Each increment is fully operational before the next begins (Federal Highway Administration, n.d.). This approach delivers real operational value early, avoids the ambiguity of evolving requirements, and gives stakeholders working systems that accumulate toward the complete solution.

What makes the incremental model genuinely demanding — and what its critics regularly miss — is that it requires more disciplined upfront architecture than the linear model, not less. As the Systems Engineering Body of Knowledge notes, proper planning of increments is both critical and challenging: stakeholders expect a master plan for the entire system, yet subsequent increments must build on the lessons learned from previous ones without destabilizing what has already been fielded (SEBoK Editorial Board, 2025). Every increment must function as a complete operational system. Each must interoperate cleanly with what came before and accommodate what will come after. This is an architectural challenge of the highest order. If the foundation of increment one is not designed with full knowledge of what increments two, three, and four will require, the seams between them become the most expensive and fragile parts of the system. The incremental model’s value is real, but it is earned through exceptional foresight, not simply by staging delivery.

The Model Behind the Models: Choosing as an Act of Engineering

Each of the three models encodes a different implicit answer to the same underlying question: what is the dominant source of uncertainty in this program, and when in the development process will that uncertainty be resolved? The linear model assumes the primary uncertainty is technical complexity and that it can be substantially resolved before implementation begins through rigorous upfront analysis. The iterative model assumes the primary uncertainty is ambiguity about what the system should do, and that it can only be resolved through successive cycles of building and learning. The incremental model assumes there is no meaningful requirement uncertainty, yet operational, financial, or logistical constraints require staged delivery of a system whose design is already fully understood.

Reading a program’s uncertainty profile correctly is, therefore, the prerequisite to choosing the right model. This is not a soft skill or a matter of intuition; it is a systems engineering task that deserves the same systematic scrutiny as requirements analysis or architecture selection. Holt (2021) is explicit that the third evil of systems engineering, lack of understanding, is often the most damaging precisely because it operates silently. Teams do not know what they do not understand until the consequences surface in cost overruns, failed reviews, or systems that work as specified but not as intended. A life-cycle model chosen without a deliberate analysis of the program’s uncertainty profile is a form of exactly this blindness: a decision made by default, dressed up as a decision made by design.

Real programs frequently demand exactly this kind of hybrid thinking. The NAVSTAR GPS system, developed and fielded by the U.S. Department of Defense over several decades, illustrates how a single program can require all three models to be applied simultaneously across several layers. The satellite constellation itself was deployed incrementally, Block I, then Block II, then successive generations, with the complete system architecture specified in advance, and each block fully operational before the next was launched. Meanwhile, the ground control software evolved iteratively as operational experience revealed capability gaps that pre-deployment analysis could not have predicted. And within each block’s development cycle, the hardware integration work followed a largely linear path, because the physics of satellite design tolerates no late-stage surprises. The sophistication of the systems engineering involved was not in applying any single model, but in recognizing which uncertainties each layer carried and governing them accordingly. Managing the seams where those models met — where iteratively developed software had to interface with incrementally fielded hardware governed by linear specifications — was itself the hardest systems engineering challenge of the program.

The greatest risk in lifecycle model selection is not choosing the wrong model but applying a recognized model as a label rather than as a genuine organizing logic. This is the pathology that might be called model theater: the adoption of a framework’s vocabulary, ceremonies, and documentation structure without internalizing the discipline it was designed to impose. In these cases, the model’s overhead is paid in full while its benefits evaporate entirely. The organization is worse off than if it had selected informality, because the framework’s structure now provides false reassurance that the problem is being managed when it is merely being described.

Conclusion

The three life cycle models explored here are not competing answers to the same question. They are answers to three different questions about the nature of the problem being solved. The linear model asks: how do we manage complexity when requirements are already understood? The iterative model asks: how do we resolve ambiguity that can only be clarified through building? The incremental model asks: how do we deliver value progressively from a system whose design is already complete? Each question, in the right context, is the right question.

What unites all three, and what Holt (2021) makes clear throughout his treatment of systems life cycles, is that a life cycle model is not a bureaucratic procedure imposed on engineering work from the outside. It is the engineer’s primary tool for imposing order on complexity, reducing ambiguity, and building the common understanding that good systems require. Choosing a model without understanding which of those problems it is designed to solve is not a minor oversight; it is a failure of systems thinking at the point where it matters most.

Ultimately, the choice of a life cycle model is a statement of what the engineering team believes about its problem. Made well, it is the first and most consequential design decision of any program. Made poorly, it is a quietness that will speak for itself in every phase that follows.

What Your Eyes Decide Before Your Brain Does: Visual Perception and the Logic of Human-Centered Design

A critical reflection on how cognition, attention, and empathy shape the design decisions that matter most

Before You Think, You Have Already Judged

Before you consciously decide to press a button, your visual system has already done an enormous amount of work. It has parsed the scene in front of you, identified regions of contrast and motion, grouped similar elements, determined what belongs together, and estimated the effort required to reach anything interactive. By the time your prefrontal cortex has a chance to weigh in, the interface has already made an impression and quite possibly a mistake.

This is not a metaphor. Pre-attentive processing is a well-documented phenomenon in perceptual psychology: certain visual properties such as color, size, orientation, and curvature are registered by the brain in under 250 milliseconds, before conscious attention is directed (Johnson, 2021). The implications for design are enormous and persistently underappreciated. Most discussions of human-centered design (HCD) begin with user research, personas, and empathy mapping. These are necessary tools, but ones that engage users as speaking, reflecting agents. The conversation about perception, about what the human visual system will do to an interface before a user has even formed an intention, tends to arrive too late, if it arrives at all.

This post argues that genuine human-centered design requires working backward from how humans actually perceive before it works forward into what they consciously want. The perceptual and the empathetic are not separate phases of design. They are the same inquiry approached from different angles, and the most consequential design decisions happen at their intersection.

A Framework That Takes the Human Seriously

The ISO 9241-210:2019 standard for human-centred design of interactive systems is, in its own way, quietly radical. It does not begin with requirements. It begins with context, with the declaration that design must account for the full situation of use: who is performing the task, what they are trying to accomplish, what the physical and organizational environment demands, and what limitations or pressures the user brings to the moment (ISO, 2019). This framing resists the temptation that runs through most engineering and product development processes, which is the temptation to treat users as a fixed category whose needs can be gathered once and converted into a requirements document.

The standard’s insistence on iterative evaluation reflects something true about cognition that static, task-based design models tend to ignore. Users are not the same person across sessions. Attention degrades with fatigue. Stress narrows the perceptual field. Environmental noise redirects cognitive resources. The worker navigating a hospital information system at hour nine of a shift is not the same cognitive agent who participated in a usability test at ten in the morning. ISO 9241-210 does not pretend otherwise. It builds in the expectation that understanding users is not a gate to pass through but a continuous discipline, one that the design process must structurally support at every stage.

The framework also draws a crucial distinction between a system’s designed functionality and a user’s actual experience of it. A feature that performs correctly in engineering terms can still create friction, confusion, or error in deployment. This happens not because users are irrational, but because the designer’s mental model diverged from the user’s mental model early in the process, and no evaluation structure caught the gap before it became embedded in the shipped software. The standard treats this divergence as an expected risk rather than an exceptional failure, and it builds iterative checkpoints into the design process to surface and resolve misalignment before deployment.

Empathy Is Not a Feeling: It Is a Research Practice

The IDEO Field Guide to Human-Centered Design introduces a vocabulary that can initially seem imprecise. Terms like “empathy,” “desirability,” and “human stories” occupy a field accustomed to functional requirements and measurable outcomes (IDEO, 2015). But the methods the Field Guide describes are rigorously empirical in spirit, even when they resist quantification. The core insight is that empathy, in a design context, is not a disposition you cultivate toward your users. It is a practice of sustained, structured attention to behavior that users themselves often cannot articulate.

People are unreliable reporters of their own cognitive processes. They can describe what they did, but they struggle to explain why. They can name frustration after the fact, but they rarely narrate the precise moment of confusion as it occurs. The ethnographic methods the Field Guide recommends, including contextual interviews, observation in natural settings, and documentation of workarounds, exist precisely because the most relevant data about human experience cannot always be gathered by asking. It must be watched over time and across contexts.

This connects directly to what Johnson (2021) demonstrates about perception and attention under task conditions: users in goal-directed states do not read interfaces. They scan them. They look for signals that match their current intent, not for features they might find useful in the abstract. A design rich with functionality but ambiguous in visual hierarchy will consistently frustrate users who are pressed for time, which is to say, most users most of the time. The IDEO approach operationalizes this insight by demanding that solutions emerge from, rather than precede, real exposure to user behavior in context. The diverge-and-converge pattern the guide recommends mirrors what cognitive research suggests about effective problem-solving: premature closure of possibilities is a reliable source of suboptimal outcomes, and the discipline of staying open longer than feels comfortable is what separates genuinely useful solutions from familiar ones dressed in new aesthetics.

The Laws the Eye Obeys

Two foundational UX laws are particularly instructive for understanding how perception constrains design decisions well before aesthetics become relevant. Jakob’s Law states that users spend most of their time on other interfaces and therefore expect yours to work the way those others do (Yablonski, 2023). This is not mere convention. It is a statement about the economy of attention. Mental models are expensive to construct. When an interface honors an established pattern, it allows users to transfer existing cognitive structure rather than build a new one from scratch. When it violates that pattern without providing a compensating benefit, it imposes a cognitive cost that most users will experience as friction, even if they cannot identify the source of their discomfort.

Fitts’s Law formalizes a different but equally important relationship: the size and distance of an interactive target directly determines the time and error rate required to reach it (Yablonski, 2023). A primary action buried in a small, remote control is not merely an aesthetic failure. It is a functional one, measurable in milliseconds of hesitation and percentage points of error. These laws describe constraints that exist before any user preference is expressed. They are properties of the human sensorimotor and perceptual system, not of user opinion, and violating them imposes measurable costs regardless of how satisfied users claim to be when surveyed after the fact.

What makes these laws significant beyond their individual applications is what they collectively reveal: usability is not primarily a matter of learning what users want. It is a matter of understanding what their bodies and brains can efficiently do under realistic conditions. That distinction changes the nature of the design problem considerably. It shifts the inquiry from preference gathering to constraint mapping, and it places perceptual science at the center of a discipline that is often discussed primarily in terms of empathy and iteration.

The Hidden Cost of Beauty Without Rigor

There is a well-documented phenomenon in HCD research called the aesthetic-usability effect: users consistently rate attractive interfaces as more usable, even when objective task performance is equivalent or worse (Tractinsky et al., 2000). The implication sounds like good news for designers. Make it beautiful and users will forgive friction. But the effect cuts in a more troubling direction. An aesthetically pleasing interface can actively mask usability problems during evaluation. Users who feel positively about a design’s appearance are more likely to attribute their errors to themselves rather than to the system, a self-blame response that inflates perceived usability scores while leaving genuine friction unaddressed (Norman, 2013).

This means that aesthetic investment without perceptual rigor does not merely fail to help. It produces false confidence. A design team relying on satisfaction surveys without behavioral observation may ship an interface that users rate highly while performing poorly on, and have no mechanism to detect the discrepancy until support tickets accumulate or usage data reveals patterns that self-report had obscured. This is why the iterative evaluation cycles mandated by ISO 9241-210 and the observational methods advocated by IDEO are not optional enhancements to a design process. They are structural corrections for a predictable cognitive bias in how users self-report their experience, and they exist in part to surface what aesthetic appeal tends to conceal.

Every Interface Is a Theory of the User

Every interface encodes assumptions. It assumes what users know, what they can perceive, how much effort they are willing to expend, and what mental models they bring to the encounter. Most of these assumptions are never made explicit. They are embedded in layout decisions, typographic hierarchy, color contrast ratios, interactive affordances, and the sequencing of tasks, and they are almost never tested against the reality of human perception until something has already gone wrong in deployment.

What human-centered design, in its most rigorous form, demands is that these implicit theories be surfaced and interrogated before they are shipped. ISO 9241-210 provides the process structure for that interrogation. The IDEO Field Guide provides the investigative methods. Johnson’s perceptual science and Yablonski’s synthesis of UX law provide the empirical grounding. Together, they point toward a design practice that takes seriously what the brain does before the user decides anything, and builds forward from that understanding rather than treating cognition as a downstream concern to be addressed after visual design is complete.

The challenge in practice is less technical than it is organizational and attitudinal. Most of what the perceptual and HCD literature recommends is not difficult to implement once a team commits to it: honor established interaction patterns, size targets for human hands rather than screen real estate, group related information visually, and reduce unnecessary competition for attention. The obstacle is the genuine willingness to treat user research as a continuous discipline rather than a phase to be completed, and to let observed behavior override confident intuition about what should work.

That willingness, more than any particular method, framework, or design tool, is what distinguishes design that is human-centered in name from design that is human-centered in practice. The eye decides before the brain. Good design knows this, and begins there.
