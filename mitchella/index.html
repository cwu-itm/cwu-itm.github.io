Complexity Is Never Gone — Only Relocated: Systems Thinking and MBSE

A critical reflection on mental models, feedback, and the human dimensions of model-based engineering

The Problem We Keep Rediscovering

Engineering failures are rarely caused by a single broken component. Bridges resonate apart. Hospital records systems, built to spec, cause dangerous delays because no one mapped the human workflow they would disrupt. Satellite software verifies perfectly on the ground and misbehaves in orbit. The failure is almost never in a component — it lives in the space between things: the interface, the assumption that traveled unexamined from one team’s document to another’s.

Addressing this gap — genuinely, not just naming it — is what systems thinking and model-based systems engineering (MBSE) are fundamentally about. But there is a dimension that engineering practice often underemphasizes: the cognitive one. Complexity does not only threaten our designs; it threatens our ability to understand our own designs. That distinction matters enormously for how we practice MBSE.

This blog post explores three interconnected ideas: the principle that complexity is always conserved and never truly eliminated, the systems thinking tradition that helps us locate where complexity lives, and the MBSE approach that attempts to make that complexity governable. Each idea sharpens the others, and together they describe what rigorous engineering practice actually requires — not just technically, but organizationally and cognitively.

Tesler’s Law and the Conservation of Complexity

Jon Yablonski, in Laws of UX, articulates a principle with striking relevance beyond interface design. Tesler’s Law states that every application has an inherent amount of complexity that cannot be reduced, only relocated (Yablonski, 2023). Simplifying a user interface does not eliminate complexity — it shifts it onto the developer, the system, or a different part of the workflow. The question is never whether complexity exists, but where it lands and who bears it.

“Every application has an inherent amount of complexity that cannot be removed or hidden. Instead, it must be dealt with, either in product development or in the user’s interaction with the product.”

— Yablonski, 2023, p. 61

This is a systems thinking insight in UX clothing, and it reframes MBSE precisely. Traditional document-centric engineering relocated complexity into human review: engineers manually traced change implications across hundreds of documents. MBSE does not eliminate this complexity — it moves it into a formal model where it can be computed and checked automatically. New complexity emerges around model governance and tool discipline, but it becomes manageable in ways that cognitive labor alone cannot match.

Recognizing complexity as a conserved quantity changes how we evaluate engineering decisions. We stop asking whether a choice is “simpler” and start asking where the displaced complexity is going and whether that destination can handle it. This is a harder question, and a more honest one. It also reveals why so many “simplification” efforts in engineering fail: they move complexity somewhere invisible rather than somewhere manageable, and the invisible rarely stays that way (Yablonski, 2023).

Systems Thinking: What It Actually Demands

The tradition of systems thinking insists on a shift in perception: from seeing the world in terms of things, to seeing it in terms of relationships, flows, and feedback loops. Donella Meadows identified three elements present in every system — components, interconnections, and purpose — and argued that the least visible of these are also the most important (Meadows, 2008). We specify components with great precision: dimensions, voltages, interfaces. But we treat interconnections as afterthoughts and purpose as a given. Systems thinking demands we invert these priorities.

Feedback loops are where system behavior actually lives. Balancing loops stabilize — a project manager tracking variance against budget is operating one. Reinforcing loops amplify — a schedule slip that causes overtime that causes defects that cause more slips is a reinforcing loop driving toward collapse. These dynamics are almost always invisible in a document-centric environment, where requirements capture static snapshots but say nothing about the causal chains connecting them.

Meadows (2008) also emphasized that the purpose of a system — what it actually does, as opposed to what we intend it to do — is revealed by its behavior over time, not by its design documentation. A hospital records system designed to improve care coordination may in practice slow down nurses who must navigate extra steps before accessing patient information. The system’s actual purpose, as expressed through its behavior, is different from its stated purpose. Only by tracing the feedback loops that shape daily use can that gap be diagnosed and corrected. This is systems thinking at work: looking beyond the component to the dynamic pattern.

  • Components are the most visible part of any system, yet often the least decisive (Meadows, 2008).
  • Interconnections and purpose shape behavior more than any individual part.
  • Balancing loops resist change and keep systems stable — for better or worse.
  • Reinforcing loops amplify change and can accelerate failure if left undetected.
  • A system’s actual purpose is shown by what it does, not what its documentation says it does (Meadows, 2008).

MBSE: Modeling the Whole

MBSE replaces — or more precisely, augments — documents with formal, structured models from which analyses, simulations, and traceability matrices can all be derived. The model becomes the single authoritative source of truth across the engineering lifecycle (Holt, 2021). The primary formal language is SysML, which provides standardized diagrams for structure, behavior, requirements, and parametrics in a single interconnected representation.

“MBSE enables a more integrated approach where models serve as the central artifacts, promoting a common understanding of the system across all stakeholders.”

— Holt, 2021

A change to an interface definition propagates through a SysML model in ways that surface conflicts automatically. Interface contradictions that might sit undetected for months in separate documents appear at the point of modeling. This is not merely an efficiency gain — it is a qualitative change in the system’s ability to learn about itself.

The value of a centralized model is especially clear when we consider traceability. In a document-based environment, tracing a requirement from its origin through design decisions, implementation choices, and test results requires manual cross-referencing across files that may not even share a consistent format. In an MBSE environment, that same traceability is embedded in the model structure and can be queried automatically. When a requirement changes, the model immediately reveals which design elements, behaviors, and tests depend on it. This is the systems thinking goal applied at the engineering level: making relationships — the interconnections that matter most — explicit, navigable, and computable (Holt, 2021).

What MBSE Cannot Do

A model that stakeholders do not understand, trust, or consult is functionally no different from the document silos it was meant to replace. Yablonski’s (2023) insight about mental model gaps applies directly: the gap between the model in the tool and the models in engineers’ heads is where MBSE programs fail. Every model is a simplification. The decisions about what to include are not technical — they are judgments about what matters. A SysML model capturing physical interfaces with great precision may say nothing about the organizational dynamics governing their implementation.

Large models develop their own inertia, becoming expensive to update and resistant to late-cycle learning. Engineers can end up managing the model rather than using it to manage the system. The deeper limitation is cultural. Meadows (2008) observed that the hardest interventions in any system are the ones requiring people to change their paradigms. MBSE is a paradigm shift — from documents to models as the authoritative artifact. Purchasing licenses does not accomplish it.

Organizations that skip the organizational and human investment end up with elaborate diagrams no one trusts: MBSE theater, where the forms of the practice are adopted without its substance. The signals are recognizable: models maintained by one specialist while the rest of the team continues to use documents, models updated after decisions rather than before them, models that grow large and expensive to navigate and are quietly abandoned when schedules tighten. Each of these failure modes is a systems failure, not a technology failure. The interconnection between the modeling tool and the human processes that should drive it was never designed.

Toward a Mature Practice

A mature MBSE practice treats the human stakeholder as part of the system, not a user of an external tool. It asks where complexity should land to serve engineers best — and recognizes that not everything that can be modeled should be. It invests in model governance as seriously as in model construction, because a model without an owner and a review cadence is not a shared artifact; it is a private one with broad access granted. Governance means deciding who can change what, how changes are reviewed, how the model is versioned, and how its relationship to reality is periodically audited (Holt, 2021).

Tesler’s Law, Meadows’ feedback loops, and MBSE’s model-centric paradigm all express the same truth: complexity in an engineered system is conserved, not eliminated. The right question is not “can we capture this in the model?” but “where should this complexity live, and who is best equipped to bear it?” That is simultaneously a systems engineering question, a cognitive design question, and an organizational one (Yablonski, 2023; Meadows, 2008).

Systems thinking teaches us that leverage lives in feedback loops and purpose — not components. MBSE, practiced well, is how that lesson gets put to work: making invisible dynamics visible, traceable, and partially governable. The challenge is ensuring the humans who build and consult the model are equipped to do so honestly — alert to the gap between the model and the system it represents, and resistant to mistaking precision for accuracy. A precise model of the wrong thing is still wrong. That is not a failure of tools; it is a failure of the systems thinking that should have guided their use. Getting both right, together, is the actual work of systems engineering.
