Introduction

Systems thinking tends to be described as a way of seeing the world, but for me it became something I had to live. When I stepped into the role as caregiver for my mom, I wasn't just helping her though medical challenges. I was learning to understand her health and well-being as a complete system. Every symptom, behavior, medication, and emotional shift was connected. Nothing existed on its own in isolation. Fast forward to now, I am studying systems thinking and model-based engineering, I realized that the same mindset that helped me care for my mother is the mindset engineers use to understand and manage complexity. Arnald and Wade note that "there is no denying the rapid growth of the complex systems that continuously spring to life...," and caregiving showed me that complexity firsthand. (Arnald & Wade, 2015).

What is Systems Thinking?

For me, systems thinking means understanding how different parts of a situation influence each other instead of treating each part like a separate problem. I learned this through my experience with caregiving long before I started studying it. When my mom was going through chemotherapy, nothing happened in isolation. Chemo affected her appetite. Her appetite affected her nutrition. Her nutrition affected her hydration. Her hydration affected her energy. Her energy affected her pain levels. Pain affected her sleep. and all that affected whether she could safely stay home or needed to go to the hospital.

If I tried to treat any of those things as a single event, I would miss the bigger pattern. Systems thinking is the ability to step back and see how all of those pieces connect. It is recognizing that one change in the system can ripple into everything else. It is understanding that the behavior you see on the surface is shaped by the relationships underneath it.

After learning the academic definitions, I realized that they matched what I have already lived. Peters explains that "at its core, systems thinking is an enterprise aimed at seeing how things are connected...(Peters, 2014). He also writes that "a system is a perceived whole, made up of parts that interact toward a common purpose" (Peters, 2014). Arnald and Wade support this idea by noting that "a basic principle of a system is that it is something more than a collection of its parts" (Arnald & Wade, 2015). These descriptions line up with what I expirienced firsthand, but my understanding comes from real life.

How My Caregiving Explains it

My caregiving experience showed me that systems thinking is not just a theory. It is a practical way of understanding complexity. When my mother's appetite dropped after chemo, it was not just a food issue or hydration issue. It was an energy issue or a pain issue. It was a hospital risk issue. Everything was connected.

Systems thinking helped me see the patterns behind her symptoms. It helped me understand that her health was not a list of separate problems. It was a system with relationships and feedback loops. Peters describes this when he explains that "feedback loops...can be reinforcing or balancing" (Peters, 2014). Arnald and Wade repeat this idea when they write that "some of the interconnections combine to form cause-effect feedback loops" (Arnald & Wade, 2015). That's exactly what I saw in my mom's day-to-day health.

How It Relates To Linear vs Systems Thinking

Linear thinking focuses on one step at a time. It assumes that if you fix one part, the whole system improves. But caregiving taught me that this is not how complex situations work. If I only focused on my mom's appetite, I would miss the dehydration. If I only focused on dehydration, I would miss the pain. If I only focused on the pain, I would miss the sleep disruption. Linear thinking breaks down when parts of a system interact.

Johnson's research on visual structure supports this idea. He writes that "prose text format forces users to read or skim everything" (Johnson, 2020). Linear thinking works the same way. It forces you to process everything in sequence, even when most of it is irrelevant.

Systems thinking is different. Johnson explains that "a visual hierarchy allows people...to quickly separate what is relevant to their goals from what is irrelevant" (Johnson, 2020). Systems thinking does the same thing. It organizes information into meaningful patterns. It highlights what matters. It reduces noise. It helps people see the whole picture instead of getting lost in the details.

Model Based Engineering

Model based engineering is built on the same principles as system thinking. Instead of describing a system in long paragraphs, engineers create structured models that show how components interact. These models use hierarchy, grouping and chunking to reduce complexity.

Johnson explains why this approach works. He writes that "presenting information in a terse, structured manner makes it easier for people to scan, understand, and remember" (Johnson, 2020). He also notes that "chinking data...helps people scan, enter, and remember it" (Johnson, 2020).

Arnald and Wade connect directly to this idea when they describe "reducing complexity by modeling systems conceptually..." (Arnald & Wade, 2015). Model based engineering uses these same principles. It breaks complex systems into meaningful parts. It organizes those parts into layers. It shows relationships visually. It reduces cognitive load. It helps engineers understand the whole system without being overwheled by the details.

How A Model Based Approach Is Applicable in the Design of Complex Digital Systems

Complex digital systems can't be understood through linear descriptions, They involve hardware, software, networks, data flows, user interactions, and external dependencies. Everything influences everything else. A model-based approach helps engineers manage this complexity by providing a structured representation of the system.

Arnald and Wade explain that "systems thinking is a set of synergistic analytic skills used to improve the capability of identifying and understanding systems..." (Arnald & Wade, 2015). These skills are essential when designing digital systems because they help engineers see how components interact and how changes ripple throughout the system.

Johnson's work supports this approach. He explains that "visual hierarchy... is a best practice way to structure information" (Johnson, 2020). Structured models do exactaly that. They chunk complexity into manageable parts. They highlight dependencies. They reduce ambiguity. They help engineers understand how changes ripple through the system.

Digital Systems Connection

Digital systems are everywhere, and they are becoming more complex every year. Systems thinking and model-based engineering provides the tools we need to understand and manage this complexity. Johnson shows that humans naturally understand structured information better than unstructured information. Peters, Arnald, and Wade show that systems thinking helps people understand how parts interact to create or shape the behavior of a whole system.

My caregiving experience taught me that understanding a system requires more than reacting to individual events. It requires seeing the patterns and relationships that shape behavior. The same mindset applies to digital systems. The principles are the same. The stakes are different, but the thinking is identical.

Conclusion

Systems thinking isn't just an academic concept. It is a practical way of understanding complexity. My caregiving experience taught me that systems thinking is essential when the parts of a system interact in ways that can't be understood through linear thinking. Johnson's research shows that humans understand structured, hierarchical information more effectively that unstructured information. Peters, Arnald, and Wade show that systems thinking helps people understand how parts interact to shape the behavior of a whole system. Model-based engineering applies these principles to the design of complex digital systems. It uses structure, hierarchy, and chunking to help engineers understand and manage complexity.

Introduction

When I first learned about systems thinking, it felt like someone had finally gave me the language to something I had already lived. Caregiving taught me to see patterns, relationships, and the ripple effects way before I learned about it from a textbook. But understanding a system is only half of the story. The other half is understanding how systems develop, how they move from an idea to a functioning system, and how they continue to evolve over time. That's where the Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC) comes in. If systems thinking helps us see the system in its entirety, the SDLC helps us guide it through changes.

What is the Systems Development Life Cycle?

The systems development life cycle is the structured process engineers use to guide a system from a concept or an idea into a working reality. Hold highlights how systems have a natural structure and they need to be molded intentionally, they say All systems have a natural hierarchy and it is... essential... to capture this hierarchy and ensure that all Elements in the model adhere to the captured hierarchy"(Holt, 2021)

In other words, systems don't just appear fully formed. They grow, change and mature, and SDLC provides the roadmap for that growth. Even though SDLC tends to be applied in the context of software, the underlying logic can be applied to any complex system. When I was caring for my mom, I didn't know of its as a "life cycle," so to speak, but looking back, I can see the phases clearly.

  • Phase 1: Assessment
  • Phase 2: Planning
  • Phase 3: Implementation
  • Phase 4: Monitoring
  • Phase 5: Adjustment
  • Phase 6: Reassessment

The stakes were different, but the structure was familiar, and digital systems follow the same logic, just with different tools and goals.

Why Life Cycle Models Exist

Life cycle models exist because there isn't a single approach that works for every system or situation. Some projects have stable requirements, and others change constantly. Some need early prototypes, and others may need strict documentation. Kyeremeh (2021) explains that as software systems grew more complex engineers need structured models to manage development, the noted that distinctive improvement lifecycle models have been characterized" To address different project needs. The three models explored in this assignment, linear, iterative, and incremental, represent three different philosophies about how systems develop or evolve.

1. The Linear Model (waterfall)

The linear model, also know as the waterfall model, moves step-by-step through a sequence of phases. Each phase needs to be completed before the next phase can begin. There are some key benefits and drawbacks using the linear model. Kyeremeh (2021) highlights that the waterfall model is "straightforward and easy to use" and works well when the requirements are "extremely well Known, clear, and fixed" (Kyeremeh,2021). However, the biggest limitation to the waterfall model is how rigid it is. Once the development process moves forward, it's very difficult to go back. Kyeremeh (2021) explains that "Once an application is in the testing stage, it is... very hard to go back and change something that was not well-thought-out in the concept stage"(kyeremeh, 2021).

This reminds me of the times when my mom's care plan was "fixed", Like a strict medication schedule or even her chemo schedule. It only worked when nothing unexpected happened. But if her symptoms changed, the whole plan had to be reevaluated. Linear models work the same way, they are great when the world behaves, and are fragile when it doesn't.

2. The Iterative Model

The Iterative model builds a small version of the system, tests it, learns from it, and revises it. Each cycle produces a new version that incorporates feedback. The iterative model also has several advantages and disadvantages. Kyeremeh (2021) descrives iteratice development as a process where "development begins by specifying and implementing just part of the software, which can then be reviewed... to identify further requirements" (Kyeremeh, 2021). This allows teams to discover requirements gradually and catch any problems early. Like any model there are the drawbacks, and the Iteration model requires discipline and user involvement. without clear goals, teams can and do get stuck in endless cycles.

I have my own experience using the iterative model, when I first started caring for my mom, I didn't know the perfect plan on day one. I tried something, observed how she responded, and adjusted. unbeknown to me, I was actively using iterative thinking, learning by doing, improving through feedback.

3. The Incremental Model

The incremental model delivers the system in fully functioning pieces. Each increment adds new capabilities, and they build on the previous increment.

Keremeh (2021) explains that incremental development "generates working software quickly and early during the software life cycle" and allows customers to "respond to each build" (kyeremeh, 2021). Like the previous models advantages come with disadvantages. The Incremental model requires strong planning. If the architecture isn't designed for incremental growth, the system can become fragmented.

I did this when caregiving as well, changing one thing in my mom's routine at a time and then building on it. For example, finding what meals work best for her. I figured out early on that high-protein foods, especially chicken, increased her energy levels. That was the first "increment" a complete improvement made a real difference. Once her energy was more stable, we added a second increment, that being short walks. At first it was fie minutes in the wheel chair. Later on, she was walking with a cane, and eventually slow, steady steps on her own. Each new step was only possible because the previous one was fully in place. That, to me, is the incremental model in real life, each improvement is complete, and each one enables the next one.

Why Life Cycle Thinking Matters for Digital Systems

Holt (2023) emphasizes that modeling must reflect real world complexity, stating "Understanding system interfaces is one of the most important aspects of developing any system, as they form the backbone of the system model" (hole, 2023) Life cycle thinking means aknowledging that systems grow and change.

Zhang ee. al. (2005) argues that modern SDLC models often overlook human needs, they said "the human interaction aspect of an information system is considered far to little and far too late in the IS development process" (Zhang et al., 2005). This resonates with me. In caregiving, you can't tack on "human needs" at the end. They have to guide every decision. Digital systems aren't any different.

Complex systems require structure. Life cycle models help engineers anticipate change, reduce risks, and maintain clarity. A system built with a linear model will behave differently than one built iteratively or incrementally. The life cycle model influences adaptability, usability, and long-term sustainability.

Why this Matters to Me

When I first learned about SDLC, it felt abstract, like something that belonged in a software engineering textbook. But the more I studied it, the more I realized it connects directly to the systems thinking I lived through. Caregiving taught me that systems evolve. They require structure, feedback, and adaptation. They need the right approach at the right time. And they succeed when the humans that are inside them are considered from the beginning. Digital systems are no different. They are built by people, for people, and they live in environments full of change. Understanding life cycle models helps engineers design systems that are not only functional but sustainable, humane, and resilient.

Conclusion

Stepping back from all of this, what stands out to me is that systems thinking and the systems development life cycle aren't just engineering concepts, they're ways of understanding how change happens. Whether I was helping my mom rebuild her strength one small improvement at a time or learning how digital systems evolve through linear, iterative, and incremental models, the pattern is the same. Systems grow through structure, feedback, and intentional guidance. Holt (2023) reminds us that systems depend on the relationships between their parts, not just the parts themselves, and Zhang emphasize that human needs have to be considered throughout the entire life cycle, not tacked on at the end. Those ideas resonate with me because they match what I've lived.

Life cycle models give engineers a way to manage complexity, reduce risk, and build systems that can adapt over time, but they also remind us that every system, digital or human, deserves a thoughtful approach that respects how it changes. Understanding these models doesn't just make me a better student, it helps me appreciate the structure behind the chaos of real life. In that sense, learning SDLC feels less like studying something new and more like finally having the language to describe what I've already learned the hard way.

Designing for People: How Perception, Attention, and Empathy Shape Human Centered Design

Human centered design begins with a simple but powerful idea. That design should start with people rather than with technology or assumptions. This means we need to understand how all humans perceive the world, how we pay attention, how we interpret cues, and how we emotionally respond to the environments and interfaces around us. Whether we're designing a mobile app, a mission critical system, or a public space, the goal is the same. We create something that aligns with human behavior not forcing everyone to adapt to the design.

This blog post explores how perception, attention, and empathy shape early design decisions, drawing on the ISO 9241 210 framework, IDEO's Field Guide, UX laws, and contemporary research on affordances. Together, these perspectives reveal a consistent truth. Design becomes intuitive when it respects the way people naturally see, think, and act.

Human Centered Design: A Foundation Built on Understanding People

ISO 9241 210 defines human centered design as an approach that focuses on the users, their needs, and their requirements throughout the design lifecycle. It emphasizes structure, traceability, and iterative evaluation. It is a systematic way to ensure usability is not an afterthought but a core requirement.

IDEO's Field Guide takes a more humanistic approach It frames human centered design as a creative, exploratory process grounded in empathy, iteration, and co-creation. IDEO describes human centered designers as people who “empathize and iterate, and we look for inspiration in unexpected places” (IDEO.org, 2025). Instead of beginning with requirements, IDEO begins with people's lived experiences.

Together, these frameworks show that human centered design is both structured and human, both analytical and empathetic. ISO emphazises discipline and stucture. IDEO emphasizes empathy and heart.

Perception and Visual Hierarchy: How People Interpret What They See

Before a person reads a word or taps a button, their brain is already making decisions. Perception is fast, automatic, and influenced deeply by visual cues. This is why early design choices like layout, contrast, spacing, and grouping can support usability or even undermine it.

Mental Models and Familiarity

Jakob's Law states that “users spend most of their time on other sites, and they prefer your site to work the same way as all the other sites they already know” (Yablonski, 2024). This reflects a core perceptual truth. People rely on mental models, which are internal expectations built from our past experiences. When a design ines up with these models, users tend to feel confident. When it violates them, the user hesitates.

Affordances: What the Environment Invites Us to Do

Affordance theory adds another layer. As Manolakelli explains, “affordances are opportunities for action that emerge from the fit between an environment and its user” (Manolakelli, 2025). A button that looks clickable, a slider that looks movable, or a card that looks swipeable are all perceived affordances. If affordances are unclear, the user should stop and think. If they are intuitive, they will act without hesitation.

This is why visual hierarchy matters. It is not only about aesthetics. It is about communicating what is possible.

My Perspective on Accessibility and Human Centered Design

In my usability and accessibility course, I have been learning how often society designs both physical and digital spaces around an assumed default user. That default user is usually someone who is able bodied, sighted, and neurotypical. Once you look from an accessibility perspective, you start to notice how many environments quietly exclude people who interact with the world differently. This applies to public spaces, classrooms, websites, and even the design of everyday objects. Human centered design becomes much more meaningful when you realize that real people are being left out of experiences that should belong to everyone.

This connects directly to the ideas of perception and affordances. Affordances describe the actions that an environment invites. A staircase invites climbing only for someone who can climb. A touchscreen invites tapping only for someone who can see it or physically reach it. A website invites navigation only when its structure is perceivable to assistive technology. When an environment only invites action for certain people, it reveals who the design was truly centered around.

In class, we have talked a lot about how something as simple as heading structure can completely change the experience for someone using a screen reader. Sighted users can visually scan a page and instantly understand hierarchy through size, spacing, and weight. A person using a screen reader does not have that luxury. They rely on semantic structure. A "Heading One" communicates that this is the main topic. A "Heading Four" communicates that this is a sub point within a larger idea. When headings are used only for visual styling instead of structure, the entire page becomes a flat wall of text. It becomes difficult to navigate, difficult to interpret, and exhausting to use.

This is where human centered design becomes more than just a method. It becomes a responsibility. Jakob's Law reminds us that people rely on familiar patterns. For screen reader users, those patterns are semantic. Hick's Law reminds us that more choices slow decision making. Poor structure forces users to make more decisions just to find information. Miller's Law reminds us that working memory is limited. When a page is not structured clearly, users must hold more information in their mind at once. All of these laws apply directly to accessibility.

Learning about accessibility has changed the way I see design. It has shown me that human centered design is not only about making things easier for the average user. It is about making sure that people with disabilities are not forced to work harder simply because the design did not consider them to begin with. Once you see how many systems fail to include disabled users, you can't unsee it. Accessibility becomes a core part of designing for real people rather than an optional add on.

Attention: Designing for Limited Cognitive Resources

Human attention is limited. Cognitive load is real. Early design decisions can either reduce or amplify that load.

Miller's Law: Working Memory Limits

Miller's Law reminds us that people can hold only about seven items in working memory. This is why menus should be chunked, forms should be broken into steps, and dashboards should avoid overwhelming users. Chunking is not a design trend. It is a cognitive necessity.

Hick's Law: Decision Time Increases with Choices

Hick's Law states that the time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of choices available. This is why less menu options improves navigation, progressive disclosure reduses uses feeling overwhelmed, and clear paths help reduce hesitation.

Good design doesn't just present information. It guides the user's attention.

Empathy: The Human Thread That Connects Everything

Empathy is the emotional sort of backbone of human centered design. IDEO describes empathy as the ability to step into another persons shoes and understand their life, needs, and constraints. Empathy pushes designers to ask what a person is trying to accomplish, what emotions they feel, what challenges they could face, and how the design can support them rather than challenge them.

Empathy transforms design from a technical exercise into a more human one.

UX Laws: Practical Tools for Supporting Human Behavior

The four UX laws all tie directly to perception, attention, and empathy.

  • Jakob's Law emphasizes familiarity
  • Fitts's Law explains how size and distance affect usability.
  • Hick's Law highlights the cost of too many choices.
  • Miller's Law reminds us that memory is limited.

Together, these laws form a practical "toolkit for designing interfaces that align with human cognition.

ISO and IDEO: Two Lenses, One Goal

  • ISO 9241 210 emphasizes a structured, requirement-driven approach to a human-centered design.
  • IDEO's Field Guide shows us how to design with empathy, explore ideas creativly, and iterate through real-world feedback.

Both insist on early user involvement, iterative refinement, and grounding design in real human behavior.

This aligns with the broader emhasis on understanding mission context, stakeholders, and operational enviornments before designing solitions. Soule (2024) reinforces this perspective explaining that “MBSE provides a formalized approach to capturing mission models, requirements, and system-of-systems relationships in a single enviornment” (Soule, 2024).

Conclusion

Designing for People Means Designing with Understanding

Human centered design isn't a checklist. It is a mindset grounded in understanding how people perceive, think, and feel. ISO highlights the inportance of structure and traceability. IDEO emphasizes empathy and creative exploration. UX laws reveal the cognitive realities designers must respect. Affordance theory shows how perception shapes action. Accessibility reminds us that design has to include everyone.

When we combine these insights, we create designs that feel natural, intuitive, inclusive, and human. That is what it means to design for everyone.
