Introduction

Systems thinking tends to be described as a way of seeing the world, but for me it became something I had to live. When I stepped into the role as caregiver for my mom, I wasn't just helping her though medical challenges. I was learning to understand her health and well-being as a complete system. Every symptom, behavior, medication, and emotional shift was connected. Nothing existed on its own in isolation. Fast forward to now, I am studying systems thinking and model-based engineering, I realized that the same mindset that helped me care for my mother is the mindset engineers use to understand and manage complexity. Arnald and Wade note that "there is no denying the rapid growth of the complex systems that continuously spring to life...," and caregiving showed me that complexity firsthand. (Arnald & Wade, 2015).

What is Systems Thinking?

For me, systems thinking means understanding how different parts of a situation influence each other instead of treating each part like a separate problem. I learned this through my experience with caregiving long before I started studying it. When my mom was going through chemotherapy, nothing happened in isolation. Chemo affected her appetite. Her appetite affected her nutrition. Her nutrition affected her hydration. Her hydration affected her energy. Her energy affected her pain levels. Pain affected her sleep. and all that affected whether she could safely stay home or needed to go to the hospital.

If I tried to treat any of those things as a single event, I would miss the bigger pattern. Systems thinking is the ability to step back and see how all of those pieces connect. It is recognizing that one change in the system can ripple into everything else. It is understanding that the behavior you see on the surface is shaped by the relationships underneath it.

After learning the academic definitions, I realized that they matched what I have already lived. Peters explains that "at its core, systems thinking is an enterprise aimed at seeing how things are connected...(Peters, 2014). He also writes that "a system is a perceived whole, made up of parts that interact toward a common purpose" (Peters, 2014). Arnald and Wade support this idea by noting that "a basic principle of a system is that it is something more than a collection of its parts" (Arnald & Wade, 2015). These descriptions line up with what I expirienced firsthand, but my understanding comes from real life.

How My Caregiving Explains IT

My caregiving experience showed me that systems thinking is not just a theory. It is a practical way of understanding complexity. When my mother's appetite dropped after chemo, it was not just a food issue or hydration issue. It was an energy issue or a pain issue. It was a hospital risk issue. Everything was connected.

Systems thinking helped me see the patterns behind her symptoms. It helped me understand that her health was not a list of separate problems. It was a system with relationships and feedback loops. Peters describes this when he explains that "feedback loops...can be reinforcing or balancing" (Peters, 2014). Arnald and Wade repeat this idea when they write that "some of the interconnections combine to form cause-effect feedback loops" (Arnald & Wade, 2015). That's exactly what I saw in my mom's day-to-day health.

How It Relates To Linear vs Systems Thinking

Linear thinking focuses on one step at a time. It assumes that if you fix one part, the whole system improves. But caregiving taught me that this is not how complex situations work. If I only focused on my mom's appetite, I would miss the dehydration. If I only focused on dehydration, I would miss the pain. If I only focused on the pain, I would miss the sleep disruption. Linear thinking breaks down when parts of a system interact.

Johnson's research on visual structure supports this idea. He writes that "prose text format forces users to read or skim everything" (Johnson, 2020). Linear thinking works the same way. It forces you to process everything in sequence, even when most of it is irrelevant.

Systems thinking is different. Johnson explains that "a visual hierarchy allows people...to quickly separate what is relevant to their goals from what is irrelevant" (Johnson, 2020). Systems thinking does the same thing. It organizes information into meaningful patterns. It highlights what matters. It reduces noise. It helps people see the whole picture instead of getting lost in the details.

Model Based Engineering

Model based engineering is built on the same principles as system thinking. Instead of describing a system in long paragraphs, engineers create structured models that show how components interact. These models use hierarchy, grouping and chunking to reduce complexity.

Johnson explains why this approach works. He writes that "presenting information in a terse, structured manner makes it easier for people to scan, understand, and remember" (Johnson, 2020). He also notes that "chinking data...helps people scan, enter, and remember it" (Johnson, 2020).

Arnald and Wade connect directly to this idea when they describe "reducing complexity by modeling systems conceptually..." (Arnald & Wade, 2015). Model based engineering uses these same principles. It breaks complex systems into meaningful parts. It organizes those parts into layers. It shows relationships visually. It reduces cognitive load. It helps engineers understand the whole system without being overwheled by the details.

How A Model Based Approach Is Applicable in the Design of Complex Digital Systems

Complex digital systems can't be understood through linear descriptions, They involve hardware, software, networks, data flows, user interactions, and external dependencies. Everything influences everything else. A model-based approach helps engineers manage this complexity by providing a structured representation of the system.

Arnald and Wade explain that "systems thinking is a set of synergistic analytic skills used to improve the capability of identifying and understanding systems..." (Arnald & Wade, 2015). These skills are essential when designing digital systems because they help engineers see how components interact and how changes ripple throughout the system.

Johnson's work supports this approach. He explains that "visual hierarchy... is a best practice way to structure information" (Johnson, 2020). Structured models do exactaly that. They chunk complexity into manageable parts. They highlight dependencies. They reduce ambiguity. They help engineers understand how changes ripple through the system.

Digital Systems Connection

Digital systems are everywhere, and they are becoming more complex every year. Systems thinking and model-based engineering provides the tools we need to understand and manage this complexity. Johnson shows that humans naturally understand structured information better than unstructured information. Peters, Arnald, and Wade show that systems thinking helps people understand how parts interact to create or shape the behavior of a whole system.

My caregiving experience taught me that understanding a system requires more than reacting to individual events. It requires seeing the patterns and relationships that shape behavior. The same mindset applies to digital systems. The principles are the same. The stakes are different, but the thinking is identical.

Conclusion

Systems thinking isn't just an academic concept. It is a practical way of understanding complexity. My caregiving experience taught me that systems thinking is essential when the parts of a system interact in ways that can't be understood through linear thinking. Johnson's research shows that humans understand structured, hierarchical information more effectively that unstructured information. Peters, Arnald, and Wade show that systems thinking helps people understand how parts interact to shape the behavior of a whole system. Model-based engineering applies these principles to the design of complex digital systems. It uses structure, hierarchy, and chunking to help engineers understand and manage complexity.
