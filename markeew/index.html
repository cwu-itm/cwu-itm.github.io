Thinking in Life Cycle

Starting with the definition of a system life cycle, it is the development of a system through six stages, Conception, Development, Production, Utilization, Support, and Retirement. (Holt, 2021) The conception stage is concered with identifiying the needs of the systems and work on stackholder analyses. The development stage works on finding possiple solutions to the proplem and work on a prototype to test the solution. The production stage takes the solution and develops and test the actual system to make sure that it is built properly. The utilization stage shows how users and trained to use it and how the system performes whem in use. The support stage helps to solve problems that come up during usage of the system. The last stage is retierment which is when the system is decommissioned and disposed of at the end of its life cycle.

Looking at the differances between the linier and nonliner life cycle models take a much more structured path that prioritises effectiveness and predictability. Nonliner models are more dynamic, flexable, and adaptable allowing for complex problems to be solved and mutiple task to be worked on a the same time. The nonliner models are better sutied for modern day system development and proplem solving.

Human-Center Design

Human Centered Design is a way of thinking that prioritizes the needs of humans when designing a product. This problem-solving technique Is used to create products, services and systems that are useful to individual by putting the audiences wants and preferences first well analyzing the pain points customers have had to deal with for similar products, services and systems that have been used in the past to make the new product, service or system more user friendly.

Harvard business school describes human centered design as a problem-solving technique that puts real people at the center of the development process, well also breaking down human centered design into 4 stages Clarify, Ideate, Develop, and Implement. The clarify stage starts the human-centered design process by collecting data and observing the needs of the customers, along with identifying pain points the customer has experienced. This stage requires the designer to put themselves in the mindset of the customer and ask themselves what this customer would need to solve their problems. The Ideate stage is where a designer would take the ideas gained in the clarify stage and brainstorm how to put the ideas together. The Develop stage is where all the ideas are gathered and combined to find all possible solutions to the problem and decide which one is best suited to solve the problem and begin development of that solution. The final stage, implementation, is where the final design is revealed and shared with the internal and external stakeholders in order to push it into the market for customer usage. This final stage does not end with the design being sold to customers because the design team need to reflect on how they worked together during this process and do they have the right mindset to continue designing for humanities ever evolving needs and wants, causing the process to begin again with new ideas for solving different needs. (Landry, 2020)

Perspective of ISO 9241-210 and IDEO's Field Guide

Two places to look for information about Human center design would be the ISO 9241-210 and the IDEO’s field guide. Both provide information about how to better develop products services or systems, where they differ is how the idea is implemented into an organization’s design team. ISO 9241-210 looks at human centered design as a standard process to be implemented in an organization to design products, services and systems that are focused on what the customer wants and needs with an emphasis on user experience throughout the lifecycle of the product, service or system. Where IDEO differs, because it covers a lot of the same ideas and priorities in development, is treating human centered design as a state of mind that the design team needs to be in instead of a process that needs to implement into the organization. IDEO looks at human centered design like an art form that is fluid and constantly changing instead of a hard science.

Effects of Visual Hierarchy and Perception

Visual hierarchy and the perception of a site can help to improve user engagement, trust, and understanding of the presented information. Visual hierarchy includes color, size, spacing, and contrast, all of which are used to guide a user clearly through the site. Size helps to illustrate the impotence of one item over others. Color helps to draw a user’s focus using brighter colors over a regular background. White space helps to separate items making it easier for users to understand the information along with the right kind of structure for the information. The use of colors and the layout of the site also shape the perception of the user, influencing how they interpret the information that is presented. Both visual hierarchy and perception work together to aid the users in the understanding of the information on the site but individually they cover different elements of a user’s experience on a site. Visual hierarchy emphases the importance of specific pieces of information on the stie to the viewer well the perception is the user's interpretation process of the information.

Four UX Laws

Like most areas of study patterns that have been identified to be true in all cases are labeled as laws or principles. In the world of user experience, UX, four of these laws that cover the psychology of human interaction with systems are Jakob’s law, Fitts’s law, Hick’s law, and Miller’s law.

Starting with Jakob’s law, we look at how humans prefer consistency and familiarity in what they experience. People will typically stick to sites they recognize and understand rather than learn a new how a new website operates. Jakob’s law pushes the idea that websites should have an established blueprint where the same kinds of symbols are used to illustrate different types of actions like a magnifying glass to symbolize the search bar, three lines or dots to illustrate more options, and a shopping cart for the checkout section on a retail site. Along with familiar symbols keeping things located in familiar places helps a lot, like keeping the navigation tabs on the top of the page or having the company information located at the bottom of the page. Using these kinds of familiar designs help the users feel more comfortable, spend more time on what they need to do instead of learning the new site’s organization, and encourage people who are not very technical to stay you the site without scarring them off when users are exploring a new website.

Moving on to Fitts’s law we look at how the human motor system works when trying to hit different sized targets at different lengths and with different amounts of space between them. When applied to user experience it shows that users have an easier time clicking on larger buttons, especially with touchscreen devices, that are grouped relatively close to each other. Fitts law specifically looked at how quickly and accurately a human can select objects at various sizes and distance between them to measure the trade off between speed and accuracy when targets are smaller and closer together vs when they are bigger and further away. This led to the realization that bigger buttons that are closer together can improve user experience with a site.

In Hick’s law we see how people get overwhelmed quickly when there are too many options presented to them at once and take a long time to make a choice. In user experience this means simplifying a site so that it is not complex to navigate. Steps to achieve this include keeping frequently used options in the open and hiding options that are not used as often behind dropdown menus, keeping similar items together, and breaking down larger process into smaller steps so that it is easier for the user to navigate. For a lot of websites, banking, government and sites for an organization, following Hick’s law is helpful for the user but in other sites like Netflix, Amazon, eBay, and more that show media libraries or e-commerce sites where people like to browse it is better to not follow Hick’s law and give users more freedom to explore. Some social network sites like Facebook, Twitter or Instagram need a balance of following Hick’s law and not following it, because these are sites where people have task that they need to complete and don’t need to get lost well doing them and need the ability to freely explore with a lot of options available to them.

Finaly, covering Miller’s law, we look at the short-term memory of humans and how much information a person can keep track of in a short period of time. It was found that the average person can only hold about seven items in their short-term memory at once before confusion sets in and the individual develops faulty memories or forgets the item. Appling this to user experience it shows that information needs to be organized and sectioned off into chunks allowing the user to better understand the information on the site. Even through the data from Miller’s experiments show that an average person can hold about seven items in their short-term memory that does not mean that number is absolute. Everyone’s memory is a little different, some people have better short-term memory them others so the main thing to keep in mind with Miller’s law is to keep the information in a website organized and separated. Most people don’t enjoy reading a long wall of text and have trouble remembering the information from the wall of text so keeping the information organized and chunked into separate groups that makes sense for the site and the type of information being presented with help the user have a better experience with site.

Interface Layout and Control Design’s Impact on User Behavior

Interface Layout is how a digital platform that a user interacts with I organized and setup. The main impact that interface layout has on user behavior is with their ability to focus and navigate the digital platform where it is a website or app on their phone, specifically it looks at the user’s cognitive load, engagement, and efficiency. How organized the interface layout is can determine how efficiently the user can complete tasks and organized grids help a user prevent accidental clicks increasing their accuracy. The organization of an interface can help to increase user engagement; a clean and structured layout keeps users interested and reduces the feeling of fatigue that users can get when looking through so many interfaces from different sites. Good logical interface organization can also reduce the cognitive load of a user making it easier for them to locate where they need to navigate to and complete their task.

Control design relates to the flexibility or restrictiveness of a site. This impacts the user’s confidence and how autonomous they feel well navigating the site. Highly flexible sites that let the users feel like they are in control of their actions and have the freedom to complete their tasks as they see fit and provide a sense of empowerment and engagement with the site. On the other hand, a restrictive or unpredictable interface on the site leads to user frustration and feelings of doubt in their ability.

UX Laws

Laws of UX are principles that can be followed to set up the best user experience for the site. Some examples of UX Laws are Postel’s law, which states that a site must be forgiving any user input and provide clear and accurate and consistent outputs. The aesthetic-usability effect, which explains how a user finds the visually appealing designs as more functional and user friendly over designs that are not as visually appealing. The Von Restorff effect, otherwise known as the isolation effect, which explains how a user is more likely to remember the odd one out in a group, or the one part of a group that is different from the rest.

Looking through all three of these UX laws I can see the value of implementing any or all of them into the design of an Interface for an app or website. Going a little deeper and looking at each one individually, focusing on the value they provide to the user experience and how they can interact with each other. Starting with Postel’s Law, I think this law provides an extremely valuable design principle for search bars that are added to any site primarily for autocorrecting inputs that are missed spelled helping users feel more efficient with the site. The Von Restorff effect proved memory value for designers by showing that a user will be able to remember the odd item out of a group allowing designers to use this if they want the user to remember something specific. Covering the aesthetic-usability effect This provides value in understanding what a user finds attractive about an interface utilizing this idea designers can improve user engagement for the site. It is hard for me to say which one of these is better than the others because they all provider their own benefits and value to both the designer and the user.
