Examining Systems Thinking and Model-Based Systems Engineering

Introduction

In contemporary high-technology environments, systems are more complicated to develop and maintain. From smart cities to planetary missions, engineers are required to create sophisticated systems to operate smoothly in the real world. Two practices stand out among others in managing complexity are systems thinking and model-based systems engineering (MBSE). Though both practices address complexity problems differently, with one differing from the other in approach, both are primarily aimed at better comprehension and modeling rather than solutions to problems through problem-solving in the classical sense (Senge, 1990).

Both MBSE and system thinking are vital tools that modern engineers need to navigate big systems with many interdependent parts. The two methods will be discussed in this blog entry in an effort to identify the advantages that stem from these two methods being used together in engineering practice. We will demonstrate how together these methods will create better, more efficient, and more trustworthy engineering solutions.

Systems Thinking

Systems thinking is an effective methodology for designing and analyzing complex systems by examining the relationships among the system components instead of the components alone. Systems thinking prompts engineers to consider systems in an integrated fashion, acknowledging that alterations in one area within the system will create ripple effects within the complete system itself.

The heart of systems thinking is to counter the conventional linear way of thinking, under which problems are analyzed discretely and solutions are focused on individual parts without taking into account how these fit into the wider context. Systems thinking instead focuses on the wider system and the dynamics that govern it. The way systems work is elucidated through the use of feedback loops. Feedback loops are either negative or positive and show how an action in one part of the system will impact other segments in the system. In an ecosystem, the growth of one plant can impact the resources that are available to other plants and this will in turn impact the living creatures that are dependent on these plants (Senge, 1990).

A main principle of systems thinking is the concept of mental models. Mental models are the frameworks or assumptions that people use to interpret the world. In engineering, these mental models dictate how problems are framed and solutions are conceived. Engineers can refine and challenge these models constantly so that they better comprehend the systems with which they are working and thus develop better solutions. Engineers are aided by mental models to see that no system exists in its own vacuum; instead, systems are linked to one another in an extensive connected system. For instance, the planning of transportation should consider how it is linked with other systems such as communication networks, the power grid system, and urban planning (Senge, 1990).

Additionally, systematic errors usually crop up when engineers overlook the interrelation of systems. In the product’s design, an engineer may concentrate on only the mechanical requirements without considering how the environmental implications could impact the sustainability of the product in the long term. Systems thinking assists in rectifying this kind of oversight by making engineers think about all the factors responsible for the success and failure of a system.

Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE)

Model-based systems engineering (MBSE) is an engineering paradigm that employs models to depict, simulate, and study complex systems. In contrast to traditional systems engineering, which makes considerable use of documents and charts to explain system components and relationships, MBSE incorporates models into all stages of the engineering process. They act both as a system design blueprint and as a means to model system behavior under diverse scenarios.

The most important advantage of MBSE is that it can simulate and represent a system in the digital world so that engineers can engage with and customize the system virtually. It does away with the physical prototype and increases the speed with which the designing is done and saves costs. Besides saving costs and time, MBSE allows engineers to experiment with several scenarios and configurations prior to physical production being undertaken. Engineers creating a new airplane can model how materials will react when subjected to varied stresses and therefore prevent erroneous decisions down the line and when production starts.

The biggest benefit of MBSE is its power to enhance communication and coordination among team members. In conventional systems engineering, the teams operate with independent documents, charts, or models that are not integrated with each other to any significant extent. As a result, there are increased chances of errors and misunderstandings. In MBSE, on the other hand, everyone—engineers, designers, project leaders, and customers—works with the same model so that everyone has the most current data on hand. It enhances communication and minimizes errors caused by miscommunication or lack of complete data.

MBSE improves the traceability of the decisions made in the design. A particular model or simulation can be traced back to any given decision with the advantage being its ability to track the system's progress with the passage of time. In the event of an issue being experienced when in subsequent stages in the developmental process, engineers can trace back to the particular decision or assumption that resulted in the fault. Traceability is very important in complicated systems whose components work together in unpredictable manners (Estefan, 2008).

Scalability is an important advantage of MBSE too. In huge projects like planning a new city infrastructure or a space mission, MBSE allows engineers to model complete systems with thousands of components and their interactions well into mind. MBSE models can be scaled up or down to suit the complexity level of the system and thus there is an adaptable approach to system design.

Critical Reflection

Although systems thinking and MBSE are vastly different in methodology, when used together, they are complementary in function. Systems thinking offers the abstract structure to comprehend the intangibles of interrelationships among system elements. It makes engineers think in cause-and-effect relationships, in cycles of action and reaction, and in long-term effects. MBSE offers the means to model, to simulate and examine these interrelationships electronically and provide a more mechanical and quantifiable method to system design.

For instance, systems thinking could cause engineers to think about the environmental consequences of a product and how its life cycle will impact the overall system. MBSE would then enable engineers to model the system's elements and how they relate to one another and simulate how alterations to the design could reduce environmental damage. Thus, the general outcomes of systems thinking drive the modeling work in this scenario so that important variables are considered when developing the model.

Moreover, systems thinking makes it possible to identify emergent properties—surprising behavior that emerges when the components of a system are interacting with one another. It is hard to make predictions about these emergent properties with traditional approaches, whereas systems thinking makes one anticipate them and act on them ahead of time. After the identification of these emergent properties, MBSE makes it possible to model them and see how they can impact the system. As an example, in an electrical power system, engineers can use systems thinking to foresee the cascaded effect of one failure, whereas MBSE makes it possible to simulate how one failure in one area can impact others (Meadows, 2008).

Through the integration of systems thinking and MBSE, engineers are able to develop designs which are technically optimized and simultaneously support broader societal and environmental imperatives. Both methodologies combined enable more enlightened decision-making and more effective communication among teams that will create more successful and sustainable systems.
