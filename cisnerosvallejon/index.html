The Thinking of Systems

In a few words, systems thinking is an integrated process of comprehending the dynamics of interaction and operation of a system as well as its components in relation to other systems. Unlike conventional approaches to studying systems that emphasize individual elements, systems thinking concentrates on the interactions and interdependencies among various components.

Those are some pretty big words for Elmo, but what does this actually mean?

Well, systems thinking focuses on interrelationships by looking at the cause-and-effect relationship; it takes into consideration how elements within the system affect one another.

It provides engineers and users with a bigger perspective of the picture by involving the overall system instead of its individual components to spot the patterns and reasons behind things.

Systems thinking also acknowledges dynamic complexity and how it refers to the fact that systems have different relationships that change over time, making it important to look at their interactions.

Systems thinking is also really good when it comes to problem-solving, as understanding the system enables people to develop solutions that tackle core issues without any side effects.

Now, after reading all of this, you might be thinking that you never experienced system thinking or won't ever cross paths with it, but in reality, it's widely used almost everywhere, such as in business, engineering, healthcare, and environmental management fields.

A Better Approach to Complex Digital Systems

What exactly is a model-based approach?

Well, first of all, a model-based approach, full name is referred to as "Model-based Systems Engineering" or MBSE for short. The MBSE is a full approach and is fully used for system engineering. The MBSE undergoes a few rounds of evolution, and it is very important to note the maturity and power it holds.

There are five conceptual stages that MBSE evolves through. These stages are stage one: Document-based, stage two: Document-centric, stage three: Model-enhanced, stage four: Model-Centric, and stage five: Model-Based.

Now, why is a model-based approach successfully applicable in designing complex digital systems?

In simple terms, this approach is fully applicable to a complex system because it enables a holistic and integrated view of the whole system through digital models. These models allow engineers to visualize and analyze the system throughout the whole process in a structured way by representing system components, behaviors, and interactions.

There are many key reasons why an MBSE is a successful approach, such as early issue detection and verification. With MBSE, it enables coupling system models to multidisciplinary analysis processes, which can aid in requirement validation and design optimization before prototype construction. Having access to early validation is beneficial in minimizing expensive mistakes down the road.

MBSE is also helpful when it comes to managing complexity, especially since digital systems usually contain multiple subsystems while having engineering disciplines work together. This method also offers an approach that uses the same language and model-based framework for such systems, allowing engineers to communicate about the complex system efficiently.

This system can also aid engineers in improving design quality and help reduce failures by being able to address system issues early and constantly through modeling, allowing engineers to enhance the quality of systems and avoid any errors after their deployment in the real world.

Cross-Disciplinary integration can also be done with MBSE, since MBSE has the ability to unite various types of engineering fields, such as software, hardware, mechanics, and much more, all in one digital environment.

Finally, MBSE allows design defects to be found earlier, making it faster to market and saving time during the design process.

Module 1.2 Blog Post

In this section of the blog, we discuss what a System Development Life Cycle is, key characteristics, the ups and downs of the life cycle models, and the importance of thinking in terms of the life cycle when it comes to design, sustainability, and evolution for digital systems.

The systems development life cycle, also known as SDLC, was formalized around 1960 when computers were still new and only a few businesses, governments, and departments used them. The SDLC "... is a set of steps that can be used by a team creating a new information technology system. It is a specific plan that guides the team members as they come up with the idea for the system, review it, design it, build it, and put it into use" (Ungvarksy, 2024). SLDC includes a list of specific steps to ensure a computer system meets the requirements for a task. During the process, there are six specific steps (although other sources claim there are seven) to be done to successfully execute SDLC.

The SDLC is an amazing yet complicated system to deploy, which can be seen as challenging because of its requirements that are always changing, technology upgrades, and cross-functional collaborations. Despite this, the SDLC has many key characteristics that make this headache worth it. According to Amazon AWS (2026), SDLC features include "Increased visibility of the development process for all stakeholders involved, Efficient estimation, planning, and scheduling, Improved risk management and cost estimation, Systematic software delivery, and better customer satisfaction." Along with this, the SDLC has different models that can help arrange the SDLC phases to optimize the developmental cycles. These models include the waterfall model that sequentially arranges phases so that each new phase is dependent on the one before it, the spiral model that combines the iterative phase to prioritize risk analysis, and the agile model that arranges the six SDLC phases into multiple developmental cycles. One of the last characteristics that the SDLC offers is how it addresses its security and how it conducts testing by using DevSecOps practices and by conducting security assessments.

A life cycle is a structural construct of set stages that describe the evolution of a living creature or entity. A life cycle model is a behavioral construct that describes the order of execution for the set of stages. In this case, the SDLC has three life cycle models that benefit, but also cause disadvantages to different types of projects.

One of the life cycle models SDLC works with is the Linear Life Cycle Model, which executes its stages around a simple linear sequence. Below is an example of the Linear Life Cycle Model based on the Waterfall model, a model that many argue is the original life cycle model.

The Linear Life Cycle model has one great advantage in that each stage can easily be visualized to help execute as described in the image above, starting from gathering and deciding the concept to commencing production, and finally the retirement stage. Each execution represents a visualized lifeline to the stages, while the stages help visualize the order of execution. Unfortunately, there are more drawbacks than advantages to using the Linear Life Cycle Model due to it only being able to be executed in a specific order after each stage is executed, and it does not offer a way to go back to the stage before it. In his writing, Holt (2023) also describes that the "...Model is used predominantly for Projects where the original needs are well specified and not likely to change." Meaning that the Linear Life Cycle Model is mostly used for projects that have their requirements specified and are unlikely to change. The Linear Life Cycle Model is also unfit to work on large, complex projects and systems that are subject to change throughout their stages. It is also not compatible with long-term projects since it is typically delivered in a single release once a project is completed.

The second model that is typically used by the System Development Life Cycle is the iterative Life Cycle Model. This model differs from the Linear Cycle Model by conducting several passes through the stages, which can be described as mini life cycles, or also known as iterations. The iteration has multiple advantages to its usage, such as that each iteration represents one single pass throughout the stages that take a short period of time to process. It begins in the concept stage and works its way to the utilization stage, where the results of "...each iteration is a workable version of the final System that can be deployed in the target environment" (Holt, 2023). There are a few drawbacks to using the iterative Life Cycle Model, such as when applying it to a Systems Project, basic needs are subject to change frequently because of stakeholders, and although it might be a good idea to have a robust needs Process plan in place, this is more than often not the case. Another disadvantage would be that although this model is heavily used in the world of technology and software, instead of larger Systems Projects, there is always a significant demand for getting it released on time, instead of waiting patiently for a system or a different model that would be more compatible.

The last model that the SDLC works with is the incremental life cycle model, which is similar to the iterative Life Cycle Model in some ways, like how there are multiple passes throughout the stages, so that when the final system is deployed, there are multiple releases. This model and the iterative model are also often known as the evolutionary Life Cycle Models. The advantage of using this model is that, instead of doing a single release like the Linear Life Cycle Model does, it can be sent off in an "incremental fashion." This method makes it compatible and good to work with longer projects that require functionality before the project is finished, since "...the final System can be seen to be working and the System is deployed, albeit in a reduced form, compared to how it was relatively early in the Project" (Holt, 2023). The disadvantage to this model, however, is that not all systems are compatible because they cannot be broken down and worked on in segments due to how the System is built or in general, this model is just not suitable for the task in hand.

Finally, I believe that it is important to take into consideration the terms of a system's lifestyle when it comes to design, sustainability, and evolution of digital systems, because it allows the user/ users to be prepared for environmental impacts, be able to manage complex situations, and ensure that any type of adaptability is long-term. It's important to take into consideration how we impact the Earth and environment when working with tech and Systems because of how wasteful and damaging the tech industry can be. It is also important to take into consideration how we can anticipate changes and disruptions to be able to respond better and faster to changes and shifts in technology, regulation, or if user needs change or update at a point in time. By having this in mind, engineers and teams can have continuous improvement in the next design or project.
