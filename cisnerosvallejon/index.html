The Thinking of Systems

In a few words, systems thinking is an integrated process of comprehending the dynamics of interaction and operation of a system as well as its components in relation to other systems. Unlike conventional approaches to studying systems that emphasize individual elements, systems thinking concentrates on the interactions and interdependencies among various components.

Those are some pretty big words for Elmo, but what does this actually mean?

Well, systems thinking focuses on interrelationships by looking at the cause-and-effect relationship; it takes into consideration how elements within the system affect one another.

It provides engineers and users with a bigger perspective of the picture by involving the overall system instead of its individual components to spot the patterns and reasons behind things.

Systems thinking also acknowledges dynamic complexity and how it refers to the fact that systems have different relationships that change over time, making it important to look at their interactions.

Systems thinking is also really good when it comes to problem-solving, as understanding the system enables people to develop solutions that tackle core issues without any side effects.

Now, after reading all of this, you might be thinking that you never experienced system thinking or won't ever cross paths with it, but in reality, it's widely used almost everywhere, such as in business, engineering, healthcare, and environmental management fields.

A Better Approach to Complex Digital Systems

What exactly is a model-based approach?

Well, first of all, a model-based approach, full name is referred to as "Model-based Systems Engineering" or MBSE for short. The MBSE is a full approach and is fully used for system engineering. The MBSE undergoes a few rounds of evolution, and it is very important to note the maturity and power it holds.

There are five conceptual stages that MBSE evolves through. These stages are stage one: Document-based, stage two: Document-centric, stage three: Model-enhanced, stage four: Model-Centric, and stage five: Model-Based.

Now, why is a model-based approach successfully applicable in designing complex digital systems?

In simple terms, this approach is fully applicable to a complex system because it enables a holistic and integrated view of the whole system through digital models. These models allow engineers to visualize and analyze the system throughout the whole process in a structured way by representing system components, behaviors, and interactions.

There are many key reasons why an MBSE is a successful approach, such as early issue detection and verification. With MBSE, it enables coupling system models to multidisciplinary analysis processes, which can aid in requirement validation and design optimization before prototype construction. Having access to early validation is beneficial in minimizing expensive mistakes down the road.

MBSE is also helpful when it comes to managing complexity, especially since digital systems usually contain multiple subsystems while having engineering disciplines work together. This method also offers an approach that uses the same language and model-based framework for such systems, allowing engineers to communicate about the complex system efficiently.

This system can also aid engineers in improving design quality and help reduce failures by being able to address system issues early and constantly through modeling, allowing engineers to enhance the quality of systems and avoid any errors after their deployment in the real world.

Cross-Disciplinary integration can also be done with MBSE, since MBSE has the ability to unite various types of engineering fields, such as software, hardware, mechanics, and much more, all in one digital environment.

Finally, MBSE allows design defects to be found earlier, making it faster to market and saving time during the design process.
