Systems Thinking and the Future of Designing Digital Environments

With such an ingrained use of technology in our lives, it can be easy to overlook how complicated the systems themselves are. Communication, travel, leisure activities, and the functionality of corporations would all be impossible without these systems in place. However, as the complexity of these technologies grows, so too has the approach taken to solve problems within them. In many cases, the sequential process used in technology is simply inadequate due to the constant interactions, evolution, and mutual effects of each part. The need for alternative approaches to understanding the digital world leads us directly to systems thinking. This type of analysis considers the dynamics of interaction between various elements in order to understand their relationships and emergent properties. The increasing importance of systems thinking, as well as the development of model-based solutions, stems from the increasing complexity of digital environments.

Defining Systems Thinking in Everyday Terms

If I were to explain the concept of systems thinking in my own words, I would say that it is a way of comprehending the world through connections rather than separations. Rather than isolating each component of a system, we are encouraged to take an objective step back and look at the big picture. We can then comprehend that all parts of the system are interconnected, affecting each other in some unforeseen ways. Thus, not only will we be able to see what happens, but we'll know why it occurs and how each element contributes to the dynamics of the whole system. The first principle of systems thinking is that of interdependence. There are no independent entities within the context of the digital space. Any modification of one part of the system, such as updating the database or changing network configurations, will have further consequences for other parts of the system. Another important concept is that of feedback loops. This refers to the occurrence of a situation where there are cycles in which the output of the system is used as the input. A classic example would be when a site records users' actions and recommends articles based on what users have read before. Systems thinking also recognizes the concept of emergence. Emergence describes a phenomenon or behavior that occurs as a consequence of the interaction within the system and not just because the parts exhibit certain characteristics individually. An example of this in the technical world is network traffic. It doesn't matter whether every single unit within a system works properly; the emergent characteristic could be traffic in that network due to the overall interactions. This idea reflects what Holt explains about systems engineering, where understanding the behavior of the whole system is just as important as understanding its individual components (Holt, 2021). Lastly, boundaries and context must be considered when thinking systemically. A system always operates in an environment, and recognizing what the boundary of the system entails allows one to make informed decisions when working with the system. In summary, the systems thinking approach is an attitude that fosters curiosity and the openness to venture into the complex instead of running away from it. It does not seek immediate answers. Rather, it aims at appreciating how different elements interact with each other in order to create sustainable solutions.

How Systems Thinking Differs from Linear Thinking

It becomes easier to understand the concepts of systems thinking when we compare it with the conventional method of thinking that we grew up learning. The process of linear thinking can be quite simple to comprehend. First, you determine your problem, then divide it into smaller units to solve them individually. It is almost similar to a recipe, where there is a definite starting point, a middle point, and an endpoint. Linear thought is suitable for straightforward or static situations. In case your computer doesn't start, you may check the power cord, battery, or power socket. This is a linear thought process, which leads to a specific result. However, modern digital systems are anything but straightforward or static. They are dynamic, interrelated, and always evolving. Using linear thought processes to solve the problem of a complex digital system can distort the reality behind it. However, systems thinking appreciates the reality that issues in complex environments cannot be attributed to one single factor or solved through one single action. Rather than asking about what one thing causes the problem, the systems approach tries to uncover how various parts within the system interact to produce the problem. This is an important change in perception since it enables one to prevent getting lost while solving surface-level problems. Let us use our previous example where a business is having issues with poor network speeds. The conventional approach would be to assume that the bandwidth is responsible for the problem and, therefore, propose a faster internet connection. Systems thinking will, however, look at the larger picture and ask questions such as whether the software used is efficient, whether the routing rules are outdated, or whether there are anomalies in the behavior of users. The other distinction between the two schools of thought relates to handling unpredictability. Linear thought posits that repeating an action yields the same result. Systems thinking recognizes that when faced with complex circumstances, even repeating an action might yield different outcomes. For example, in digital systems, the outcome varies due to the unpredictable nature of user behavior, dependencies on third-party entities, and real-time data. Human-centered design is also affected by this distinction. According to Jeff Johnson, users do not always act logically and consistently because the basis for their actions lies in their experiences and expectations (Johnson, 2020). As a result, the assumption of linear behaviors leads designers astray and makes it difficult for them to understand the actual interactions between humans and technologies. Thus, the two approaches to problem-solving differ from each other because linear thought implies control, whereas systems thinking allows one to better understand the environment and develop flexible solutions to address the problems. Both schools of thought offer valuable insights; however, systems thinking provides a more adequate way to work with digital environments than linear thinking does.

Why Model-Based Approaches Matter in Complex Digital Systems

As complexity increases in digital systems, intuition and language-based communication alone become increasingly unreliable. This is why there is a need for model-based systems engineering. Model-based methods utilize the power of modeling, whether it be visually, conceptually, or computationally, to provide an abstract representation of how a system is built and operates. Such models work as a common map through which the system can be explored, understood, communicated about, and analyzed. One of the major advantages of models lies in their ability to minimize uncertainties. The description of a certain system in words may have several interpretations depending on who describes the system. On the other hand, models give an opportunity for a consistent visualization of the system. It is extremely helpful in big teams where everyone, developers, engineers, designers, and other stakeholders must share a common picture of the future application or software. Another important benefit of using models for designing systems is that they help visualize the connections between various parts of a system and its interaction with other elements. As a result, one may easily spot the problems that may be caused by bottlenecks, conflicts, or a lack of some elements in the process of system interaction. Using model-based approaches is also advantageous because of the simulation of the system's performance. It is especially beneficial for the digital world, where errors may prove to be extremely expensive. Using such an approach lets us investigate possible situations, learn about the performance of our software in various conditions, test it thoroughly, and assess its impact. The best thing is that it is completely consistent with the methodology of systems thinking, which means we get an opportunity to analyze the interaction, feedback and emergent processes in our model. Modeling helps organizations identify risks earlier and design more resilient systems (National Institute of Standards and Technology, 2022). The third big advantage of the use of models is increased effectiveness of communication between teams. As a rule, there are significant differences between the people who work on the project and the methods of their thinking. The developer uses programming concepts, the designer uses user interface, and manager uses the ideas of outcome. This concept also relates to what Johnson says about cognitive load. He states that “individuals understand information better when information is displayed visually and made meaningful” (Johnson, 2020). The use of models can facilitate this process since they reduce cognitive burden. Finally, model-based approaches support scalability and long-term sustainability. As systems evolve, models can be updated to reflect new requirements, technologies, or constraints. This creates living documentation that grows with the system, making it easier to maintain and expand over time. Additional research supports this approach. The International Council on Systems Engineering notes that model-based methods improve accuracy, reduce development errors, and strengthen collaboration across teams (INCOSE, 2023). Similarly, the National Institute of Standards and Technology emphasizes that modeling helps organizations identify risks earlier and design more resilient systems

Conclusion

Systems thinking offers a powerful way to understand and design digital environments by focusing on relationships, interactions, and long-term behavior rather than isolated parts. It differs from linear thinking by embracing complexity instead of simplifying it, and by recognizing that problems in digital systems often emerge from the system as a whole rather than from a single cause. Model-based approaches bring systems thinking to life by giving us tools to visualize, test, and communicate complex systems in clear and meaningful ways. As digital environments continue to grow in complexity, systems thinking and model-based design will become even more important. These approaches help us build systems that are not only functional, but also resilient, scalable, and capable of adapting to the challenges of an ever-changing digital landscape.

Module 1.2 Blog Post

Life Cycle Models as Ways of Thinking: A Systems Engineering Perspective

Prior to this class, my understanding of systems engineering was limited to the design of complex objects. That is, the sort of activity carried out at the drawing board or modeling application software. However, the more I understand, the clearer it becomes to me that systems engineers do not only create systems; they also lead them through their whole life cycle. There is always a birth, growth, maturity, and death for any system, and life cycle models are simply the navigation tools we have for the ride. The most surprising part was how influential these frameworks are on our thought process. These are not simply processes. They are philosophies regarding uncertainty, risks, and learning. Each linear, iterative, and incremental framework that we examined in Systems Engineering Demystified has its own philosophy concerning the predictability of the environment and the degree of adaptability required when things don't turn out as expected. As I looked deeper into INCOSE's System Engineering Handbook and other references, I began to view these frameworks as problem-solving approaches rather than checklists to be blindly followed (INCOSE, 2023).

The Linear Model: When Predictability Matters More Than Flexibility

The linear model is the image everyone conjures up when they think about engineering. It's a series of neatly laid-out steps, all of which have to be completed before you move on to the next step. This model, called the "waterfall" method by INCOSE, is a structured method wherein each phase has its own set of criteria to be met (INCOSE, 2023). On the surface, it seems almost too simple. However, the more I reflected on this concept, the more I came to realize that there were cases where such a level of rigidity was required. The Federal Highway Administration's recommendation for the development of Intelligent Transportation Systems relies heavily on a linear, traceable approach due to the fact that ITSs are costly, have a long lifespan, and affect public safety (Federal Highway Administration, 2007). It is simply not acceptable to “iterate” a traffic control system the same way as a smartphone app. What made things click for me is the fact that the linear approach is not naïve. It is deliberate; it makes it hard for change to happen. That might seem cruel, but it ensures that everybody involved, whether it's the engineers or the stakeholders or even the regulators, will have to make decisions at an early stage, when doing so is cheap and conceptual.

The Iterative Model: Admitting We Don't Know Everything Up Front

While the linear model represents control, the iterative model embraces curiosity. Instead of assuming we can know all we need to from the very start, iteration recognizes the fact that knowledge will come during the process. As described by INCOSE, an iterative approach is a cycle of construction, evaluation, and improvement with each iteration providing new insights that help adjust our perspective (INCOSE, 2023). The idea of iteration seems quite fitting for the development of systems dominated by software and characterized by constant changes, as well as evolving user requirements. In fact, according to the Federal Highway Administration, an iterative approach should be chosen when requirements are vague or expected to change (Federal Highway Administration, 2007). The one aspect of the iterative model that I enjoy the most is its humility. The engineers must accept that the initial attempt will not be flawless, which is perfectly fine. The model also demands trust, since the system will develop publicly and sometimes unexpectedly. There will be no "one big unveiling," just continuous growth. Such transparency can be challenging. Documentation may not always be up-to-date, integration can be a nightmare, but iteration seems to be the only honest course of action in an environment filled with uncertainty.

The Incremental Model: Delivering Value Without Losing the Big Picture

The incremental approach falls somewhere in between the other two methodologies and, quite frankly, is the methodology which I had the hardest time comprehending completely. On one hand, incremental is less strict than the linear model, yet on the other hand, it is stricter than the iterative model. The thing is that with incrementation, we first create the full picture of the project: the architecture, its major requirements, and only then implement it gradually. According to INCOSE, with incremental approaches, the process goes in cycles of life cycle steps, implementing each part of the product while the total design remains unchanged (INCOSE, 2023). Such an approach is common in the context of large-scale infrastructures and enterprises. For example, the Federal Highway Administration mentions incremental release in which capabilities are released sequentially, with lessons learned from one iteration informing another one (Federal Highway Administration, 2007). This is an approach that enables real-world feedback to be obtained without having to wait several years until a system is fully operational. The literature review conducted by Sandra Furterer enabled me to gain a completely different understanding of incremental development. Furterer suggests classifying predictive (linear), iterative, and incremental approaches to software development as three basic ones, which can all comply with the V model provided verification and validation are implemented properly (Furterer, 2021). Such an interpretation transformed my view of incremental development, turning it into something beyond "waterfall in pieces." The aspects of the incremental model that appeal to me include its ability to provide early value without compromising the overall objective. In other words, each increment must be able to stand alone in terms of being coherent, testable, and relevant, yet at the same time it must be integrated into the larger system.

Life Cycle Models as Risk Strategies, Not Recipes

It was at that point that my perception about them changed from treating them as alternative approaches to perceiving them as alternate means of risk management. - The linear approach preloads risk. It requires clarity upfront and values stability. - The iterative approach distributes risks over the cycles. It sacrifices clarity upfront in favor of sustained learning. - The incremental approach provides a balance. It stabilizes the architecture yet allows for incremental delivery. It is important to note that, according to INCOSE, the life cycle phases do not have to be performed sequentially, and they may overlap, loop, or even vary from situation to situation (INCOSE, 2023). This is where the element of art enters the process of systems engineering. No real-life systems can ever be described by only one model, and one project could utilize all three models for different tasks. The perspective adopted when considering life cycle models changes their nature from rules into lenses through which the stakeholder experiences the development of the system. The linear life cycle model might satisfy the regulatory agencies' requirements, while the iterative approach gives control back to the user.

How This Shapes the Kind of Systems Engineer I Want to Be

With these models in mind, there is something that keeps recurring in my thoughts. The life cycle model should not be an add-on but rather a well-thought-out decision based on the system, the stakeholders, and the uncertainties. I strive to be a systems engineer who asks the right questions before opting for any particular model. What areas of uncertainty exist in the system? Where does the risk lie? Who needs stability and who needs flexibility? Additionally, I wish to inform the stakeholders about the meaning of the selected model. For instance, if we choose the linear model, I wish to let all parties know that changing course in the latter stages will be difficult. If we choose the iterative model, I would like to alert stakeholders to the evolving nature of the system. Finally, if we adopt the incremental model, I wish to ensure that each increment provides sufficient value. Above all, I would like to adopt life cycle models as a way of ensuring that the focus on the system's intended purpose is maintained. According to INCOSE, systems engineering involves finding optimal solutions to multiple competing interests throughout the system's lifecycle (INCOSE, 2023). Therefore, the “perfect” model will be the one that enables effective decision making while acknowledging reality and ensuring consistency with the intended mission. Ultimately, systems engineers not only create but also guide the system through its lifecycle. Thus, choosing the right life cycle model becomes quite important.
