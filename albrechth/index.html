Systems Thinking and the Future of Designing Digital Environments

With such an ingrained use of technology in our lives, it can be easy to overlook how complicated the systems themselves are. Communication, travel, leisure activities, and the functionality of corporations would all be impossible without these systems in place. However, as the complexity of these technologies grows, so too has the approach taken to solve problems within them. In many cases, the sequential process used in technology is simply inadequate due to the constant interactions, evolution, and mutual effects of each part. The need for alternative approaches to understanding the digital world leads us directly to systems thinking. This type of analysis considers the dynamics of interaction between various elements in order to understand their relationships and emergent properties. The increasing importance of systems thinking, as well as the development of model-based solutions, stems from the increasing complexity of digital environments.

Defining Systems Thinking in Everyday Terms

If I were to explain the concept of systems thinking in my own words, I would say that it is a way of comprehending the world through connections rather than separations. Rather than isolating each component of a system, we are encouraged to take an objective step back and look at the big picture. We can then comprehend that all parts of the system are interconnected, affecting each other in some unforeseen ways. Thus, not only will we be able to see what happens, but we'll know why it occurs and how each element contributes to the dynamics of the whole system. The first principle of systems thinking is that of interdependence. There are no independent entities within the context of the digital space. Any modification of one part of the system, such as updating the database or changing network configurations, will have further consequences for other parts of the system. Another important concept is that of feedback loops. This refers to the occurrence of a situation where there are cycles in which the output of the system is used as the input. A classic example would be when a site records users' actions and recommends articles based on what users have read before. Systems thinking also recognizes the concept of emergence. Emergence describes a phenomenon or behavior that occurs as a consequence of the interaction within the system and not just because the parts exhibit certain characteristics individually. An example of this in the technical world is network traffic. It doesn't matter whether every single unit within a system works properly; the emergent characteristic could be traffic in that network due to the overall interactions. This idea reflects what Holt explains about systems engineering, where understanding the behavior of the whole system is just as important as understanding its individual components (Holt, 2021). Lastly, boundaries and context must be considered when thinking systemically. A system always operates in an environment, and recognizing what the boundary of the system entails allows one to make informed decisions when working with the system. In summary, the systems thinking approach is an attitude that fosters curiosity and the openness to venture into the complex instead of running away from it. It does not seek immediate answers. Rather, it aims at appreciating how different elements interact with each other in order to create sustainable solutions.

How Systems Thinking Differs from Linear Thinking

It becomes easier to understand the concepts of systems thinking when we compare it with the conventional method of thinking that we grew up learning. The process of linear thinking can be quite simple to comprehend. First, you determine your problem, then divide it into smaller units to solve them individually. It is almost similar to a recipe, where there is a definite starting point, a middle point, and an endpoint. Linear thought is suitable for straightforward or static situations. In case your computer doesn't start, you may check the power cord, battery, or power socket. This is a linear thought process, which leads to a specific result. However, modern digital systems are anything but straightforward or static. They are dynamic, interrelated, and always evolving. Using linear thought processes to solve the problem of a complex digital system can distort the reality behind it. However, systems thinking appreciates the reality that issues in complex environments cannot be attributed to one single factor or solved through one single action. Rather than asking about what one thing causes the problem, the systems approach tries to uncover how various parts within the system interact to produce the problem. This is an important change in perception since it enables one to prevent getting lost while solving surface-level problems. Let us use our previous example where a business is having issues with poor network speeds. The conventional approach would be to assume that the bandwidth is responsible for the problem and, therefore, propose a faster internet connection. Systems thinking will, however, look at the larger picture and ask questions such as whether the software used is efficient, whether the routing rules are outdated, or whether there are anomalies in the behavior of users. The other distinction between the two schools of thought relates to handling unpredictability. Linear thought posits that repeating an action yields the same result. Systems thinking recognizes that when faced with complex circumstances, even repeating an action might yield different outcomes. For example, in digital systems, the outcome varies due to the unpredictable nature of user behavior, dependencies on third-party entities, and real-time data. Human-centered design is also affected by this distinction. According to Jeff Johnson, users do not always act logically and consistently because the basis for their actions lies in their experiences and expectations (Johnson, 2020). As a result, the assumption of linear behaviors leads designers astray and makes it difficult for them to understand the actual interactions between humans and technologies. Thus, the two approaches to problem-solving differ from each other because linear thought implies control, whereas systems thinking allows one to better understand the environment and develop flexible solutions to address the problems. Both schools of thought offer valuable insights; however, systems thinking provides a more adequate way to work with digital environments than linear thinking does.

Why Model-Based Approaches Matter in Complex Digital Systems

As complexity increases in digital systems, intuition and language-based communication alone become increasingly unreliable. This is why there is a need for model-based systems engineering. Model-based methods utilize the power of modeling, whether it be visually, conceptually, or computationally, to provide an abstract representation of how a system is built and operates. Such models work as a common map through which the system can be explored, understood, communicated about, and analyzed. One of the major advantages of models lies in their ability to minimize uncertainties. The description of a certain system in words may have several interpretations depending on who describes the system. On the other hand, models give an opportunity for a consistent visualization of the system. It is extremely helpful in big teams where everyone, developers, engineers, designers, and other stakeholders must share a common picture of the future application or software. Another important benefit of using models for designing systems is that they help visualize the connections between various parts of a system and its interaction with other elements. As a result, one may easily spot the problems that may be caused by bottlenecks, conflicts, or a lack of some elements in the process of system interaction. Using model-based approaches is also advantageous because of the simulation of the system's performance. It is especially beneficial for the digital world, where errors may prove to be extremely expensive. Using such an approach lets us investigate possible situations, learn about the performance of our software in various conditions, test it thoroughly, and assess its impact. The best thing is that it is completely consistent with the methodology of systems thinking, which means we get an opportunity to analyze the interaction, feedback and emergent processes in our model. Modeling helps organizations identify risks earlier and design more resilient systems (National Institute of Standards and Technology, 2022). The third big advantage of the use of models is increased effectiveness of communication between teams. As a rule, there are significant differences between the people who work on the project and the methods of their thinking. The developer uses programming concepts, the designer uses user interface, and manager uses the ideas of outcome. This concept also relates to what Johnson says about cognitive load. He states that “individuals understand information better when information is displayed visually and made meaningful” (Johnson, 2020). The use of models can facilitate this process since they reduce cognitive burden. Finally, model-based approaches support scalability and long-term sustainability. As systems evolve, models can be updated to reflect new requirements, technologies, or constraints. This creates living documentation that grows with the system, making it easier to maintain and expand over time. Additional research supports this approach. The International Council on Systems Engineering notes that model-based methods improve accuracy, reduce development errors, and strengthen collaboration across teams (INCOSE, 2023). Similarly, the National Institute of Standards and Technology emphasizes that modeling helps organizations identify risks earlier and design more resilient systems

Conclusion

Systems thinking offers a powerful way to understand and design digital environments by focusing on relationships, interactions, and long-term behavior rather than isolated parts. It differs from linear thinking by embracing complexity instead of simplifying it, and by recognizing that problems in digital systems often emerge from the system as a whole rather than from a single cause. Model-based approaches bring systems thinking to life by giving us tools to visualize, test, and communicate complex systems in clear and meaningful ways. As digital environments continue to grow in complexity, systems thinking and model-based design will become even more important. These approaches help us build systems that are not only functional, but also resilient, scalable, and capable of adapting to the challenges of an ever-changing digital landscape.

Module 1.2 Blog Post

Life Cycle Models as Ways of Thinking: A Systems Engineering Perspective

Prior to this class, my understanding of systems engineering was limited to the design of complex objects. That is, the sort of activity carried out at the drawing board or modeling application software. However, the more I understand, the clearer it becomes to me that systems engineers do not only create systems; they also lead them through their whole life cycle. There is always a birth, growth, maturity, and death for any system, and life cycle models are simply the navigation tools we have for the ride. The most surprising part was how influential these frameworks are on our thought process. These are not simply processes. They are philosophies regarding uncertainty, risks, and learning. Each linear, iterative, and incremental framework that we examined in Systems Engineering Demystified has its own philosophy concerning the predictability of the environment and the degree of adaptability required when things don't turn out as expected. As I looked deeper into INCOSE's System Engineering Handbook and other references, I began to view these frameworks as problem-solving approaches rather than checklists to be blindly followed (INCOSE, 2023).

The Linear Model: When Predictability Matters More Than Flexibility

The linear model is the image everyone conjures up when they think about engineering. It's a series of neatly laid-out steps, all of which have to be completed before you move on to the next step. This model, called the "waterfall" method by INCOSE, is a structured method wherein each phase has its own set of criteria to be met (INCOSE, 2023). On the surface, it seems almost too simple. However, the more I reflected on this concept, the more I came to realize that there were cases where such a level of rigidity was required. The Federal Highway Administration's recommendation for the development of Intelligent Transportation Systems relies heavily on a linear, traceable approach due to the fact that ITSs are costly, have a long lifespan, and affect public safety (Federal Highway Administration, 2007). It is simply not acceptable to “iterate” a traffic control system the same way as a smartphone app. What made things click for me is the fact that the linear approach is not naïve. It is deliberate; it makes it hard for change to happen. That might seem cruel, but it ensures that everybody involved, whether it's the engineers or the stakeholders or even the regulators, will have to make decisions at an early stage, when doing so is cheap and conceptual.

The Iterative Model: Admitting We Don't Know Everything Up Front

While the linear model represents control, the iterative model embraces curiosity. Instead of assuming we can know all we need to from the very start, iteration recognizes the fact that knowledge will come during the process. As described by INCOSE, an iterative approach is a cycle of construction, evaluation, and improvement with each iteration providing new insights that help adjust our perspective (INCOSE, 2023). The idea of iteration seems quite fitting for the development of systems dominated by software and characterized by constant changes, as well as evolving user requirements. In fact, according to the Federal Highway Administration, an iterative approach should be chosen when requirements are vague or expected to change (Federal Highway Administration, 2007). The one aspect of the iterative model that I enjoy the most is its humility. The engineers must accept that the initial attempt will not be flawless, which is perfectly fine. The model also demands trust, since the system will develop publicly and sometimes unexpectedly. There will be no "one big unveiling," just continuous growth. Such transparency can be challenging. Documentation may not always be up-to-date, integration can be a nightmare, but iteration seems to be the only honest course of action in an environment filled with uncertainty.

The Incremental Model: Delivering Value Without Losing the Big Picture

The incremental approach falls somewhere in between the other two methodologies and, quite frankly, is the methodology which I had the hardest time comprehending completely. On one hand, incremental is less strict than the linear model, yet on the other hand, it is stricter than the iterative model. The thing is that with incrementation, we first create the full picture of the project: the architecture, its major requirements, and only then implement it gradually. According to INCOSE, with incremental approaches, the process goes in cycles of life cycle steps, implementing each part of the product while the total design remains unchanged (INCOSE, 2023). Such an approach is common in the context of large-scale infrastructures and enterprises. For example, the Federal Highway Administration mentions incremental release in which capabilities are released sequentially, with lessons learned from one iteration informing another one (Federal Highway Administration, 2007). This is an approach that enables real-world feedback to be obtained without having to wait several years until a system is fully operational. The literature review conducted by Sandra Furterer enabled me to gain a completely different understanding of incremental development. Furterer suggests classifying predictive (linear), iterative, and incremental approaches to software development as three basic ones, which can all comply with the V model provided verification and validation are implemented properly (Furterer, 2021). Such an interpretation transformed my view of incremental development, turning it into something beyond "waterfall in pieces." The aspects of the incremental model that appeal to me include its ability to provide early value without compromising the overall objective. In other words, each increment must be able to stand alone in terms of being coherent, testable, and relevant, yet at the same time it must be integrated into the larger system.

Life Cycle Models as Risk Strategies, Not Recipes

It was at that point that my perception about them changed from treating them as alternative approaches to perceiving them as alternate means of risk management. - The linear approach preloads risk. It requires clarity upfront and values stability. - The iterative approach distributes risks over the cycles. It sacrifices clarity upfront in favor of sustained learning. - The incremental approach provides a balance. It stabilizes the architecture yet allows for incremental delivery. It is important to note that, according to INCOSE, the life cycle phases do not have to be performed sequentially, and they may overlap, loop, or even vary from situation to situation (INCOSE, 2023). This is where the element of art enters the process of systems engineering. No real-life systems can ever be described by only one model, and one project could utilize all three models for different tasks. The perspective adopted when considering life cycle models changes their nature from rules into lenses through which the stakeholder experiences the development of the system. The linear life cycle model might satisfy the regulatory agencies' requirements, while the iterative approach gives control back to the user.

How This Shapes the Kind of Systems Engineer I Want to Be

With these models in mind, there is something that keeps recurring in my thoughts. The life cycle model should not be an add-on but rather a well-thought-out decision based on the system, the stakeholders, and the uncertainties. I strive to be a systems engineer who asks the right questions before opting for any particular model. What areas of uncertainty exist in the system? Where does the risk lie? Who needs stability and who needs flexibility? Additionally, I wish to inform the stakeholders about the meaning of the selected model. For instance, if we choose the linear model, I wish to let all parties know that changing course in the latter stages will be difficult. If we choose the iterative model, I would like to alert stakeholders to the evolving nature of the system. Finally, if we adopt the incremental model, I wish to ensure that each increment provides sufficient value. Above all, I would like to adopt life cycle models as a way of ensuring that the focus on the system's intended purpose is maintained. According to INCOSE, systems engineering involves finding optimal solutions to multiple competing interests throughout the system's lifecycle (INCOSE, 2023). Therefore, the “perfect” model will be the one that enables effective decision making while acknowledging reality and ensuring consistency with the intended mission. Ultimately, systems engineers not only create but also guide the system through its lifecycle. Thus, choosing the right life cycle model becomes quite important.

How Perception Shapes Human-Centered Design

Human-centered design is often described as a mindset, a process, and even a philosophy, but at its core, it is a way of seeing. Long before a designer sketches a wireframe, conducts a usability test, or builds a prototype, countless design decisions have already been made through the simple act of observing the world. Designers notice details that others overlook. They pay attention to the small frictions in daily life. They empathize with people whose needs differ from their own. They interpret behavior, emotion, and context. In other words, design begins with perception. This module emphasized the formal processes that guide this early stage of design, including frameworks such as ISO 9241 210 and the IDEO Field Guide. Yet it also highlighted something deeper. Cognitive psychology and UX principles remind us that users bring complex mental processes to every interaction. They do not simply click buttons or read screens. They perceive, interpret, guess, assume, and sometimes misunderstand. When we bring these ideas together, a clear truth emerges. Design begins long before anyone realizes that design has begun. In this expanded reflection, I explore how perception, attention, empathy, and mental models shape human-centered design. I also examine how early design decisions can either support or undermine usability. My goal is to connect theoretical concepts to practical design choices in a way that strengthens the understanding of what it truly means to design for real people.

Human-Centered Design Starts With Perception

The second major principle of ISO 9241 210 states that human-centered design is a cyclical process in which designers continuously learn about users, create solutions, evaluate them, and iterate (ISO, 2019). The first stage of this cycle involves understanding users, their tasks, and their environments. At first glance, this seems simple. In reality, it requires designers to set aside their own assumptions and learn to see the world from someone else's perspective. The IDEO Field Guide reinforces this idea by emphasizing that the design process begins with empathy work. Designers are encouraged to look around, listen carefully, and notice the small details that signal unmet needs or opportunities for improvement (IDEO, 2015). This is more than research. It is perceptual awareness. It is the ability to observe what others overlook. However, perception is not a perfect window into reality. Jeff Johnson explains that the human brain relies on heuristics that help us survive but can mislead us in design contexts. We see patterns that are not there, ignore information that matters, and assume consistency even when none exists (Johnson, 2020). Designers are not immune to these tendencies. In fact, their familiarity with interfaces can make them more vulnerable. They may assume that something is obvious simply because it is obvious to them. This is why human-centered design begins with perception. Designers must understand how humans actually perceive the world, not how they believe people perceive it. The difference between those two viewpoints can determine whether a design succeeds or fails.

Attention Is Limited, and Design Must Respect That

One of the most difficult truths for designers to accept is that users do not examine interfaces with the same care that designers use when creating them. Users scan. They guess. They follow the path of least resistance. They do not read instructions unless they absolutely must. Johnson notes that attention is an expensive cognitive resource, and users spend it only when necessary (Johnson, 2020). This means that early design decisions that reduce cognitive effort have an enormous impact on usability. Consider a homepage cluttered with competing visual elements. Even if every item is useful, the overall effect is overwhelming. Users lose focus because their attention is pulled in too many directions. This is not a content problem. It is a perceptual design problem. Jakob's Law reinforces this idea. Users spend most of their time on other websites, so they expect your site to behave like the ones they already know. When designers break these expectations, they increase cognitive load. Users must stop, think, and reinterpret what they see. Every moment of confusion is a moment of friction. Designers cannot force users to pay attention. They can only create conditions that make attention effortless.

Empathy is a Design Skill

Empathy is often described as a soft skill or an innate talent, but in human centered design it is a deliberate practice. The IDEO Field Guide defines empathy as a method that involves observing, asking, listening, and synthesizing. It is not about feeling what users feel. It is about understanding what shapes their behavior. Empathy becomes especially important when designing for people whose experiences differ from our own. For example:

  • A designer with perfect vision may not notice how difficult low contrast text is for someone with visual impairments.
  • A tech savvy designer may underestimate how confusing a complex onboarding process is for a novice user.
  • A younger designer may not realize how frustrating small touch targets are for older adults with reduced motor control.
Fitts' Law helps explain why these issues matter. The time required to interact with a target depends on its size and distance (MacKenzie, 2018). Tiny buttons and crowded menus are not merely aesthetic choices. They are barriers that prevent certain users from completing tasks efficiently. Empathy allows designers to anticipate these barriers before they become usability problems. It transforms design from a personal expression into a human centered practice.

The Invisible Architecture of Usability

Mental models are one of the most powerful concepts in human centered design. A mental model is the internal representation a person forms about how something works. When a system aligns with a user's mental model, the experience feels intuitive. When it does not, confusion follows. Johnson argues that mismatched mental models are among the most common causes of usability problems (Johnson, 2020). Examples include:

  • A user clicks a shopping cart icon expecting to reach checkout but is taken to account settings instead.
  • A navigation menu uses vague labels such as Discover or Spaces that do not match common conventions.
  • A form requires information in an unusual order, forcing users to rethink their expectations.
These issues often originate in early design decisions. Vocabulary, imagery, and structure shape the mental models users form. Once a user forms an incorrect model, it becomes difficult to correct. This is why ISO 9241 210 emphasizes iterative evaluation. Designers must test their assumptions early and often to ensure that the mental models they intend to support are the ones users actually form.

Early Stage Design Choices

Early design decisions may seem trivial. Should a button be blue or gray? Should a layout be horizontal or vertical? Should a form ask for an email address before a name? Individually these choices appear minor, but together they shape the entire user experience. By the time usability testing begins, many perceptual and cognitive issues are already embedded in the design. Three areas illustrate how early decisions influence usability:

  • A. Visual Hierarchy
    • A strong visual hierarchy guides the eye. It tells users what matters and what to do next. Contrast, spacing, and typography are not decorative choices. They are cognitive tools that reduce effort and improve clarity.
  • B. Information Architecture
    • Information architecture determines how content is organized and how users move through it. A structure that aligns with users' mental models creates flow. A structure that contradicts those models creates friction that no amount of visual polish can fix.
  • C. Interaction Design
    • Predictable interactions require less thought. Innovative interactions can be exciting, but they must offer meaningful benefits. Otherwise, they simply increase cognitive load.
These decisions shape the foundation of the design. Once that foundation is set, it becomes difficult to change without rebuilding the entire experience.

Why Designers Must Understand Human Perception

Designers are not just creators of interfaces. They are interpreters of human behavior. Understanding perception allows designers to:

  • Predict how users interpret visual information
  • Reduce cognitive load
  • Create intuitive interactions
  • Avoid accessibility barriers
  • Build empathy for diverse users
Human-centered design emphasizes early research and continuous iteration because perception is dynamic. It varies across individuals, cultures, environments, and emotional states. A design that works perfectly in one context may fail in another. Designers must therefore remain flexible, curious, and willing to revise their assumptions.

Designing for Minds, Not Screens

The deeper I explore human-centered design, the more I realize that design is not about screens, buttons, or layouts. It is about minds. Human minds are brilliant, biased, emotional, easily distracted, and deeply shaped by experience. Users do not interact with interfaces logically. They interact perceptually. They notice what stands out. They ignore what blends in. They follow familiar patterns. They avoid friction. They make mistakes. They get frustrated. They take shortcuts. Designing for minds also means acknowledging the emotional context users bring with them. A beautifully crafted interface can still fail if it demands too much precision from an anxious parent, too much memory from a stressed student, or too much patience from someone dealing with a difficult situation. Real life is messy. Human-centered design embraces that messiness instead of designing for an idealized user who does not exist. Effective design anticipates this. Exceptional design accepts it.

Conclusion: Seeing With Intention

Perception, attention, and empathy form the foundation of human-centered design because human experience begins with these processes. Frameworks such as ISO 9241 210 and the IDEO Field Guide provide structure, but cognitive psychology and UX principles reveal how the human mind actually works. When designers understand how people perceive, think, and behave, they can make better decisions from the very beginning of the design process. These early decisions shape usability, accessibility, and emotional resonance. They determine whether a design feels intuitive or confusing, supportive or frustrating, human or mechanical. Human-centered design is ultimately an approach to seeing. It is a commitment to observe the world with intention, to understand people deeply, and to design experiences that honor the complexity of the human mind.

Usability is Not Just About Making Things Easy, it's About Making People Think Less

Just because something is modern and has an aesthetic design doesn't make it usable. Something becomes usable when it is aligned with how humans think. This includes habits, human memory, human tendencies, and mental models formed based on interactions with systems throughout their lifetime. Failure to account for human cognition leads to frustration, confusion, and cognitive overload. In today's world, nearly everything is done through interfaces, making cognitive overload an obstacle to accomplishing even simple tasks. This post explores the role that memory, mental models, consistency, and feedback play in our experience of technology. Through a discussion of concepts from Designing with the Mind in Mind as well as examples from Tesler's Law, I will argue that the role of the designer is not to make things better. Instead, it is to remove obstacles to success by designing with limitations in mind.

Memory, Mental Models, and the Invisible Work of Using Technology

Image: Funic Tech, used for educational purposes.

Human memory was not meant for perfect recall; rather, human memory functions based on efficiency. Human memory remembers patterns more than details and processes workflows better than exact steps. Thus, the role of mental models in human perception and interaction with systems becomes crucial. Mental model represents a story that a user forms about how a particular system operates. The better such story fits an interface design, the more intuitive it becomes. However, when such match is lacking, the user needs to create another story, leading to an additional cognitive load. Thus, as Johnson mentions, users rely more on recognition than recall since the former requires fewer cognitive resources (Johnson, 2020). For instance, if an interface design hides essential interactions with the help of obscure icons, the system will be more complicated to navigate because even a simple task, like clicking the icon to make something happen, will require memorizing the meaning of the icon first. The human memory remains the most fragile element of the cognitive system. The hamburger menu is a good example of this. While designers enjoyed its simplicity and space-saving abilities, users were confused initially since the symbol was not an intuitive representation of a menu for them. It involved recalling rather than recognizing the concept. Users gradually got used to it since it became a widely known interface pattern. Therefore, mental models are developed based on experience rather than logical reasoning. When users encounter a completely new pattern, no matter how elegant it may be, it will confuse them. Another example could be a comparison between Apple and Android operating system settings. Although there is little difference between features offered in these OSs, the settings' organization and terminology are different enough to require users to learn how to use the second system as if it is another language. It is not due to a lack of skill; rather, it is caused by the necessity to form a new mental model.

When Design Fights Habit, Habit Usually Wins

Habits are powerful since they allow humans to think on autopilot. When an object interferes with a person's habit, the individual is required to use conscious thoughts in order to solve the problem instead of automatic ones. Solving a problem consciously demands a great deal of effort from the person. For example, when social networks switched from the chronological feed view to the algorithmic feed view, their users got confused and angry. They were no longer able to automatically predict what content will appear next in their feed. It demanded a significant cognitive effort from them. According to Johnson, our brains are trying to be efficient. Thus, when we have acquired a habit, breaking it becomes much harder (Johnson, 2020). This is why even small interface adjustments can make users spend excessive efforts. It happens in real-world applications as well. The redesign of Snapchat in 2018 caused a massive backlash among the user base. The features remained the same, but the design change was so severe that users had to get used to operating the app again. Millions of users even signed a petition for Snapchat to roll back the redesign. This did not happen out of sheer dislike of the new version. The lesson here is easy to understand. The user habits need to be considered part of the system as a whole.

Tesler's Law: Complexity Does Not Disappear, it Moves

Tesler's Law states that there is an inherent amount of complexity for any system, which somebody will need to manage. It could be the responsibility of the designer, or it could fall on the end-user (Tesler, 1984). Shifting the responsibility to the user increases the user's cognitive load, while shifting the responsibility to the designer decreases usability.

Diagram from ResearchGate, used under Creative Commons or academic fair use.

A good example of this would be the online tax filing processes that some websites employ. One approach makes the user classify all their forms and deductions, while another takes all this effort out of the hands of the end-user. An additional case would be the password requirement. Certain sites insist on having the password adhere to some exact parameters such as incorporating a symbol, a digit, a capital letter, and being of some length. In such instances, although the system has the ability to create a secure password automatically, the decision to do the hard work is left to the user. Usability, according to Tesler's Law, is not only a design aspect but rather an ethical issue. It is up to the designer to determine the success or failure of the user experience.

Interface Consistency

One of the key ideas in usability is consistency, which helps avoid having to learn again. Predictable designs mean that users will be able to take their previous experience from one app into another. According to Johnson, consistency helps with recognition, minimizes memory strain, and reinforces mental models (Johnson, 2020). It's what makes design systems such as the Apple Human Interface Guidelines and Google Material Design so successful, creating a common language throughout the interface. However, inconsistencies occur all the time. Smart TV interfaces provide a great case study here. All major manufacturers build menus and buttons differently, so each time a user turns on a new smart TV, they have to learn everything from scratch. Even just from within one application, consistency becomes important because if you press the “back” button and it sends you to the last screen at some points and then closes the application, users will not trust that application anymore. It will become difficult for them to remember all actions. Consistency might be boring, but it has power. Consistency helps convert learning into recognition.

The Difference Between Confidence and Confusion

Feedback is a way to let the user know what is going on. In the absence of feedback, the user experiences uncertainty, resulting in high levels of cognitive load. Positive feedback happens instantly and conveys the result clearly. A button needs to change its visual state after being pressed; if a file was successfully uploaded, it needs to tell the user. Negative feedback makes the user wonder. Did they fill out a web form successfully? Was the transaction completed? Was the document sent? All of this results in multiple tries, mistakes, and annoyance. A classic case is a spinning loading icon that tells nothing about itself. Numerous studies prove that users experience shorter wait times and have fewer problems with patience if they have an idea about the process and how long it takes (Bailenson, 2018). Lack of feedback causes people to perceive time as more prolonged, leading to frustration. Yet another example would be the difference between a responsive touchscreen and an untimely responsive one. The former reassures a user, while the latter will make them tap multiple times, resulting in mistakes. Feedback is not a nice-to-have feature but rather a mandatory usability component.

Examples of Poor Design that Lead to Cognitive Load

The Overcomplicated Restaurant Kiosk

Most restaurant kiosks bury the crucial options under many menu levels. The user needs to figure out where everything is and understand vague icons. This complicates matters for the user, in violation of Tesler's Law.

The Hidden Cancel Subscription Flow

Some sites make the cancellation process hard by burying it under confusing labels and lengthy confirmations. This makes users engage in problem solving and adds cognitive load.

The Strange Smart Home App

The language used in smart home apps can be highly inconsistent, leading to confusion. Navigation becomes random, forcing users to create a mental model from scratch for each brand, despite similar tasks.

The Gesture Interface Without Any Hints

There are not always hints in gesture interfaces. The user needs to memorize all the different gestures required for different contexts, relying mostly on recall.

"Are You Sure?" Pop Up That Tells the User Nothing

There are some confirmations that make you wonder if what you are doing is something you really intended to do. It makes the user stop and interpret what the pop up is telling him/her. In all cases, the issue here does not concern technology but rather the additional strain being put on the user.

Why Usability is All About Respect

Ultimately, usability is all about respecting the cognitive capacities of human beings. It takes into consideration that human beings are already busy, that they have a lot on their plate, and that their memory space, habits, and mental models are limited. In fact, it tells us that users should not be required to think of the interface but of their goal. Principles such as consistency, feedback, and Tesler's law are used by good designers when creating systems that will appear natural and safe. Those that ignore them design interfaces that are tiring and confusing.
