Understanding Systems Thinking in a Real-World Context

When I first heard the term “systems thinking,” I thought it was just another complicated way of saying “look at the big picture.” But after going through the readings, I realized it's much more than that. Systems thinking is about understanding how different parts of a system interact with each other, and how those interactions create behaviors that you wouldn't notice if you looked at each part individually. It emphasizes patterns, feedback loops, and interdependencies rather than just isolated elements. This way of thinking allows us to see why some problems persist even when we try multiple solutions, because they are not caused by one single component, but by the system as a whole.

One thing that stood out to me is how systems thinking focuses on relationships instead of isolated components. In traditional problem solving, we usually break things down into smaller parts to understand them. But systems thinking does the opposite. It encourages us to step back and see how everything connects. This shift in perspective is especially important in modern engineering, where systems are becoming increasingly complex and interconnected (Span et al., 2022). Engineers now often deal with cyber-physical systems, networks, and smart technologies that rely on multiple interacting subsystems. In these contexts, understanding isolated components is not enough, because recognizing how changes in one area can ripple through the system is crucial..

A good example of this is something as simple as traffic. If you look at one car, it is easy to understand what is happening: speed, lane position, and driver behavior. But when you look at thousands of cars interacting on a highway, patterns like traffic jams suddenly appear. These patterns are emergent behaviors, arising not from a single driver, but from the collective interactions of many drivers. Systems thinking helps us understand and anticipate these emergent behaviors, which is something that traditional linear approaches might miss. Other examples can include environmental systems, where the impact of pollution, human activity, and climate change interact in ways that cannot be fully understood by looking at each factor individually.

Personally, I think this mindset is useful beyond engineering too. Even in daily life, many problems arise from not seeing the full system. For instance, when students struggle academically, it is easy to blame motivation or intelligence. But if you approach the issue systemically, you might consider additional factors such as classroom environment, teaching methods, mental health, social pressures, family circumstances, and even access to resources. Thinking this way changes not only how you analyze a problem, but also how you approach solutions. Instead of quick fixes, you are encouraged to identify root causes and systemic interventions, which often lead to more sustainable outcomes.

What is Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE)?

After understanding systems thinking, MBSE feels like the practical tool that brings that mindset into real engineering work. Model-Based Systems Engineering replaces traditional document-based methods with models that represent the system visually and structurally. Instead of creating long, text-heavy reports, engineers use diagrams, simulations, and interconnected data to represent a system. These models show how each part interacts and how the system as a whole behaves, which makes complex systems easier to analyze and manage.

According to McGrath and Jonker (n.d.), MBSE helps teams improve communication, reduce errors, and manage complexity more effectively. I found it interesting that MBSE acts like a shared language for teams. Large engineering projects often involve multiple disciplines, including software, hardware, electrical, mechanical, business, and operations. Traditional documents are prone to misinterpretation, version control issues, and inconsistencies. A shared model ensures everyone is looking at the same system representation, reducing confusion and aligning understanding across teams. This is particularly important in projects where miscommunication can lead to costly mistakes or safety issues.

Another benefit of MBSE is that models can be updated dynamically. In traditional engineering, changing one component might require revising multiple documents manually, which is time-consuming and error-prone. With MBSE, changes propagate automatically through the model, ensuring consistency and reducing the likelihood of mistakes. For example, in aerospace engineering, updating a subsystem model can automatically reflect changes in flight dynamics, sensor inputs, and safety constraints. This dynamic aspect makes MBSE an efficient tool for managing large-scale, evolving systems.

However, MBSE is not perfect. One challenge is that it can become overly complex. If a model tries to capture every possible detail, it can become as hard to understand as a long document. Additionally, creating and maintaining high-quality models requires training, experience, and continuous effort. The model is only as good as the assumptions and information put into it (Chądzyński, 2023). This makes MBSE a powerful but carefully balanced tool: too little detail, and the model is useless; too much, and it becomes overwhelming.

Connecting Systems Thinking and MBSE

What really helped me understand both concepts is realizing that systems thinking and MBSE are closely connected. Systems thinking is a mindset, a lens through which we understand interactions and feedback loops. MBSE is a practical method for applying that mindset to real-world engineering problems. Without systems thinking, MBSE risks becoming a purely technical exercise. You could create models, but without an understanding of how system components interact, the models would only offer limited insight. Conversely, systems thinking without tools like MBSE can be hard to apply to complex projects where hundreds of interconnected components exist.

The reading from Systems Engineering Demystified emphasized that modern systems are too complex to manage using traditional approaches alone. Large-scale projects, such as cloud infrastructure, smart grids, or autonomous vehicles, cannot rely on linear, document-driven methods. This is where the combination of systems thinking and MBSE becomes powerful. Systems thinking allows engineers to conceptualize the interconnections, while MBSE allows them to model, test, and predict system behavior before implementing changes (Holt, 2021).

This combination is particularly relevant to cybersecurity, which is an area I am interested in. Cyber systems are highly interconnected, with dependencies across networks, devices, and software applications. A small vulnerability in one area can lead to cascading failures across the system. Systems thinking helps identify potential weak points and understand how threats can propagate, while MBSE can model these interactions to simulate scenarios, predict risks, and strengthen defenses. For example, using MBSE, a cybersecurity team could simulate how an attack on a cloud service might affect connected applications and users, allowing them to prioritize patches and preventive measures more effectively.

Critical Reflection: Strengths and Limitations

One of the strengths of systems thinking is that it changes the way you approach problems. Instead of jumping to quick solutions, it encourages a focus on underlying causes and patterns. This is something I have struggled with in the past. I tend to fix immediate issues without considering long-term consequences. Systems thinking teaches patience and encourages holistic analysis. It also promotes awareness of feedback loops, showing how actions in one part of a system might influence other parts over time, sometimes in unexpected ways.

At the same time, systems thinking can feel overwhelming. When everything is connected, it can be difficult to know where to start. There is a risk of overanalyzing without taking action, which can be frustrating in fast-paced environments. Similarly, MBSE requires skill and training. Not everyone is familiar with modeling tools, and it can take time to develop the ability to create and interpret models effectively. Maintaining accurate models is also a challenge, because outdated or incomplete models can lead to incorrect conclusions.

Another limitation is that MBSE depends heavily on how well the model is designed. A poorly constructed model, missing critical relationships or feedback loops, can give a false sense of understanding. This reminds me that even advanced tools cannot replace critical thinking and human judgment. MBSE is not a solution by itself, it is a method that amplifies the effectiveness of thoughtful analysis and collaboration.

Why This Matters for My Future

As someone studying IT management and cybersecurity, I see clear value in both systems thinking and MBSE. Today's technology systems are not isolated, they are interconnected networks of hardware, software, and human operators. Understanding individual components is not enough, it is crucial to understand how the system as a whole functions.

In cybersecurity, a single weak point can compromise an entire network. Systems thinking helps identify those vulnerabilities by showing how components interact. MBSE allows modeling of these interactions, simulating attacks, and testing interventions in a controlled virtual environment. For instance, a system engineer could use MBSE to visualize a company's network, identify high-risk nodes, and model potential attacks before they occur. This proactive approach can save time, resources, and prevent large-scale security breaches.

Moreover, both systems thinking and MBSE support teamwork. In large projects, communication is often the biggest challenge. A shared model reduces misunderstandings and keeps teams aligned, whether they are working on cybersecurity, software development, or IT infrastructure. MBSE models act as a common reference point for discussion, reducing the friction that comes from inconsistent documentation or assumptions.

I also see applications beyond engineering. Systems thinking can help in personal decision-making and organizational leadership. For example, understanding how policies, culture, and workflow interact can lead to better management decisions. MBSE concepts can also be adapted for project management, where visualizing processes and dependencies helps identify bottlenecks and risks.

Overall, learning about systems thinking and MBSE has changed how I view complex problems. I now see them not as puzzles to be solved piece by piece, but as interconnected systems where each part influences the others. This perspective encourages more thoughtful solutions, proactive planning, and collaboration, which are valuable skills in both professional and personal contexts.

Conclusion

Systems thinking and Model-Based Systems Engineering are both essential for understanding and managing complex systems. Systems thinking provides the mindset needed to see relationships and interactions, while MBSE offers the tools to model and analyze those systems effectively.

While both have their challenges, their benefits are clear. They improve problem-solving, enhance communication, and help manage complexity in ways that traditional approaches cannot.

For me, this topic was more than just another assignment. It actually changed how I think about problems, not just in engineering, but in everyday life. And I think that's what makes it valuable.

Understanding the Systems Development Life Cycle and Life Cycle Models

When I first learned about the Systems Development Life Cycle, or SDLC, I thought it was just a checklist that engineers follow when building software. But after reading more about it, I realized it is much more than that. The SDLC is basically a structured way of thinking about how systems are created, used, and improved over time. It is not just about building something and finishing it. It is about guiding a system from the initial idea all the way through its long-term use and maintenance.

In simple terms, the SDLC is a process that breaks down system development into clear stages. These stages usually include planning, analysis, design, development, testing, deployment, and maintenance. Each phase has a specific purpose and helps make sure the final system actually works and meets user needs (IBM, n.d.; Ungvarsky, 2024). Instead of jumping straight into coding, teams follow this structured approach so they can reduce mistakes, manage risks, and stay organized.

What I found interesting is that the SDLC is not just about technical work. It also includes communication, planning, and decision-making. For example, during the planning phase, teams are not writing code yet. They are thinking about what problem the system is solving, who will use it, and what resources are needed. This shows that building systems is not just about programming. It is also about understanding people and real-world needs.

Another thing that stood out to me is that the SDLC has evolved over time. In the early days of computing, systems were much simpler, so development processes were less structured. But as systems became more complex, especially with modern technology like cloud computing and cybersecurity, a more organized approach became necessary (Ungvarsky, 2024). This is why the SDLC is still widely used today. It provides a reliable framework for managing complexity.

Key Characteristics of the SDLC

One key characteristic of the SDLC is that it is structured and phased. Each stage has specific goals and deliverables that guide the next stage. For example, the design phase creates a blueprint for the system, which developers then use during the coding phase. This structure helps teams stay organized and ensures that nothing important is skipped.

Another important characteristic is that the phases are interconnected. Even though the SDLC is often shown as a sequence of steps, it is not always strictly linear. Teams may go back and revise earlier phases if new information comes up. For example, if testing reveals a major issue, developers might return to the design phase to fix it. This shows that the SDLC is both structured and flexible at the same time.

The SDLC also focuses heavily on risk management. By breaking the process into stages, teams can identify problems early before they become too expensive or difficult to fix. For instance, catching a design flaw early is much easier than fixing it after the system has already been deployed. This is one of the biggest advantages of following a life cycle approach (IBM, n.d.).

Moreover, another key feature is its emphasis on collaboration. Different stakeholders are involved at different stages, including developers, managers, and end users. This helps ensure that the system meets both technical requirements and user expectations. Without this collaboration, systems might work technically but fail in real-world use.

Linear Life Cycle Model

The linear model, often called the waterfall model, is one of the oldest and most traditional approaches to system development. In this model, each phase of the SDLC is completed in sequence before moving on to the next. For example, the team must finish planning before starting analysis, and finish analysis before starting design (IBM, n.d.).

One of the main advantages of the linear model is its simplicity. It is easy to understand and manage because everything follows a clear order. This makes it useful for projects with well-defined requirements that are unlikely to change. It also provides clear documentation at each stage, which can be helpful for large organizations or regulated industries.

However, the linear model also has significant drawbacks. The biggest issue is its lack of flexibility. Once a phase is completed, it is difficult to go back and make changes. If a problem is discovered later in the process, it can be very expensive and time-consuming to fix. This makes the model less suitable for projects where requirements may change over time.

Another limitation is that users do not see the final product until late in the process. This can lead to situations where the system does not meet user expectations, even if it technically follows the original requirements. In modern technology environments, where change is constant, this rigidity can be a major disadvantage.

Iterative Life Cycle Model

The iterative model takes a different approach by focusing on repeated cycles of development. Instead of building the entire system at once, developers create a basic version first and then improve it through multiple iterations. Each cycle adds new features or improves existing ones.

A major advantage of the iterative model is flexibility. Because the system is developed in stages, teams can adapt to new requirements or feedback more easily. This makes it well suited for projects where the final requirements are not fully known at the beginning. It also allows teams to identify and fix problems early in the process.

Furthermore, users can interact with early versions of the system. This provides valuable feedback that can guide future development. It reduces the risk of building a system that does not meet user needs, since adjustments can be made along the way.

However, the iterative model also has drawbacks. One challenge is that it can be harder to manage compared to the linear model. Since development is ongoing, it may be difficult to predict timelines and costs accurately. There is also a risk of scope creep, where the project keeps expanding as new features are added.

Despite these challenges, the iterative model is widely used today, especially in modern software development practices like agile. It reflects the reality that most systems are not perfect from the start and need continuous improvement.

Incremental Life Cycle Model

The incremental model is similar to the iterative model, but with a key difference. Instead of improving the same version repeatedly, the system is built in smaller parts, or increments. Each increment adds a new piece of functionality until the full system is complete.

A prime benefit of this approach is that it allows parts of the system to be delivered and used earlier. This can be beneficial for organizations that want to start using the system before it is fully finished. It also spreads out the workload, making the development process more manageable.

To add to that, it also reduces risks. Since the system is built in smaller pieces, problems can be identified and fixed within each increment. This makes it easier to control quality and avoid large-scale failures.

However, the incremental model also has limitations. One challenge is that it requires careful planning to ensure that all increments fit together properly. If the system is not well designed from the beginning, integrating different parts can become difficult.

Additionally, while users can access parts of the system early, they may not get the full experience until all increments are completed. This can sometimes lead to confusion or incomplete functionality during early stages.

Why Life Cycle Thinking Matters

Thinking in terms of a system's life cycle is important because systems do not exist in isolation. They evolve over time, and their impact extends beyond the initial development phase. By considering the full life cycle, engineers can design systems that are not only functional, but also sustainable and adaptable.

Life cycle thinking matters because of sustainability. Systems require maintenance, updates, and support long after they are deployed. Without planning for these factors, systems can become outdated or fail over time. The maintenance phase of the SDLC ensures that systems continue to function and improve as user needs change (IBM, n.d.).

efficiency is also a significant aspect long-term . A well-designed system can save time and resources in the future. For example, investing more effort in the design phase can reduce the need for costly fixes later. This shows that decisions made early in the life cycle can have long-term consequences.

Life cycle thinking is also critical for cybersecurity, which is something I am interested in. Security is not just something you add at the end of development. It needs to be considered at every stage, from design to maintenance. If security is ignored early on, it can lead to serious vulnerabilities later. This is why approaches like DevSecOps integrate security throughout the entire life cycle (IBM, n.d.).

To add on, this mindset is important because of adaptability. Technology is constantly changing, and systems need to evolve to keep up. By thinking in terms of a life cycle, engineers can design systems that are flexible and easier to update. This is especially important in modern environments where user needs and technologies change quickly.

Personal Reflection

Learning about the SDLC and different life cycle models has changed how I think about building systems. Before, I mostly thought about coding as the main part of development. Now I realize that coding is just one step in a much larger process. Planning, design, testing, and maintenance are just as important.

I realized how important communication and collaboration are. Systems are not built by one person. They involve teams working together across different roles. The SDLC helps organize this collaboration and ensures everyone is aligned.

Overall, this topic helped me see systems as something that evolves over time, not just something that is built and finished. This mindset will be important for working in any technology-related field.

Conclusion

The Systems Development Life Cycle is a structured approach that guides the creation and management of systems from start to finish. It provides a clear framework that helps teams stay organized, reduce risks, and deliver effective solutions.

Different life cycle models, such as linear, iterative, and incremental, offer different ways of approaching system development. Each model has its own strengths and weaknesses, and the choice of model depends on the nature of the project.

Most importantly, thinking in terms of a system's life cycle helps ensure that systems are not only functional, but also sustainable and adaptable over time. In today's fast-changing technological world, this perspective is essential for designing systems that can grow and evolve with user needs.

Designing for People: Why Human-Centered Design Matters

When people talk about “design,” it is easy to think about visuals first. Things like colors, layouts, or how modern an interface looks. But after learning more about user experience, I realized that good design is not really about how something looks. It is about how it works for people. Designing for people means understanding how users think, what they feel, and how they interact with systems in real situations.

This idea is at the core of human-centered design. Instead of starting with technology or features, human-centered design starts with people. It focuses on understanding users' needs, behaviors, and limitations before making design decisions. According to ISO 9241-210, this approach ensures systems are usable and useful by keeping users involved throughout the design process (ISO, 2019).

At first, this sounds obvious. But in reality, many systems fail because they are built on assumptions instead of real user understanding. A designer might assume users understand technical language, while in reality they may feel confused or overwhelmed. This gap shows why perception, attention, and empathy are so important. These factors shape how users experience a system, and even small design decisions can make a huge difference.

Human-Centered Design: ISO vs. IDEO

Two perspectives that helped me understand human-centered design are ISO 9241-210 and IDEO's Field Guide to Human-Centered Design. ISO presents a structured and systematic approach. It emphasizes involving users, understanding context, and refining designs through evaluation (ISO, 2019). This makes it reliable and consistent, especially in professional environments.

IDEO's Field Guide takes a more creative and flexible approach. It focuses on empathy, observation, and experimentation. Instead of strict steps, it encourages designers to learn directly from users and adapt based on real-world insights (IDEO.org, 2015).

The difference is clear. ISO focuses on structure and process, while IDEO focuses on mindset and creativity. In practice, both are necessary. Structure keeps projects organized, while empathy ensures the design actually works for people.

Empathy as the Foundation of Design

Empathy is one of the most important parts of human-centered design. It means understanding not just what users do, but also what they think and feel. One useful method is empathy mapping, which helps designers visualize user experiences by focusing on what users say, think, do, and feel (Gibbons, 2018).

This approach goes deeper than surface-level observations. For example, a user might say they like a system, but their behavior might show confusion or hesitation. Empathy helps uncover these hidden issues and leads to better design decisions.

In real-world design, empathy can make systems more inclusive. For example, designing for older users might involve larger text, simpler navigation, and clearer instructions. Without empathy, these needs could easily be ignored.

Perception and Visual Hierarchy

Perception affects how users interpret what they see. Designers must guide attention through visual hierarchy, which is the arrangement of elements based on importance. This can be done using size, color, contrast, and placement.

For example, on a website, a bright and large “Sign Up” button draws attention immediately. If all elements look the same, users may feel lost and unsure where to click. This shows how perception directly impacts usability.

Another example is error messages. If they are not visually distinct, users might miss them completely. Good design ensures that important information stands out and is easy to understand.

UX Laws in Design

Jakob's Law

Jakob's Law states that users expect your system to work like others they already know. Familiar patterns improve usability and reduce confusion (Yablonski, 2020).

Fitts's Law

Fitts's Law explains that larger and closer targets are easier to interact with. This is why important buttons are often large and easy to reach, especially on mobile devices.

Hick's Law

Hick's Law shows that too many choices slow down decision-making. Simplifying options helps users make faster and better decisions.

Miller's Law

Miller's Law suggests that people can only process a limited amount of information at once. Designers should organize content into smaller sections to reduce cognitive overload.

Why These Concepts Matter Together

All of these concepts work together. Empathy helps understand users, perception guides attention, and UX laws explain behavior. When combined, they create systems that feel intuitive and easy to use.

If any of these are ignored, the design can fail. A system might look good but still confuse users. Good design requires balancing all these elements.

Personal Reflection

Before learning about human-centered design, I thought design was mainly about visuals. Now I see that it is more about understanding people. Small details like layout or button size can completely change the user experience.

This is especially important in fields like cybersecurity. If systems are too complicated, users might avoid using them correctly. Designing with people in mind can improve both usability and security.

Conclusion

Designing for people means focusing on how users think, feel, and behave. Human-centered design ensures systems are not only functional, but also intuitive and meaningful.

ISO and IDEO provide different perspectives, but both highlight the importance of understanding users. Concepts like empathy, perception, and UX laws help guide better design decisions.

Overall, this topic changed how I view design. It is not just about making things look good, but about making them work for people in the best way possible.

What Makes Digital Systems Usable?

When using digital systems every day, most people do not stop to think about why some apps and websites feel easy to use while others feel frustrating. Usually, users just expect systems to work naturally. Whether someone is using a banking app, gaming platform, school portal, or social media app, usability affects the entire experience. A usable system is not only visually appealing, but also designed around the way humans think, remember information, and build habits over time.

After learning more about usability and user psychology, I realized that good design is heavily connected to memory, habits, and mental shortcuts. People rely on previous experiences to understand new systems, and designers must account for how the human brain processes information. If a system ignores these behaviors, users experience confusion, frustration, and unnecessary cognitive load. Concepts like mental models, interface consistency, feedback, and cognitive psychology all play important roles in creating usable systems.

Memory and Mental Models in User Interaction

One of the biggest factors influencing usability is memory. Humans naturally use past experiences to predict how systems should behave. These expectations are called mental models. According to Designing with the Mind in Mind, users form mental models based on repeated interactions with technology and familiar patterns (Johnson, 2020). When systems align with these expectations, users feel comfortable and confident. When systems break those expectations, users often become confused.

For example, most people already understand that a gear icon usually represents settings. Shopping cart icons represent purchases, and a magnifying glass represents search. Users do not need instructions because these patterns have become universal over time. This reduces the amount of thinking required during interaction.

I notice mental models a lot when using school portals or online dashboards. Sometimes different classes organize assignments, grades, and announcements in completely different ways. Even though the information exists, it becomes harder to find because the layout does not match the user's expectations. Instead of focusing on schoolwork, students spend extra time trying to understand navigation.

Mental models are also connected to habits. Once users become familiar with a system, they expect actions to remain consistent. Sudden redesigns or major layout changes can create frustration because users feel like they have to relearn everything. This shows how usability is not only about functionality, but also about reducing mental effort.

Recognition Over Recall

Another important concept discussed in Designing with the Mind in Mind is that humans recognize information more easily than they recall it from memory (Johnson, 2020). Good systems support recognition instead of forcing users to memorize information manually.

Modern apps use this principle constantly. Browsers save passwords and suggest autofill options. Streaming platforms recommend recently watched content. Phones remember recently opened apps. These features reduce the need for users to remember details themselves.

In contrast, poorly designed systems increase cognitive strain by requiring memorization. For example, some older websites require users to remember long navigation paths or specific commands. Instead of helping users, the system places the burden entirely on them. This creates unnecessary frustration for tasks that should be simple.

Recognition over recall is especially important in fast-paced environments. For example, in gaming interfaces, players rely heavily on recognizable icons and predictable menu layouts. If settings are hidden behind confusing categories, players may struggle to quickly adjust important options. Small usability problems become much more noticeable when users are under pressure or multitasking.

Interface Consistency and Why It Matters

Consistency is another major factor that improves usability. Interface consistency means similar actions should behave similarly throughout a system. According to the Nielsen Norman Group, consistency helps users transfer knowledge from one part of a system to another because they already understand how interactions work (Nielsen Norman Group, 2024).

Smartphones are good examples of consistency. Whether someone uses social media, messaging apps, or streaming services, gestures like tapping, swiping, and scrolling remain predictable. Because interactions are familiar, users can quickly learn new applications without needing instructions.

On the other hand, inconsistent systems increase confusion. I notice this a lot after software updates where menus or settings suddenly move locations for no clear reason. Even though the features remain the same, the inconsistency forces users to relearn navigation. Instead of improving usability, the redesign creates unnecessary frustration.

Consistency also helps reduce mistakes. If one button deletes information while another similar-looking button saves information, users can easily make accidental errors. Good design ensures that actions, layouts, colors, and navigation patterns remain predictable throughout the interface.

System Feedback and User Confidence

Another important part of usability is system feedback. Feedback tells users whether their actions worked correctly. Without feedback, users may feel uncertain or think the system is broken.

Feedback can appear in many forms, including loading animations, confirmation messages, progress bars, sounds, or visual highlights. For example, when sending a message on social media, users usually see a checkmark or “message sent” confirmation. These small details reassure users that the system is responding properly.

Poor feedback creates confusion and frustration. Many people have experienced clicking a button multiple times because nothing appeared to happen after the first click. Eventually, duplicate actions may occur because the interface failed to communicate clearly.

This problem becomes especially stressful in payment systems or online forms. If users are unsure whether a payment processed successfully, they may panic or repeat the transaction. Good feedback improves trust because users understand what the system is doing at all times.

Cognitive Load and Poor Design

Cognitive load refers to the mental effort required to process information and complete tasks. Poor design increases cognitive load by forcing users to think harder than necessary. Instead of focusing on goals, users focus on figuring out the interface itself.

One common example is cluttered websites filled with too many menus, ads, notifications, and text. When everything competes for attention, users become overwhelmed. This is connected to Miller's Law, which suggests humans can only process a limited amount of information at once (Yablonski, 2020).

Another example is outdated administrative or government websites. Many contain dense paragraphs, confusing navigation, and technical language that make simple tasks feel stressful. Even printing documents or finding forms can become frustrating because the interface requires so much mental effort.

Personally, I notice unnecessary cognitive load most often in academic systems. Some registration pages contain dozens of tabs, announcements, and links on a single screen. Instead of guiding users step by step, everything is presented at once. This creates confusion and makes the system feel overwhelming even for basic tasks.

Tesler's Law and System Complexity

Tesler's Law is another important usability principle. It states that every system contains a certain amount of unavoidable complexity. Designers cannot completely remove complexity, but they can decide whether users or the system itself will handle it.

“Complexity cannot be eliminated, only shifted.”

This idea changed the way I think about usability. Good systems hide complexity behind simple interactions. For example, Google Search performs extremely advanced processing behind the scenes, but users only interact with a clean search bar. The complexity still exists, but users do not need to manage it directly.

Bad design often shifts too much complexity onto users. Some software requires users to manually configure confusing settings for basic tasks. Instead of simplifying interaction, the system forces users to do extra work that should happen automatically.

Tesler's Law connects strongly to human-centered design because it emphasizes reducing mental burden whenever possible. A usable system does not eliminate complexity completely, but it manages complexity in ways that feel intuitive for users.

Why These Concepts Work Together

All of these concepts are connected. Mental models help users predict behavior, consistency creates familiarity, feedback builds confidence, and cognitive principles reduce mental effort. Together, these ideas make systems feel natural and intuitive.

If even one of these elements is ignored, usability can suffer. A visually attractive system may still frustrate users if navigation is inconsistent or overwhelming. Similarly, a powerful system may fail if users cannot understand how to interact with it efficiently.

Usability is ultimately about designing around human behavior. Instead of forcing users to adapt to systems, good designers adapt systems to the way people naturally think and interact.

Personal Reflection

Before learning about usability principles, I mostly judged digital systems based on appearance and features. Now I pay much more attention to how systems actually feel during interaction. Small details like navigation layout, feedback animations, and button placement can completely change the user experience.

This topic also made me realize how important usability is in fields like cybersecurity and IT management. If security systems are too confusing or difficult to use, people may avoid using them correctly. Designing systems around human behavior can improve both usability and security at the same time.

I also notice usability problems much more often now in daily life. Whether it is a confusing website, poorly organized dashboard, or frustrating software update, many design problems come from ignoring how humans process information.

Conclusion

Usable digital systems are designed around the way humans think, remember, and behave. Memory, habits, mental models, consistency, and feedback all influence user interaction. Good design reduces cognitive load and helps users complete tasks naturally and efficiently.

Principles from Designing with the Mind in Mind explain why recognition, memory limitations, and predictable interactions are so important. Tesler's Law further shows that complexity cannot disappear completely, but good systems prevent users from dealing with unnecessary complexity directly.

Overall, learning about usability changed how I view technology and design. Good design is not just about making systems look modern. It is about creating experiences that feel intuitive, comfortable, and easy for real people to use.
