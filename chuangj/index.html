What is Systems Thinking?

Systems thinking is about looking at the whole system instead of focusing on just one part. It helps us understand how different parts are connected and how they affect each other. It also includes important concepts such as boundaries, feedback loops, and interdependence. Systems can be open or closed, and different stakeholders may be affected in different ways (Morganelli, 2024). Another important part of systems thinking is asking the right questions instead of making assumptions. We need to consider multiple possible answers and take time to test ideas and learn from the results (Morganelli, 2024). Systems thinking is not about proving that one perspective is right. Instead, it helps us understand different viewpoints and combine them to find better solutions. This makes it easier to deal with complex problems (Morganelli, 2024).

Systems Thinking vs. Linear Thinking

Linear thinking is a common way of solving problems step by step. It is based on logic, past experience, and existing solutions. For example, if we have seen a similar problem before, we may try to apply the same solution again (Interaction Design Foundation, n.d.). This approach is useful because it creates clear and predictable outcomes. However, it can become limiting when dealing with complex problems, especially in areas like user experience design. Linear thinking often starts with one fixed assumption about the problem, which may not always be correct. As a result, it can limit creativity and reduce the number of possible solutions (Interaction Design Foundation, n.d.). In contrast, systems thinking takes a broader view. Instead of focusing on one starting point, we look at multiple factors, relationships, and perspectives. This helps us better understand the problem and develop more effective solutions. Even though linear thinking has its limits, it is still important. It works best after we explore different ideas and need to organize, evaluate, and refine them. In this way, systems thinking and linear thinking can work together.

Applications in Digital Systems

Systems thinking and MBSE are widely used in modern digital environments such as software development, cybersecurity, and data systems. Systems thinking helps us look at the system as a whole instead of focusing on individual parts. This makes it easier to understand user needs and create better designs. In cybersecurity, systems thinking is especially important because attacks often come from connections between different parts of a system. Instead of targeting just one component, attackers take advantage of multiple weaknesses. Systems thinking helps us identify these connections and find problems more effectively. MBSE helps engineers design complex systems by using models. These models show how different parts of a system work together, which makes it easier to improve performance, security, and reliability. Especially in large systems used by companies like Amazon or Google.

Why Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) Matters

Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) is important because it helps us manage the complexity of modern digital systems. Instead of relying on traditional document-based methods, MBSE uses models to represent systems and show how different components interact. This makes it easier for us to understand the system as a whole and identify potential issues early in the design process (Shevchenko, 2020). One key advantage of MBSE is that it supports different stages of system development, including design, analysis, verification, and validation. By using models, we can test ideas, explore different scenarios, and make better decisions before the system is fully built. This reduces the risk of errors and improves overall system quality (Shevchenko, 2020). MBSE is especially useful in complex systems where many components and stakeholders are involved. It improves communication by giving teams a shared way to visualize the system. It also helps us identify problems, including security risks, earlier in the development process instead of fixing them later. Overall, MBSE matters because it allows us to build systems in a more efficient, accurate, and collaborative way. It connects closely with systems thinking by focusing on relationships and the system as a whole.

Personal Reflection

Many technologies today are connected in ways that are not always obvious, and understanding those connections makes a big difference when solving real-world problems. It is not just about fixing one issue, but about understanding how everything works together. I have also learned that focusing on just one part of a system does not work in the long run. For example, we can improve security, but if we ignore user experience, people might not use the system properly. On the other hand, if we only focus on performance and ignore scalability, the system might work well at first but fail when it needs to handle more users or data. These trade-offs show why looking at the bigger picture is important. Systems thinking has helped me slow down and think more carefully instead of jumping to quick solutions. It encourages me to ask better questions, consider different possibilities, and understand how different parts of a system are connected. This is especially important in cybersecurity, where small weaknesses can affect the entire system. MBSE also stands out to me because it provides a practical way to apply these ideas. Instead of only thinking about systems in theory, MBSE uses models to show how systems actually work. This helps us better understand complex systems, communicate with others, and test ideas before implementation. Overall, I believe systems thinking and MBSE will be important skills for my future career.

Conclusion

In conclusion, systems thinking helps us understand complex systems by focusing on how different parts are connected rather than looking at them separately. MBSE builds on this idea by providing tools that help us design and manage these systems more effectively. While linear thinking is still useful in certain situations, it is not enough on its own when dealing with complex digital systems. By combining systems thinking with MBSE, we are able to approach problems in a more flexible and thoughtful way. As technology continues to evolve and systems become more interconnected, these approaches will become even more important. From my perspective, learning systems thinking and MBSE has changed how I approach problems, and I believe these skills will play an important role in my future career in cybersecurity.

What is the Systems Development Life Cycle?

The Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC) is a widely used concept that describes the evolution of a system from conception through six stages, including conception, development, production, utilization, support, and eventual retirement, as defined in ISO 15288. Instead of viewing a system as something build once and finished, systems in SDLC are expected to change and need ongoing updates. In systems engineering, a system isn’t a single unit but is made of multiple elements organized in a hierarchy, where higher-level components are composed of lower-level parts such as system, subsystem, assembly, and component. This layered structure is important because it helps clarify complex systems and makes them easier to understand and manage.

<3>What is the Systems Development Life Cycle?

The Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC) is a widely used concept that describes the evolution of a system from conception through six stages, including conception, development, production, utilization, support, and eventual retirement, as defined in ISO 15288. Instead of viewing a system as something build once and finished, systems in SDLC are expected to change and need ongoing updates. In systems engineering, a system isn’t a single unit but is made of multiple elements organized in a hierarchy, where higher-level components are composed of lower-level parts such as system, subsystem, assembly, and component. This layered structure is important because it helps clarify complex systems and makes them easier to understand and manage.

Linear Life Cycle Model

The linear model is also known as the waterfall model, follows in a specific order where each stage is completed before moving on to the next. One major advantage of linear model is that it is simple and easy to understand. It works well for projects with clearly defined and stable needs, well-understood technologies, easy management, readily available resources and short timeframes. However, for complex, adaptive systems engineering projects, linear models may not be suitable because they are too rigid to handle changing requirements.

Iterative Life Cycle Model

The iterative model is a development approach in which a basic version is created first, and the system is then completed through continuous modification and improvement. Unlike the linear model, it does not require all requirements to be fully defined at the outset. Instead, it involves developing partial functionality first, then continuously updating and refining it based on test results and user feedback. In each iteration, the team goes through steps such as requirements analysis, design, development, and testing. With each completed cycle, the system gains new features or fixes existing issues, gradually making the product more complete. This approach allows the development team to continuously learn and adjust during the process, rather than waiting until the project is finished to discover problems. I believe this model is well-suited for modern software development because user needs often change over time. Through repeated testing and revisions, the team can identify errors earlier and deliver the product to users more quickly. The advantages of this model include early problem detection, reduced development risks, and the ability to adapt to actual needs. Additionally, users can provide feedback during the development process, ensuring the final product better meets their requirements. However, it also has some disadvantages. For example, managing each update requires a significant investment of manpower and time. If the system architecture is not well-planned, subsequent modifications may become more difficult. Furthermore, the development team typically needs to possess a high level of technical and communication skills to effectively manage project progress and quality (TutorialsPoint, n.d.).

Incremental Life Cycle Model

The Incremental Model is an approach to system development that divides the system into several smaller parts. Instead of completing all features at once, the development team first implements the most critical features and then gradually adds new ones. Each increment goes through the steps of requirements analysis, design, development, testing, and deployment, allowing the system to become complete over time. There are two main approaches to this model. The first is the Staged Delivery Model, in which features are rolled out in phases according to a schedule, allowing users to see new versions on a regular basis. The second is the Parallel Development Model, where different teams work simultaneously on different features, thereby shortening the overall development time. I find the incremental model very practical because users don’t have to wait for the entire system to be completed before they can start using it. Instead, they can begin using the features that are already finished. This not only allows for earlier feedback but also helps the development team identify areas that need improvement. Its advantages include the ability to deliver products quickly, reduce project risks, and improve system quality through continuous testing. If requirements change, it’s also easier to adjust subsequent development work. However, this model also has its drawbacks. Since each increment requires testing and integration, it may increase development costs. Furthermore, if the initial system design is not well-planned, integration issues may arise when adding new features later on. Therefore, the development team must possess sufficient technical expertise and strong communication skills to ensure the project is completed successfully.

Why Thinking in Terms of a System's Life Cycle Is Important

I believe it is crucial to consider the system lifecycle, because the development of a digital system does not end once. In fact, it is only after the system goes live that it enters a phase of long-term maintenance and improvement. Technology is advancing at a rapid pace, and user needs are constantly changing. If a development team focuses solely on completing the system without considering subsequent maintenance and updates, the system may quickly become obsolete. For example, the Windows operating system, iPhones, and various mobile apps we use every day all release regular updates to fix issues, add new features, and enhance the user experience. Without these updates, the system might develop security vulnerabilities or fail to meet user needs. Thinking in terms of the lifecycle also helps reduce costs. When development teams consider future maintenance and expansion needs during the design phase, it becomes much easier to modify the system later on. Conversely, without proper planning, more time and money may need to be invested in the future to fix problems. Furthermore, information security is a critical factor. New cyberattacks and security threats emerge daily, so systems must be continuously updated and maintained to protect user data. Even if a system is secure today, that does not guarantee it will remain secure in the future. Therefore, maintenance and updates are an indispensable part of the system lifecycle. Overall, I believe the concept of the system lifecycle helps development teams think about systems from a long-term perspective, rather than focusing solely on immediate development tasks. Through continuous maintenance, updates, and improvements, systems can remain stable, secure, and aligned with user needs (GeeksforGeeks, 2026).

Human-Centered Design

Human-Centered Design (HCD) is an approach that takes the guesswork out of service design by understanding people’s needs. HCD focuses on truly understanding the challenges people face in the real world and designing solutions that address them (Human-centered design, n.d.). According to the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST,2021), ISO 9241-210 is the international standard for human-centered design (HCD) for interactive systems. It applies human factors and ergonomics principles to continuously iterate and improve human health, safety, and performance.

Srikant Datar, Dean of Harvard Business School, breaks human-centered design down into four stages: Clarify, Ideate, Develop and Implement. The first stage, Clarify, focus on data collection and observing customers to assess their needs and identify buyers’ pain points. During the second stage, Ideate, it is time to apply design thinking tool to overcome cognitive fixedness in order to amplify creativity, reduce costs, save time, and improve the quality of final products. The Develop phase is where ideas are combined and evaluated based on desirability, feasibility and viability. The final phase of the process is Implementation. It is essential to communicate the value of innovation to internal and external stakeholders to successfully bring the product to market. Customer’s wants and needs will continue to be change, so keeping humans at the center requires continuous adaption to meet these changes and achieving product-market fit (Landry, 2020).

Human-centered design places particular emphasis on empathy, as designers need to think from the user’s perspective and create products that truly meet their needs. Consequently, HCD is not merely a design methodology; it represents a shift in mindset from focusing on what a system can do to prioritizing user needs.

ISO 9241-210 vs IDEO’s Field Guide

The ISO 9241-210 standard developed by the International Organization for Standardization and the IDEO Field Guide to Human-Centered Design present complementary perspectives on human-centered design. ISO 9241-210 focuses on human-computer interaction (HCI) and ergonomics and is built around three key objectives: effectiveness (whether users can successfully complete tasks), efficiency (whether users can complete tasks with minimal effort or time), and satisfaction (how users perceive their experience). By emphasizing users and their needs, this standard aims to create systems that are usable, safe, and useful (International Organization for Standardization [ISO], 2019).

The six principles of human-centered design defined in ISO 9241-210 emphasize that design should be based on a deep understanding of users, tasks, and environments; involve users throughout the entire design process; be driven and refined through user-centered evaluation; follow an iterative design process that improves through cycles of testing, feedback, and refinement; consider the overall user experience; and be carried out by multidisciplinary teams (ISO, 2019).

In contrast, the IDEO Field Guide to Human-Centered Design is more practice-oriented and functions as a toolkit, emphasizing creativity, empathy, and action throughout the design process. Its goal is to identify innovative solutions by deeply understanding users’ real needs. IDEO typically organizes the design process into three phases: Inspiration, Ideation, and Implementation. The first phase focuses on observation and exploration to generate insights, the second phase encourages the generation of diverse ideas, and the final phase transforms those ideas into tangible solutions. This approach helps designers uncover the root causes of problems. In addition to emphasizing innovation, it also values practical application, making it widely used in product and service innovation (IDEO.org, 2015).

Although ISO emphasizes structured processes and standardization to ensure consistency in design, IDEO highlights creativity and empathy to better align solutions with user needs. However, these two approaches are not conflicting. In practice, combining both perspectives can lead to more effective design outcomes. Designers can first use IDEO’s approach to understand user needs and then apply ISO’s structured framework to refine and optimize the system, ultimately achieving solutions that are both standardized and user-centered.

Perception & Visual Hierarchy Example

Visuals play an indispensable role in our daily lives. The application of perception and visual hierarchy can be observed in many user interfaces. When users browse a screen, they tend to notice larger, high-contrast, or more visually prominent elements first, reflecting the human tendency to prioritize distinct features. Designers organize elements according to their importance to guide users’ attention toward key content. For example, on an e-commerce checkout page, the “Submit” and “Buy Now” buttons are often designed as larger elements and placed in prominent positions to guide users toward completing the transaction. By applying the principles of human perception and visual hierarchy, modern design becomes more intuitive and efficient, significantly enhancing the overall user experience (Figma, n.d.).

UX Laws

Jakob’s Law indicates that users spend most of their time on other websites, so they prefer new websites to operate in the same way as the sites they are familiar with. This is because users transfer their expectations of a familiar product to another product that looks similar. This also means that designers can leverage users’ existing mental models to create a better user experience, allowing users to focus on what matters rather than spending extra time learning a new model. This approach effectively reduces the learning curve and cognitive load, thereby minimizing friction (Yablonski, 2024).

Fitt’s Law indicates that the time users take to move to a target depends on the target’s size and distance. In other words, the larger the target and the closer it is, the faster users can complete the action. Conversely, when the target is smaller, closer, or farther away, the difficulty of the task increases, and the time required also increases. Therefore, when designing critical interactive elements, icons should be made larger and placed in prominent, easily accessible locations to improve accuracy. However, sufficient spacing should also be maintained between targets to prevent accidental taps. These design choices help reduce difficulty and enhance the user experience (Yablonski, 2024).

Hick’s Law indicates that the time users spend making decisions increases with the number and complexity of choices. When a website’s interface offers too many options, users must spend more time understanding each one, which increases cognitive load and reduces efficiency. Therefore, when designing a user-friendly interface, unnecessary options should be appropriately reduced to avoid information overload, and complex tasks should be broken down into smaller steps to help users achieve their goals quickly and clearly. However, be careful not to oversimplify to the point of abstraction. These design choices help simplify complex information, making it easier for users to navigate (Yablonski, 2024).

Miller’s Law states that human working memory and the capacity for processing information are limited to approximately seven items. Therefore, when an interface presents too much information, users are likely to feel overwhelmed, which in turn affects their efficiency in navigating the page. To reduce cognitive load, content should be broken down into smaller chunks to help users process, understand, and remember it more easily. These design strategies help users better comprehend the content and enhance the user experience (Yablonski, 2024).

These four principles complement one another; by applying them, designers can create interfaces that are both highly effective and tailored to user needs, thereby improving user behavior and increasing user satisfaction. This also aligns with the core principles of human-centered design. Even as we continuously focus on user needs throughout the design process, we must ground our work in science and psychology to create products that best resonate with people (Yablonski, 2024).

<3>Introduction

A system with good usability allows users to perform tasks effectively, efficiently, and satisfactorily. Usability is important because it improves the user experience, reduces frustration and cognitive load, makes systems easier to learn and remember, and increases business success. A study indicate that people have a short attention span. If they cannot find the information they need within 10 seconds, they will typically give up using the application. This emphasizes the critical of incorporating usability considerations from the very beginning of the design process. However, usability is not merely about the aesthetics of the interface; it is deeply intertwined with how humans think, their memory, and their decision-making processes (Intelequia, n.d.). When users encounter a new system, they often rely on past experiences and existing mental models to understand it, expecting the new system to operate in a familiar way. If the design fails to meet these expectations, users can easily become confused or even stop using the system. This demonstrates that human thinking does not start from scratch but rather applies existing experiences to new contexts.

Therefore, understanding human thinking is particularly important in the design process. Concepts such as memory, mental models, and cognitive load can explain why some designers are able to accurately align with user needs and help users get up to speed quickly. A good design can help solve real user problems, making products more useful and enhancing the overall customer experience.

<3>Memory & Mental Models

In digital design, memory and mental models have a profound impact on how users interact with interfaces. Cognition is not only about processing information, but also about building internal mental models. When users interact with a system, they do not start by learning step by step, but rather draw upon their memory, experience, and perception. The brain combines this information to form an understanding of how the system works; in other words, mental models allow people to predict what will happen and make decisions in advance, so they do not have to relearn how to use the system each time. When design aligns with cognition, users find it easy to use and intuitive; on the other hand, if it doesn't align, users experience confusion, frustration, and may even abandon the system (Psychology Today, 2025).

This idea can also be connected to Jakob’s Law from the course textbook, which explains that users prefer interfaces that feel familiar to them. This concept highlights that users spend most of their time on other websites. This means that users expect a new interface to be just as easy to use as the other websites they are familiar with (Yablonski, 2024). This reliance on prior experience makes familiar design patterns particularly important. For example, in 2017, YouTube launched its new Material Design interface while retaining elements of the old design. However, the platform did not force users to adapt to the new interface immediately; instead, it allowed desktop users to choose their own path. They can decide whether to provide feedback on the new interface or switch back to the old version. This gradual change approach allows users to adapt more naturally to the new system while reducing the confusion caused by being constrained by established mental models (Yablonski, n.d.).

<3>Consistency & Feedback

Consistency is a key factor in improving usability by allowing users to apply their previous experiences and reducing the need to relearn how a system works. In digital system design, consistency and feedback are considered key factors influencing usability, as they directly impact how users understand, anticipate, and interact with the interface. Consistency is defined as the system being uniform and coherent. It refers to maintaining a unified design language and interaction logic across different pages and features. For example, the location of checkout buttons, the use of colors, and the flow of operations. When a system is consistent, users can focus on achieving their goals rather than having to learn a new system every time they encounter a new interface. A successful digital system should be intuitive and easy to use, rather than requiring users to learn a complex interface. This design approach helps reduce the user’s cognitive load, as users do not need to expend extra mental energy figuring out how the system works; instead, they can intuitively complete the tasks they need to accomplish. Furthermore, consistent design enhances operational efficiency and fluidity, as users can anticipate what will happen next during the process. When each step aligns with past experiences, users are more likely to develop trust in the system (Yale University, n.d.). Conversely, if operations vary across different pages and the checkout location changes arbitrarily, users must constantly reinterpret the system’s logic. This not only burdens the user but also makes them more likely to feel confused and abandon the system.

In addition to maintaining consistency, effective feedback is a core element in enhancing system usability. This is because it allows users to understand the system’s status in real time while interacting with it. For example, when a user performs a website search, the system provides clear and immediate responses, such as a loading indicator or an e04 error. Which can help the user confirm whether their action has been accepted and processed. This real-time feedback can effectively reduce anxiety or feelings of helplessness caused by uncertainty, thereby enhancing the overall user experience (Dern, 2016).

<3>Cognitive Load

Cognitive load refers to the amount of mental effort and working memory required when completing a task, making it an important factor that directly influences user experience. When a website offers too many options or has an overly complex interface, users must expend more mental effort to understand how the system works. When this happens, users are likely to give up on using the system.

Many UX design theories hold that if users do not have to think too hard while operating a system, the system becomes easier to use. In the textbook, Hick’s Law effectively explains that when an interface offers more options, users tend to spend more time thinking and making decisions. Therefore, a good design should avoid unnecessary options to reduce the user’s cognitive load. Similarly, Miller’s Law also states that human short-term memory capacity is limited. Therefore, information should not be presented in large volumes at once but should be appropriately segmented and categorized to make it easier for users to understand and navigate. These design principles demonstrate that when system design aligns with the limits of human cognitive capacity, users can complete tasks more easily (Yablonski, 2020).

For example, Amazon categorizes products by type and provides filtering options to help users narrow down their choices. During the checkout process, Amazon divides the steps into distinct phases, such as adding to cart, confirming the order, and payment. Through clear step-by-step instructions, users can complete the process without feeling overwhelmed or confused by an excessive amount of information.

<3>Tesler’s Law + Designing with the Mind in Mind

Tesler’s Law states that every system possesses a certain level of complexity, and this complexity cannot be reduced; therefore, it must be borne by either the system or the user. However, good design should have the system absorb this complexity rather than placing the burden on the user. Take the autofill feature as an example; the system takes over the process that would otherwise require the user to fill in information and think through it, thereby reducing the user’s burden and improving efficiency (Yablonski, 2024).

This concept also aligns with the principles outlined in Designing with the Mind in Mind. Research indicates that humans are not good at recall but are better at recognition. Therefore, design should allow users to complete tasks through recognition. For example, through menu or button options, users can make direct selections rather than having to recall how to perform an action. Furthermore, human memory is limited; design should avoid presenting too much information at once and should use clear chunking to help users complete tasks. When a system adheres to these cognitive principles, users can complete tasks more easily, thereby improving efficiency and the user experience (Johnson, 2020).
