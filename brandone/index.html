Module 1.1 Blog Post

Systems Defined

According to Jon Holt, at its core, systems thinking is about understanding the interconnectedness of things and applying that interconnectedness to problem-solving. It is human nature to understand linear cause-and-effect relationships; systems thinking requires a different mindset. It provides the perspective needed to address complex, interdepended problems and the tools to understand how systems function. Systems thinking offers an alternative perspective to understand how things influence one another within embedded contexts. Let’s take the example of obesity: a linear approach would be as simple as changing the foods you eat and starting an exercise regimen. While there is some truth to that part, as a systems thinker, you must understand the parts and the whole. Obesity exists because of a combination of physical, mental, social, economic, and environmental factors. Not only do these factors affect the individual, but they are also reliant and affect one another. Every system has complexity; some complexity is inherent to the system itself, while other complexity arises from a lack of understanding of the system and human error.

The Nature and Classification of Systems

Systems fall into several categories: natural systems, designed physical systems, designed abstract systems, human activity systems, and transcendental systems. These classifications help systems thinkers understand what kind of system they work with and which traits make it function. For example, engineers have more control over designed physical systems, while transcendental systems are beyond understanding. Natural systems are open and beyond human control, such as ecosystems and the relationships between the environment, plants, and animals. Designed abstract systems have no physical artifacts but help people understand ideas, like mental models, processes, and strategies. Systems thinking itself is a designed abstract system that helps us make sense of systems. Human activity systems may be the most complex. Working with people is harder than working with gears and properties that always react the same way. Human relationships are much more dynamic and harder to model effectively. An example of human activity systems are things like a school board or workplace environment.

Stakeholders and Communication

Stakeholders are the roles of people or organizations who interact with or have an interest in the system. They do not exist within the system but interact with it in different ways, and a system can have many stakeholders who take on different roles. Communication between stakeholders is critical, which requires a domain-specific language (DSL). DSL defines specific terminology and concepts for a given domain to keep communication uniform and easy to understand for stakeholders with different perspectives. The different roles stakeholders take outside of a system shape how the system operates and what its goals and needs are. In the obesity example, there are a variety of different stakeholders, all with diverse perspectives and roles to play in the system. The individual person is a stakeholder, as is the healthcare system (enabling systems are a type of stakeholder); there are also urban planners, city officials, and food manufacturers.

System Structure and Elements

Beyond the individual characteristics of system types, there are traits that all systems have in common. All systems have a structure and elements that interact with each other and form relationships. Systems also interact with each other, and their elements have crossover. Two systems working together concurrently do not lack a boundary between them and instead function as two elements in a larger system. This structural perspective clarifies the organization of various elements into an integrated whole. Again, that depends on perspective.

Attributes and Boundaries

Attributes are high-level properties that describe a system or its elements. These attributes make up the relationships between elements; for example, two gears in a clock have a specific attribute, like the diameter between teeth, which allows those two elements to form a relationship: the gear ratio. Systems have a multitude and variety of boundaries, either physical, like the body of a car, or conceptual boundaries for abstract systems. It is also important to consider that, given how different stakeholders interact with the system at different points, they will perceive the boundaries of the system as different. Stakeholders play a large role in the system but exist outside of the boundaries. While there may be a boundary between two systems, elements from those two systems interface and may be related, even if a boundary exists between them. Let’s take the earlier example of obesity and elaborate on how different stakeholders may view the system. A doctor may view the boundary of the system as the patient's body, whereas a politician might see it as more of a food desert problem.

Constraints

Constraints are considerations that limit how a system may be realized. Workplaces are constrained by the regulations imposed by the state. Workers in Washington state are required to have a thirty-minute lunch break for every six hours they work. This puts a constraint on the way that workplaces structure their time and define when breaks are given. Let’s say you are building a Lego castle; however, you don’t have any grey blocks. In that case, you would be limited to using other colors. These are implementation constraints that limit the way the system can be built. But beyond that, the castle will be subject to external constraints like the laws of physics or what Legos have been manufactured. Another factor to consider is the environment in which a system is realized. Due to overfishing and the role they play within the environment, seining boats in Alaska are prohibited from catching halibut. Constraints play an important role in the realization of systems, but rather than limiting them, they create boundaries that define and narrow down the solution space.

System Needs

Needs describe the purpose of the system. The need is the "why" for a system; it gives the system a purpose and reason for being. The features of a system represent higher-level needs related to a collection of functions. Requirements are the specific, measurable criteria of what a system must achieve to reach its goal. Systems have purposes and goals inherent within them; the component elements are working together to achieve something, be it a physical operation, nature, or a governmental body. Requirements are needed that define the boundaries of a system, features are functions of collections of elements, and goals are the primary objectives of systems. Let's say a college student is looking for a new living situation and they don’t like their current apartment. A need of theirs could be that they cannot afford their current place. A requirement of their new place could be that it fits within their given affordable price range. They may also want certain features, like a number of bathrooms or a parking garage. All of this is with the goal of lowering their financial stress and finding an affordable place to live.

Linear Perspectives and Their Shortcomings

Linear perspectives fail when met with complexity. They are unable to consider the outside context and fail to adapt to new information. Systems thinking can adapt and add new elements to a system and account for new relationships and variables. Linear thinking is inflexible and cannot bend to evolving challenges. The framework and structure of systems thinking allow iteration and feedback to improve the process and have defined constraints. Linear perspectives are reductionist and lack an understanding of contextual relationships. This means you are unable to "think outside the box," so to speak.

Model-Based System Applications

In traditional engineering, knowledge is spread out over many documents, making it hard to organize and conceptualize a clear goal. Model-based systems engineering, or MBSE, takes the complete opposite approach, where the model is the "single source of truth" rather than a mountain of different documents. The first step of MBSE is making an abstraction of a system. The model simplifies the system to make it easier to grasp, but it lacks all the detail that the full system entails. The hard part is striking the balance within the model where only as much information as needed to realize the system is captured, but not too much so that it distorts the purpose. Another important aspect of the model is the views of stakeholders. In order for the single source of truth to be useful for the different stakeholders involved, it must be specialized to meet the needs of the stakeholders. Views are made up of notations and diagrams specific to the stakeholder needs. In designing a car, electrical, mechanical, and software engineers are all required in order for the realization of the system to come about. A software engineer would need a view with code and UX design, and specific functions and formulas. The electrical engineer needs circuit diagrams and specific types of wire. Modeling specifically helps fight against the "three evils" of systems: complexity, communication, and understanding. Models provide a way to visualize the essential complexity of a system, making the chances of accidental complexity, like human error, less common. Holt describes five stages of MBSE. Initially, all knowledge for a project is scattered and lives as disconnected documents. Stakeholders work with traditional productivity tools like Word or Excel in isolated documentation. In stage two, the engineers begin building the diagrams and notation that will eventually make up the views of the model. By stage three, the beginnings of a model start to take shape; some information from the documents has not been integrated into the model, and it is incomplete, but the notation and diagrams have been formalized. In stage four, the model has taken ownership of the documents, and MBSE processes can be followed for the most part. Stage five is the fully realized model; documents can be automated, and the workflow is at its most efficient. A hypothetical project that could use MBSE would be a CWU shuttle app for students to track public transport so they can arrive at classes on time. In stage one, there is a folder with PDFs that list route times and an Excel sheet that tracks bus maintenance and costs. By stage two, there is a flowchart for how the app login process might look, but unlike a realized model, it will have to be manually rewritten if processes change. In stage three, the early coding of the model is complete with the routing and bus maintenance factored in, but not all information will be fully integrated. In stage four, the prototype of the model is complete, with users being able to track buses and receive notifications. By stage five, Central could host the model and use it as a tool; if a student wanted to know all the route times, they could query the model and receive automated documentation.

Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC)

The systems development life cycle is the structure that defines the stages a system goes through from initial deployment to retirement. The SDLC structure is more than just the stages that a system goes through, but also the order in which they take place. These stages and their order will change depending on the nature of the system or entity (Holt 46). It’s also important to understand the distinction between the system of interest (SoI) and enabling systems. In the SDLC the System of interest is the one being developed, the enabling systems are the systems that exist in context with the system of interest. For example, the market economy system would be the system of interest, and an enabling system would be the law and regulations that “enable” and regulate the market. The six stages of systems development are as follows: conception, development, production, utilization, support, and retirement. Conception is the birthplace of the system, utilizing a combination of needs and stakeholder feedback the purpose for the system is devised and put into motion. Development is the next step in system realization; this is where some of the early problems get worked out and the first stages of prototyping begin. Production is where the documentation starts being modeled, and formal verification and validation testing begins. Utilization and Support take place concurrently; this is where the system has been realized but is still early in its use. This is also where stakeholders are being trained on the use of the system and systems engineers are finding errors, performing maintenance, and making sure the system is working as intended. Retirement is the final stage of the SDLC where the system has fulfilled its needs and can be safely disposed of this is also where lessons can be learned for future system iterations.

System Architecture

The Model Based System Engineering (MBSE) process includes terminology key to understanding system architecture. System hierarchy is an important factor to consider; systems have varying degrees of complexity and hierarchy is one tool systems engineering uses to manage it. Systems are composed of elements, subsystems, and further down, components and assemblies. Aside from hierarchy the interactions between system elements, enabling systems, and subsystems will need to be explicitly defined if the model is to be a useful tool in system realization (Holt 4). It’s also key to ensure that the hierarchy is useful in modeling these interactions, do elements interact between different levels? Or is the hierarchy more strictly designed where there is no cross-level interaction. These interfaces between different elements and enabling systems need to be fully understood before the production phase begins to prevent excessive maintenance and errors, also for consistency and clarity for stakeholders who use the model views.

Life Cycle Models & Characteristics

The three main life cycle models are Linear, Iterative, and Incremental. The main purpose of having a defined life cycle model has more predictability, more robust risk management, and stakeholder clarity at each transition period. The Linear Model is straightforward and easy to understand. The steps take place sequentially and in the specified order. They go from concept to retirement in that order, no loops or skipped steps. A good alternative descriptive term for the Linear model is the Waterfall model. Water always acts according to its nature; it flows to the lowest point and fills its containment fully. In that way the linear method is like water, it follows its steps sequentially and always does. This is easier for stakeholders to understand and follow but its rigidity may not be as well suited to a problem where the needs change over time and new technologies are being worked with that are less well understood (Holt 56). However, one of the key advantages to a linear process is that the timeframe usually ends up being a bit shorter and resources can be assigned to the different stages ahead of time due to the process being fleshed out from start. The Iterative life cycle is more well suited to complex projects and systems engineering. While it follows the traditional phases of SDLC the order in which the process takes is not linear and has loops to “iterate” and make changes as the different stages help designers draw conclusions about the strengths and weaknesses of the current system and processes. Essentially the iterative model breaks down one complex project into a series of mini projects (Holt 58). Iterations can be helpful in complex projects where when a new iteration gets worse and is unable to fully meet the needs of the system, you can always roll back to a previous iteration and begin the process again from there. There is also less individual pressure on each iteration to be perfect where it would be in a linear approach. Mistakes are more of a learning opportunity and less of a flaw within the system, especially if that error is made earlier and is more foundational it’s a lot harder to rewrite. The iterative approach has a lot of applications for software development, and engineering complex systems in the automotive and aerospace industry. The iterative approach does have some drawbacks, due to the nature of the process the basic needs and may change frequently which can make consistency and communication for stakeholders challenging. The incremental model has more in common with the iterative process than the linear one but still has meaningful differences. Both the iterative and Incremental models fit into the collective definition of “evolutionary life cycle models” (Holt 59). Unlike the incremental model where the needs of the project can change at any time and shift the future iterations and induce stakeholder confusion; the incremental model has some of its strengths in that the conceptual stage isn’t returned too after its complete; all of the needs are finished in the concept stage. In that sense the incremental model is more similar to the linear model than the iterative model is. The incremental model is also nice because it doesn’t require the system to be fully realized for it to be deployed. For example, when I built my PC GPU prices were through the roof due to crypto mining. Thankfully I had a friend who had recently upgraded his rig and so I was able to use his old card. This was the first increment in my PC build and was fully functional even if not fully realized. Unfortunately, due to the AI boom my pc will likely remain unrealized until SSD and RAM prices return to earth if they ever do. The primary drawback of the incremental model is that its not applicable to all systems, as not all systems can be broken down into subsets of the overall picture (Holt 60).

Standards and management processes

The ISO15288 guidelines define a series of standards, categories, and procedures that pertain to systems engineering. The standard includes everything from life cycle model management processes to human resources management processes. It also includes a variety of technical processes that factor into the system life cycle like project planning, decision management, risk management, and information management. Schirn describes how the purpose of the system life cycle is the minimization of project risks so that software meets customer and stakeholder needs. The ISO 15288 also governs the “agreement processes” which govern the way that system elements are acquired. This is especially useful when a system of interest requires an enabling system. This is where the technical management processes come in, they make sure a project stays on track. Configuration management maintains the integrity of the interfaces between system elements and enabling systems. Having defined decision management processes is also critical for key decisions, NASA defines these as Key Decision Points, or (KDP’s). In an iterative or incremental life cycle model there would be milestones where an implementation is ready, or where the iteration process moves forward onto the next stage.

System Life cycle reflection, sustainability, digital system evolution

The NASA systems engineering handbook describes the V-Model. Where the left side represents decomposition and definition and the right side represents integration and validation. This shows how life cycle thinking is about considering the whole of the process from beginning to end. This has transformed the way systems engineers approach sustainability of systems. Instead of sustainability being an afterthought that’s too costly to think about, disposal and retirement is part of the process from the start. This encompasses processes in digital systems like data migration, hardware recycling and surplus. Digital systems must be evolutionary in today’s environment. They can undergo incremental changes while still being a functional product in the utilization stage. Digital environments always have vulnerabilities which necessitate evolution purely on the security side of things. Thinking in terms of a systems life cycle embodies systems thinking in how it incorporates the concepts of systems thinking into the process of systems development itself. It’s also necessary for the evolution of digital systems, the system design life cycle gives structured processes and guidelines that are critical to teams working with complex software and hardware. Having a working model of a system is critical to understanding it, but that model can’t exist without the structured process that gives it life. In conclusion, the system design life cycle is what allows a fully realized robust and sustainable system to take shape and meet the needs of stakeholders and the system itself.

The Philosophy of Human-Centered Design (HCD)

Human-centered design or HCD is a conceptual framework that prioritizes designing systems with human needs from the start of the design process. It considers patterns and limitations of human behavior in order to make systems easier for humans to use and understand. At the core of HCD is empathy; empathy is about understanding what someone else is feeling. This is crucial for designers because they want the software they create to be understood by more than just them. Humans work on lots of mental models or heuristics that may be universal or internal. An application of empathy in the HCD process would be at my job at the service desk where one of my primary responsibilities is to maintain our knowledge base. The knowledge base is important because it is a collection of our internal and external documentation for both information services personnel and users. Users can include faculty, students, or parents of students and so understanding the variety of needs they possess is critical, and without empathy that component of understanding is incomplete. When I first started working on the project, I was reading article feedback and noticed a common theme popping up; users were overwhelmed with technical jargon and inconsistent article structure. One of the first changes I made to our article rework process was to hire a technical writing intern to write our documentation to be as simple and jargon-free as possible. While having IT technicians write articles will give you a high degree of technical accuracy, the heuristics and assumptions that you get from familiarity with the processes of the problems can lead to skipped steps and too much complexity. Having an intern who is not as tech-savvy applies the empathy that a new user who is not familiar with multi-factor authentication or how to map a network drive would need in order to understand a new technical process. Jakob’s Law states that users leverage similar mental models when navigating new sites, which makes it easier for new users to understand. The other change I made to our process is for every user-facing article, we would have a consistent template so it was easier to find what they needed. This template used principles of human perception like visual hierarchy to guide users to the information they need most. Overall, the philosophy of human-centered design is a necessary component in creating effective, efficient, and understandable systems for users of all backgrounds.

ISO 9241-210 vs. IDEO

Both the IDEO field guide and the ISO 9241-210 are focused on the topic of HCD however both take different philosophical approaches. ISO is a more structured and explicit framework with six formal steps in the process of HCD, whereas the IDEO framework is more of mindset to solve complex social problems with creative less structured iterative steps. While both take different approaches to the same idea they both share some similarities. ISO standard criteria follow this series of steps: design is based on an understanding of users their needs and environment, they are involved in the design process, refined through user feedback, iterative, encompasses the whole of the user experience, and the design team is multidisciplinary and has a balance of perspectives (). By contrast, the IDEO field guide is more about the way a designer approaches problem solving. The key tenants of the field guide are to see every problem as solvable, and to embrace ambiguity, failure, and empathy (IDEO, 2015). IDEO also outlines how a HCD process has three overlapping phases: inspiration, ideation, and implementation (SEO, 2025). While both approaches differ in the sense that ISO is more about rigidity and following a set of procedures, and IDEO is about the way that designers think about the problems they seek to solve; these strategies work best when applied harmoniously, using the strengths of both systems to create the most effective HCD.

The Mechanics of Perception and Attention

A core facet of understanding human perception and consciousness is how it is shaped, both by the patterns that apply to humans generally and how their specific experiences dictate how they see the world. One person’s perception will meaningfully differ from another based on their past, present, and future. This does not mean that one person will see a blue book as a different color, unless they are colorblind, in which case they will perceive a different spectrum of colors compared to someone else; but when they see the blue, their perceptual filters will shape how they interact with and understand the blue of the book. Although how do we even know that our colors are the same? You cannot experience someone else’s consciousness and so there really is no way to know. Regardless, our perception is biased by our goals, expectations, and mental models. We may see the same things, but we do not "see" the same things, if that makes sense. However, aside from how our experiences shape our perception, there are common foundational principles that apply to all humans. Our brains attempt to organize what we see even if it is not directly connected; these principles fall into the category called visual hierarchy. Our brain will fill in gaps depending on what we focus on. In Designing with the Mind in Mind, Jeff gives the example of the "LIFE" optical illusion, where a seemingly random collection of buildings in black creates the word "life" in the white space between them, and by focusing on either element your perception changes.

Designers can also influence user design by using Gestalt principles, which are some of the elements that make up visual hierarchy. These principles are some of the common trendlines that psychologists have used to understand human perception. UX designers factor in these principles like proximity to group similar elements in menus. An example of proximity is putting similar formatting options in Word together, like paragraph, styles, and font, and then following the chain up to the home tab, insert tab, and layout. This is similar to taxonomies in information architecture, where you group things in a hierarchical order. On the subject of information architecture, effective architecture, much like UX design, is about reducing the cognitive load of your users. For example, in the Brooks Library, the placements of the bathrooms and water fountains are the same on each floor. For students, this makes it easier for them to navigate and focus on what is important, like the reading or assignments they have to do. Instead of being lost and confused about where to find things, putting essentials in the same spaces makes it easy for users to get where they need to go. In UX, this would be like following similar structures for the main homepage of a webpage; users expect there to be elements in certain locations depending on the mental model of the page they are expecting. Nike and Etsy are both digital marketplaces, and so designers leverage user expectations to make navigation stress-free.

The mechanics of perception are subtle and are not things we usually pay attention to. They are the invisible hand that guides the way we understand the world. But understanding those guidelines is key for the architects of digital environments.

Applying the Laws of UX

I’ve already briefly discussed Jakob’s Law and its role in making UX design easy to navigate for unfamiliar users with a new website. It essentially states that you should leverage the preexisting mental models people already have. It is important to distinguish between sameness and this principle because obviously if every app looked the same, not only would that be boring, but it would also reduce overall functionality. It also does not mean that developers should not iterate and innovate on existing designs simply because they are familiar. There are good and bad examples of this: Spotify makes changes rapidly and changes the way that elements of their software looks. In 2023, they changed the heart-shaped "like" button to a generalized "add to playlist" button, with liked songs becoming essentially another playlist. This change received a lot of backlash from users because it fundamentally changed the experience of using the app and did not give users much of a choice. Compare that to the way that Microsoft has redesigned Outlook, where users are given a choice between “classic” and “new” that can be selected in the same location for both versions.

This is a great application of Jakob’s law and respects user decision making; this is also a great way to integrate stakeholder decision making as a core design choice. Fitt’s Law is focused on how the size and spacing of elements impact the way users interact with them. For example, if an option is larger, a user is on average going to select the element faster. Aside from size, spacing plays a role in directing user attention and helping them make decisions. In the game Polytopia, when you level up a city, you are given a choice between two different options. These choices have strategic implications, and by placing them beside each other, it forces the user to consider which they prefer.

Hick’s Law centers on decision-making time. Have you ever been to the grocery store and been overwhelmed by the sheer amount of different options of what is essentially the same product? Hick's Law focuses on reducing decision-making time by minimizing the number of available choices. By filtering your options down to what is essential, it makes the choice a lot simpler. A place where Hick’s law applies is when playing “the neutral game” in Smash Ultimate; you do not have to be focused on every available option that your opponent has to hit you with, just the three to four most likely options in the given context. Smash requires a lot of quick decision making, and being overwhelmed by the options that can be thrown out is very easy to fall into. Today at my tournament I am going to attempt to use Hick’s law to make quicker and smarter decisions. Miller’s law is about the limits of our working memory. Miller states that it is around seven items plus or minus two. This is important because of what we have already stated before about being overwhelmed by options; designers can keep this law in mind as a general, but not hard and fast, rule. Seven may even be a high-end estimate, with newer research postulating that it is closer to about four. While there is a numerical limit to the amount of information we can actively keep track of at once, there are ways to augment this. Chunking is a strategy where you section more information into small groups. If you were given this series of numbers, 5099632001, and told to memorize it, it would likely be fairly challenging. Format it as a phone number, (509)-963-2001, and it becomes almost effortless. UI designers can chunk segments of a webpage together to guide user attention and not overwhelm them with the lack of structure that our brain uses to understand the world around us. Wireframes are an early stage of designing a front-end, and it utilizes drawing boxes where you want to put different elements. Aside from chunking, semantic encoding is another tool that can aid in making permanent memories. Assigning meaning to something bridges more connections to other ideas, which is more deeply rooted in your brain. In total, the laws of UX give designers a toolkit and checklist of different items to hit when creating effective HCD.
