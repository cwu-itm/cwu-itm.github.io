What is Systems Thinking

Systems thinking is a way of understanding complex systems by looking at the relationships, interactions, and patterns between components rather than analyzing them in isolation. Unlike traditional linear thinking, which moves step-by-step from cause to effect, systems thinking emphasizes feedback loops, emergent behaviors, and interconnectedness (Blanchard, 2019).

Why Linear Thinking Falls Short

Linear thinking assumes that problems have straightforward causes and solutions. In complex digital systems, this approach often fails because it ignores interactions between subsystems, hidden dependencies, and emergent properties. Systems thinking, on the other hand, helps designers anticipate unintended consequences and design more resilient solutions (Johnson, 2020)

The Role of Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE)

Model-based systems engineering (MBSE) applies systems thinking principles through formalized models. Using MBSE, engineers can simulate, analyze, and document complex systems before implementation. This approach improves communication, reduces errors, and supports iterative design in digital environments (Blanchard, 2019; Johnson, 2020).

Practical Applications in Digital Design

Systems thinking and MBSE play important roles in modern digital environments such as UX design, cloud systems, cybersecurity, and software architecture. By analyzing relationships and feedback loops, designers can prevent issues early, optimize performance, and ensure scalability (Yablonski, 2021; Sherugar & Budiu, 2016).

Key Takeaways

  • Systems thinking focuses on interconnections rather than isolated components.
  • Linear thinking often misses hidden dependencies in digital systems.
  • MBSE uses models to visualize, simulate, and refine complex system designs.

Understanding the Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC)

The systems development life cycle (SDLC) is a structured process used to plan, design, build, test, and maintain systems over time. Instead of treating system development as a single task, SDLC breaks it into organized phases that help engineers manage complexity and reduce risk. These phases ensure that systems are not only functional but also reliable, scalable, and aligned with user needs (INCOSE, 2023).

SDLC is important because most modern digital systems are too complex to be built without structure. Without a life cycle approach, teams can easily miss requirements, skip testing, or build systems that are different to maintain later.

The Linear Life Cycle Model

The linear model is one of the simplest SDLC approaches. It follows a step-by-step process where each phrase must be completed before moving to the next. This includes requirements gathering, system design, implementation, testing, deployment, and maintenance.

One advantage of the linear model is clarity. Everything is planned in advance, which makes it easier to estimate cost and timelines. This makes it useful in projects where requirements are stable and unlikely to change.

However, the biggest drawback is its lack of flexibility. Once a phase is completed, going back to changes is difficult and expensive. This is a major issue is modern digital systems where requirements often change during development (Sommerville, 2016).

The Iterative Life Cycle Model

The interative model improves on the linear approach by developing systems in repeated cycles. Instead of building everything at once, developers create an early version of the system and then improve it over multiple iterations based on feedback.

This model is useful because it allows teams to discover problems early and adjust the system before too much time is spent in the wrong direction. It also reflects how real users interact with systems, since feedback often changes what is needed.

A key advantage of the iterative model is flexibility. It supports continuous improvement and reduces the risk of major system failure. However, it can lead to scope creep if there there is no clear endpoint or project control (NASA, 2021).

The Incremental Life Cycle Model

The incremetal model builds systems in smaller parts called increments. Instead of delivering the full system at once, each increment delivers a working piece of functionality.

One advantage of this approach is that users can start using parts of the system earlier. This provides early value and allows feedback to shape future increments.

Another benefit is risk reduction. Since the system is built in pieces, problems can be isolated more easily. However, the challenge is ensuring that all increments integrate properly into a complete system at the end.

Why Life Cycle Thinking Matters

Thinking in terms of a system life cycle is important because systems are not static. They evolve over time, requires updates and eventually need to be replaced. A life cycle approach helps engineers design systems that can adapt and remain useful over long periods.

It also improves sustainability and long-term planning. Instead of focusing only on initial development, engineers consider maintenance, upgrades, and scalability from the beginning. This reduces technical debt and prevents systems from becoming outdated too quickly.

From my perspective, the most important idea is that no single life cycle model works for everything. The linear model works best when requirements are stable, the iterative model works best when feedback is important, and the incremental model works best when systems need to be delivered in parts. Most real systems actually use a mix of all three approaches depending on the situation.

Overall, SDLC is not just a development process-it is a way of thinking about how systems evolve over time.

Module 2.1 Blog Post: Designing for People

Introduction: What It Means to Design for People

Designing for people means more than making something visually appealing or functional-it means understanding how humans perceive, think, and behave in real-world contexts. Human-centered design (HCD) is an approach that prioritizes users at every stage of the design process, ensuring that systems are usable, meaningful, and aligned with human needs.

Rather than starting with technology or business goals, HCD begins with empathy. Designers must understand users' environments, limitations, motivations, and mental models. This shift is critical because even powerful systems fail if they do not align with how people naturally interact with information.

According to ISO 9241-210, human-centered design is defied as an iterative process that involves understanding users, specifying requirements, designing solutions, and evaluating outcomes continuously throughout development (ISO, 2019).

ISO 9241-210 vs. IDEO: Two Perspectives on Human-Centered Design

ISO 9241-210 provides a structured, process-driven framework for HCD. It emphasizes formal steps, documentation, and repeatable methods. This makes it especially useful in engineering and enterprise environments where reliability and consistency are essential. The ISO model ensures that user needs are identified early and validated through evaluation cycles (ISO, 2019).

In contrast, IDEO's Field Guide to Human-Centered Design takes a more flexible and creative approach. Instead of strict phases, IDEO emphasizes empathy, field research, and rapid prototyping. Designers are encouraged to observe users directly, ask open-ended questions, and iterate quickly based on real-world feedback (IDEO.org, 2015).

They key difference is structure versus exploration. ISO focuses on controlled processes, while IDEO focuses on human insight and adaptability. In practice, strong ux design often combines both: ISO ensures systems are reliable, while IDEO ensures they are meaningful and user-informed.

Perception and Visual Hierarchy in Design

Human perception plays a major role in how users interact with digital systems. People do not process interfaces linearly; instead, they scan for patterns, contrast, and familiar structures. This makes visual hierarchy one of the most important tools in UX design.

For example, users naturally focus on larger elements, bold text, and high-contrast buttons. If important information is not visually distinct, it may be ignored entirely. This is not a user failure—it is a design failure.

Research in cognitive psychology shows that attention is selective and limited. Users filter information based on relevance and expectation, meaning designers must reduce unnecessary cognitive load (Ware, 2013). A well-structured interface guides attention automatically without requiring instructions.

Jakob's Law: Designing for Familiarity

Jakob's Law states that users prefer interfaces that work like the ones they already know. This means consistency with existing design patterns reduces learning effort and improves usability (Nielsen Norman Group, 2020).

When interfaces behave unpredictably, users must pause and relearn interactions. This increases cognitive load and frustration. Familiar navigation structures, iconography, and layouts help users transfer prior knowledge into new systems, making interaction more intuitive.

Fitts's Law: Speed and Physical Interaction

Fitts's Law explains how long it takes to move to a target based on its size and distance. In UX design, this directly affects how quickly users can interact with buttons and controls.

Larger targets that are closer to the user are faster and easier to interact with. This is why mobile apps place key actions near the thumb zone and use large buttons for primary tasks (Fitts, 1954).

Hick's Law: Reducing Decision Overload

Hick's Law states that the more choices a user has, the longer it takes to make a decision. This is due to increased cognitive processing time when evaluating multiple options (Hick, 1952).

In UX design, reducing unnecessary choices improves usability. For example, simplifying navigation menus or breaking complex tasks into smaller steps helps users make decisions faster and with less mental effort.

Too many options at once can overwhelm users, leading to hesitation or abandonment of tasks.

Miller's Law: Limits of Working Memory

Miller's Law suggests that humans can only hold about 7 ± 2 items in working memory at once (Miller, 1956). This has direct implications for interface design and information architecture.

When too much information is presented at once, users become overwhelmed and struggle to retain details. Designers address this by grouping related items into chunks, simplifying forms, and breaking content into digestible sections.

Connecting Perception, Empathy, and Design Decisions

When combined, perception principles and human-centered design create interfaces that feel intuitive. Designers who understand how users perceive information can guide attention, reduce confusion, and create smoother interactions.

Empathy ensures designers are solving real user problems, while perception principles ensure solutions are presented in ways humans can naturally understand. Together, they bridge the gap between intention and interaction.

Conclusion: Designing for Humans, Not Systems

Human-centered design reminds us that successful systems are built around human behavior, not technical capability. ISO 9241-210 provides structure for this process, while IDEO provides creativity and empathy. UX laws like Jakob's, Fitts's, Hick's, and Miller's explain how humans interact with systems on a cognitive level.

The most effective digital experiences are not the most complex—they are the most intuitive. Good design reduces friction, supports perception, and respects cognitive limits.

Ultimately, designing for people means designing with an understanding of how people actually think, see, and decide.

Introduction: Supporting User Thinking in Design

Interface design is not just about aesthetics—it is about how systems support human thinking. Every interaction requires users to perceive information, interpret meaning, and make decisions under cognitive constraints. According to Norman (2013), good design reduces the need for users to rely heavily on memory by making actions visible and intuitive.

Cognitive Load and Human Limits

Cognitive load refers to the mental effort required to complete a task. Sweller (1988) explains that working memory has limited capacity, meaning users can easily become overwhelmed when too much information is presented at once. Designers reduce this burden by simplifying layouts, grouping content, and minimizing unnecessary steps.

Mental Models and User Expectations

Users rely on mental models—assumptions formed from past experiences—to navigate systems. When interfaces align with these expectations, interaction feels natural. When they do not, users must stop and rethink their approach. Norman (2013) emphasizes that aligning design with mental models improves usability and reduces cognitive strain.

Consistency and Recognition

Consistency reduces cognitive effort by allowing users to apply learned behaviors across different parts of a system. Nielsen Norman Group (n.d.) highlights that recognition is easier than recall, meaning users should be able to identify options rather than remember them. Consistent layouts, icons, and navigation structures support this principle.

Recognition is closely related but even more powerful than memory recall. Nielsen Norman Group (n.d.) explains that users perform better when they can recognize options visually rather than remembering steps or commands from memory. For example, a navigation bar with clearly labeled icons is much easier to use than a system where users must remember hidden gestures or menu locations.

A good real-world example is social media platforms. Apps like Instagram or TikTok maintain consistent interaction patterns such as double-tap to like or swipe gestures for navigation. Because these actions remain consistent, users build muscle memory and can navigate without conscious effort. If these patterns suddenly changed, even slightly, users would feel confused and frustrated because their learned expectations would no longer apply.

However, inconsistency is still common in many digital systems. Some websites place buttons in different locations depending on the page, or use different icons for the same action. This forces users to stop and reprocess the interface each time, increasing cognitive effort and slowing down task completion. Over time, this can lead to frustration and even abandonment of the system entirely.

Feedback and User Confidence

Feedback helps users understand the outcome of their actions. Without it, users experience uncertainty and may repeat actions unnecessarily. The W3C (2023) states that clear system feedback is essential for usability and accessibility, ensuring users always understand system status.

Critical Reflection: Where Design Fails

Although many systems aim to reduce cognitive load, some fail by introducing unnecessary complexity through inconsistent navigation, excessive notifications, or hidden controls. These issues force users to spend more mental effort than necessary, reducing efficiency and satisfaction.

Conclusion

Effective interface design respects human cognitive limitations by reducing memory demands, supporting recognition, maintaining consistency, and providing clear feedback. When these principles are applied correctly, users can focus on their goals rather than the system itself.

Designing for Flow: How Interface Design Shapes User Experience

Most people use websites and apps every single day without really thinking about how much the design affects their experience. I never paid much attention to interface design before this class either, but after learning more about UX principles, I started noticing how certain layouts make things easier while others make them frustrating. Sometimes a website feels smooth and simple to use, and other times it feels confusing even when the task itself should be easy.

Interface design matters because users depend on visual cues to figure out what they should do next. The placement of buttons, spacing between sections, colors, headings, and feedback all influence how people move through a website. When these elements are organized well, users can complete tasks faster and with less frustration. When the design is cluttered or inconsistent, people waste time searching for information or trying to understand how the interface works.

UX laws help explain why some interfaces feel natural while others feel stressful. The Aesthetic-Usability Effect, Von Restorff Effect, and Postel’s Law all connect to how users notice information, trust websites, and recover from mistakes. These ideas also connect to concepts discussed in Designing with the Mind in Mind and Laws of UX, because both focus on how real people interact with technology.

Visual Hierarchy and User Attention

Visual hierarchy is important because it controls what users notice first on a page. Designers use things like size, spacing, contrast, and color to guide attention. For example, larger headings usually stand out before regular text, and bright buttons naturally pull attention toward actions the user needs to take. If a page has poor hierarchy, users can feel overwhelmed because everything competes for attention at the same time.

The Von Restorff Effect explains that things that stand out are more likely to be remembered (Lidwell, Holden, & Butler, 2010). In interface design, this means important buttons or messages should look different from everything else around them. A submit button should not blend into the page because users might miss it completely. Designers often use contrast and spacing to help users recognize what actions are most important.

I have definitely noticed this problem on some school websites. There are times when pages are packed with announcements, tabs, links, and buttons all in one place. When everything looks the same, it becomes harder to find assignments or important due dates quickly. I end up clicking around more than I should just trying to figure out where something is. A cleaner layout with better spacing would make those pages easier to use.

Johnson (2020) explains that users rely heavily on patterns and recognition while using interfaces. This makes sense because most people expect websites to follow similar structures. Users expect menus to be easy to find and buttons to clearly look clickable. When a website follows familiar patterns, users spend less time trying to understand the interface and more time actually completing their task.

The Aesthetic-Usability Effect

The Aesthetic-Usability Effect explains that users often believe attractive interfaces are easier to use (Lidwell et al., 2010). Even if two websites function the same way, people usually feel more comfortable using the one that looks cleaner and more organized. Appearance affects trust, confidence, and overall experience.

I notice this most when shopping online. If a website looks modern and organized, I usually feel more comfortable browsing products and entering payment information. If the page feels cluttered or outdated, I start second guessing whether I trust the website at all. Even if the products are good, the overall design still affects how safe and easy the experience feels.

The Interaction Design Foundation (2024) explains that UX laws are based on patterns in human behavior. This matters because users react emotionally to design. A page with clean spacing and readable text feels calmer and easier to use. A crowded interface filled with pop ups or confusing navigation can feel stressful very quickly.

At the same time, appearance alone is not enough. Some websites look modern but still have confusing menus or unclear controls. A page can look visually impressive but still frustrate users if they cannot figure out how to complete simple tasks. Good interface design needs both usability and appearance working together.

Postel’s Law and Flexible Interfaces

Postel’s Law is about making systems flexible with user input while still giving clear output and feedback. In UX design, this means websites should help users recover from mistakes instead of making the experience harder. Users type things wrong, forget information, and misunderstand instructions all the time. Good interfaces expect this and try to guide users instead of punishing them.

Search engines are a good example because they usually understand what users mean even with spelling mistakes. Online forms can also follow Postel’s Law by accepting different phone number or date formats. A strict interface that constantly rejects information without explanation can make users frustrated very quickly.

I have experienced this while filling out online job applications. Some websites require passwords or phone numbers in extremely specific formats but do not explain the rules clearly. If the website only says “invalid,” it forces users to guess what they did wrong. That becomes annoying really fast, especially when trying to finish applications quickly. Clear feedback would make the process much easier.

Feedback is another important part of interface logic. Users need confirmation that the system is responding to their actions. Buttons changing color, loading messages, and confirmation screens all help reassure users that something worked. Sherugar and Budiu (2016) explain that direct manipulation improves usability because users can immediately see the results of their actions.

A Personal Experience with Interface Logic

One thing I have realized is that interface logic can completely change how stressful or easy a task feels. I have used websites where everything flowed naturally and I barely had to think about what to do next. I have also used websites where finding a single button felt harder than the actual assignment or application itself.

One positive example is online checkout pages that break the process into clear steps. Usually they separate the process into shipping, payment, and review. This makes the experience feel more organized because users always know what step they are on. The website also gives feedback after each section is completed, which makes the process feel smoother.

On the other hand, I have had frustrating experiences with school portals that put too much information on one screen. Sometimes I spend more time trying to locate the assignment than actually completing it. This usually happens when pages have weak visual hierarchy or poor organization. Instead of guiding users, the interface makes them search through everything manually.

Good interface design should reduce stress, not create more of it. When layouts are organized and controls are easy to understand, users feel more confident and in control. Strong design helps users focus on their goals instead of struggling with the interface itself.

Why Designing for Flow Matters

Designing for flow means thinking carefully about how users move through an interface from beginning to end. Every design decision affects the experience in some way. Layout affects where users look first. Buttons and controls affect how users interact with the system. Feedback affects whether users feel confident that something worked correctly.

Accessibility also matters because interfaces should be usable for different people and different devices. Responsive images, readable text, and clear spacing all help improve usability. If a website only works well on one type of screen or is difficult to read, the experience becomes frustrating for many users.

Overall, learning about UX laws changed the way I think about websites and apps. Before this class, I mostly focused on whether something looked good visually. Now I understand that design also affects behavior, trust, and usability. The Aesthetic-Usability Effect explains why appearance matters, the Von Restorff Effect explains why important information needs to stand out, and Postel’s Law explains why interfaces should be flexible and forgiving.

Good interface design is really about helping people complete tasks with less confusion and frustration. When users can move through a website naturally and confidently, the entire experience feels better. That is why designing for flow matters so much in digital environments today.

Interaction That Matters: How Timing, Feedback, and Ethical Design Shape User Experience

Before taking this course, I mostly judged websites and apps based on how they looked. If a website had a clean design, nice colors, and modern graphics, I usually assumed it was well designed. However, after learning more about user experience, I have realized that appearance is only one part of the overall experience. What really shapes a user's opinion is how the system responds to them, how quickly feedback is provided, and how easily they can complete tasks without frustration.

Every interaction between a user and a digital system creates an impression. Whether someone is shopping online, submitting homework, applying for a job, or using social media, they are constantly receiving feedback from the interface. Small details such as loading indicators, animations, confirmation messages, and error prevention techniques can have a major impact on how users feel during and after an interaction. Good interaction design helps users feel confident and in control, while poor interaction design often creates confusion and frustration.

Several UX laws help explain why some digital experiences feel natural while others feel difficult. The Peak-End Rule, Doherty Threshold, Jakob's Law, and Tesler's Law all describe different aspects of human behavior and how people interact with technology. These concepts connect closely to ideas discussed in Designing with the Mind in Mind by Johnson (2020) and Laws of UX by Yablonski (2024). Together, these principles show that effective design is not just about creating attractive interfaces. It is about understanding how people think, behave, and remember experiences.

Feedback Timing and User Confidence

One of the most important parts of interaction design is feedback. Users need to know that the system is responding to their actions. When feedback is immediate, users feel confident that their action was successful. When feedback is delayed or missing, uncertainty begins to develop. Even simple actions like clicking a button can become frustrating if users are unsure whether the system registered their input.

I notice this often when submitting assignments online. If Canvas immediately displays a confirmation message after I upload an assignment, I feel confident that my work was submitted correctly. However, if the page takes a long time to load without displaying any feedback, I start wondering whether something went wrong. Sometimes I feel tempted to click the submit button again because I am unsure whether the first click worked. This shows how important feedback is for reducing user anxiety.

The Doherty Threshold states that productivity increases when systems respond quickly enough to keep users engaged (Yablonski, 2024). Although users understand that some processes require time, they still expect some indication that progress is being made. Loading bars, progress indicators, and status messages can make waiting feel shorter because users understand what is happening. Without these indicators, users are left guessing, which often creates frustration.

Good feedback also helps prevent mistakes. For example, online forms that immediately identify missing information allow users to fix errors before submitting. This approach is much more helpful than waiting until the end of the process to display a list of problems. Clear and timely feedback reduces confusion and helps users complete tasks more efficiently.

Motion and Responsiveness in User Experience

Motion is another important element of interaction design. Animations, transitions, and movement can help users understand how a system works. For example, a navigation menu that smoothly slides open provides a visual cue that something has changed. Likewise, a button animation can reassure users that their click was recognized.

However, motion should always have a purpose. Too much movement can become distracting and make it harder for users to focus on their goals. I have visited shopping websites where pop-ups, advertisements, and animated banners compete for attention at the same time. Instead of making the experience more engaging, the excessive motion makes the website feel overwhelming.

Johnson (2020) explains that people have limited attention and working memory. Because of these limitations, designers should use motion carefully. Animations should guide users toward important information or provide useful feedback rather than simply adding visual effects. When motion supports the user’s task, it improves usability. When it exists only for decoration, it can become a distraction.

Responsiveness is closely connected to motion because it reflects how quickly a system reacts to user input. Fast and responsive interfaces create a feeling of control. Slow interfaces can make users feel disconnected from the system. Even a small delay can affect how users perceive the overall experience.

Nielsen (1993) explains that users perceive delays differently depending on response time, which is why fast feedback is so important in digital systems.

The Peak-End Rule and Emotional Memory

The Peak-End Rule suggests that people judge experiences largely based on the most intense moment and the ending rather than remembering every detail equally (Yablonski, 2024). This concept is especially important in UX design because users often remember how a digital interaction made them feel more than the specific actions they performed.

For example, a website may function well throughout most of the experience, but if it crashes at the final step, users are likely to remember the interaction negatively. On the other hand, if a process includes a stressful moment but ends with clear feedback and a successful outcome, users may remember it more positively.

I often think about this when submitting assignments online. The final confirmation message is important because it reassures me that everything was completed successfully. That ending leaves me feeling confident rather than worried. Without a clear confirmation, I might continue thinking about whether my work was actually submitted.

Emotional memory plays a major role in how users evaluate products and services. Designers who understand the Peak-End Rule can create experiences that leave users with positive lasting impressions. Strong endings, helpful feedback, and successful task completion all contribute to a better overall experience.

Error Prevention and Tesler's Law

One of the most effective ways to improve user experience is by preventing errors before they happen. Users make mistakes for many reasons. They may be distracted, tired, in a hurry, or simply unfamiliar with the system. Good design anticipates these situations and provides support that helps users avoid problems.

Tesler's Law states that every system contains a certain amount of complexity that cannot be eliminated completely (Yablonski, 2024). Designers must decide whether that complexity will be handled by the system or by the user. The best experiences reduce unnecessary effort for users by simplifying processes whenever possible.

A good example is an online form. Instead of expecting users to remember the correct format for dates, phone numbers, or passwords, the system can provide examples, automatic formatting, or real-time validation. These features help users complete tasks successfully without needing to guess what is expected.

I have experienced situations where job applications required information in a very specific format but provided little guidance. If the system rejected an entry, it often displayed a vague error message that did not explain what needed to be fixed. As a result, I spent extra time trying different formats until I found the correct one. Better design could have prevented that frustration entirely.

Error prevention is often more effective than error correction because it reduces interruptions and helps users maintain their focus. When users encounter fewer obstacles, they are more likely to complete tasks successfully and feel positive about the experience.

Jakob's Law and Familiar Design Patterns

Jakob's Law states that users spend most of their time on other websites, so they expect new websites to work similarly to the ones they already know (Yablonski, 2024). This principle explains why familiar layouts and navigation structures are often beneficial. Users do not want to learn a completely new system every time they visit a website.

For example, most people expect navigation menus near the top of a page, logos that return users to the homepage, and shopping carts located in the upper corner of an online store. These patterns have become familiar through repeated exposure across thousands of websites.

I have noticed that school websites sometimes become difficult to use when they organize information differently from what students expect. If assignments, grades, announcements, and modules are located in unusual places, students spend more time searching for information instead of focusing on their coursework. Consistency helps users accomplish tasks more efficiently because they already understand how similar systems work.

This does not mean every website should look identical. Creativity and branding are still important. However, creative decisions should not interfere with usability. Designers can create unique visual experiences while still respecting the expectations users bring from other digital environments.

Ethical Design and Persuasive Technology

Designers have a responsibility to consider the ethical impact of their decisions. Many digital products use persuasive techniques to influence user behavior. In some situations, persuasion can be helpful. Progress indicators encourage users to finish tasks, reminders help people stay organized, and warning messages can prevent costly mistakes.

However, persuasive techniques become problematic when they are used to manipulate users rather than support them. Some websites intentionally make it difficult to cancel subscriptions, hide privacy settings, or pressure users into making purchases. These practices are often called deceptive patterns because they prioritize business goals over user interests.

I have personally encountered websites that made signing up for services extremely easy but made cancellation difficult to find. This type of design creates frustration because it feels dishonest. Instead of helping users make informed decisions, the design attempts to push them toward a specific outcome.

Ethical design should be transparent and respectful. Users should understand what is happening, why it is happening, and what choices they have available. Trust is one of the most valuable aspects of user experience. When users feel manipulated, that trust can disappear quickly.

How These Ideas Changed the Way I View Technology

One of the biggest things I have learned from studying user experience is that design decisions are rarely accidental. Before this course, I mostly focused on visual appearance. If a website looked professional, I assumed it was designed well. Now I pay much more attention to how systems respond to users and how those responses influence behavior. I notice when websites provide clear feedback, when navigation feels intuitive, and when certain features are designed to encourage specific actions.

I have also become more aware of how different digital experiences affect my emotions. When a website is responsive and easy to use, I feel more confident and less stressed. When a website is confusing or slow, even simple tasks become frustrating. This has helped me understand why user-centered design is so important. The goal is not simply to create attractive interfaces but to create experiences that help users accomplish their goals efficiently and comfortably.

Learning about UX laws has changed how I evaluate websites, apps, and online services. I now recognize examples of the Peak-End Rule, Jakob's Law, and the Doherty Threshold in many of the technologies I use every day. These concepts have given me a better understanding of the relationship between human behavior and design. As technology continues to evolve, I believe ethical and user-focused design will become even more important because people increasingly rely on digital systems in both their personal and professional lives.

Conclusion

Interaction design affects much more than how a website looks. Timing, responsiveness, motion, error prevention, and ethical decision-making all influence how users feel during and after an interaction. Small design choices can create confidence, reduce frustration, and improve overall satisfaction.

The UX laws discussed in this blog help explain why these experiences matter. The Doherty Threshold highlights the importance of responsiveness, the Peak-End Rule explains emotional memory, Tesler's Law addresses complexity, and Jakob's Law focuses on familiarity. Together, these principles show that successful design requires an understanding of human behavior as much as technical skill.

After studying these concepts, I pay much more attention to the interactions I experience every day. I notice when websites provide helpful feedback, when systems prevent mistakes, and when designs feel ethical and trustworthy. Good interaction design creates experiences that support users rather than frustrate them. That is what makes interaction truly matter.
