Module 1.1 Blog Post

What is Systems Thinking

Systems thinking is a way of understanding complex systems by looking at the relationships, interactions, and patterns between components rather than analyzing them in isolation. Unlike traditional linear thinking, which moves step-by-step from cause to effect, systems thinking emphasizes feedback loops, emergent behaviors, and interconnectedness (Blanchard, 2019).

Why Linear Thinking Falls Short

Linear thinking assumes that problems have straightforward causes and solutions. In complex digital systems, this approach often fails because it ignores interactions between subsystems, hidden dependencies, and emergent properties. Systems thinking, on the other hand, helps designers anticipate unintended consequences and design more resilient solutions (Johnson, 2020)

The Role of Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE)

Model-based systems engineering (MBSE) applies systems thinking principles through formalized models. Using MBSE, engineers can simulate, analyze, and document complex systems before implementation. This approach improves communication, reduces errors, and supports iterative design in digital environments (Blanchard, 2019; Johnson, 2020).

Practical Applications in Digital Design

Systems thinking and MBSE play important roles in modern digital environments such as UX design, cloud systems, cybersecurity, and software architecture. By analyzing relationships and feedback loops, designers can prevent issues early, optimize performance, and ensure scalability (Yablonski, 2021; Sherugar & Budiu, 2016).

Key Takeaways

  • Systems thinking focuses on interconnections rather than isolated components.
  • Linear thinking often misses hidden dependencies in digital systems.
  • MBSE uses models to visualize, simulate, and refine complex system designs.

Module 1.2 Blog Post

Understanding the Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC)

The systems development life cycle (SDLC) is a structured process used to plan, design, build, test, and maintain systems over time. Instead of treating system development as a single task, SDLC breaks it into organized phases that help engineers manage complexity and reduce risk. These phases ensure that systems are not only functional but also reliable, scalable, and aligned with user needs (INCOSE, 2023).

SDLC is important because most modern digital systems are too complex to be built without structure. Without a life cycle approach, teams can easily miss requirements, skip testing, or build systems that are different to maintain later.

The Linear Life Cycle Model

The linear model is one of the simplest SDLC approaches. It follows a step-by-step process where each phrase must be completed before moving to the next. This includes requirements gathering, system design, implementation, testing, deployment, and maintenance.

One advantage of the linear model is clarity. Everything is planned in advance, which makes it easier to estimate cost and timelines. This makes it useful in projects where requirements are stable and unlikely to change.

However, the biggest drawback is its lack of flexibility. Once a phase is completed, going back to changes is difficult and expensive. This is a major issue is modern digital systems where requirements often change during development (Sommerville, 2016).

The Iterative Life Cycle Model

The interative model improves on the linear approach by developing systems in repeated cycles. Instead of building everything at once, developers create an early version of the system and then improve it over multiple iterations based on feedback.

This model is useful because it allows teams to discover problems early and adjust the system before too much time is spent in the wrong direction. It also reflects how real users interact with systems, since feedback often changes what is needed.

A key advantage of the iterative model is flexibility. It supports continuous improvement and reduces the risk of major system failure. However, it can lead to scope creep if there there is no clear endpoint or project control (NASA, 2021).

The Incremental Life Cycle Model

The incremetal model builds systems in smaller parts called increments. Instead of delivering the full system at once, each increment delivers a working piece of functionality.

One advantage of this approach is that users can start using parts of the system earlier. This provides early value and allows feedback to shape future increments.

Another benefit is risk reduction. Since the system is built in pieces, problems can be isolated more easily. However, the challenge is ensuring that all increments integrate properly into a complete system at the end.

Why Life Cycle Thinking Matters

Thinking in terms of a system life cycle is important because systems are not static. They evolve over time, requires updates and eventually need to be replaced. A life cycle approach helps engineers design systems that can adapt and remain useful over long periods.

It also improves sustainability and long-term planning. Instead of focusing only on initial development, engineers consider maintenance, upgrades, and scalability from the beginning. This reduces technical debt and prevents systems from becoming outdated too quickly.

From my perspective, the most important idea is that no single life cycle model works for everything. The linear model works best when requirements are stable, the iterative model works best when feedback is important, and the incremental model works best when systems need to be delivered in parts. Most real systems actually use a mix of all three approaches depending on the situation.

Overall, SDLC is not just a development process-it is a way of thinking about how systems evolve over time.

Module 2.1 Blog Post: Designing for People

Introduction: What It Means to Design for People

Designing for people means more than making something visually appealing or functional-it means understanding how humans perceive, think, and behave in real-world contexts. Human-centered design (HCD) is an approach that prioritizes users at every stage of the design process, ensuring that systems are usable, meaningful, and aligned with human needs.

Rather than starting with technology or business goals, HCD begins with empathy. Designers must understand users' environments, limitations, motivations, and mental models. This shift is critical because even powerful systems fail if they do not align with how people naturally interact with information.

According to ISO 9241-210, human-centered design is defied as an iterative process that involves understanding users, specifying requirements, designing solutions, and evaluating outcomes continuously throughout development (ISO, 2019).

ISO 9241-210 vs. IDEO: Two Perspectives on Human-Centered Design

ISO 9241-210 provides a structured, process-driven framework for HCD. It emphasizes formal steps, documentation, and repeatable methods. This makes it especially useful in engineering and enterprise environments where reliability and consistency are essential. The ISO model ensures that user needs are identified early and validated through evaluation cycles (ISO, 2019).

In contrast, IDEO's Field Guide to Human-Centered Design takes a more flexible and creative approach. Instead of strict phases, IDEO emphasizes empathy, field research, and rapid prototyping. Designers are encouraged to observe users directly, ask open-ended questions, and iterate quickly based on real-world feedback (IDEO.org, 2015).

They key difference is structure versus exploration. ISO focuses on controlled processes, while IDEO focuses on human insight and adaptability. In practice, strong ux design often combines both: ISO ensures systems are reliable, while IDEO ensures they are meaningful and user-informed.

Perception and Visual Hierarchy in Design

Human perception plays a major role in how users interact with digital systems. People do not process interfaces linearly; instead, they scan for patterns, contrast, and familiar structures. This makes visual hierarchy one of the most important tools in UX design.

For example, users naturally focus on larger elements, bold text, and high-contrast buttons. If important information is not visually distinct, it may be ignored entirely. This is not a user failure—it is a design failure.

Research in cognitive psychology shows that attention is selective and limited. Users filter information based on relevance and expectation, meaning designers must reduce unnecessary cognitive load (Ware, 2013). A well-structured interface guides attention automatically without requiring instructions.

Jakob's Law: Designing for Familiarity

Jakob's Law states that users prefer interfaces that work like the ones they already know. This means consistency with existing design patterns reduces learning effort and improves usability (Nielsen Norman Group, 2020).

When interfaces behave unpredictably, users must pause and relearn interactions. This increases cognitive load and frustration. Familiar navigation structures, iconography, and layouts help users transfer prior knowledge into new systems, making interaction more intuitive.

Fitts's Law: Speed and Physical Interaction

Fitts's Law explains how long it takes to move to a target based on its size and distance. In UX design, this directly affects how quickly users can interact with buttons and controls.

Larger targets that are closer to the user are faster and easier to interact with. This is why mobile apps place key actions near the thumb zone and use large buttons for primary tasks (Fitts, 1954).

Hick's Law: Reducing Decision Overload

Hick's Law states that the more choices a user has, the longer it takes to make a decision. This is due to increased cognitive processing time when evaluating multiple options (Hick, 1952).

In UX design, reducing unnecessary choices improves usability. For example, simplifying navigation menus or breaking complex tasks into smaller steps helps users make decisions faster and with less mental effort.

Too many options at once can overwhelm users, leading to hesitation or abandonment of tasks.

Miller's Law: Limits of Working Memory

Miller's Law suggests that humans can only hold about 7 ± 2 items in working memory at once (Miller, 1956). This has direct implications for interface design and information architecture.

When too much information is presented at once, users become overwhelmed and struggle to retain details. Designers address this by grouping related items into chunks, simplifying forms, and breaking content into digestible sections.

Connecting Perception, Empathy, and Design Decisions

When combined, perception principles and human-centered design create interfaces that feel intuitive. Designers who understand how users perceive information can guide attention, reduce confusion, and create smoother interactions.

Empathy ensures designers are solving real user problems, while perception principles ensure solutions are presented in ways humans can naturally understand. Together, they bridge the gap between intention and interaction.

Conclusion: Designing for Humans, Not Systems

Human-centered design reminds us that successful systems are built around human behavior, not technical capability. ISO 9241-210 provides structure for this process, while IDEO provides creativity and empathy. UX laws like Jakob's, Fitts's, Hick's, and Miller's explain how humans interact with systems on a cognitive level.

The most effective digital experiences are not the most complex—they are the most intuitive. Good design reduces friction, supports perception, and respects cognitive limits.

Ultimately, designing for people means designing with an understanding of how people actually think, see, and decide.

Module 2.2 Blog Post

Introduction: Supporting User Thinking in Design

Interface design is not just about aesthetics—it is about how systems support human thinking. Every interaction requires users to perceive information, interpret meaning, and make decisions under cognitive constraints. According to Norman (2013), good design reduces the need for users to rely heavily on memory by making actions visible and intuitive.

Cognitive Load and Human Limits

Cognitive load refers to the mental effort required to complete a task. Sweller (1988) explains that working memory has limited capacity, meaning users can easily become overwhelmed when too much information is presented at once. Designers reduce this burden by simplifying layouts, grouping content, and minimizing unnecessary steps.

Mental Models and User Expectations

Users rely on mental models—assumptions formed from past experiences—to navigate systems. When interfaces align with these expectations, interaction feels natural. When they do not, users must stop and rethink their approach. Norman (2013) emphasizes that aligning design with mental models improves usability and reduces cognitive strain.

Consistency and Recognition

Consistency reduces cognitive effort by allowing users to apply learned behaviors across different parts of a system. Nielsen Norman Group (n.d.) highlights that recognition is easier than recall, meaning users should be able to identify options rather than remember them. Consistent layouts, icons, and navigation structures support this principle.

Recognition is closely related but even more powerful than memory recall. Nielsen Norman Group (n.d.) explains that users perform better when they can recognize options visually rather than remembering steps or commands from memory. For example, a navigation bar with clearly labeled icons is much easier to use than a system where users must remember hidden gestures or menu locations.

A good real-world example is social media platforms. Apps like Instagram or TikTok maintain consistent interaction patterns such as double-tap to like or swipe gestures for navigation. Because these actions remain consistent, users build muscle memory and can navigate without conscious effort. If these patterns suddenly changed, even slightly, users would feel confused and frustrated because their learned expectations would no longer apply.

However, inconsistency is still common in many digital systems. Some websites place buttons in different locations depending on the page, or use different icons for the same action. This forces users to stop and reprocess the interface each time, increasing cognitive effort and slowing down task completion. Over time, this can lead to frustration and even abandonment of the system entirely.

Feedback and User Confidence

Feedback helps users understand the outcome of their actions. Without it, users experience uncertainty and may repeat actions unnecessarily. The W3C (2023) states that clear system feedback is essential for usability and accessibility, ensuring users always understand system status.

Critical Reflection: Where Design Fails

Although many systems aim to reduce cognitive load, some fail by introducing unnecessary complexity through inconsistent navigation, excessive notifications, or hidden controls. These issues force users to spend more mental effort than necessary, reducing efficiency and satisfaction.

Conclusion

Effective interface design respects human cognitive limitations by reducing memory demands, supporting recognition, maintaining consistency, and providing clear feedback. When these principles are applied correctly, users can focus on their goals rather than the system itself.
