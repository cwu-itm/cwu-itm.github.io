Module 1.1 Blog Post

Introduction

In an increasingly interconnected digital world, designing and managing systems has grown more complex than ever before. From autonomous vehicles that rely on a blend of hardware, software, and sensors, to global telecom networks supporting millions of devices, today’s systems are less like machines, and more like ecosystems. This complexity demands more than a linear, step-by-step way of thinking. It calls for a shift in mindset. A mindset that sees the bigger picture, anticipates interactions, and embraces feedback. That’s where systems thinking comes in.

At its core, systems thinking is a way of viewing a system not just as a collection of parts, but as an interconnected whole. It’s about seeing how each element relates to others and how the behavior of the system emerges from those interactions. Unlike traditional thinking, which tends to isolate problems and address them in sequence, systems thinking emphasizes interdependence, context, and dynamics. A systems thinker steps back to consider not only the components of a system, but also how those components behave over time and in response to internal and external forces.

For example, consider a smart city transportation system. A linear thinker might break it into pieces: traffic lights, buses, pedestrian flow. But a systems thinker considers how all those pieces interact, how a delayed train could affect road congestion, which could impact emergency response times, which in turn might affect public safety metrics. This broader, connected view is key to identifying root causes, anticipating downstream effects, and designing solutions that adapt, rather than just react.

When systems fail, the consequences aren’t always minor. From software glitches that delay airline traffic, to cybersecurity flaws that expose millions of user records, the inability to manage complexity can have real-world consequences. These challenges aren’t just technical, they’re organizational, social, and even ethical. It’s no longer enough to patch individual problems; we need a framework for designing resilience from the start.

As Shevchenko (2020) describes it, systems thinking “emphasizes interconnectedness” and encourages engineers to observe feedback loops and causality patterns, often revealing issues or inefficiencies that would otherwise go unnoticed. Jon Holt (2020) echoes this by identifying one of the “Three Evils” of poor systems engineering as a lack of understanding, something systems thinking directly addresses by making relationships and dependencies more visible and manageable.

Why Linear Thinking Falls Short

Traditional problem-solving often leans on a linear, reductionist mindset: break down a problem, isolate the pieces, solve each one, and reassemble. This works in simple or predictable systems, think factory assembly lines or basic troubleshooting flows. But as digital systems have evolved into sprawling, interconnected networks, this kind of thinking falls behind. It assumes stability, ignores relationships, and fails to capture how change in one area can ripple unpredictably across others.

Jon Holt (2020) warns that unmanaged complexity, poor communication, and shallow understanding are the three core reasons why modern systems fail. The main blind spots of linear thinking. It can’t account for the dynamic, evolving nature of today’s systems, nor can it track how decisions made early in a project might affect outcomes much later in the lifecycle.

Consider the evolution of automotive systems. A car from 1970 had mostly mechanical components and a fairly straightforward structure. In contrast, a modern electric vehicle depends on integrated software systems, communication networks, sensors, and real-time data. Its complexity isn’t just in the number of parts, but in how those parts interact and change based on users, environmental factors, and updates over time. A linear approach might manage the car’s powertrain or interface, but it will likely overlook how a software bug in the infotainment system could cascade into navigation failures, connectivity issues, or even driver distraction.

This limitation isn’t exclusive to the automotive or aerospace industries. In healthcare, for example, the patient care experience is shaped by interactions between clinical departments, data systems, staff scheduling, and insurance networks. A delay in imaging can push back a surgery, which impacts bed availability in post-op recovery, leading to overcrowding in the ER. Linear thinking might focus on optimizing each department in isolation, but it fails to account for the interconnected bottlenecks that affect outcomes across the entire system. Logistics and supply chain systems face similar challenges, when one supplier fails, ripple effects can span continents.

As Holt (2020) explains, this growth in system complexity isn’t just quantitative, it’s qualitative. It introduces new types of relationships, new domains of failure, and new demands for adaptability. Without a way to think holistically, system engineers risk treating symptoms instead of solving root causes. That’s why linear thinking, while still useful for certain tasks, is no longer sufficient for systems operating in today's digital world.

The Model-Based Advantage

If systems thinking offers the mindset needed to understand today’s digital complexity, model-based systems engineering (MBSE) delivers the tools to manage it. MBSE shifts the focus from static documentation to dynamic, digital models that can evolve alongside the system itself. Rather than keeping track of requirements, architecture, and behavior in disconnected text files, spreadsheets, or diagrams, MBSE centralizes everything into a shared, interactive model that updates in real time and serves as a single source of truth.

This approach isn’t just a trend, it’s a necessary evolution. As Shevchenko (2020) explains, MBSE allows engineers to represent systems in ways that expose inconsistencies early, validate system behavior visually, and simulate interactions across disciplines. Instead of discovering integration failures late in development, teams using MBSE can detect and correct them at the modeling stage, drastically reducing rework and risk.

The benefits go beyond error reduction. MBSE enhances collaboration across departments and disciplines, something traditional document-based methods struggle to support. According to the Georgia Tech Professional Education report (2024), MBSE creates intuitive visualizations that help software developers, hardware engineers, and even non-technical users understand and engage with system designs. It’s a bridge between perspectives, a way to keep everyone aligned, even as the system grows in size and complexity.

MBSE also supports parallel development, a major advantage over the sequential workflows of traditional engineering. This means teams can design, test, and refine subsystems simultaneously, accelerating innovation and shortening timelines. When paired with standardized modeling languages like SysML (Systems Modeling Language), the MBSE environment becomes not just a workspace, but a living system that reflects the real one, adaptive, scalable, and always in sync.

MBSE isn’t just a development tool, it provides support across the entire system lifecycle. From early-stage concept design to deployment, maintenance, and even system retirement, MBSE enables traceability of every requirement, decision, and change. This lifecycle visibility is especially important in industries with long-term regulatory obligations, like aerospace, healthcare, and defense, where documentation and validation often extend across decades.

To make this all work, engineers use modeling languages and tools tailored to systems design. SysML is one of the most widely used frameworks, offering diagram types that map system structure, behavior, and interactions. Tools like Cameo Systems Modeler, IBM Rhapsody, and Eclipse Capella bring these models to life with simulation capabilities and collaborative features. These tools reduce ambiguity and make it easier to share a consistent view of the system among all users, from developers to executives.

As digital transformation accelerates, the need for scalable, intelligent systems will only intensify. MBSE is already critical in fields like aerospace, defense, and healthcare, but its impact is expanding alongside emerging technologies like artificial intelligence, edge computing, and autonomous platforms. The future of MBSE lies in deeper integration with cloud-based tools, real-time analytics, and even predictive modeling. This will enable systems to adapt not just over project timelines, but during operation. Likewise, systems thinking must grow with it, encouraging engineers to design not only for functionality, but for ethical, social, and environmental impact. Together, these approaches represent the evolution of engineering itself, from building machines to building adaptive, responsible systems.

Conclusion

As systems grow more complex, our thinking and tools need to evolve with them. Systems thinking provides the lens needed to see the bigger picture, how components interact, where feedback loops emerge, and what hidden consequences might unfold. It challenges the old habit of isolating problems and solving them in silos, encouraging us to instead design with awareness of the whole.

Model-based systems engineering puts that mindset into practice. By shifting from static documentation to dynamic modeling, MBSE empowers teams to build smarter, more adaptable systems with fewer surprises and better collaboration. It enables parallel development, real-time updates, and deep integration across hardware, software, and human workflows. In doing so, it transforms system design from a rigid sequence of deliverables into a living, evolving process.

In a world where complexity is the norm, not the exception, MBSE isn’t just a helpful upgrade. It’s the foundation for building the next generation of digital systems. And systems thinking is the mindset that makes it all possible.

Looking ahead, engineers and decision-makers will need to embrace this combination not only as a technical necessity, but as a strategic imperative. The ability to manage complexity, foresee unintended consequences, and adapt systems in real time is quickly becoming the defining skillset in digital transformation. Whether in healthcare, defense, education, or the private sector, applying systems thinking with MBSE allows organizations to stay resilient, competitive, and responsible in a rapidly changing world. It’s not just about building better systems, it’s about building systems that are better for the future.

Module 1.2 Blog Post

Introduction

As digital systems grow more complex, designing them requires more than technical know-how, it takes a mindset that sees the bigger picture. Systems thinking helps engineers and designers understand how different parts interact and evolve together, rather than viewing components in isolation. It’s about building solutions that work not just now, but over time.

One way this mindset takes shape in practice is through the systems development life cycle (SDLC). The SDLC is a structured approach for planning, creating, testing, and maintaining systems throughout their lifespan. Different life cycle models, linear, iterative, and incremental, offer unique strategies for managing development and change. Understanding their strengths and trade-offs helps us make smarter decisions during design. In this post, I’ll explain SDLC in my own words, explore the advantages and drawbacks of the three core models, and reflect on how life cycle thinking, and tools like Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE), contribute to more sustainable digital systems.

Defining the Systems Development Life Cycle

The systems development life cycle (SDLC) is a structured framework that guides the creation and evolution of a system from its initial concept to its eventual retirement. Rather than approaching development as a one-time event, SDLC breaks the process into clearly defined stages including planning, design, implementation, testing, deployment, and maintenance. This helps teams stay aligned, manage complexity, and ensure that the final system meets both functional and stakeholder needs.

Unlike ad hoc development, the SDLC offers a repeatable structure that can be adapted across different projects and industries. As outlined in Systems Engineering Demystified, a life cycle defines the stages through which a system evolves and provides a framework for representing that evolution over time. This structure supports consistency in planning, development, and long-term system maintenance. By formalizing these stages and how they relate to each other, SDLC helps teams organize their work and make informed decisions at each point in the system’s development.

Thinking in terms of a system’s full life cycle encourages us to plan for more than just the “build” phase, it prompts consideration of future updates, integrations, user interactions, and eventual decommissioning. This mindset not only supports better design, but also lays the foundation for sustainability and adaptability. Whether using a rigid or flexible model, the SDLC gives teams a way to organize their work in a methodical, outcome-driven way that keeps the bigger picture in focus.

Life Cycle Models in Practice

Linear Model

The linear life cycle model, often referred to as the waterfall model, is one of the earliest and most structured approaches to system development. In this model, each stage of development (requirements, design, implementation, verification, and maintenance) is completed in a strict sequence. Once one phase is finished, the project moves forward to the next without looping back. This makes the linear model easy to plan and manage, especially for projects with clearly defined goals and stable requirements.

One major advantage of the linear model is its simplicity. As outlined in Comparing Life-Cycle Models for System Development, it is often perceived as effective and applicable in scenarios where the scope is fixed and predictable. The structured flow can help reduce ambiguity, making it easier to track progress and assign responsibilities.

However, its biggest limitation is inflexibility. If user needs change, or errors are discovered late in the process, the linear model makes it difficult and costly to revisit earlier phases. The same study highlights drawbacks such as poor risk manageability and limited adaptability when compared to other models. Because of its rigid structure, this model is often not well-suited for projects where requirements are expected to evolve.

Despite these challenges, the linear model still has value. Particularly in regulated industries or environments where documentation, predictability, and control take priority over adaptability.

Iterative Model

The iterative life cycle model takes a more flexible, feedback-driven approach to system development. Rather than moving through stages just once in a fixed order, this model repeats cycles of design, implementation, and testing, refining the system with each pass. Each cycle, or iteration, builds on the results of the previous one, gradually evolving the system toward its final form.

One of the biggest strengths of the iterative model is its support for continuous improvement. Early versions of the system can be tested with users, providing valuable input and reducing the risk of building something misaligned with user needs. According to Comparing Life-Cycle Models for System Development, iterative methods are positively perceived for their flexibility, speed, and ability to manage risk early in the process. Because feedback is incorporated in near-real time, issues are caught and resolved faster than in traditional models.

However, this model isn’t without its drawbacks. The same study notes that iterative approaches can struggle with scope creep, where constant refinements lead to a project ballooning beyond its original plan. There’s also the risk of not knowing when to stop, if each cycle adds new changes, teams may lose track of what “done” looks like.

Even so, for systems that demand frequent input, are being built in uncertain or evolving environments, or require early demonstrations of functionality, the iterative model offers a strong foundation. Its adaptability makes it ideal for modern digital systems where user feedback and agility are key to success.

Incremental Model

The incremental life cycle model divides the system into smaller, manageable parts that are developed and delivered in stages. Each increment adds a functional component to the overall system, allowing portions of the product to be released and used before the full system is complete. Unlike the iterative model, which emphasizes repetition and refinement, the incremental model focuses on building a system piece by piece in a controlled, additive way.

One of the clearest advantages of this approach is its ability to deliver early value. As highlighted in Comparing Life-Cycle Models for System Development, developers often appreciate the ability to see and use working features sooner, even if the system isn't yet fully complete. This makes it easier to validate assumptions, adjust priorities, and respond to evolving requirements without needing to rebuild from scratch. It’s particularly well-suited for projects where key functionalities can be grouped and delivered independently.

However, incremental development isn’t without complications. Integrating new increments into an evolving system can introduce compatibility challenges, especially if early design decisions don’t anticipate future needs. Additionally, managing dependencies between increments can become complex as the system scales. The model also relies on careful planning and clear architectural foresight to prevent rework or redundancy.

Still, the incremental model strikes a balance between structure and flexibility. It allows for predictable delivery while accommodating change, making it a strong choice for projects that benefit from staged implementation and early feedback, especially in software or modular digital systems.

The Role of MBSE in Modern Life Cycle Thinking

As systems have grown more complex and interdependent, traditional methods of development have begun to show their limitations, especially in terms of traceability, consistency, and adaptability. This is where Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) offers a significant shift. Rather than relying on static documents, MBSE emphasizes the use of interconnected models to represent requirements, behaviors, architecture, and validation strategies throughout a system’s life cycle.

Defined by INCOSE as “the formalized application of modeling to support system requirements, design, analysis, verification and validation activities,” MBSE supports system development from conceptual design through retirement. In Model-Based Systems Engineering: Evaluating Perceived Value (Campo et al., 2023), researchers reviewed over 2,900 claims across 60 academic sources and found that MBSE is consistently associated with improvements in verification and validation, consistency, and risk management. Notably, it also helps teams visualize and reason through complex system interactions early, before costly design flaws are locked in.

However, the same study highlights challenges with approach complexity, tool maturity, and the learning curve for teams unfamiliar with model-driven workflows. Additionally, many of the perceived benefits are still anecdotal or unsupported by robust metrics, meaning MBSE isn’t a silver bullet. Rather it’s a methodology that demands investment and long-term commitment to fully realize its value.

When integrated into the SDLC, MBSE reinforces life cycle thinking by making it easier to plan across multiple phases of development. It supports better requirements traceability, enables simulation of system behaviors, and enhances communication across multidisciplinary teams. This modeling approach also contributes to the sustainability of digital systems by reducing rework and supporting long-term evolution through structured system knowledge.

By providing a living, traversable representation of a system, MBSE transforms the development process into a more adaptive, feedback-rich experience. As industries move toward digital engineering, MBSE serves not just as a complement to life cycle models, but as a tool that strengthens them. Especially when thinking about systems as evolving, interconnected entities.

The Value of Life Cycle Thinking

Thinking in terms of a system’s life cycle is more than a development strategy, it’s a mindset that promotes long-term sustainability, adaptability, and resilience. Life cycle thinking encourages teams to consider not just how a system will work today, but how it will evolve, scale, integrate with other systems, and eventually be retired. This broader perspective leads to systems that are more responsive to change and less likely to become obsolete prematurely.

In digital environments where user needs and technologies shift rapidly, this approach helps reduce costly redesigns and system failures. It pushes designers to plan for updates, anticipate integration needs, and prioritize maintainability. For example, modular design can make future upgrades easier, while forward-thinking architecture ensures compatibility with tools or standards that haven’t emerged yet.

Life cycle thinking also supports sustainability by encouraging more responsible use of resources. Designing systems with long-term use in mind reduces waste, lowers energy demands over time, and promotes reusability. It aligns development with environmental goals, something that’s becoming increasingly important in sectors like digital infrastructure, smart cities, and enterprise technology.

Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) reinforces these benefits by making a system’s evolution traceable and testable. Through simulation and model-based documentation, MBSE allows teams to plan for various outcomes, manage complexity, and preserve institutional knowledge for future contributors. It transforms system design into a dynamic process. One that values feedback, adaptation, and clarity.

Ultimately, life cycle thinking reminds us that system development isn’t just about launching a product. It’s about building a foundation that can adapt, scale, and improve over time. By combining thoughtful model selection, systems thinking, and approaches like MBSE, we create systems that are not only functional, but future-ready. This kind of intentional, long-term planning helps ensure that the digital systems we design today, continue to deliver value well into tomorrow.

Module 2.1 Blog Post

Designing for People

Design isn’t just about making things look good, it’s also about making things work for the people who use them. At its core, human-centered design (HCD) is the practice of creating products, services, and systems that start and end with the user. It’s a mindset and methodology that values empathy, iteration, and deep understanding. Whether you’re redesigning a website or developing a public service app, HCD insists that we ask: “Who is this for, and what do they really need?”

IDEO, a leader in this space, describes human-centered designers as “optimists and makers” who dive into problems not with pre-determined answers, but with curiosity and openness. It’s an approach that involves listening before building and observing before solving. Instead of designing for people, it’s about designing with people. Inviting many perspectives to shape each stage of the process.

In today’s fast-moving digital world, this mindset matters more than ever. Users, who are often under time pressure or stress, interact with dozens of systems daily. If we fail to consider their attention spans, their perceptions, or their emotional states, we risk building systems that confuse, frustrate, or exclude. HCD reminds us that good design is not just functional, it’s respectful. It sees users as collaborators, not just results. And when done right, it leads to products that feel intuitive, inclusive, and (perhaps most importantly) human.

ISO vs. IDEO Approaches

While human-centered design is a shared philosophy, the path to achieving it can vary widely. Two influential approaches, ISO 9241-210 and the IDEO Field Guide, both aim to center users in the design process, but they differ in structure, tone, and application.

The ISO 9241-210 standard is widely adopted in professional UX, systems engineering, and software development. It outlines a six-step framework for incorporating user needs throughout the design process: understanding the context of use, specifying user requirements, producing design solutions, and evaluating them against real-world tasks. It emphasizes the value of iterative testing and cross-functional teams, especially in complex environments like healthcare, enterprise systems, and critical infrastructure. The language is formal and technical, reflecting its roots in ergonomic standards. This makes ISO an excellent choice when the stakes are high, user errors are costly, or regulatory compliance is required.

In contrast, the IDEO Field Guide presents a more flexible and emotionally driven interpretation of human-centered design. Rather than offering strict procedural steps, IDEO organizes the process into three nonlinear phases: Inspiration, Ideation, and Implementation. The Field Guide encourages designers to build deep empathy through interviews and immersion, rapidly prototype ideas, and embrace failure as part of the creative journey. Its tone is accessible and motivational, favoring storytelling and team-driven exploration over formal documentation. This makes it well-suited for community-driven projects, startups, and social innovation. The areas where emotional connection and adaptability are as critical as efficiency.

Beyond format, the core values of both models align closely. Both prioritize empathy, active engagement with users, and iteration based on feedback. However, where ISO emphasizes precision and traceability, IDEO celebrates ambiguity and experimentation. For example, ISO would require structured documentation of a banking app’s accessibility goals, while IDEO might begin the same project by observing how older adults struggle with digital interfaces in real time. The insights gained from both are valuable, but the methods reflect different design cultures.

Choosing between them depends on context. In many cases, the best solution is not picking one over the other but instead blending their strengths. IDEO’s mindset-driven approach can humanize ISO’s structure, while ISO’s rigor can give form and focus to IDEO’s creativity. When used together, they offer a holistic view of human-centered design. One that honors both measurable usability and emotional resonance.

Perception in Design

When users interact with a digital interface, they’re not analyzing every detail. Instead, they’re scanning, interpreting, and reacting almost instantly. That’s why perception and visual hierarchy are central to usability. A designer’s job is to guide the user’s eye and attention where it matters most, reducing confusion and cognitive strain.

Visual hierarchy refers to the arrangement and styling of elements to signal their relative importance. Through contrast, size, spacing, color, and alignment, designers influence what users notice first, what they perceive as interactive, and how they move through a page. If the layout clashes with user expectations, or if everything looks equally important, people quickly become disoriented or frustrated.

Several UX laws highlight how perception shapes user behavior. For instance, Fitts’s Law shows that the time to interact with an object depends on its size and distance. Small, distant buttons slow users down and increase the chance of error. Good interfaces, like Amazon’s product pages, place large, high-contrast buttons (“Buy Now,” “Add to Cart”) near the relevant content to maximize speed and clarity.

Similarly, Hick’s Law reminds us that too many choices can overload users and increase decision time. Netflix, for example, combats this by offering curated lists like “Top Picks” or “Trending Now” to simplify selection and keep users engaged.

Perception is also tightly linked to user trust. If visual cues are inconsistent, poorly aligned, or unintuitive, users may assume the product is unreliable. Even if the underlying functionality is sound. Thoughtful design creates a seamless first impression which in turn gives users confidence in their ability to navigate and understand the system.

Ultimately, visual hierarchy is more than an aesthetic tool, it’s a psychological one. By aligning layout with perceptual tendencies, designers reduce friction and make digital interactions feel natural. In a well-designed interface, users don’t think about where to go next, they just know.

Attention and Empathy

Designing for people requires more than technical know-how, it demands an understanding of how people think and feel. Two core psychological principles come into play here, attention and empathy. Together they shape how users engage with a product and whether that experience is useful, usable, and emotionally resonant.

Attention is a limited resource. Users don’t absorb everything on a screen, they skim, prioritize, and ignore. This is where Miller’s Law becomes relevant. It suggests that people can hold about 7 ± 2 pieces of information in their working memory at once. If a design presents too many options or clutters the screen with competing elements, users may disengage. To accommodate attention limits, designers often use grouping (chunking), progressive disclosure, and whitespace to keep interfaces digestible.

On the other hand, empathy allows designers to anticipate emotional and cognitive responses. Especially from users whose needs may differ from their own. The neuroscience of empathy breaks this down into two main types: cognitive empathy, understanding another person’s thoughts and perspectives, and affective empathy, emotionally resonating with their experiences. Effective designers use both. For example, a developer working on a financial app for older adults must not only understand their limited tech experience (cognitive empathy) but also their anxiety or distrust of digital tools (affective empathy).

Empathy also helps uncover hidden pain points that data alone might miss. As shown in The Neuroscience of Empathy, students at Stanford's d.school institute created financial education materials in Spanish after connecting emotionally with immigrant janitors who were being taken advantage of in transactions. Their ability to feel and understand the janitors’ experience led to a solution that was both useful and meaningful.

Furthermore, empathetic design builds inclusion. People with different abilities, backgrounds, or education levels interact with technology in diverse ways. When designers truly consider these perspectives through interviews, observation, and direct engagement, they create experiences that feel welcoming instead of alienating.

At its best, human-centered design respects users’ attention and honors their humanity. By reducing mental strain and designing with empathy, we don’t just make interfaces easier to use, we make them kinder, more intuitive, and ultimately more effective.

UX Laws in Practice

Human-centered design isn’t just about good intentions, it’s also grounded in behavioral science. Several well-established psychological principles, often called UX laws, help designers predict how people will interact with systems. Understanding these laws allows us to build more intuitive, efficient, and satisfying user experiences. Four of the most widely applied are Jakob’s Law, Fitts’s Law, Hick’s Law, and Miller’s Law.

Jakob’s Law states that users spend most of their time using other websites or apps, so they prefer new experiences to work similarly to ones they already know. In practice, this means leveraging common patterns in layout, navigation, and iconography. For instance, placing a shopping cart icon in the top-right corner or using a hamburger menu for mobile navigation meets user expectations and reduces the learning curve while improving usability.

Fitts’s Law focuses on speed and accuracy of interactions. It tells us that the time it takes to click or tap a target depends on the size of the target and its distance from the user’s starting point. Designers use this insight to make important buttons larger, group them near related content, and avoid small, hard-to-reach tap zones. This is especially critical on mobile devices.

Hick’s Law helps designers manage complexity. It describes the relationship between the number of choices and decision time. The more options you present, the longer it takes users to choose. This is why streaming platforms like Netflix simplify decisions with curated lists, or why websites often break up forms across multiple steps. When information is chunked and revealed gradually, it’s easier to process and far less overwhelming.

Miller’s Law, covered earlier, reminds us of the brain’s memory limitations. When designers group related options, limit simultaneous choices, and present information in digestible bits, they’re not just cleaning up a layout, they’re supporting the way human memory works.

These laws are especially powerful when used together. For example, a well-designed checkout page on an e-commerce site may include:

  • Familiar layout elements (Jakob’s Law)
  • Large, easy-to-tap purchase buttons (Fitts’s Law)
  • Streamlined steps to minimize choices (Hick’s Law)
  • Clearly grouped billing, shipping, and payment sections (Miller’s Law)

By grounding decisions in these psychological principles, designers avoid guesswork and create interfaces that “just make sense” to users. These laws turn empathy into action by bridging the gap between what users feel and what they can do.

Why HCD Matters

At its core, human-centered design is about respect. Respect for users’ time, their attention, their limitations, and their lived experiences. It’s not just a methodology, but a design mindset that challenges us to listen, design, and continually refine based on real feedback. When done well, it results in products and services that feel natural, accessible, and even empowering.

Throughout this post, we’ve seen how tools like ISO 9241-210 and IDEO’s Field Guide provide different paths to the same goal. Meaningful, usable solutions. We’ve also explored how visual hierarchy, cognitive load, and empathy shape how people experience design. And with the support of UX laws, designers can apply these insights consistently across platforms and industries.

Human-centered design matters because it puts people, not features and trends, at the center of the process. In a world where technology is constantly evolving, this mindset helps ensure that innovation stays inclusive, intuitive, and human. It’s not just about building something that works, it’s about building something that works for people.

Module 2.2 Blog Post

Introduction

We’ve all felt the sting of bad design. Maybe it was a cluttered website that buried the action you needed, or an app that made you guess what each icon meant. These aren’t just aesthetic problems, they are usability failures. And usability isn’t just about what looks nice, it’s about how a system aligns with how people think, remember, and navigate the world.

User experience design, at its best, doesn’t force people to adapt to a system. The system adapts to them. That means understanding how memory works, how people pay attention, and how they respond to feedback. It also means recognizing that mental effort is a limited resource. When users are asked to juggle too much information, or guess what to do next, they lose confidence and patience.

This post explores the psychology behind good design. I’ll break down how memory, attention, consistency, and feedback shape user interactions and discuss how thoughtful UX design can reduce friction by working with the brain, not against it.

Memory and Mental Models in User Interaction

When users interact with a digital system, they’re not starting from scratch. They bring mental models, internalized expectations shaped by past experiences. These models guide how they believe a system should behave and aligning with these models helps reduce the mental effort required to complete tasks. When systems depart from user expectations, it forces people to pause, problem-solve, or worse, give up.

A core principle behind designing for memory is favoring recognition over recall. As Designing with the Mind in Mind explains, the brain is wired to recognize patterns quickly, especially when a user encounters something they’ve seen before. Recognition works by activating existing neural patterns formed by prior exposure, whereas recall requires the brain to recreate those patterns without cues. This is cognitively taxing and error-prone (Johnson, 2014). That’s why people remember icons and menu labels more easily than command strings or abstract instructions. Good interfaces incorporate familiar visual elements and reduce the need to retrieve information from memory unassisted.

Designing with the Mind in Mind echoes this principle, noting that “familiar icons, patterns, and labels” help users stay oriented and remember actions more easily. For instance, systems that use breadcrumbs or progress indicators enable users to “remember” where they are without having to mentally track steps themselves (Holston, 2024). These are external memory aids, visual anchors that reduce the user’s reliance on limited short-term memory.

Storytelling also plays a subtle but powerful role in memory retention. If information is delivered in a narrative structure, like onboarding through a digital character journey, it’s more relatable, and therefore, easier to remember. This technique personalizes the experience and supports recall by tying features to meaningful goals or outcomes.

Designers can also tap into user mental models by maintaining consistent interaction patterns. If every app handles search filters or navigation menus differently, users must relearn the structure each time. But when behavior matches expectation, like tapping a shopping cart icon to check out, users experience what feels like intuitive design, but is really just cognitive alignment.

By designing around how memory works, we make interfaces feel effortless. The less users are forced to think about the interface, the more they can focus on achieving their goals.

Reducing Cognitive Load

Cognitive load refers to the mental effort required to interact with a system. When users are overwhelmed by cluttered layouts, vague instructions, or too many choices, frustration rises, and usability plummets. Great design doesn’t just minimize how much a user must remember, it also organizes content in a way that aligns with how the brain naturally processes information.

“Young man covered in sticky notes, work overload” by Luis Villasmil is licensed through Unsplash

According to Designing with the Mind in Mind, reducing cognitive load begins with structure. Designers use principles like chunking, breaking information into smaller, manageable pieces, to make content easier to process. Instead of displaying a dense block of instructions, interfaces might use bullet points, tabs, or collapsible menus to guide attention. Gestalt principles like proximity and similarity further reduce mental strain by helping users visually group related items without conscious effort. Placing labels near their input fields, for example, makes form-filling faster and less error prone as well (Holston, 2024).

Another key concept is Hick’s Law. This law states that the more options a user has, the longer it takes to make a decision. This doesn’t mean stripping functionality, it means designing flows that present only the most relevant choices at a given time. Progressive disclosure is one solution: show basic options first and reveal advanced features as needed. This ensures users aren’t flooded with complexity all at once, and can grow into a product at their own pace.

But not all complexity is avoidable. That’s where Tesler’s Law comes in. Tesler’s Law (also known as the Law of Conservation of Complexity) states that every system has a certain level of unavoidable complexity. If designers try to eliminate it entirely, they risk shifting the burden onto the user. A good example is replacing a contact form with a mailto link. It may seem cleaner, but for users who don’t use a default email client, it creates unnecessary friction (Schwarz, 2024).

Instead of oversimplifying, effective UX design structures complexity. Well-organized, single-page layouts with interactive elements, like accordions or anchors, are one way to balance information density and ease of use. A better approach is to begin with user research, such as card sorting or A/B testing, to identify which information matters most and when it should be introduced. The goal isn’t to eliminate decisions, but to support them.

Cognitive load doesn’t disappear, it’s redistributed. The best systems anticipate user needs and help guide them through complexity at their own pace.

Importance of Consistency and Feedback

A well-designed interface should help users stay focused on their goals, not on figuring out how the interface works. This is why consistency and feedback are essential elements of usable design. When systems behave in expected ways and provide clear signals about what’s happening, users feel in control, even when navigating complex tasks.

“3/4 MI paint on gray pavement” by Mick Haupt is licensed through Unsplash

Chapter 8 of Designing with the Mind in Mind explains that users devote very little attention to the tools they’re using, most of their attention is focused on achieving their goal. If a tool or interface draws attention to itself, by behaving inconsistently or requiring the user to stop and think about how to proceed, it disrupts that goal-oriented flow. This is why the principle of “Don’t Make Me Think” is so important. Good design fades into the background, allowing users to remain focused on what they’re trying to accomplish, not how to operate the system (Johnson, 2014).

Inconsistency forces users to reorient themselves. If a button moves between screens, or if similar functions are labeled differently in different areas, the user must pause to figure out whether the function has changed, or if they're just seeing it in a new context. This added mental effort pulls focus away from their task and increases cognitive load. Consistency helps avoid this by ensuring familiar patterns carry over across screens, platforms, and interactions.

Laws of UX emphasizes how teams can build consistency by embedding psychological principles into their design processes. Not just using them as one-off tools, but internalizing them into team practices. By developing and applying shared design principles like “familiarity over novelty,” teams ensure decisions are grounded in user expectations. The book gives examples of how companies like Stripe use progressive disclosure to provide a consistent, clear path through complex systems (Yablonski, 2024).

Feedback plays an equally crucial role. Whether it’s a progress indicator showing how far a user has gone through a form, or an animated vibration drawing attention to an error message, timely feedback confirms that user actions have registered. Without it, people can become uncertain or assume a system is broken.

By aligning design with how people attain and process information, we reduce confusion and build trust. Consistent design patterns paired with meaningful feedback help users stay focused, avoid mistakes, and ultimately feel more confident in the tools they’re using.

Real-World Examples of Cognitive Overload

I’ve run into cognitive overload more times than I can count while using everyday websites and apps. One that always sticks out is booking flights. I’ve used airline websites that pile on every optional upgrade, seats, bags, meals, insurance, all on the same screen. Even though I understand why they offer them, it feels overwhelming trying to make five or six decisions all at once just to move forward. A cleaner, step-by-step layout would make the process feel far less exhausting.

“a large sign with a lot of numbers on it” by Josh Withers is licensed through Unsplash

Government websites have caused similar frustration. I remember trying to update my information when doing my taxes this year and I saw two different buttons, one labeled “Update Profile,” the other “Change Account Settings.” They led to basically the same place, but it took me some time to realize that. It made me second-guess where to click going forward which added unnecessary stress to something that should’ve been simple.

Even social media apps have tripped me up. On Instagram, I’ve had trouble finding settings to hide or manage posts, it’s often hidden behind menus or requires knowing about long-press features I only discovered by accident. Without obvious feedback or labels, it felt like I was guessing my way through the app.

These moments remind me how easy it is for systems to shift complexity back onto the user when design isn’t handled carefully.

Conclusion

At its core, usability is about respecting the way people think. When digital systems align with memory patterns, reduce unnecessary mental effort, and provide clear, consistent feedback, they become easier to trust, and easier to use. Throughout this post, I’ve explored how concepts like recognition over recall, cognitive load, and feedback loops aren’t just design buzzwords, they’re rooted in how our brains actually function.

Whether we’re designing apps, websites, or tools for everyday tasks, the goal should be the same: to support users, not slow them down. By embedding psychology into our design thinking, we’ll create systems that don’t just work, they’ll feel intuitive. And that’s what makes technology genuinely usable.

Module 3.1 Blog Post

Introduction

We interact with digital interfaces every day, apps, websites, even smart devices, and we usually don’t think twice about how they’re built until something goes wrong. When a layout feels clunky, buttons are hard to find, or a form keeps rejecting your input for no clear reason, it’s frustrating. On the flip side, when an interface feels smooth and intuitive, we barely notice it at all. That’s because the logic behind how interfaces are designed has a huge impact on how we experience them.

In this post, I’m taking a closer look at how interface structure affects usability by reflecting on a real experience I’ve had and connecting it to a few key UX laws: the Aesthetic–Usability Effect, the Von Restorff Effect, and Postel’s Law. I’ll also be pulling in insights from our course readings and a couple of outside sources to show how layout, feedback, and flexibility play a big role in shaping how we interact with digital systems, for better or worse.

Personal Experience with Interface Logic

One of the most frustrating digital experiences I’ve ever had was trying to cancel a subscription through a mobile app. The layout looked nice at first, clean and modern, but the process of finding the actual cancel option was buried deep within a series of vague menus. Every time I thought I was getting closer, I’d hit a dead end or get redirected to a support FAQ. The worst part was the feedback: after finally locating the cancel button, pressing it just refreshed the page without confirming whether anything had actually happened. I had to check my email to confirm the cancellation went through.

This experience stuck with me because it felt like the interface was designed to discourage cancellation, not to support the user. From a usability standpoint, this shows how layout and feedback directly affect user confidence. According to Johnson (2014), users rely heavily on system feedback to confirm their actions and reduce cognitive load. Without clear feedback, we’re left guessing, and that’s frustrating.

Cognitively, this experience also highlighted the difference between automatic and controlled processes. Normally, navigating a familiar app feels seamless because those actions are handled by our "System 1" thinking, fast, intuitive, and effortless (Johnson, 2014). But when interfaces break from expected patterns, or hide information, we’re forced to switch into "System 2" mode. In this, we are slower and everything requires more mental power. This shift not only slows down the process, but also increases the chance of user error or abandonment.

In short, even a visually polished interface can fail if its structure doesn’t support the user's goals. It’s not just about looking good, it’s about making sure people can actually do what they came to do.

Aesthetic–Usability Effect

We’ve all been there, using a product that looks so good we assume it works well too. That’s the Aesthetic–Usability Effect in action. It’s the idea that people tend to perceive attractive interfaces as more usable, even when they’re not actually more functional (Yablonski, 2024). I experienced this in my personal life with a budget-tracking app I tried out last year. It had a sleek, modern interface with pastel colors, rounded buttons, and smooth transitions. On first use, I assumed it would be easy to navigate. But the longer I used it, the more problems I ran into. Important features were hidden under icons with no labels, and there was no clear feedback when syncing my bank account. Still, I kept using it for weeks, convincing myself the issues were just “user error.”

“a hand holding a smart phone” by Typerium App is licensed through Unsplash

This is where aesthetics mask usability problems. According to Johnson (2014), “System 1” thinking takes over when we encounter visually appealing designs. It gives us a gut reaction that something is trustworthy or intuitive. And because “System 2” thinking is slower, and more effortful, we often don’t question that first impression unless we’re forced to.

External research backs this up. A study from UXPin (2022) explains how aesthetic design can influence a user’s emotional state, making them more patient with minor usability issues. That’s exactly what happened to me. I gave the app more chances than I would have, had it looked outdated or clunky. The visual appeal lowered my defenses. Design matters, but only if it supports usability. If aesthetics mislead users or hide functionality, it can result in frustration, not delight.

Von Restorff Effect

The Von Restorff Effect, also called the isolation effect, is one of the design principles that makes a huge difference in user experience. Even if people don’t realize it. It’s the idea that when multiple similar elements are present, the one that stands out, is more likely to be noticed and remembered (Yablonski, 2024). A good example of this in action is how some apps or websites highlight destructive actions, like “Delete Account” or “Unsubscribe,” by using bold colors like red. That contrast helps prevent accidental clicks and ensures the user really sees what they’re about to do.

“a black background with a red and green sign” by Claudio Schwarz is licensed through Unsplash

I once used a cloud storage app that completely failed to follow this principle. The “permanently delete” option looked just like every other option in the menu. Same color, same size, no warning icon. I clicked it by accident one day while trying to clear out a folder and instantly lost a bunch of important files. There was no confirmation prompt, and I had to contact support to finally confirm what happened.

This experience showed me how important it is to guide user attention effectively. According to Johnson (2014), users rely on perceptual cues, like color, shape, and placement, to make fast decisions using “System 1”. If everything looks the same, users can’t quickly differentiate between safe and risky actions.

Schwarz (2024) explains that the Von Restorff Effect is especially helpful for notifications, pricing tables, and action buttons. Designers can use contrast not just to make something eye-catching, but to highlight what’s important or risky. The takeaway? Visual differentiation isn’t just about style, it’s about preventing mistakes and helping users make smart choices fast.

Postel’s Law

Postel’s Law is one of the design principles that doesn’t seem like a big deal until you run into an interface that totally ignores it. The law basically says: “Be conservative in what you send, be liberal in what you accept” (Yablonski, 2024). In UX terms, that means designing systems that are flexible, forgiving, and capable of handling all kinds of user input. Even the messy or imperfect kind.

A good example of where I’ve seen this go wrong was with an online scholarship application form. It had super strict formatting rules: my phone number had to be in one specific format, my address couldn't include apartment numbers, and it wouldn’t accept certain special characters. Every time I tried to submit, it threw errors without explaining what needed to be fixed. It felt like I was being punished for not thinking like a machine.

“black flat screen computer monitor” by Markus Spiske is licensed through Unsplash

This kind of rigid input validation violates what Johnson (2014) refers to as the importance of supporting automatic, fast-thinking behavior “System 1”. When users are forced to slow down and figure out hidden rules or redo inputs they thought were fine, it shifts the experience into the more effortful and error-prone “System 2” mode.

Postel’s Law encourages designs that work the way people expect, not the other way around. That means accepting names with hyphens, interpreting various date formats, and offering helpful error messages instead of cryptic alerts. As Yablonski (2024) points out, anticipating variability and responding with flexibility makes interfaces more robust and more human-friendly. Now, the bottom line: a well-designed interface should help users succeed, even if their input isn’t “perfect.”

Conclusion

Looking back at these examples, it’s clear that interface logic does more than just guide users, it shapes how we feel about the experience. When designs apply principles like the Aesthetic–Usability Effect, Von Restorff Effect, and Postel’s Law, they don’t just look good, they work better. Each law highlights a different part of the interaction puzzle: aesthetics influence first impressions and patience, contrast guides attention, and flexibility reduces friction.

What really ties it all together is how these laws align with how our minds process information. As Johnson (2014) explains, our brains are constantly balancing quick judgments with deeper reasoning. Good interfaces support that by making key actions obvious, minimizing frustration, and helping users recover when they make mistakes. The more an interface respects how people think and behave, the more intuitive it feels.

These UX laws aren’t just academic theories though, they’re practical tools that help designers create better experiences. And after reflecting on both good and bad examples, I can say with confidence: the best designs aren’t just functional, they’re thoughtful.

Module 3.2 Blog Post

Introduction

In today’s digital world, the quality of user experience can make or break a product. Whether someone is shopping online, navigating an app, or checking their bank balance, the way they interact with a system plays a huge role in how they feel about it. A few seconds of lag, an unclear error message, or an unexpected motion, can leave users frustrated. Or worse, cause them to abandon the experience altogether. On the flip side, well-timed feedback, smooth transitions, and intuitive design, can leave a lasting positive impression.

In this post, we’ll explore how thoughtful interaction patterns, such as feedback timing, motion, and error prevention, not only shape how people feel in the moment, but also influence their emotional memory of an experience. It will also look at how persuasive design techniques can be used ethically to guide behavior without manipulating users. Throughout the post, several UX laws and psychological principles will be discussed as well to show how designers can create more meaningful and responsible digital interactions. By reflecting on both our course concepts and real-world examples, this post will highlight what it means to design not just efficiently, but ethically and empathetically.

Interaction and Feedback

At the heart of every positive user experience is a simple but critical principle: responsiveness. When users interact with a digital system they expect it to respond quickly and clearly. Responsiveness isn’t just about technical speed, it’s about how well the system keeps up with the user’s expectations and provides clear, meaningful feedback. Delays, even those lasting just a second, can make the experience feel broken or frustrating. According to the Doherty Threshold, productivity increases significantly when users and systems interact within a 400-millisecond window (Yablonski, 2024). This timing helps maintain a user’s sense of control and supports a state of focused engagement known as “flow,” where the interaction feels seamless and natural. Too much delay, on the other hand, disrupts that experience. This will often leave users second-guessing whether their input was registered at all.

Human cognitive limits further underscore the importance of fast, timely feedback. Research in neuropsychology shows that we begin to perceive systems as unresponsive when visual or motor feedback is delayed by more than 100 milliseconds (Johnson, 2014). Even small bits of lag can interfere with our perception of causality and essentially break the mental connection between our action and the system’s response. This can be especially problematic when using digital tools in high-stakes or time-sensitive situations. But it’s just as important for everyday interactions too, like clicking a “Submit” button on a form. If nothing happens right away, users might click again or assume there’s an error, increasing frustration and the chance of mistakes. However, simple feedback mechanisms, like a loading animation or a visual ripple effect, can go a long way toward making the user feel acknowledged and in control, even when the underlying task takes a bit longer to process.

In short, responsiveness is more than just a performance benchmark, it’s a psychological contract between the user and the interface. Meeting the expectations set by that contract builds trust and increases satisfaction, while failing to do so creates friction and confusion. By understanding how people perceive time and feedback, designers can craft systems that not only function well, but also feel intuitive, empathetic, and respectful of the user's attention and effort.

Motion, Timing, and Emotional Memory

When people reflect on a digital experience, their memory isn’t based on every single moment. People tend to remember the emotional highs and the final impression. This psychological phenomenon, known as the Peak–End Rule, plays a major role in how users recall and evaluate digital interactions. According to Yablonski (2024), users disproportionately judge a product or service based on its most intense point and how it ends, regardless of the total duration or consistency of the experience. This has serious implications for design, especially when it comes to the use of timing, motion, and pacing throughout an interface. Smooth transitions, thoughtful animations, and a well-considered sense of flow don’t just make a product feel polished, they help shape a user’s long-term perception of it.

Well-timed motion can reinforce positive peaks or soften negative ones. For instance, Spotify Wrapped uses playful animations and personalized data to make users feel seen and celebrated at the end of the year. This emotional high sticks with people, encouraging them to share their results and continue using the app. Similarly, Duolingo enhances its language lessons with gamified feedback like streaks, animated milestones, and celebratory rewards. These visual cues are designed to coincide with moments of achievement, reinforcing motivation and making the experience memorable. Even subtle animations, like progress indicators or checkmark confirmations, can improve emotional recall by reassuring users that they’re on the right track.

Designers can also use motion to reduce the impact of negative moments. A loading screen, for example, that includes playful animation or a status update can ease the frustration of waiting. As Johnson (2014) notes, responsiveness and perceived flow rely on managing user expectations, and animations can help smooth out moments of transition that might otherwise feel jarring or disjointed. When motion is used with intention, it does more than decorate, it communicates, guides, and leaves an impression that lasts.

Designing with Ethics in Mind

Persuasive design is a powerful tool in user experience, but with power comes responsibility. At its best, it can gently guide users toward beneficial outcomes, like healthier habits or easier decision-making. At its worst, it can manipulate users into giving up their data, making purchases they don’t need, or feeling locked into services they didn’t mean to join. As Philips (2023) explains, persuasive design techniques, such as scarcity cues, social proof, and urgency, have become common in digital interfaces. While these tools are effective, their ethical use depends entirely on the designer’s intent and how transparently they are implemented.

The line between influence and manipulation often lies in the level of user autonomy. Take the example of a hotel site showing “only 2 rooms left at this price!” This could be helpful, offering information that supports a timely decision, or it could be misleading. Especially if the claim is exaggerated or fabricated. Such tactics are known as dark patterns, and they exploit users’ cognitive biases to achieve business goals at the expense of user trust. Social proof, for example, might show that “7 people are viewing this item,” but if that data is fake or irrelevant, it undermines the user’s ability to make informed decisions. As Philips (2023) warns, repeated exposure to these kinds of tricks can erode trust, reduce satisfaction, and even damage brand loyalty.

However, persuasive design doesn't have to be shady. When used responsibly, it can support well-being and positive behavior change. The Ultimate Guide to UX Design Principles emphasizes transparency, clarity, and consistency as key values in ethical UX (Fard, n.d.). For instance, apps that encourage regular exercise, medication adherence, or focused work sessions often rely on persuasive nudges like streaks, reminders, and motivational prompts. These features help users meet their own goals without deceiving them. What makes these techniques ethical is that they are aligned with the user’s interests, clearly communicated, and easy to opt out of.

Ethical design is not about avoiding influence altogether, it’s about designing interactions that respect the user’s freedom to choose. By being intentional about how we apply persuasive elements, and staying grounded in empathy, designers can create experiences that are not only effective, but also trustworthy and human-centered.

Applying UX Laws Thoughtfully

UX laws aren’t just abstract theory, they’re practical tools that help designers build more usable, intuitive, and respectful digital experiences. Throughout this post, several key laws have come into play. These laws include the Doherty Threshold, which highlights the importance of fast feedback (Yablonski, 2024), and the Peak–End Rule, which explains how emotional memory influences long-term impressions. Another crucial law, Fitts’ Law, introduced in Johnson’s (2014) work, reminds designers to consider the size and distance of on-screen elements. When targets are closer and larger, they are faster and easier to interact with. But even beyond these, one of the most user-centered laws we can apply is the principle of error prevention.

As Yablonski (2024) notes, great digital systems aren’t just reactive, they’re proactive. Preventing mistakes before they happen is more valuable than giving users good error messages afterward. This can mean confirming potentially destructive actions (like deleting a file), offering real-time feedback during form entry, or ensuring that buttons and labels are clearly distinguishable. Fard (n.d.) also stresses the importance of user control and forgiveness, like the ability to undo actions or easily recover from errors. These small safety nets make a big difference in how users experience a system, especially when trying something unfamiliar.

Applying UX laws with care shows respect for users’ time, attention, and cognitive limits. It turns theoretical insights into practical design decisions that reduce friction, build trust, and make technology feel more humane.

From User to Designer

Before taking this course, I didn’t realize just how much timing, motion, and feedback shape our digital experiences. What I’ve also learned, is how easily those same elements can be misused. One experience that really stood out was using a travel booking website. I was bombarded by urgency cues like “only 1 room left” and countdown timers. At first, it worked, I booked something quickly. But while working in this module, I realized those messages probably weren’t accurate, and it left me feeling tricked rather than helped. Now, after reading about dark patterns and ethical persuasion (Philips, 2023), I see that kind of design as more manipulative than helpful.

On the flip side, I’ve had great experiences with apps like Duolingo, where feedback is instant and progress feels genuinely rewarding. As Yablonski (2024) describes in the Peak–End Rule, those positive emotional peaks are part of why I keep returning to the app. It isn’t just functional, it’s fun and respectful. This course has really helped me connect those emotional responses to design choices, especially the importance of aligning features with the user’s goals and expectations. It’s made me more aware, not just as a student of UX, but also as a user who now looks at interfaces with a more critical, empathetic eye.

Conclusion

Designing a meaningful user experience goes far beyond aesthetics or speed. It’s about understanding how people think, feel, and respond in the moment and over time. Small details like timely feedback, smooth transitions, and respectful nudges, can shape emotional memory and build long-term trust. At the same time, ethical considerations must remain at the forefront of design and ensure that persuasive techniques serve the user, not the business. By applying UX laws thoughtfully and always designing with empathy, we can create digital interactions that are not only functional, but also responsible, memorable, and human-centered.
