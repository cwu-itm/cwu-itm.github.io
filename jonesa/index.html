I digress the education system was designed by people, and the idea was then brought to life. For these systems to come together to fruition, all the different elements need to be in place first. Three Life Cycle Models:

In abstraction, there are a few life cycle models in MBSE that I will discuss further in the blog. For now, I am touching and going over the concept of life cycle models in its entirety. In the words from Jon Holt in chapter four of Systems Engineering Demystified he talked about how these concepts are associated with ontology heavily (Holt, 2023). Meaning the technological “being” that exists broken into some subcategories (Holt, 2023). Including entities, life cycles, life cycle models, and the stage of said entity that evolves overtime (Holt, 2023). Without an entity all the other parts of the life cycle model will and always be irrelevant in my opinion. Because the evolution of the system/entity determines the stage then that will give you an accurate representation of the life cycle of that system and how far in development it is in (Holt, 2023). I digress on the definition of what a life cycle in MBSE is Holt’s words there are six stages using an SysML block conception, development, production, utilization, support, and retirement (Holt, 2023). Conception is identified with the needs of the system involved in stakeholders’ analysis to prove it (Holt, 2023). Development provides solutions for problems associated with the needs of preferred solutions often using prototypes before the stage of production (Holt, 2023). Production takes solutions and creates it in the system itself requiring various testing and trials to ensure the system is built and verified/validated correctly (Holt, 2023). Utilizations happen when systems are used by stakeholders and end users including the training of the stakeholders to know how to use the system effectively parallelly, with the support stage of next (Holt, 2023). Support entices all services with error, maintenance, and verbatim ensure that the system is running effectively (Holt, 2023). In sequence or not the retirement stage will follow as Holt discussed how and when the system will be disposed of and decommissioned safely and securely (Holt, 2023). Continuation, the fact that the scope of the system plays a role in the actual parameters of the system life cycle I found fascinating further in the chapter. Three different scopes being the project, program, and product life cycles (Holt, 2023). As Holt elaborates further, the Project life cycle is the evolution of a specific project possibly contained in multiples within a single system life cycle linked to projects and programs indefinitely (Holt, 2023). Program life cycles abstractly sit above the life cycle of projects kind of as a folder in your file explorer or a portfolio if you will of programs (Holt, 2023). Product life cycles are what is finalized and sold to stakeholders or customers and not all can be products (Holt, 2023). Because certain systems have different parameters and cannot run without others, buying them individually can lead to miscues with the system. Sometimes relying on each other to perform specific tasks or functions that the customer needs. Funneling into, life cycles cannot be without evolution of entities that is the structure (Holt, 2023). For life cycle models to proceed to proper stages there must be a base like a foundation when building a house. Furthermore, I will discuss in this mindful blog what I think Linear, Iterative, and Incremental life cycles are to me.

Linear:

Diving into linear systems model without using the words of Holt I would think being a straightforward line model. A step-by-step iteration following a logical form of steps. Holt specifies that the steps are executed in a linear sequence in other words a waterfall model (Holt, 2023). Specifically staged in order a point A to B model with no way of going back to the previous stages. (Holt, 2023). Dominalty used for projects where needs are well depicted and thorough, I compare it as an instruction manual to putting together a dresser (Holt, 2023). Which in fact the steps for an Amazon dresser are nice especially if you only bought it for 50 bucks. I assume that Amazon uses similar linear models or when discussing MBSE with system engineers. Linear models are not suitable for larger projects and longer ones as specified by Holt since the complexity of engineering these systems are vast (Holt, 2023). But you would think the linear model would be most efficient, but I do not deem it so.

Iterative/Incremental:

Linyanage wrote in an article published by Medium about the iterative model and how it affects software development. She elaborated on what this model is, meaning a system is developed in small sections or iterations if you will (Liyanage, 2025). Linyanage describes how this model works and makes sense on how she worded it. Iteratively it is cyclical involving planning, designing, implementation, testing, and evaluation and feedback (Liyanage, 2025). The moment an iteration is complete whatever feedback is received will help improve the next cycle (Liyanage, 2025). We as software engineers will repeat this until the cycles are complete leading to the desired results and quality (Liyanage, 2025). Diving deeper into the five ends of iterative models each having its own function in a system. Requirement/planning talks about what is needed for current and future iterations taking place in the starting stage (Liyanage, 2025). Designing initially what the architecture of the system will look like with flexibility for change and new features are implemented incrementally (Liyanage, 2025). When you study for tests the knowledge you obtain is in increments, I like to compare it as that. Implementation, being small parts of software during development are tested reducing in development time frames (Liyanage, 2025). Testing is basic just involves iteration revising during development including bug fixes, errors, improving the overall quality of the system (Liyanage, 2025). Finally, there will be the evaluation portion which is reviewed by iteration via a stakeholder, and this is where feedback will be provided to tend to customer needs and expectations (Liyanage, 2025). Ensuring the customer leaves happy and fully understood with how their product was made and its functionality.

Transgressing into the Incremental Life cycle models, Geeksforgeeks specifies that this model is used for systems in development in smaller parts that are manageable otherwise incrementally (geeksforgeeks, 2026). Building a system in smaller parts rather than larger parts sometimes less is more the way I see it. Advancing into the phases of incremental model I will touch on this briefly. You have four phases being requirement analysis, design and development, testing, and implementation like the iterative model but not quite according to geeksforgeeks. Requiring that the current increments are identified and gathered and design similar as before where new features are developed (geeksforgeeks, 2026). Testing is rather obvious which is making sure everything is up to code and things work perfectly (geeksforgeeks, 2026). Implementation includes the tested increments that are integrated into previous versions of the system and are delivered, and rinse and repeat these steps until the final system is completed (geeksforgeeks, 2026). Very effective when working with larger/longer projects I feel this would be the best choice. As an example, the gaming software development community especially with how the graphics are now. It takes a lot of technological resources and knowledge to complete down to the last animation. If a character in their video game moves incorrectly then the development team must then go back into the code, find the bug and fix it all together.

Subcategories in this incremental model include staged delivery and parallel development models each serving a unique purpose in MBSE. Staged delivery involves a sequence of software development in planned stages where each stage serves to the system (geeksforgeeks, 2026). As the system evolves overtime gradually it will lead to a completion of the product needed (geeksforgeeks, 2026). Delivered at normal intervals to make progress of system creation manageable and clear (geeksforgeeks, 2026). Versus the parallel development model kind of divides and conquers the system development in its entirety (geeksforgeeks, 2026). Occasionally branching off but multiple iterations are developed simultaneously by different teams of engineers (geeksforgeeks, 2026). Help reduce the workload I feel and overall product quality and the desired results the stakeholders are looking for. I also would not mind that considering making a system on a larger scale I can only imagine is harder to accomplish alone. But with proper teamwork and coordination the efficiency of that system development will follow suit. All depends on if you have the team that has the necessary skillsets to complete these tasks alone and stay aligned with the end goal of the best product quality.

Perspectives Of ISO 9241-210 & IDEO’s Field Guide:

Furthermore, the perspectives of ISO 9241-210 compared to the vision of IDEO’s field guide. By the sounds of it to me “IDEO’s Field Guide” provides mindset expansion. While “ISO 9241-210” gives us a more technical approach on human-centered design. Fundamentally the perquisite towards “ISO 9241-210” is as follows: “the system, product, or service design must base around the understanding of the users, tasks, and work environment” (Design, 1999). To provide and develop a solution you must collect feedback regarding solutions (Design, 1999). As further discussed, there are six steps in “ISO 9241-210” when it comes to human-centered design. “Planning the human-centered design process” involves the overall development process (Design, 1999). Basically, defining your roadmap if you will but with a purpose and each leading to the product. Next is to “Understand and specify the Context of Use”, is comparing your context to existing systems or similar ones (Design, 1999). Helping you identify weaknesses and determine what the future of your context will look like (Design, 1999). Examples being what characteristics fall on the users, stakeholders, goals of tasks, and the environment of the system (Design, 1999). Essentially the foundation and behind the scenes of your design. Pivoting towards “Specifying the User Requirements”, is split between users and stakeholders (Design, 1999). Involving different stakeholders leads to catering the user requirements to fit the needs of the stakeholders (Design, 1999). While one team of users may not agree with another, we must find a medium for implementation, ideally free from any contradictions (Design, 1999). Any new idea will bring new problems which ultimately will also lead to new solutions. All for the benefit of the user, stakeholders, and eventually the systems market environment. But how do users produce solutions for their designs? Users work alongside the stakeholders by using iteration showcasing “UI parts, simulations, or prototypes” (Design, 1999). Kind of like when you have a group class presentation as an example. Your project is your prototype and the fellow group members are stakeholders. They all must come to an agreement upon what the final design will look like. Hopefully eliminating all errors and leading you towards an effective final design implementation. One of the final steps of “ISO 9241-210” is “evaluating the design” in preparation for finalization. Evaluation follows this process, “getting new information, understanding strengths and weaknesses, checking user requirements, comparing variants, and benchmarking” (Design, 1999). Ensuring that all the elements of the design are met and fulfilled. In conclusion, “ISO 9241-210” is the showcasing of different representations, or different emphases on a human-centered design (Design, 1999). Like I said earlier comparing the “IDEO” perspective, to “ISO 9241-210” it is the mindset approach versus the technical approach. When “human-centered design” enters the playing field of the system design we can categorize our approach accordingly.

Perception And Visual Hierarchy Affects Usability:

Transcending into the human visual perception and attention influences the interface usability. Human visual perception varies in a few different factors. Every human being has different perception of how they see something. That is one of the very things that make us human. If we all perceived the same way the world would be a totally different place. Author Jeff Johnson of “Designing with The Mind in Mind” dives into this further. Our brain can “prime” our perception to be especially sensitive to what we are looking for (Johnson, 2010). When we single something out our brain focuses on that specific item. Example being when we are looking for something in the grocery store. The box of cereal that is the only thing left to buy we single it out on the shelf. And our brain ignores everything since we have only that one box of cereal in mind. Without us even realizing it we primarily just focus on what is needed. The way our perception affects us with a system design I feel is quite similar. Oluwatobi Akindunjoye a system designer wrote the article “Visual perception in user experience design”, backing this comparison up. He breaks up human visual perception in three stages “Feature detection, pattern identification, and object recognition” (Akindunjoye, 2019). Feature detection is how the human eye breaks down visual features like “colors, shades, angles, slopes, etc.” (Akindunjoye, 2019). It is a very sudden and quick detection our brain makes when something pops out at us. Pattern identification is how we compose those features into patterns or groups, and he briefly discusses the “Gestalt principle” is the idea we see the whole of something before the individual parts” (Akindunjoye, 2019). Like when we are shopping on Amazon, we see the entire page of items. Before we single out the “purse or speaker” we want to add to our shopping cart. Lastly the combination of the first two stages ultimately leads to “object recognition” (Akindunjoye, 2019). Information that is not immediately visible proves to eventually draw attention, which points how the interface is being used (Akindunjoye, 2019). How I understand it now is the perception of humans entices the focus of attention. Which leads to how the human centers interface usability. If something grabs the attention of the user quickly, or flashes out at them, that then forces the user to use the interface. Absorbing their perception fully because the design is catered to do so.

Four UX Laws, With My Analysis:

The finale being, “Four UX Laws and the application of design elements”. Starting with “Jakobs’ law”, is all about familiarity, users expect websites to be similarly designed and convenient (Stevens, 2024). Without reinventing the wheel making sure the design is fundamentally grounded and simplistic. ‘Fitts’s Law” considers, how quickly and easily a user can reach a particular target to interact with it (Stevens, 2024). How quickly can you add that new sweatshirt to your shopping cart? “Hick’s Law’ explains, the more options a user is presented with, the more complex the options are, the harder it is to decide (Stevens, 2024). With fewer options the user can make a quicker choice and vice-a-versa. And in the end, we got “Miller’s law” stating, the average person can only hold 7 items in their working memory, plus or minus 2” (Stevens, 2024). The goal here would be to have an organized and clean design to reduce the workload placed on the user. Allowing stress-free system interaction, providing efficient and easy access. The resolution being these four laws are all user-focused, and as system designers we must follow these laws. Because, at the end of the day the user is what makes the product. Since they will be the ones interacting with the system on a daily basis.

Cognitive Limitations with Digital Systems:

Introduction, cognitive limitations affect human interaction with digital systems tenfold. With more studies being released alongside the invention of new technologies, there is a concern about prolonged screen use. The article published by the “National Library of Medicine” explains how cognitive limitations play into this. A concept by the name of “Digital Dementia” came about in the year 2012 A condition that causes individuals’ memory and calculation abilities to decline due to excessive reliance on digital devices (Zeeshan Ali, 2024). Prolonging your engagement with such devices can lead to sleep disturbances, social isolation, mood swings, depression, increased stress, and long-term memory (Zeeshan Ali, 2024). Over time, these issues can decline your academic and occupational performance, increase your errors, and lead to chronic health problems and cardiovascular diseases. Excessive screen time can lead to a loss of brain growth and development. In the younger generation, studies have shown that this concept can also increase anxiety, due to the stereotypes around social media. Relying on technology can defeat the purpose of being able to develop your own thoughts. Individuals who experience this can also be at risk of laziness and negative emotional states (Zeeshan Ali, 2024). Affecting both the younger generation and the old, however, increasing creativity and digital literacy, but decreasing cognitive abilities, as mentioned previously. The “Natural Library of Medicine” suggests having a balance of technology use, via “Digital Detoxing” (Zeeshan Ali, 2024). Balancing in such a way to reduce cognitive deterioration and enhance cognitive functionality. Perfectly balanced as all things should be.

Recognition Vs. Recall Interfaces:

Furthermore, I will compare “Recognition vs. Recall” in interfaces with the help of the author “Jeff Johnson”. Chapter 9 of “Designing with the Mind in Mind” states that “Recognition is Easy”, but “Recall is Hard” and provides the differentiation (Johnson, 2010). Recognition is defined as “retrieving memories without perceptual support.” Essentially, perception and long-term memory work in tandem. Perceptions enter through our sensory systems, then their signals reach the brain (Johnson, 2010). Each of these signals then constitutes a different memory. Recalling involves the reactivation of long-term memories by old patterns without perceptual input. People can recall memories, so it is possible to have input from other parts of the brain. As “Johnson” says, timing and coordination are required to recall a memory. Today, we rely on more external sources to recall something rather than internal (Johnson, 2010). Interface design plays a big role in these two concepts; people recognize pictures very quickly, also enticing the recall associated with the information. Whereas recalling options for users to choose rather than being forced. An example of a recall, as “Johnson” mentions, is when a UI has a search box involved (Johnson, 2010). Moving towards “Tesler’s Law.

Tesler’s Law:

In Chapter 9 of “Laws of UX,” written by Jon Yablonski, he defines Tesler’s Law. It is a set of principles, standards, and best practices for defining the structure and behavior of interactive systems. Tesler realized that interface consistency would benefit not only users but also developers, because standards could be shared across software libraries. This enables developers to build their own applications by modifying the generic application in an object-oriented manner. One goal here would be to provide software to the standard of mass markets and reduce complexity for customers (Yablonski, 2024). One example would be writing an email requiring a sender and the message receiver. To reduce complexity here to a modern email client, the sender is pre-populated and provides suggestions for the recipient based on your writing style. The complexity is abstracted away to reduce the effort required by the user. Which was developed by a team that took the complexity into account. Yablonski also mentions that platforms such as Gmail leverage Artificial Intelligence to “Smart Compose” your emails. Saving you time when coming up with a message. Another example he utilizes is online shopping and how tedious it can be. To simplify this process for customers, a common way is to combine shipping addresses with billing addresses. This option simplifies the checkout process for the customers, preventing duplication of information input. Without the designers and developers, this option would not be possible. Lastly, Amazon has been able to incorporate this in its Amazon Go stores. If the user has the Amazon Go application installed on their smartphone, a customer can simply check in with the app (Yablonski, Laws of UX, 2024). Enter the store as they please, grab what they need, and walk out. The app will bill the information they have on file. The end goal of Tesler’s law is to reduce the complexity of digital systems as much as possible. To provide more ease of access for the user, designers and developers must take these complexities into account. To provide the best market available UI possible to benefit not only the customer but also the partnered business.

Principles, Supporting Memory/Reduce Cognitive Load:

Queueing up design principles that support memory retention and reduce cognitive load. Jon Yablonski wrote a blog post and defined what cognitive load is and the principles to reduce it. Yablonski explains the cause behind cognitive load as being too many choices, too much thought required, and a lack of clarity (Yablonski, 2015). Methods for reducing cognitive load include various principles. Avoiding unnecessary elements, any element that isn’t helping the user achieve their goal is working against them. They must process it and store it in working memory, along with things that will help them. Avoid excessive colors, imagery, design flourishes, or layouts that don’t add value. He says that simplicity comes with the caveat of not overvaluing it at the cost of clarity (Yablonski, 2015). Leveraging common design patterns when it makes sense, as Yablonski elaborates. Give the user familiarity with what they already understand, reducing the amount of learning. Enabling them to move along to achieve their goal. Eliminating unnecessary tasks, wherever you cause the user to read content, information contributes to cognitive load (Yablonski, 2015) . Minimizing choices, our working memory is limited when confronted with too many choices, and cognitive load will increase. Minimizing the choices the user must make is pivotal. Especially in locations such as navigation, forms, and drop-down menus. Displaying choices as a group, Yablonski describes that when choices are split into separate groups, users often make mistakes. Meaning the user is likely to never find additional choices that are available to them (Yablonski, 2015). Making it is very difficult to select an option because the user is not aware of other options. Striving for readability, making content readable is more than legible. The typography must be aesthetically pleasing, appropriate, and easy to read. Ensuring as few distractions as possible are in place for the user as much as possible (Yablonski, 2015) Helping the user better understand the content that is being presented to them. Final principle Yablonski believes is using iconography with caution. Iconography can be hard to memorize, increasing cognitive load. Icons do work well, however, Yablonski says otherwise because icons are subject to user understanding. He says it is best to accompany iconography with text labels to communicate the meaning and reduce ambiguity.

Interface Elements Supporting User Flow and Learning:

Starting with key interface elements that support user flow and learning. Learning and flow are universal concepts. Designing software requires an understanding of the user, their goals, and the tasks the tools are intended to support. Jeff Johnson discusses a few ways to achieve this in Chapters 10-12 of “Designing with the Mind in Mind”. Designing with a conceptual model that is “task-focused, simplistic, and consistent.” (Johnson, 2010). So that it minimizes the time and experience to use that application. One of the most important elements of a conceptual model is “object/action analysis”. Meaning the “declaration” of concepts that users are exposed to. Examples of this in an interface would be software dedicated to managing online banking. Including “transactions of deposits/withdrawals, checks have a number amount, and accounts of owners and the current balance”. Simple, so the interface has functionality. Having extra concepts in your interface design can lead to unnecessary complexity (Johnson, 2010). A “To-Do List application, search function, and a ticket machine at a train station offering tickets for other routes are some examples to consider. If the interface elements can't provide simplicity, then leave them out of the design. Because they will hinder the learning processes of users. The consistency of these elements affects how quickly a user can interact with a system without thinking, becoming more habitual than requiring monitoring while still learning.

Reducing the learning process while increasing the flow of information. Interface element consistency can fall under two levels, however: “Conceptual, and keystroke” levels (Johnson, 2010). The “conceptual” level is determined by the placement of “objects, actions, and attributes” of the conceptual model. Consistency at the “keystroke” level is based upon the mapping of conceptual actions and physical movement. Like how long it takes the user to locate the “checkout” attribute for an online shopping interface. Another element that can be used for flow and learning is an “object/actions matrix” (Johnson, 2010). To help better understand the actions that objects pose. Examples: lines, ellipses, arcs, rectangles, triangles, text labels, etc., just some related to a drawing program in specific. These objects require similar actions, so that will make them easier to learn. In the end, we learn faster when terminology is task-focused, familiar, and consistent. Task-focused is just taking what is challenging to users and bringing it to light. So that designers focus more on the task at hand rather than the technology. Familiarity comes with using similar vocabulary when designing. Unfamiliarity can cause people to not use your interface. Consistency can be a search bar being labeled “query” instead. It might take a user longer to realize that it is the object's action and vice versa.

Aesthetic-Usability Effect / Von Restorff Effect:

Now we are moving towards the “aesthetic-usability effect" and the “Von Restorff Effect". And how these concepts apply to perception, clarity, and recognition in digital systems. The origin of “Aesthetic-Usability Effect, Japanese researchers Masaaki Kurosu and Kaori Kashimura in 1995 (Yablonski, 2024). Investigating the relationship between inherent and apparent usability in digital interfaces. Correlation in people’s perceptions of visual attractiveness. Suppose a digital interface is not aesthetically pleasing. People’s usability rate will decrease significantly. An aesthetically pleasing design creates positive emotional responses. This can enhance people’s cognitive abilities. An example that Yablonski uses is a “Braun SK4 record player”. It's lavish ornamental wood, all-white design, and metal case to absorb sound. A Glass cover, turning this from furniture to a “beautiful and functional” standalone entity. Another example is Apple’s attention to aesthetics. People overlook the usability issues in the pleasing aesthetics of interface designs. Usability testing is an effective way to test design aesthetics. The end goal is to uncover problems or flaws. There are three phases of usability testing. “Planning and conducting the test, and synthesizing data”. Planning and conducting are straightforward. Plan your goals for what you are trying to accomplish when testing. What kind of task are the users trying to perform? Conduct users to perform realistic tasks. Avoiding bias because everyone performs at different speeds. Testing the design, not the user's abilities. Synthesizing data through observation. Understanding pain points, goals, priorities, interactions, tools, etc.

Documenting what every team member experiences running the usability test. Once again, collaboration requires no bias, so finding the medium. That, in turn, will improve the aesthetic quality at the cost of interface usability. Moving on to the “Von Restorff Effect,” German psychiatrist and pediatrician Hedwig von Restorff in 1933. Meaning, the study of how memory improves with items visually/conceptually isolated from others. In the technological era we live in today. Surrounded by distractions. Attention plays a pivotal role in how we perceive the world around us. Restorff mentions “Banner Blindness,” the tendency for people to ignore elements of an interface that serve no purpose. Patterns of interface designs that help people achieve their goals are more likely to grab attention. There must be a balancing act between drawing attention and directing people to valuable information. Being interactive interface elements, buttons, text links, etc. (Yablonski, 2024). Such as a confirmation button for account deletion. Both models serve the same function. However, one model lacks visual contrast versus the other. Having red accent colors within the element itself. The importance of what the interface is conveying. Color contrast and visual cues are used in moderation to improve clarity and recognition. Making these items stand out so they are easily identified. Leading to higher participation rates. Making your interface elements unique will significantly increase memory and clarity.

Postel’s Law:

I digress towards Postel’s UX law. Also known as the “Robustness Principle”. Formulated by Jon Postel, an early Internet advocate. This law is a guideline for software development, specifically regarding “TCP and networks.” (Yablonski, 2026). Essentially, early guidance for internet communication with different networks. Implementations should follow a principle of robustness: “be conservative in what you do, be liberal in what you accept from others. Programs sending messages to other machines should conform to the specifications. Program messages should maximize inputs to prevent system failure. In other words, be empathetic about the actions the user could take. Anticipate input, access, and capability while providing a reliable interface. With anticipation, the design will become stronger. Understanding user input and defining clear feedback. Meeting the requirements of the interface. With this in mind, we can ensure that communication on the early internet was reliable. The robust principle extended far beyond just network engineering. It also applies to software architecture. (Yablonski, 2024). One example of this is the declarative computer languages HTML and CSS. Being very adaptable when solving problems, they can be handled with flexibility. Yablonski also applied this to user experience design. And how we handle user inputs and outputs. But we must not assume things about the user. Everyone uses technology at different levels. Since humans and computers process things in different ways. It is the responsibility of the design bridge the gap between the two. Inputs and outputs in systems have evolved since the early stages of the Internet.

Postel’s law dictates that computers should be robust enough to accept varying types of human input. And not only making sense of it, but also processing it into readable formats. Another example would be Apple’s facial recognition software for its devices. This enables Apple users to authenticate themselves on their mobile devices without entering a username or password. In short, Postel’s law helps us bridge gaps between humans and machines. Taking human inputs and turning them into machine-friendly outputs. Allowing designers to build products and services that are adaptable and robust. Ensuring more human, user-friendly experiences. Allowing the user to return.

Interface Decisions Supporting Intuitive Experience:

Finalizing my analysis on how the interface decisions support intuitive experience. Princeton University in New Jersey wrote an article on this matter. An intuitive interface works the way the user expects it to (Kreitzberg, 2017). UX designers use research and prototypes to understand the user. Then center their designs around the user's mental model. People's mental models are their internalization of the situation. Consisting of how they view and understand the world. Ideally, you want your design to align with the user. Ultimately leading to an intuitive design. An example of an intuitive design is that a user has the prompt “update database”. The user has the idea of clicking the button. But you must also understand what updating the database means. Make it make sense for you and the user. Another example using real life would be going out to eat. What do you expect to happen play-by-play? You will be greeted, seated, handed a menu, order food, the server brings food, eat it, and then pay. Interface decisions support intuitive experience in the same fashion. A design that is not intuitive leads to more problem-solving that the user must do. The goal for this case would be to reduce cognitive load as much as possible. Procedurally, there are a couple of steps in assessing your users’ mental model. Conducting user research via a 1-1 interview to understand how the user works on relevant tasks. Providing you with how they conceptualize workflow. Another is usability testing. Observing users working on prototypes can help see their expectations. Especially if you allow them to express their thoughts while working. In conclusion, intuitive designs create happy users. It takes effort to create a design this way. But overall, this will reduce frustration and increase efficiency. To design intuitively is to seek and understand the users’ mental model. Through usability testing, you identify where your design has flaws. Ultimately leads to a design that matches user expectations. Put yourself in the user's mind. What would you want in a system design? Then put those thoughts into your design.

Interaction Timing, Motor Targeting, Peak-End Rule:

In terms of interaction timing, motor targeting, and the peak-end rule. To a degree, these concepts all involve user interaction with systems. Relating to Fitt’s Law one way or another. Interaction timing focuses on optimizing user speed, reducing cognitive load, and providing instant feedback to create seamless experiences. Key principles include minimizing action time based on target size/distance, keeping response times low (0.25) secs, and ensuring loading feedback has a 2-second maximum. The time to acquire a target is a function of its distance and size. Larger objects are faster to interact with, and smaller objects are slower (figma, 2025). An example is how long it takes for buttons to change color. Motor targeting is based upon efficient system interactions. The closer the target is to the cursor position, the faster it can be reached, so if your design layout makes certain actions more frequent, they should be placed closer to the cursor's expected position (mailchimp.com, 2001-2025). Increasing the hit area for smaller elements to provide more clickable areas. The Peak-End rule is that people judge an experience based on how they feel at its peak and end, rather than the total sum of every moment (Yablonski, 2024). Paying close attention to the most intense points and final moments of the user is key. Being able to identify when your product is helpful, valuable, or entertaining in design to bring user happiness. And try to avoid negative user experiences because people tend to remember those more. Some examples of the “peak-end rule” are understanding first-time user experience. If you can provide a simple model by infusing the right elements, then you are on the right track. Elements vary from illustrations, animations, and even humor. These types of elements can help defuse a stressful user experience. Again, back to a negative user experience. Can provide emotional peaks. Uber was able to correct this matter with their user interactions. By focusing more on people’s perceptions of time and waiting. I am sure everyone likes to spend their time efficiently. So, taking these thoughts into account can ensure a more comfortable user experience.

Slight detour on the origin of the peak-end rule. Discovered in 1993, originally titled “When More Pain Is Preferred to Less: Adding a better End. By Kahneman, Fredrickson, Charles Schreiber, and Donald Redelmeier, providing this evidence. Trialing subjects to two different versions of an unpleasant experience. The first trial had people submerge their hands in cold water for one minute. In the second, each subject used a different hand. Keeping their hands in the water for an additional 30 seconds, they lowered the temperature one degree. Subjects were then asked which trial to repeat. Concluding that subjects chose the longer trial because they liked the memory of it better. In other words, I disliked it a lot less than the alternative.

Interfaces Maintaining Responsiveness:

Transitioning to how interfaces can maintain responsiveness effectively. A method here is the “Doherty Threshold”. Productivity soars when interfaces and users interact at the same pace. To ensure that neither must wait on the other to perform. System feedback within 400ms to keep the user’s attention is the key. Using perceived performance improves response time and reduces perception of waiting. (Yablonski, 2024). Animations are one example to visualize people loading to different screens. Progress bars to help make waiting times tolerable. Filling out an application online can be dreadful. When the progress bar's accuracy is lacking. Purposefully adding a delay to the process. Increase value and trust perceptions. Even when these processes take less time than we think. Comparing this to real-world scenarios. Google searches predictive loading. Providing results in milliseconds, reducing the waiting time. TikTok/Instagram's infinite scrolling. Instant video playbacks and content loading prevent interruptions for the user. “Skeleton screens”, companies like Facebook and LinkedIn use gray boxes emulating content presence. Making the application seems faster than it is.

The list goes on and on. The efficiency of visual feedback, performance, and optimization is pivotal for the users. I digress towards the origins of the “Doherty Threshold”. In 1982, Walter J. Doherty and Ahrvind J. Thadani published in the IBM Systems Journal. Setting the requirement for a computer system's response time to be 400 milliseconds. The previous standard was 2,00 seconds. When a command by a human being is executed and returned in under 400 milliseconds. It exceeds the “Doherty Threshold.” Using applications in such a way is deemed addictive to users.

Ethical Implications Of Persuasive Design Strategies:

Understanding the ethical implications of persuasive design strategies. Ultimately leads to building trust, integrity, honesty, and respect for the user. Designing with the right intentions because there are two sides to a coin. There must be a balancing act between user manipulation and autonomy. Fundamentally aiming to influence user decisions, encourage specific actions, and achieve desired outcomes. Corporations commonly deploy certain techniques, such as scarcity, social proof, and gamification, to point users to desired behaviors. Like making a purchase, signing up for services, or sharing content on social media. Design in such a way involves intentionally shaping that user’s behavior.

Veering towards the darker side of persuasive design. Manipulation of the user to take actions that may not be of interest to them. Hiding unsubscribe buttons or making it difficult to cancel subscriptions. Achieving short-term gains for businesses. While eroding trust, damaging user relationships, and being transparent and honest. Ethical implications of persuasive design originate from its potential to infringe upon user autonomy and well-being (Walker, 2024). Exploiting cognitive biases and psychological vulnerabilities, persuasive design techniques can entice users into making decisions they may regret. Or manipulate them into behaviors that do not align with the true intention. One concern surrounding persuasive design is the impact it has had on user autonomy. Being able to make informed decisions free from external influences is an important principle of ethical design. When companies adopt persuasive design techniques, they inhibit users' ability to make their own decisions. Designing in such a way can also lead to negative consequences for user well-being. Example being encouraging addictive technologies or harmful behaviors. As before, comparing social media platforms to persuasive design. Meaning these platforms maximize user engagement and time spent. Raising concerns about the addictive attributes of social media. And how it plays into impacting people’s mental health. As it says, there must be a balance between manipulation and the autonomy of the user. Designers should leverage these techniques to create positive and engaging user experiences.

Transparency is the key when it comes to persuasive design. Ensuring users know that their behavior and decisions matter. In addition, designers must prioritize the well-being of users. Avoid using persuasive strategies that exploit users, making them feel vulnerable and manipulated in harmful ways (Walker, 2024). Prioritizing well-being can eliminate long-term consequences for the designer. This can involve ethnical assessment, soliciting feedback from stakeholders, and advocating for user-centered design practices.

Analysis On Feedback Systems With Error Reduction:

Concluding my analysis with feedback systems that support confidence and reduce errors. Chapter 15 of “Designing with the Mind in Mind’ by Jeff Johnson, “We Make Errors”. People make mistakes and commit errors. Designers must live with this fact. Avoiding designs that make users likely to make errors (Johnson, 2010). Mistakes and slips are the types of errors that people make in two categories. Errors of conscious decision and slips are unintended. A choice that presents itself has pros and cons. Mistakes are universal in life and apply to anything.

One here would be you buy a memory card for your camera online. It is incompatible with your camera. People make mistakes because they have an incorrect understanding of the choices due to a lack of information. Slips are something a person does not mean to do. Like someone falling down the stairs. Ideally, you would not want that to happen to you. Or when you reply to everyone on an email thread with sensitive information. When you intend to just respond to one individual. Slips are divided into eight sub-categories.

Capture, description, data-driven, loss-of-activation, closure, mode, attention, and motor slips. Here are some guidelines to avoid such errors. Capture is a sequence being executed. Like another more frequent or better-learned sequence. Your first day on the job. You click close on a project you have been working on for an hour. A confirmation dialogue box opens, asking you if you want to close without saving. Obviously, you would want to click no so you can save your work. Description is the right action on the wrong object. Having consistency that clearly distinguishes objects or areas that have different actions or gestures. Data-driven is external data interfering with attention. Guide users toward their goal. Making the path straightforward and not distracting. Loss-of-activation: the goal is lost from short-term memory. Provide memory aids and a clear display of system progress and status. Closure is the final step of a task dropped from working memory when the goal is achieved. Warn users of incompleteness, the engine left running, or the document left in the copier. Complete tasks automatically, modern vehicles turn off headlights automatically when the user exits the vehicle. People don’t have to remember to do it. Mode is the right action, but the wrong system status. Indicate a status that is clear and strong. Revert to normal mode after a timeout of more than 10 seconds.

The average unit task duration is when the user quits. Make modes spring-loaded so users must physically revert from exceptional mode to normal. Holding the shift key when working with specific functions. Attention is missing an important feature of the information presented. Direct users' attention to important details using visual hierarchy, perceptual pop, movement, vibration, or sounds. Motor is the user’s fingers, mouth, or legs not doing what is intended. Make click, tap, and swipe targets large. When buttons or links have graphic elements, they make both clickable to maximize the space. Long story short, to be a successful digital product or service. Designers help their users recover from errors.
