What is Systems Thinking?

Systems thinking is a problem-solving approach that views problems as interconnected parts of a system, rather than individual, isolated issues. As a result, systems thinkers must see and think beyond the symptoms of a given problem to examine the patterns, relationships, connections, and the influence that parts of system have on one another. There are six different characteristics that can be associated with all different types of systems: system elements, stakeholders, attributes, boundaries, needs, and constraints. System Characteristics

A stakeholder is, “the role of any person, organization, or thing that has an interest in the system,” (Holt, 2023). The role of the stakeholder is most important when identifying and considering stakeholders, as a single individual or organization can occupy multiple different roles as stakeholder, and there is no limit to the number of individuals that can occupy a single role. For example, as someone renting an apartment, I occupy multiple stakeholder roles in different contexts. I am a resident, primary maintainer, sponsor, and decorator, and I occupy each of those roles in tandem with my wife. My cat and dog also occupy the role of resident (often also decorator, much to our chagrin), and the leasing office also occupies the roles of maintainer, designer, and enforcer with lease rules or local regulations. As my complex offers low-income housing options, the U.S. government occupies the roles of enforcer and sponsor. My neighbors even occupy a stakeholder role, as they have an interest in the occupants of my apartment following the stated rules and expectations.

Attributes define and describe a system and its elements. They can be simple, such as dimension measurements, weight, value, or name, or can be complex, like data structures representing an entire MP3 or MP4 file. While there are multiple types of boundaries, it’s key that the boundary allows certain key aspects of the system to be identified: what is contained inside the boundary (system elements), what is outside the boundary (stakeholders & enabling systems), and key interfaces between the outside world and the system. The purpose of a system is determined by its various needs, usually defined by stakeholder expectations and tempered by the applied constraints. Constraints are the ways in which the system is limited in realization, such as quality, environmental, safety, or legal constraints that impose restrictions on the potential output of a system (Holt, 2023).

What is Linear Thinking?

Traditional linear methods of thinking provide a simple, step-by-step approach to issues to produce a clear roadmap and make outcomes easier to predict. Within simpler, straightforward issues, the linear approach can be an effective tool to reduce the issue down to individual parts for easier understanding rather than considering the entire system the issue exists within. Reducing the problem down to individual parts for separate analysis can be a useful tool for managing simple problems with seemingly simple solutions and can make communication of the cause-and-effect of these issues significantly easier (Johnson, 2024).

The Drawbacks of Linear Thinking

However, by focusing on individual parts and not the interactions/impact on other parts or relationships within a system, this can result in a disjointed outcome due to a lack of consideration of the system as a whole. For instance, in my opinion, the current state of generative AI is an example of linear thinking rather than systems thinking. The advancement of the capabilities and integration of GenAI is focused on short-term financial gain and data control and ignores all other impacts on the world and society. The push to integrate AI into every software, every system that humans use on a day-to-day basis without consideration of the impacts of its use on human cognition and interpersonal interactions shows an extremely linear thought process: GenAI companies are actively hemorrhaging funds, and rather than pivoting or taking a step back to consider any other viewpoint, they opt to triple down in their crusade for financial gain at any cost. If these companies were to approach this from a systems approach, there would be more emphasis on the creation of actual tools with reliable and logical outcomes. Instead, the current versions of most publicly accessible GenAI tools behave in a way that pushes users to engage with them more (see: ChatGPT’s current sycophantic bot model).

Linear thinking can fail to predict how seemingly small changes can have unexpected impacts on other aspects of an organization and can fail to notice the ripple effects as they occur. This method of thinking can also fail to notice or acknowledge recurring patterns over time and both positive and negative feedback loops (Holt, 2023). When a seemingly “simple” issue it addressed using linear thinking, unless the systemic cause or catalyst is also addressed, the issue is likely to continue to occur in different scenarios and may not be recognized as being related to the initial problem. This can result in repeated unsuccessful attempts to fix a problem that requires a systemic approach.

Linear vs. Systems Approach

As linear thinking is only effective in dealing with small-scale, simple issues, adopting the systems thinking mindset and model-based approach is imperative for complex issues or when designing complex digital systems. Model-based systems use digital modeling to create a visual and interactive representation of the various components and relationships between them. Using these models, simulations can be tested to explore the impact of various changes or issues without the risk of downtime or frustration for end-users. By utilizing a digital model, it’s significantly easier to keep record of changes and prevent inconsistencies through increased traceability. The digital model also helps to facilitate effective collaboration between individuals or teams by providing a single source of truth rather than the traditional engineering methods that rely on text-based documents and manual processes (McGrath & Jonker, n.d.).

The MBSE approach allows for the full simulation of a system prior to ever building it, allowing for thorough troubleshooting and examination of the various interactions between components. This not only reduces the potential time and financial cost of designing systems but enables more effective communication and understanding for stakeholders with less technical knowledge. These types of models are also effective for designing and managing systems that are composed of inputs from various other systems, as they allow for such complex and intricate relationships to be represented. While the MBSE is especially effective for larger and more complex undertakings, it can be adapted for projects of various sizes and complexities, as it can be scaled up or down for a wide range of applications (McGrath & Jonker, n.d.).

If a linear approach is used to design a complex digital system, this can lead to unexpected and potentially harmful complications, as each component would be structured independently without full consideration for how it may interact or impact another component. Through systems thinking, the interconnections between components can be better understood, leading to a more proactive approach to addressing issues, reducing the effort and downtime necessary to fix. When an organization encourages systems thinking, this enables individuals to step back and consider the bigger picture, which can ultimately lead to unconventional and innovative solutions that would likely not have come up in a step-by-step process.

As digital systems become more complex and interconnected, the traditional linear approach becomes less viable. Many industries are using the MBSE approach, especially for software engineering and development, including aerospace companies like NASA, automotive companies like Ford or BMW, the U.S. Department of Defense, and organizations in the telecommunications sector for developing infrastructure for emerging technologies and optimizing network layouts (McGrath & Jonker, n.d.).

The Systems Development Life Cycle

The Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC) is composed of seven phases: Planning, Analysis, Design, Coding/Development, Testing, Deployment/Implementation, and Maintenance. Each of these phases has its own objectives and outputs that impact the next phase, which altogether form a roadmap that balances stakeholder needs, project requirements, and customer expectations in the resulting deliverable. Through the SDLC, stakeholders can better estimate project costs, anticipated timeframes, and identify/address potential risk factors early in the development process.

Planning

The intention of the planning phase is to establish the goals and scope of a project. This phase is where the overall problem or opportunity is defined, often using high-level preliminary analysis. The analysis determines the potential benefit of implementing this project compared to the estimated time and resources necessary to see it to fruition. “Overall, the planning phase aims to both pinpoint the project’s goals and establish what the project does not need, which helps prevent it from becoming too bloated” (IBM, n.d.). This is also the phase where stakeholders consider alternative courses of action before continuing to the next phase of the project’s life cycle: analysis.

Analysis

Once a project successfully exits the planning phase, the development team begins conducting deeper analysis of user requirements and the creation of a practical implementation plan. Market research, feasibility testing, prototype evaluation, and resource allocation are all important steps of the analysis process. During this phase, various information is shared, including organizational data, cybersecurity regulations, available IT resources, or other requirements for the project across stakeholders and developers. While the planning phase establishes the “why,” the analysis phase establishes the “what.” This includes what features or functions must be included in the finished project, the types of data to be processed, and the rules that must be adhered to. One of the primary outputs from this phase (or from the previous phase, depending on the situation) is the software requirement specification document, which outlines the software’s functions, potential risks, project timeline, and the resources necessary to complete.

Design

During the design phase, development teams establish what the finished product will look like. They design and build the architecture, which may include diagrams, UI/UX mockups, workflow charts, and various prototypes to share with stakeholders and end users. This phase is also when development teams may decide which (if any) third party apps or services will be used, which technology stacks will be utilized, and what programming languages may be used in the development. The output from this phase is the software design document (SDD), which is used as a roadmap for the coding phase by the development team. The SDD generally includes a flow chart that lays out each step of the process and who is responsible for each task or phase of the process (Ungvarsky, 2024).

Coding/Development

In this phase, the development team finally begins writing code and building the software based on the guidelines and documents created in the previous phases. If the languages or technology stack was not specified in the previous phase, then developers would assess the needs of the project and determine the best options during the coding phase prior to building. This is also the phase in which developers would build any required additional systems for the software to run properly, like web pages or APIs. “Programmers design languages and software, database experts build the system that will house them, and network engineers begin putting together the hardware system that will support it all” (Ungvarsky, 2024).

Testing

Once the development team has created a functional piece of software, the testing phase begins, where they work to identify and eliminate bugs and issues from the final product. Testing is more of an iterative process than its own distinct phase, as it requires constant back-and-forth with the development team. When the testers identify a bug or issue, they document it and send the information back to the development team to fix, who then return it to the testing team to be tested again to ensure that fixing the issue did not cause issues elsewhere in the environment. There are various types of testing that may occur in this phase, including integration testing, system testing, unit testing, and acceptance testing to ensure that all components of the software work effectively and can function withing the organization’s existing IT environment. Security testing is also an important part of this phase, to identify and document any potential security risks to be fixed prior to rollout.

This is the phase I am most familiar with, as I spent a year as a project operations assistant. During that time, my job was to assess user feedback on issues or requested changes in the (VERY buggy) CRM software my company had invested a lot of money in. I would then attempt to replicate those bugs within a test environment and provide additional information for the developers using JIRA tickets. Once they fixed the bugs or released a new version of the software, my job was to try my best to break the software, identify potential bugs resulting from the changes, and return that information to the developers to address prior to the new version rollout. As the CRM software contained personal health information/records/payment information and was used daily in over 150+ audiology clinics across New Zealand for scheduling and all client interactions, it was imperative that the new version releases were free from obstructive bugs and didn’t interfere with operations. It was an extremely iterative process, with some larger changes taking multiple weeks of back-and-forth with the developers before we were comfortable releasing. Examples of various testing I performed included: clicking around the different tabs of the software as quickly as it would load to see if it would crash, processing test payments, creating new client records and editing existing, creating/editing/moving client appointments within the schedule to ensure that the established boundaries couldn’t be subverted (such as booking client appointments in occupied or blackout dates/times).

Deployment/Implementation

In this phase, the hardware necessary for the function of the software is installed, the new software is installed and information in existing systems migrated to it, and finally, the software is available for users. Sometimes this is done in incremental phases to minimize the risk of interruptions or issues from bugs, like beta testing or releasing a pilot version to a limited number of users to prevent large scale issues from impacting the user experience.

This is also a phase I have worked with, as software updates with significant user experience changes would be released to a set of pilot clinics for a week or two prior to releasing company wide. We would have regular meetings with these pilot clinics to receive feedback and address any issues they experienced; both with the implemented changes and with any issues they encountered that may have been a result of the changes.

Maintenance

Once the software has been deployed, the developers continue addressing bugs and user issues and monitor the system for any potential issues. Users are also often surveyed to provide feedback on whether the software meets their needs/expectations, and developers aim to ensure the longevity of the software by providing regular maintenance and support as needed.

On paper, this was my role as project operations assistant, but I performed the functions of the testing and deployment/implementation phases on a longer-term basis, after the initial software had been deployed. In the case of my company at the time, users were entirely unhappy with the new software and all the bugs and performance issues it had, so they worked to rebuild the software to meet the users’ and clinics’ needs.

Linear Life Cycle Models

Linear life cycle models, like the waterfall model, is a sequential development model where the previous stage is finished prior to the next one beginning. These models take a sequential approach, and are effective for smaller, well-defined and stable projects where stakeholders only want to be involved at key milestones of the process. This model allows for better predictability in achieving milestones as each phase is clearly documented, though it is also rigid in its process and is not suited for longer-term projects due to their changing requirements (Holt, 2023).

Iterative Life Cycle Models

Iterative life cycle models, such as Agile, involve several passthroughs on each stage of the process, as opposed to the linear model’s single pass through each step. This allows for a more adaptable process and can more easily accommodate longer-term projects or those requiring active stakeholder involvement. Each iteration of the process results in a workable version of the final product which can be deployed and tested. Unlike the linear process which results in a single final product, there is less risk involved for iterative projects; in the case that an iteration is entirely unworkable or breaks some other functionality, the system can be rolled back to a previous, working iteration until the next iteration is ready. One of the greatest downsides of the iterative approach is also an upside: while the iterations are intended to be shorter-term and easier to replace with a new iteration, this can lead to the focus being on releasing an iteration within a certain time frame instead of waiting for a version that works (Holt, 2023).

Incremental Life Cycle Models

Incremental life cycles are like iterative models, but with the emphasis being on making the product a little better each time. Unlike iterative models, incremental models seek to gradually build the system with active stakeholder input and with functionality at the forefront. Each incremental update is integrated with existing systems or with increments from previous iterations, resulting in a gradual evolution and providing tangible functionality early in the process (GeeksforGeeks, n.d.). This also makes incremental models effective for longer-term projects that require some amount of functionality early on in the process, but he approach is not suitable for systems that cannot be broken down into subsets, or separate increments (Holt, 2023).

Why is it Important?

Thinking in terms of a system’s life cycle instead of focusing on the finished product can be massively helpful for understanding the potential impact of the system itself on its environment and on its stakeholders. By approaching from a systems perspective, this allows for adaptability within the development process and more active stakeholder engagement. With how interconnected everything is nowadays, it is not sustainable to take a linear approach most of the time, as it does not consider the various interactions and relationships with other systems. Stakeholder needs are constantly changing, and iterative/incremental life cycle models allow for the flexibility to adapt with them. With how quickly digital environments and needs change, this approach is also imperative for the longevity and evolution of these systems. When taking a linear approach to a complex system, it is unlikely that the project will be considered successful, as they do not allow for much flexibility in the process. This would likely result in an unsuccessful attempt and require multiple iterations of this linear approach to meet stakeholder needs, which would skyrocket the budget and leave stakeholders feeling unsatisfied.

For instance, if a company uses a linear approach to create a website or an app, this does not leave room for adaptation to phone OS updates or browser updates that may impact the layout or functionality of an established website/app. Users may also be frustrated by a lack of compatibility with other functionalities on their phone or computer and refuse to use it. An iterative or incremental approach allows for user feedback, updates for new versions, and additional accessibility features as needed.

Human-Centered Design

At the heart of human-centered design is the belief that every problem can be solved – even seemingly insurmountable, systemic issues can be solved by working with the affected communities or individuals. Through a human-centered approach, problem solvers aim to understand the perspectives and needs of the communities they’re looking to serve and create solutions that address specific needs. Rather than having a linear plan laid out in advance, human-centered designers openly embrace creativity, experimentation, and failure, leading to an iterative process.

HCD Phases

Regardless of the challenge faced, there are three overall phases of the HCD Process: Inspiration, Ideation, and Implementation. In the Inspiration phase, the goal is to better understand the impacted people or communities through observation, interviews, and ethnographic studies to identify patterns in their experiences and uncover needs that they may not communicate directly. By the end of this first phase, the expected outcomes are a clear and thorough understanding of who you are designing for, the problems they’re facing, and why those problems matter.

In the second phase, Ideation, problem-solvers will synthesize their research from the Inspiration phase to generate ideas and begin creating prototypes. In the beginning of this phase, the goal is to generate as many ideas as possible: quantity is more important than quality at this point. Once the ideas are all listed, they’re pared down by evaluating them against accumulated insights and deciding which ideas will be viable to prototype to quickly test core concepts. Prototypes are tested with real users/stakeholders to gather feedback and refine concepts prior to committing to a single solution.

In the final Implementation phase, the emphasis is on iterating the chosen prototype into a finished product through testing with end-users, such as a pilot program to test for bugs prior to full roll-out. This phase is also where HCD meets the constraints of reality, such as budget restrictions, timeline pressures, or technical limitations. Using empathy and the principles established in the earlier phases of the process, the designers can conduct appropriate trade-offs based on what would matter most to users. The process is, however, not considered “complete” at launch – HCD teams continue gathering feedback, measuring impact, and making iterative changes and improvements based on user observations and feedback (Syracuse University, 2026).

HCD Mindsets

At IDEO, there are seven philosophies that set them apart from traditional problem-solvers: Empathy, Optimism, Iteration, Creative Confidence, Making, Embracing Ambiguity, and Learning from Failure (IDEO, 2015). Through the empathy mindset, designers can view from the perspective of the people they’re designing for, which allows them to free themselves from preconceived notions or old ways of thinking and centers people over institutions or profit. Optimism encourages designers to keep trying when faced with failures or dead-ends and embrace endless possibilities. Embracing Iteration allows HCDs the opportunity to explore, to try out various solutions without needing to have the solution first. “Instead of hiding out in our workshops, betting that an idea, product, or service will be a hit, we quickly get out in the world and let the people we’re designing for be our guides,” (IDEO, 2015).

Creative Confidence is the belief that any individual can be creative, and that creativity isn’t confined to artistic creativity – instead, it’s a way of understanding the world. Humans are inherently creative beings, and when coupled with creative confidence, create the belief that you can come up with creative solutions to big issues; you just need to get started. HCD is also rooted in the process of Making: turning an idea into a tangible object/product is much more impactful than presenting a theory. Many people struggle with visualization of complex theories or ideas, and the Make it mindset improves understanding and accessibility across users and stakeholders alike. Embracing Ambiguity is integral to human-centered design, as there is often not a single clear-cut solution when investigating a problem, and embracing ambiguity allows designers to innovate and test different ideas which also lends itself to the iterative process. Failure is an inherent part of the HCD process, and it is extremely unlikely that the first solution proposed/tested is going to be successful. Human-centered designers must Learn from Failure to create long-term, effective solutions (IDEO, 2015).

The ISO 9241-210 Standard

The “Ergonomics of human-system interaction—Part 210: Human-centered design for interactive systems,” is a standard established in 1999, but the basic ideals of it remain applicable today: to build interactive systems, products, or services that have good usability. Similar to HCD, the users along with their needs and requirements are central during development. As a result, the designers/developers must have a comprehensive understanding of users, tasks, and working environment, gained through personal experience and immersion into their experience. The process is expected to be iterative, with regular assessment of user feedback and ideas used to adjust and make revisions.

Unlike HCD’s three phase process, ISO 9421-210 is composed of five distinct steps: planning the design process, understanding/specifying use context, specifying user requirements, producing design solutions, and design evaluation. Overall, when compared to the IDEO human-centered design, the ISO 9421-210 standard is similar in ideals, but significantly more structured throughout the process. HCD doesn’t appear to have a particular set of steps to follow; just rough phases guided by principles. I would consider steps 1-3 as comparable to the Inspiration phase, step 4 to the Ideation phase, and step 5 to the Implementation phase.

Steps 1-3 (Inspiration)

In the first step of planning the design process, the designer seeks to determine the scope of the project and identify/define suitable methods/resources, procedures for integration, responsibilities, feedback channels, milestones, and the timing of the design process. At the end of this step, the result is a “construction manual” that significantly speeds up implementation and provides a rough plan for future steps. In the second step, designers analyze the current context of use to determine the potential future context of use. This can be through the investigation of existing systems that address a similar problem or observing how current users solve or circumvent the issue. The expected outcome of this step is a context of use description, which includes who the users and stakeholders are, the characteristics of users and user groups, user goals/tasks, and the environments in which the system will be used (How to "Do" Usability and UX Design). In Step 3, the goal is to formalize and define goals and requirements for both end-users and stakeholders. This includes external stakeholder requirements, such as legal or regulatory standards or organizational processes. When formulating requirements, the user requirements should be auditable and measurable, stakeholders should support user requirements, and the requirements should be free from contradictions.

Step 4 (Ideation)

During the production of design solutions, the intent is to create prototypes to realize ideas cheaply and quickly to garner user and stakeholder feedback for future iterations. This is also the step in which initial designers will begin communicating with the future implementers, which can save a lot of unnecessary work in the next step. The initial prototypes are often quite rudimentary and simple but increase in complexity and sophistication throughout the different iterations of user feedback (How to "Do" Usability and UX Design).

Step 5 (Implementation)

In step five, designers seek to evaluate the design solution with the help of user involvement, both in the form of written and active feedback. According to ISO 9421-210, the focus of design evaluation will be on the following things: gaining new insights on user requirements, defining the strengths/weaknesses of the proposed design solutions, auditing whether user requirements have been met, comparing variants to each other, and benchmarking for comparison later. At the end of this step, the goal is to assess whether the design solution is viable or if it needs to return to a previous step in the process before moving on. If no new user requirements have emerged, the current design solution fulfills all user requirements, and the overall usability is adequate, then the process can be ended (How to "Do" Usability and UX Design).

Perception and Visual Hierarchy in Usability

Perception bias can occur in many forms, including perception biased by experience, biased by goals, and biased by current context. As humans, we tend to generate “frames” for the expected layout based on our past experiences in similar situations or with similar products (Johnson, 2020). As a result, this can lead to users clicking on incorrect buttons or links on a website without reading them – they often act based off previous experience, the context of the interaction, and their expectations based on other websites they’re familiar with. According to the Laws of UX, Jakob’s Law states that users prefer that a website functions similarly to websites that they spend more time on, which is reflected in perception biased by experience (Yablonski, 2024). Many ecommerce websites utilize existing mental models of website designs to improve the user learning experience and maximize the likelihood that a user will return and spend more time there. “By conforming to users’ expectations about the process of selecting products, adding them to the virtual cart, and checking out, designers can ensure users are able to apply their accumulated knowledge from previous ecommerce experiences; the whole process feels comfortable and familiar,” (Yablonski, 2024).

Perception biased by goals is impacted by Miller’s Law in the Laws of UX. When biased by specific goals, users are unlikely to pay attention to or retain information that does not pertain to their specific goal at the time. According to Miller’s law, chunking information into more manageable and digestible portions significantly improves understanding and retention and makes the process easier for those whose perceptions are biased by their goals. For instance, instead of having to glance through a wall of text for specific relevant keywords or topics, chunking information and separating similar topics into visual hierarchies greatly improves navigation and information retention by improving the accessibility of information.

According to Fitts’s Law of UX, part of maximizing usability is to ensure that each interactive element on a page or display is clearly visible and distinct from its neighbors and are centralized at the main focal point of the display, which can be different locations depending on the context (for instance, pages optimized for smartphones will have their primary focus in the center or lower center of the screen due to the physical limitations of human hands). This also enhances usability in visual hierarchy – often, websites will have a navigable bar across the top portion of the display (underneath the logo) with touch targets that when activated, will open a temporary visual hierarchy of the different topics or pages relevant to the specified target to explore.

Fitts’s Law is also intertwined with Hick’s Law: reducing the potential choices or outcomes for a user significantly decreases decision time. This is also where visual hierarchy comes into play: by using various design elements to indicate importance at a glance, this improves user comprehension and enables users to make quick, informed decisions. If a website listed all potential touch targets and topics on their home page, the UI would become overwhelming and cluttered, and likely lead to users migrating to a different option with better design and readability. Utilizing size and scale in important elements, color and contrast to create emotion and clarity, structured typography for style and improving readability, effective use of whitespace to guide the reader’s eye, position and alignment of key elements to make the layout intuitive, and repetition and patterns to reinforce recognition are all effective ways to implement visual hierarchy for enhanced usability while adhering to the four laws of UX discussed in this paper (Gerencer, 2025).

Mental Models

Mental models are the mental pictures or internal maps that users create to understand how to interact with systems, regardless of accuracy. They are based on the user’s individual experiences interacting with digital systems, physical interactions with the world around them, and cultural expectations. In practice, a user’s mental model is a representation of what they know (or think they know) about how digital systems and interfaces operate (Chan, 2024). Systems and interfaces that use familiar concepts of mental models are more user-friendly, as they don’t require the user to learn novel model concepts prior to pursuing their goals and can be immediately productive based on their existing mental models. Interfaces that repeatedly disrupt the users’ expectations with unfamiliar models are more likely to have a negative reputation and decreased engagement due to negative emotional reactions from users (UX UI Principles, n.d.).

In the designing of digital systems, designers can use research on mental models to take an evidence-based approach to design best-practices to develop interfaces that work cooperatively with human cognition instead of against it. This not only aids in user adoption of new systems but reduces the overall cost of the design life cycle by avoiding costly redesign cycles (UX UI Principles, n.d.). In development, this requires consistent system behaviors where actions produce predictable results. This means that the user-facing interfaces should be designed with the users in mind, not full of technical details or jargon, and error messages should be in plain language so that users can easily understand how to fix it (or, in some cases, how to effectively communicate the issue to support staff).

Memory

While traditional views of memory separated it out into three distinct categories: Perception, where initial stimuli are received, then transferred to short-term memory, then transferred to long-term memory for later recall. However, recent studies have indicated that long-term and short-term memory are functions of a single system that is more closely linked to perception than initially believed (Johnson, 2020). In the design and development of digital system interfaces, this indicates an importance on UI elements helping users remember essential information from one moment or screen to the next. Due to the volatile nature of working (also referred to as short-term) memory, information can easily be lost if not contextualized or rehearsed enough. For instance, in my experience as someone who also has ADHD, I find that my brain will often drop things into the abyss, so I have established tools to help prevent frustration from it. To ensure I remember my goal when walking into another room, I will often mutter my task to myself or say it out loud to my wife so she can help remind me if I can’t recall on my own.

Working-Memory in UI Design

By providing essential information on the digital interface, this helps users achieve their goals with minimal frustration or run-around. Users struggle to keep track of what they’ve already done or encountered because their attention is focused on completing their goal and any steps taking to progress towards it. When designing a voice user interface (VUI), it’s best to limit the amount of information provided and establish a hierarchical system: distinguish the different possible goals that users may have when calling and guide them through the different options in smaller chunks to aid in memory. For example, when calling a bank, the initial options could be new member information, loan management, account management, business management, and investment management. From those options, if an account holder wanted to check the balance in their account, they would select the account management option, which may contain options like check account balance, check card status, request a new card, change account information, or report fraudulent transactions or activity. By separating the options into smaller deposits of information, this helps to prevent overwhelm due to too many choices up front. Similarly, if too many options are listed and the user is expected to select a number that corresponds to their goal in a list, they may have to listen to the menu multiple times to decipher which option is best for their case and what number they need to press.

When considering calls to action, it is also highly recommended to avoid overwhelming the user with more than a single call to action to achieve their goal. With the rise of advertisements being shoved into every interface, this is becoming more of a rarity: users are constantly having to close obstructive ads to even access the website. As someone who loves to cook, simply reading a recipe on a website is becoming a chore. When I click into a recipe from a search, I usually must also click off a pop-up for the recipe site’s mailing list, scroll past multiple unrelated advertisements and paragraphs of the recipe-holder’s life story with additional advertisements or sponsored product sections integrated into the text before reaching the actual recipe. Once I can view the recipe itself, it’s not uncommon for full-page ads to pop up if I linger on the page for an extended period, preventing me from reaching my goal. Not only that, but there’s now the additional cognitive load of determining whether the recipe I’m considering is illogical slop output by ChatGPT. My goal is simply to read a recipe for ideas and to determine ratios for ingredients, but I am constantly bombarded with unrelated calls-to-action that only lead to frustration and a refusal to engage further with interfaces like these.

Long-Term Memory in UI Design

In digital systems design, long-term memory is often unduly burdened by things like authentication, which are typically designed to be easy for back-end developers and automated systems to understand rather than for ease of use by the intended users. This is apparent in the design of verification questions being a set list of options that many users may not have solid, consistent answers for. This includes questions like the user’s favorite band/artist, their favorite dessert, their favorite way to relax, etc. Even questions that seem like they may have more consistent answers can create problems: I ran into this issue when trying to answer a security question asking where my wife and I first met. We technically met in Auckland, New Zealand, but Auckland covers a large portion of the North Island and contains many different cities and districts within. I tried every possible option I could think of (Auckland, North Shore, Warkworth, Birkenhead, Takapuna) and while they were all technically correct, I never did find out what the actual answer was – I had to reset the questions for my account instead. These interfaces also tend to recommend that the user writes down their answers somewhere, which is a terrible idea from a security standpoint and, in my opinion, negates the security benefits that the imposed authentication can provide. To prevent these issues, it’s generally recommended to allow users to set their own security questions or allow the user to create their own hints to guide them in the right direction without giving the answer away to a third party (Johnson, 2020).

Tesler's Law in UI Complexity

According to Tesler’s Law, every system has a certain amount of complexity that cannot be reduced (Yablonski, 2024). This means that while designers and developers can shift the burden of complexity from end-users to the back end, there will always be some portion that cannot be reduced or adjusted and will impact users. In retail establishments, there is significant innovation in methods of shifting complexity from users to the back-end design/development teams. In interfaces like e-commerce stores, for instance, companies aim to reduce the complexity and input required to complete a purchase for users by increasing complexity for developers. This is seen in the carry-over of billing address information to the shipping address, or browsers storing card information to autofill the payment portion. Or, in Amazon’s case, the addition of the “Buy Now” button – this process completes the product purchase using the customer’s saved default payment method and their default shipping/billing information to reduce the purchasing process to a single step on the user’s side. This requires that the development team shoulders the complexity of designing systems that enable UX simplicity to this degree.

Implementing techniques such as Progressive Disclosure can also help in reducing the user’s complexity and cognitive load by displaying only important information or actions by default, with additional features and content easily accessible from the main menu. This reduces the overall cognitive load for users by reducing clutter and preventing overwhelm. Using tools such as visual hierarchies to allow for easy navigation of a website’s offerings creates a more streamlined experience for users without removing the functionality offered by the site itself. On ecommerce websites, a product’s primary blurb may include a few key features, but the entire list of features and specifications may be on a different page or under a dropdown button further down the page. Progressive disclosure is especially important to mobile app and website design due to the space limitations imposed by mobile phone screen sizes. By deferring secondary material to another location and limiting the main page to only essential material, this helps focus user attention where it matters. If the main page is too dense with content, then users are more likely to bounce to other options rather than sift through secondary content to achieve their goals.

Aesthetic-Usability Effect

The layout of any given digital interface is integral to achieving user buy-in. Both aesthetic design and intuitive functionality are important; and they impact each other. According to the aesthetic-usability effect, “an aesthetically pleasing design creates a positive response in people’s brains and leads them to believe the design actually works better,” (Yablonski, 2024). As a result of this effect, an aesthetically pleasing interface with moderate usability issues is more likely to be viewed favorably than a similarly usable interface with a less-than pleasing aesthetic design. Studies have even shown that uses form an opinion regarding a website within about 50 milliseconds of seeing it, based mostly on visual appeal, and that opinion rarely changes as users spend more time on the site (Yablonski, 2024). This indicates that users rarely are influenced to sway from their first impressions, highlighting the importance of aesthetically pleasing design in interface layouts.

One of the primary downsides of the aesthetic-usability effect is that users’ tendency to be more forgiving with attractive interface designs can result in usability issues going unnoticed when conducting usability testing. During usability testing, identifying any potential issues is crucial, so users/testers overlooking issues due to the aesthetically pleasing interface can cause problems in the long run when the product is released, leading to costly fixes and adjustments that could have been fixed before it was a system-wide issue. This can also lead to a loss of confidence and a negative reputation for the company itself due to affected customers/users.

Von Restorff Effect

According to the Von Restorff effect (also known as the isolation effect or unusual effect), components or experiences that stand out are more likely to be remembered. In digital interfaces, this is commonly achieved through using color/contrast, using different typography attributes (font style, size, and weight), icons, and movement or animations. Methods such as applying a high-contrast color to a particular button or using bold text to draw the user’s eye are not only good practices in digital design but also are more likely to be remembered once the user has left the site. By having eye-drawing components across the page, this also helps to drive user engagement and aid in achieving user goals while reducing their cognitive load (GeeksforGeeks, 2024). However, it is equally important to avoid over-use of the Von Restorff effect. If every component on a website is displayed with high importance, then users are more likely to bounce due to overwhelming site design and difficulty in navigation to achieve their goals. By integrating the Von Restorff effect to highlight important information or interactable components, this helps with user navigations and encourages them to remain on the webpage for longer to explore.

Due to humans’ limited attention, the Von Restorff effect is extremely helpful in highlighting critical information without causing visual overwhelm. However, also due to the limited attention capacity of humans, users are likely to engage in a behavior known as “banner blindness.” This is the tendency to disregard elements of an interface that they identify as advertisements. These can be actual ads, website components that are in close proximity to advertisements (also an example of the Gestalt principle of proximity), or simply content that resembles an advertisement. As advertisements are often visually striking and tend to be more “in-your-face” than typical website content, components highlighted using similar techniques to catch user attention are likely to be disregarded entirely unless/until the user takes a closer look to investigate.

Postel's Law

Postel’s Law states, “Be conservative in what you do, be liberal in what you accept from others,” (Yablonski, 2024). In digital design, this translates to designing systems that are empathetic to, tolerant of, and flexible regarding any of the potential actions that a user may take while maintaining strict guidelines/expectations for system output and response. Humans are mistake-prone, distracted, emotion-driven, and inconsistent, and expect that the systems we interact with to understand us and be forgiving when we make mistakes. By being liberal in what the system accepts from users, this includes various device sizes and types, feature support, input from assistive technologies, connection speed, and input mechanism, which should all be served a version of the digital interface that works. Implementing strict formatting rules on user input fields may seem beneficial but can cause confusion and frustration based on user expectations and cultural differences. Forms containing strict input rules are likely to cause issues for individuals who may not have a first, middle, and last name, or result in a user inputting their birthday in an incorrect format for the form if they’re from outside the U.S. (DD/MM/YYYY vs. MM/DD/YYYY).

Regarding the first half of Postel’s Law, “be conservative in what you do,” this is typically reflected in what the system expects from user input. For instance, when designing online forms to gather information from users, it’s best to minimize the number of fields to fill out and ask only for information that is absolutely necessary. It is also best to avoid doubling up on information, when possible, to reduce the users’ cognitive load (for instance, not asking users to duplicate information on different forms) (Yablonski, 2024). Humans also have the tendency to learn the wrong lesson from making mistakes because they lose track of the specific actions they took up to that point that may have led to the issue (Johnson, 2020). By being conservative in what we do, as designers, this means that we need to give very clear feedback in plain language to guide the user on how to fix the problem. Clarity in input field text is another way in which designers can be conservative in action: use terminology consistently across any interface. If the site uses a username/password system to log in, it’s most effective to be clear in whether the username is self-chosen or the user’s email/phone number instead. This reduces the cognitive load on the user and aids in achieving their goals on the site rather than having to decipher the inputs necessary to simply login.

Failure to Adhere

When a UI is not intuitively designed and lacking in accessibility, this can cause significant frustration and demotivate users from engaging further with the interface. As someone who is pretty good with troubleshooting and figuring out why things don’t work, I don’t tend to remember bad/non intuitive designs unless they’re really bad. My first example was from a few years ago while trying to access old tax documents from TurboTax. I was able to login and found where the archived tax returns should have been, but I was unable to access them on my browser. I tried multiple troubleshooting steps – restarting my computer, verifying my internet connection, refreshing and ensuring I was logged in – but I was still unable to access my documents. I called their support line, and the first time a representative picked up, the call dropped so I had to wait another 30 minutes in the queue, which added to my frustration. Once I finally got on the line with a representative, they ran me through the troubleshooting steps I’d already tried, and both of us were becoming more frustrated that none of the steps were working. They opened the files without issue on their end, and every time I tried, nothing showed up. After 15 minutes or so of back-and-forth, trying to figure out this issue, we realized it’s simply due to the browser I was using not being supported by their software. I was using Microsoft Edge at the time, and that browser just didn’t function with their site, but that information was not surfaced, nor was it a troubleshooting step that the representative brought up. There’s very little chance that I was somehow the first or only individual to encounter this problem; especially since Microsoft Edge was the third most popular browser at the time and functions on the same foundation (Chromium) as the Google Chrome browser window I had to use to access my files. So, I spent over an hour in frustration due to their failure to effectively test usability features on various browsers and ensure that the content is accessible under Postel’s Law. Since that issue, I have not engaged further with TurboTax (due to that situation and to their continued lobbying of the U.S. government to prevent tax filings from being free and accessible) and have no reason to engage with the company or any of their products any further.

I had a similarly frustrating experience when trying to sign up for a free trial of an app last year. I wanted to sign up for a free 10-day trial to try out a journalling app that my wife recommended, had to input my card information for after the trial completed, and was immediately charged $120 for the full year’s subscription. Nowhere on the interface was I warned that the full amount was to be taken immediately and that I somehow was ineligible for the free trial, and I had initiated the process by clicking on a button that offered the free trial. Fortunately, I was able to get my money refunded by Google Play, but it was a frustrating and stressful experience that was overcomplicated by unclear design and the interface’s failure to communicate clearly. I also stopped using this app after this experience.

Conclusion

UI/UX design is pivotal in shaping user adoption/engagement and the overall user experiences in digital interfaces. By designing effective UI, this not only enables user task completion, but can also lead to enhanced user trust and engagement. However, it is equally important to consider the ethical implications and potential risks of utilizing persuasive design techniques, which aim to influence user behavior, interactions, emotions, and attitudes to push them towards a specific action or outcome. They are effective for driving engagement, but they can also raise ethical concerns regarding user autonomy, data privacy, and user well-being. Designers must ensure they aim to respect user autonomy, protect user privacy, mitigate harmful effects, promote ethical design practices, and engage in ethical reflection to ensure that user well-being and fostering meaningful engagement is prioritized over time spent on the website/app (Priyadarshini, 2024). UI design has far-reaching implications for everyone, including those with disabilities, older generations, and individuals from diverse cultural backgrounds.

Responsiveness

Responsiveness is the system’s ability to keep up with users, keep them informed regarding its status, and not make them wait unexpectedly, and over the past few decades, researchers have found that it’s extremely important to determine user satisfaction. It’s typically measured in terms of human time requirements/expectations and user satisfaction, and systems can be considered responsive in spite of low performance. If a system cannot fulfill a user’s request immediately, it can keep the user informed and provide feedback on what the user has done, what is happening, and prioritize feedback based on human deadlines and expectations (Johnson, 2020). For instance, if a user initiates a download of a large file, the website will typically show some sort of progress bar with an estimated time to completion and a description of the file itself.

In Firefox, when downloading a file, there will typically be a pop-up with the file description, and it asks the user what to do with the requested file: Open with: ____ or save the file. When saving the file, there will be an additional widget that appears in the top bar of the browser to cache the status of the download queue, which can run in the background while the user focuses on a different task. An example of bad responsiveness, however, is in the current iteration of Windows Updates. While Windows is performing an update, there is sometimes a progress percentage indicator, but no estimation of time to completion and no ability to multitask while updates are occurring. Not only is the system locked during updates, but the user is not in control of the updates, and they can occur without user initiation on a system reboot. As a user, this has caused me frustration when intending to perform a quick reboot to finish an installation or to address performance issues, but Windows decides it’s time for an update and renders my pc unusable for an unknown amount of time. However, other examples of poor responsiveness in design include delayed feedback for user interactions (scroll movement, button presses, or object manipulations), time-consuming and system-monopolizing operations that the user cannot abort or pause, jerky, sudden, or hard-to-follow animations, or ignoring user inputs while performing background housekeeping tasks (Johnson, 2020).

Peak-End Rule
A user’s overall impression of an experience (or digital interface/system) is based primarily on their emotional peak and the final moments of the interaction rather than considering the entire duration. Rather than recollecting the experience as a cohesive video, their brain takes “emotional screenshots” of their experience and uses them to determine whether they’d engage in that experience again or recommend it to others. This means that while a user may have had a positive/neutral experience for most of their interaction on a website, a single frustrating moment or being unable to accomplish their goal according to their expectations may be enough to color their experience in a negative light. As humans tend to recall negative experiences more vividly than positive ones, this highlights the importance of paying close attention to the most intense points of user interaction along with the final moments of their journey to establish and ensure user satisfaction (Yablonski, 2024).
Journey Mapping
One of the methods to aid in easing the user’s journey through your website/app is called Journey Mapping: this process helps to visualize how users will interact with a product or service and accomplish certain tasks or goals. These journey maps are typically composed of three different key elements: the lens, experience, and insights. The lens establishes the perspective of the user represented in the journey and lists the specific scenario and expectations of specified user. The experience portion depicts the mindset, actions, and emotions of the end-user across their journey timeline, and provides a sort-of “emotional overlay” on top of their actual digital journey to enhance contextual understanding and determine the journey’s emotional peaks. Depending on the intent of the journey mapping, this step may also include information from the user, such as general feedback, pain points, questions, or motivations that may affect their overall experience. In the final part of the journey, important takeaways are identified and are often listed as opportunities to improve the overall experience, also known as insights (Yablonski, 2024).

Doherty Threshold

The Doherty Threshold dictates that productivity improves when neither the computer nor its users must wait on each other (the actual threshold is around 400ms response time). Ideally, when a user initiates an interaction on a website or app, the system should load the request within 400ms. However, in some cases, this goal is not attainable due to the size or complexity of the content or request, so in these cases the designers should implement some method of feedback to communicate to the user that their request is acknowledged and in-process within that 400ms. This can be in various different forms, including skeleton screens, which display a rough outline of the overall webpage while loading in content as it becomes available, rendering small versions of images while the full resolution version loads, progress bars, or progress animations to indicate forward momentum (Yablonski, 2024). By providing some amount of system feedback to users while they wait for their request to be processed, this helps alleviate impatience, frustration, and uncertainty for the user themselves. In early Windows systems, the default cursor settings included a separate animation to indicate when an application was in the process of performing an operation. While this is still active in Windows’ default settings today, neither the hourglass (flipping or stationary), nor the blue circle are really seen anymore – even when a process or application is taking a longer time to load. As a kid on the internet (starting with Windows 95) I could tell when a webpage was broken or failed to load when the hourglass icon was replaced by the default cursor and nothing changed on the screen. Nowadays on my Windows 11 pc, it is much more difficult to tell the status of an action without additional insight (i.e. checking the refresh button on the browser to stop or cancel the page loading).

Motion

Motion is a key component of enhancing user experience and is a great way to communicate feedback to users. By utilizing motion design and animation, designers can show visual cues to inform users about the state of the system in response to the user’s actions. This makes digital interactions feel more intuitive and satisfying. In digital design, motion, animations, and micro-interactions can be used to transmit emotions, inject personality into the interface, and make user interactions more enjoyable. Micro-interactions are small, purposeful animations to express the success of a completed action, validate user inputs, or provide status updates. They add a layer of interactivity and responsiveness to user interactions and help users feel more connected to the system (Pérez Souto, 2023). Micro-interactions can come in many forms, such as a button changing color when clicked, positive feedback animations like Canvas’ confetti animation when a student submits an assignment, and dynamic loading animations to transform wait/load times into informative and engaging experiences.

Motion and animation can be used effectively in user onboarding and education regarding the platform. Interactive tutorials or guided tours enhanced by animation and motion help users to familiarize themselves with the features and functionalities of the platform, along with important navigational information. By making walkthroughs and learning opportunities interactive and visually engaging, this makes the learning process more intuitive and enjoyable for users and reduces frustration and abandonment (Oumous, 2024). Animation in digital design is also an effective method for guiding the attention of users to desired pathways, which is an example of the Von Restorff Effect: the object that stands out from a group of like-objects is more likely to be remembered. For an e-commerce website, using subtle animation or highlighting the “Check out,” button is effective for catching the user’s eye and influencing them towards the completion of their transaction. However, it is equally important to avoid over-using animation or motion in digital design to prevent user overwhelm due to conflicting messaging, visual clutter, or system slow-down. Animation is a great tool in digital design, but it needs to be used with intention.

Persuasive Design Techniques

Incorporating UI design best-practices also requires that designers be aware of and put user well-being at the forefront of their design process to prevent ethical issues later. Respecting user autonomy is an important ethical consideration, and designers must ensure that their design choices are transparent, respectful, and aligned with the values and interests of their users. Designers should also avoid manipulative or coercive tactics and practices that undermine user autonomy or exploit user vulnerabilities. Designers must also design with user privacy in mind and aim to safeguard user data by implementing privacy-by-design principles, remain transparent about data collection practices, obtain informed consent from users, and provide users with a way to adjust and control their own privacy settings. This also includes the default security and privacy settings, which many users will never even look at or change (Yablonski, 2024). By making the default settings emphasize user and data privacy and giving user the ability to opt-out of restrictive settings, this protects users that may never seek to change the settings while providing flexibility to those that prefer it. Designers have the ethical responsibility of assessing the potential impact of design interventions on their users’ physical, mental, and emotional well-being and, “avoid design patterns that promote addictive behaviors, foster negative emotions, or exacerbate social comparison and self-esteem issues” (Priyadarshini, 2024). On an organizational scale, designers should seek to promote ethical design practices and foster a culture of ethical design within the organization. This includes promoting awareness, education, and discussions around ethical considerations in UI design and critically evaluating the ethical considerations of proposed decisions to prioritize those that align with ethical best-practices. The organization should also engage in ethical reflection and seek input from diverse stakeholders (including users, ethicists, and advocacy groups). The goal should be to consider the long-term effects or design choices and weigh the potential benefits against the potential for harm.

Friction

In digital design, friction is incredibly important, and a lack of friction can lead to severe ethical considerations due to user addiction and a lack of consent in media consumption. So many digital systems are specifically designed to maximize user attention and time spent on the site to increase profits from advertisements, and it is undoubtedly impacting many peoples’ attention span and mental/emotional/physical well-being. Most social media sites have removed as much friction as possible in their designs (infinite scrolling feeds and auto-playing videos without user involvement), without consideration or acknowledgement of their users’ best interests, or whether they care about their users at all (spoiler alert: they don’t). Many of these social media companies also started off with a more restricted, user-centric design, such as Facebook, which would only display posts from other users/groups that the user had intentionally connected with. Now, however, the Facebook feed is composed of about a third of the content on a user’s feed is from actual, intentional connections, a third of it is “recommended” content and reels, and the other third is just ads. Facebook/Instagram Reels and TikTok are designed to be infinitely scrollable to maximize user time spent on the platform, regardless of the impact on the user’s emotional or mental well-being. Digital designers must work to bring UX/UI design back from the hands of the advertisers and from the corporate bigwigs looking to line their pockets with the outrage, despair, and emotional destruction of its users.
