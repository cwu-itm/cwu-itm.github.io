What is Systems Thinking?

Systems thinking is a problem-solving approach that views problems as interconnected parts of a system, rather than individual, isolated issues. As a result, systems thinkers must see and think beyond the symptoms of a given problem to examine the patterns, relationships, connections, and the influence that parts of system have on one another. There are six different characteristics that can be associated with all different types of systems: system elements, stakeholders, attributes, boundaries, needs, and constraints. System Characteristics

A stakeholder is, “the role of any person, organization, or thing that has an interest in the system,” (Holt, 2023). The role of the stakeholder is most important when identifying and considering stakeholders, as a single individual or organization can occupy multiple different roles as stakeholder, and there is no limit to the number of individuals that can occupy a single role. For example, as someone renting an apartment, I occupy multiple stakeholder roles in different contexts. I am a resident, primary maintainer, sponsor, and decorator, and I occupy each of those roles in tandem with my wife. My cat and dog also occupy the role of resident (often also decorator, much to our chagrin), and the leasing office also occupies the roles of maintainer, designer, and enforcer with lease rules or local regulations. As my complex offers low-income housing options, the U.S. government occupies the roles of enforcer and sponsor. My neighbors even occupy a stakeholder role, as they have an interest in the occupants of my apartment following the stated rules and expectations.

Attributes define and describe a system and its elements. They can be simple, such as dimension measurements, weight, value, or name, or can be complex, like data structures representing an entire MP3 or MP4 file. While there are multiple types of boundaries, it’s key that the boundary allows certain key aspects of the system to be identified: what is contained inside the boundary (system elements), what is outside the boundary (stakeholders & enabling systems), and key interfaces between the outside world and the system. The purpose of a system is determined by its various needs, usually defined by stakeholder expectations and tempered by the applied constraints. Constraints are the ways in which the system is limited in realization, such as quality, environmental, safety, or legal constraints that impose restrictions on the potential output of a system (Holt, 2023).

What is Linear Thinking?

Traditional linear methods of thinking provide a simple, step-by-step approach to issues to produce a clear roadmap and make outcomes easier to predict. Within simpler, straightforward issues, the linear approach can be an effective tool to reduce the issue down to individual parts for easier understanding rather than considering the entire system the issue exists within. Reducing the problem down to individual parts for separate analysis can be a useful tool for managing simple problems with seemingly simple solutions and can make communication of the cause-and-effect of these issues significantly easier (Johnson, 2024).

The Drawbacks of Linear Thinking

However, by focusing on individual parts and not the interactions/impact on other parts or relationships within a system, this can result in a disjointed outcome due to a lack of consideration of the system as a whole. For instance, in my opinion, the current state of generative AI is an example of linear thinking rather than systems thinking. The advancement of the capabilities and integration of GenAI is focused on short-term financial gain and data control and ignores all other impacts on the world and society. The push to integrate AI into every software, every system that humans use on a day-to-day basis without consideration of the impacts of its use on human cognition and interpersonal interactions shows an extremely linear thought process: GenAI companies are actively hemorrhaging funds, and rather than pivoting or taking a step back to consider any other viewpoint, they opt to triple down in their crusade for financial gain at any cost. If these companies were to approach this from a systems approach, there would be more emphasis on the creation of actual tools with reliable and logical outcomes. Instead, the current versions of most publicly accessible GenAI tools behave in a way that pushes users to engage with them more (see: ChatGPT’s current sycophantic bot model).

Linear thinking can fail to predict how seemingly small changes can have unexpected impacts on other aspects of an organization and can fail to notice the ripple effects as they occur. This method of thinking can also fail to notice or acknowledge recurring patterns over time and both positive and negative feedback loops (Holt, 2023). When a seemingly “simple” issue it addressed using linear thinking, unless the systemic cause or catalyst is also addressed, the issue is likely to continue to occur in different scenarios and may not be recognized as being related to the initial problem. This can result in repeated unsuccessful attempts to fix a problem that requires a systemic approach.

Linear vs. Systems Approach

As linear thinking is only effective in dealing with small-scale, simple issues, adopting the systems thinking mindset and model-based approach is imperative for complex issues or when designing complex digital systems. Model-based systems use digital modeling to create a visual and interactive representation of the various components and relationships between them. Using these models, simulations can be tested to explore the impact of various changes or issues without the risk of downtime or frustration for end-users. By utilizing a digital model, it’s significantly easier to keep record of changes and prevent inconsistencies through increased traceability. The digital model also helps to facilitate effective collaboration between individuals or teams by providing a single source of truth rather than the traditional engineering methods that rely on text-based documents and manual processes (McGrath & Jonker, n.d.).

The MBSE approach allows for the full simulation of a system prior to ever building it, allowing for thorough troubleshooting and examination of the various interactions between components. This not only reduces the potential time and financial cost of designing systems but enables more effective communication and understanding for stakeholders with less technical knowledge. These types of models are also effective for designing and managing systems that are composed of inputs from various other systems, as they allow for such complex and intricate relationships to be represented. While the MBSE is especially effective for larger and more complex undertakings, it can be adapted for projects of various sizes and complexities, as it can be scaled up or down for a wide range of applications (McGrath & Jonker, n.d.).

If a linear approach is used to design a complex digital system, this can lead to unexpected and potentially harmful complications, as each component would be structured independently without full consideration for how it may interact or impact another component. Through systems thinking, the interconnections between components can be better understood, leading to a more proactive approach to addressing issues, reducing the effort and downtime necessary to fix. When an organization encourages systems thinking, this enables individuals to step back and consider the bigger picture, which can ultimately lead to unconventional and innovative solutions that would likely not have come up in a step-by-step process.

As digital systems become more complex and interconnected, the traditional linear approach becomes less viable. Many industries are using the MBSE approach, especially for software engineering and development, including aerospace companies like NASA, automotive companies like Ford or BMW, the U.S. Department of Defense, and organizations in the telecommunications sector for developing infrastructure for emerging technologies and optimizing network layouts (McGrath & Jonker, n.d.).

The Systems Development Life Cycle

The Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC) is composed of seven phases: Planning, Analysis, Design, Coding/Development, Testing, Deployment/Implementation, and Maintenance. Each of these phases has its own objectives and outputs that impact the next phase, which altogether form a roadmap that balances stakeholder needs, project requirements, and customer expectations in the resulting deliverable. Through the SDLC, stakeholders can better estimate project costs, anticipated timeframes, and identify/address potential risk factors early in the development process.

Planning

The intention of the planning phase is to establish the goals and scope of a project. This phase is where the overall problem or opportunity is defined, often using high-level preliminary analysis. The analysis determines the potential benefit of implementing this project compared to the estimated time and resources necessary to see it to fruition. “Overall, the planning phase aims to both pinpoint the project’s goals and establish what the project does not need, which helps prevent it from becoming too bloated” (IBM, n.d.). This is also the phase where stakeholders consider alternative courses of action before continuing to the next phase of the project’s life cycle: analysis.

Analysis

Once a project successfully exits the planning phase, the development team begins conducting deeper analysis of user requirements and the creation of a practical implementation plan. Market research, feasibility testing, prototype evaluation, and resource allocation are all important steps of the analysis process. During this phase, various information is shared, including organizational data, cybersecurity regulations, available IT resources, or other requirements for the project across stakeholders and developers. While the planning phase establishes the “why,” the analysis phase establishes the “what.” This includes what features or functions must be included in the finished project, the types of data to be processed, and the rules that must be adhered to. One of the primary outputs from this phase (or from the previous phase, depending on the situation) is the software requirement specification document, which outlines the software’s functions, potential risks, project timeline, and the resources necessary to complete.

Design

During the design phase, development teams establish what the finished product will look like. They design and build the architecture, which may include diagrams, UI/UX mockups, workflow charts, and various prototypes to share with stakeholders and end users. This phase is also when development teams may decide which (if any) third party apps or services will be used, which technology stacks will be utilized, and what programming languages may be used in the development. The output from this phase is the software design document (SDD), which is used as a roadmap for the coding phase by the development team. The SDD generally includes a flow chart that lays out each step of the process and who is responsible for each task or phase of the process (Ungvarsky, 2024).

Coding/Development

In this phase, the development team finally begins writing code and building the software based on the guidelines and documents created in the previous phases. If the languages or technology stack was not specified in the previous phase, then developers would assess the needs of the project and determine the best options during the coding phase prior to building. This is also the phase in which developers would build any required additional systems for the software to run properly, like web pages or APIs. “Programmers design languages and software, database experts build the system that will house them, and network engineers begin putting together the hardware system that will support it all” (Ungvarsky, 2024).

Testing

Once the development team has created a functional piece of software, the testing phase begins, where they work to identify and eliminate bugs and issues from the final product. Testing is more of an iterative process than its own distinct phase, as it requires constant back-and-forth with the development team. When the testers identify a bug or issue, they document it and send the information back to the development team to fix, who then return it to the testing team to be tested again to ensure that fixing the issue did not cause issues elsewhere in the environment. There are various types of testing that may occur in this phase, including integration testing, system testing, unit testing, and acceptance testing to ensure that all components of the software work effectively and can function withing the organization’s existing IT environment. Security testing is also an important part of this phase, to identify and document any potential security risks to be fixed prior to rollout.

This is the phase I am most familiar with, as I spent a year as a project operations assistant. During that time, my job was to assess user feedback on issues or requested changes in the (VERY buggy) CRM software my company had invested a lot of money in. I would then attempt to replicate those bugs within a test environment and provide additional information for the developers using JIRA tickets. Once they fixed the bugs or released a new version of the software, my job was to try my best to break the software, identify potential bugs resulting from the changes, and return that information to the developers to address prior to the new version rollout. As the CRM software contained personal health information/records/payment information and was used daily in over 150+ audiology clinics across New Zealand for scheduling and all client interactions, it was imperative that the new version releases were free from obstructive bugs and didn’t interfere with operations. It was an extremely iterative process, with some larger changes taking multiple weeks of back-and-forth with the developers before we were comfortable releasing. Examples of various testing I performed included: clicking around the different tabs of the software as quickly as it would load to see if it would crash, processing test payments, creating new client records and editing existing, creating/editing/moving client appointments within the schedule to ensure that the established boundaries couldn’t be subverted (such as booking client appointments in occupied or blackout dates/times).

Deployment/Implementation

In this phase, the hardware necessary for the function of the software is installed, the new software is installed and information in existing systems migrated to it, and finally, the software is available for users. Sometimes this is done in incremental phases to minimize the risk of interruptions or issues from bugs, like beta testing or releasing a pilot version to a limited number of users to prevent large scale issues from impacting the user experience.

This is also a phase I have worked with, as software updates with significant user experience changes would be released to a set of pilot clinics for a week or two prior to releasing company wide. We would have regular meetings with these pilot clinics to receive feedback and address any issues they experienced; both with the implemented changes and with any issues they encountered that may have been a result of the changes.

Maintenance

Once the software has been deployed, the developers continue addressing bugs and user issues and monitor the system for any potential issues. Users are also often surveyed to provide feedback on whether the software meets their needs/expectations, and developers aim to ensure the longevity of the software by providing regular maintenance and support as needed.

On paper, this was my role as project operations assistant, but I performed the functions of the testing and deployment/implementation phases on a longer-term basis, after the initial software had been deployed. In the case of my company at the time, users were entirely unhappy with the new software and all the bugs and performance issues it had, so they worked to rebuild the software to meet the users’ and clinics’ needs.

Linear Life Cycle Models

Linear life cycle models, like the waterfall model, is a sequential development model where the previous stage is finished prior to the next one beginning. These models take a sequential approach, and are effective for smaller, well-defined and stable projects where stakeholders only want to be involved at key milestones of the process. This model allows for better predictability in achieving milestones as each phase is clearly documented, though it is also rigid in its process and is not suited for longer-term projects due to their changing requirements (Holt, 2023).

Iterative Life Cycle Models

Iterative life cycle models, such as Agile, involve several passthroughs on each stage of the process, as opposed to the linear model’s single pass through each step. This allows for a more adaptable process and can more easily accommodate longer-term projects or those requiring active stakeholder involvement. Each iteration of the process results in a workable version of the final product which can be deployed and tested. Unlike the linear process which results in a single final product, there is less risk involved for iterative projects; in the case that an iteration is entirely unworkable or breaks some other functionality, the system can be rolled back to a previous, working iteration until the next iteration is ready. One of the greatest downsides of the iterative approach is also an upside: while the iterations are intended to be shorter-term and easier to replace with a new iteration, this can lead to the focus being on releasing an iteration within a certain time frame instead of waiting for a version that works (Holt, 2023).

Incremental Life Cycle Models

Incremental life cycles are like iterative models, but with the emphasis being on making the product a little better each time. Unlike iterative models, incremental models seek to gradually build the system with active stakeholder input and with functionality at the forefront. Each incremental update is integrated with existing systems or with increments from previous iterations, resulting in a gradual evolution and providing tangible functionality early in the process (GeeksforGeeks, n.d.). This also makes incremental models effective for longer-term projects that require some amount of functionality early on in the process, but he approach is not suitable for systems that cannot be broken down into subsets, or separate increments (Holt, 2023).

Why is it Important?

Thinking in terms of a system’s life cycle instead of focusing on the finished product can be massively helpful for understanding the potential impact of the system itself on its environment and on its stakeholders. By approaching from a systems perspective, this allows for adaptability within the development process and more active stakeholder engagement. With how interconnected everything is nowadays, it is not sustainable to take a linear approach most of the time, as it does not consider the various interactions and relationships with other systems. Stakeholder needs are constantly changing, and iterative/incremental life cycle models allow for the flexibility to adapt with them. With how quickly digital environments and needs change, this approach is also imperative for the longevity and evolution of these systems. When taking a linear approach to a complex system, it is unlikely that the project will be considered successful, as they do not allow for much flexibility in the process. This would likely result in an unsuccessful attempt and require multiple iterations of this linear approach to meet stakeholder needs, which would skyrocket the budget and leave stakeholders feeling unsatisfied.

For instance, if a company uses a linear approach to create a website or an app, this does not leave room for adaptation to phone OS updates or browser updates that may impact the layout or functionality of an established website/app. Users may also be frustrated by a lack of compatibility with other functionalities on their phone or computer and refuse to use it. An iterative or incremental approach allows for user feedback, updates for new versions, and additional accessibility features as needed.
