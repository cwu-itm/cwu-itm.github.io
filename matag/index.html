Understanding Systems Life Cycle Models: Why the Way We Build Systems Matters

Modern digital systems do not just appear, they were develop over time through a structured process called systems development life cycle, or SDLC. Systems engineers use life cycle models to plan, build, test, and maintain these systems. These models are more than technical frameworks they represent different ways to solving problems, managing risks, and adapting to change. The systems development life cycle is a series of stages that a system follows from its first idea to when it is done. These stages usually include planning, design, development, testing, deployment, and maintenance. Instead of seeing a system as a finished product, the SDLC highlights that systems need to change as the user needs, technology, and environments evolve (Holt, 2021). It is important to understand life cycle models because the model you choose affects how flexible the system will be, how fast it can adapt, and how well it meets user needs. The three main models linear, iterative, and incremental each have their own strengths and weaknesses.

The Linear Life Cycle Model

The linear life cycle model, which is also known as the “waterfall model,” that uses a clear, step by step process. Each phase is finished before the next one starts. For example, the design phase must be done before development begins, and testing happens only after the system is built. A major strength of the linear model is its clarity, since each stage is clearly defined, it is easier to manage schedules, assign tasks, and track progress. This structure works well for projects with clear requirements and few expected changes. In fields where documentation and predictability are important, such as government or healthcare, the linear model offers control and accountability. The linear model also has some major flaws, its strict structure makes it hard to change if changes needed after the project has started. If user needs change or new issues come up, revising earlier stages can take a lot of time and money. This is a big problem in digital settings, where user demands and technology often change quickly. From a user experience point of view, this model can be limiting. As Johnson (2014) explains in Designing with the Mind in Mind, good systems should be shaped by user feedback and how people think. The linear model puts off user testing until late in the process, which makes it more likely to create a system that does not match how users actually think or act.

The Iterative Life Cycle Model

The iterative model uses a different approach that focuses on repeating and improving the system. Instead of finishing the system all at once, developers build a version, test it, collect feedback, and then make changes through several cycles. This model is especially useful when requirements are unclear at the start. By reviewing and improving the system, teams can adjust to new information and user feedback. This fits the ideas in Laws of UX, which stress the value of usability, feedback, and ongoing improvement in design (Yablonski, 2020). A key advantage of the iterative model is its flexibility. Since changes are expected and planned for, teams can respond better to changing needs. This usually results in better user experiences because systems are improved based on real feedback instead of guesses. But,flexibility can also be a drawback, Iterative development often takes more time and resources because it involves many rounds of testing and changes. It can also be harder to manage, since the project’s goals may change over time. And ithout clear limits, projects might become inefficient or never fully finish.

The Incremental Life Cycle Model

The incremental model mixes parts of both the linear and iterative approaches, the system is built in smaller, manageable sections called increments. Each increment has its own mini life cycle, including design, development, and testing, before it is delivered. This method lets teams release working parts of the system early, instead of waiting for the whole system to be finished. Users can start using the system sooner, and their feedback can help shape later increments. A main strength of the incremental model is that it balances structure and flexibility. It gives a clear plan for development but still allows for changes and improvements. This is especially helpful for large, complex systems that cannot be built all at once. The incremental model also helps to manage risks better. By delivering the system in stages, teams can find and fix problems early, which lowers the chance of big failures later on (Holt, 2021). Although, the incremental model also has its difficulties, It needs thoughtful planning to make sure each increment fits well with the others. If coordination is poor, there can be inconsistencies or technical problems in the system. Good communication in a team and its members is also needed to keep a clear, common idea.

Why Life Cycle Thinking Matters

Thinking about a system’s life cycle is important when it comes to building systems that work well, last over time, and can adapt. Digital systems are not fixed, they exist inside environments that change because because of user behavior, new technology, and social trends. Life cycle thinking helps designers and engineers think about the long-term effects of their choices. For example, a system built quickly with a linear model might be hard to update or maintain later. On the other hand, models that rely on flexibility and change can better support lasting growth. From a user centered design view, life cycle thinking brings attention to the need for ongoing feedback and usability testing. As Yablonski (2020) points out, good design is not a one time thingbut a process of constant improvement. Systems need to change with their users to stay useful and effective. Thinking about a system’s life cycle also supports sustainability, this means not just technical aspects like maintainability and expandability, but also ethical issues like accessibility and inclusiveness. Systems designed with their whole life cycle in mind are more likely to meet several user needs and stay useful over time.

Conclusion

The systems development life cycle helps us understand how systems are built and maintained. The linear, iterative, and incremental models each offer different ways to handle complexity, uncertainty, and change. The linear model gives structure and predictability but is not flexible. The iterative model focuses on adaptability and flexibility and ongoing improvement but can use more resources. The incremental model handles both by delivering systems in stages and allowing changes. In conclusion, no single model is always the best choice. How well a life cycle model works depends on the project’s goals, limits, and user needs. By learning about these models and thinking carefully about their effects, systems engineers and designers can make better decisions and create much more sustainable digital systems.

Why, perception, attention and empathy matter

Good design is what we call something that is easy to understand, use and even looks nice but good design is more than just easy to use and nice to look at. Good design is HCD, which stands for Human Centered Design, it is about creating systems, products and interfaces that actually work for the people using them. The whole point is that it is centered around people and how to best assist them and work best for them. Instead of just focusing on the looks of a design or the performance of a system only, HCD focuses on the user making sure they are enjoying the design and having a positive interaction. Designing for people is not easy, you have to understand attention, perception and empathy. There are many factors that play a role in how people interpret information like, how comfortable they are using a system where their attention is focused when using the system. If designers neglect the needs and wants of the users then the system will fail no matter how aesthetic the design is.

Understanding Human centered design

Lets brake down HCD, Human Centered Design, is a way of thinking that is focused around the user. ISO 9241-210 states that HCD is defined as an iterative process that focuses on understanding users, user needs and their context of use during design and development ISO 2019. The framework highlights usability, accessibility and continuous evolution. IDEO’s field is a much more flexible and creative method. IDEO is mainly focused on empathy it encourages designers to think deeply about users and learn from observing them and immersion themselves into the mind of the user. (It focuses heavily on empathy and encourages designers to deeply understand users through observation, interviews, and immersion (IDEO.org, 2015).) ISO provides a structured and formal framework, IDEO emphasizes storytelling, creativity, and real world user experiences. While each approach is different they both work well together, because ISO makes sure the design processes are consistent and are able to be measured. While the IDEO encourages designers to connect emotionally with the users. Both ISO and IDEO together show us that designing for people needs both structure and empathy.

The Role of Perception in Design

The perception of a design is a very big part if how users experience and interact with a design. Because people don’t process every detail they see instead they rely on patterns and mental shortcuts to quickly make sense of information. This is what makes visual hierarchy so important, we consume content with our eyes first and form an opinion form just a first look. Visual hierarchy is how elements are arranged and organized to grab guide the users attention. Designers use size, contrast, color and spacing to show users what content is most important. For example when you go onto a website the bold headline will catch your attention, this is intentional. If everything on a page were to look the same users wouldn’t know where to start and what the content is about without having to read the whole page, this is why design details like heading titles and much more are so helpful in directing us and giving us information on what content were about to consume. This is just how our brains are wired like Johnson (2020) states, users often scan interfaces rather than reading them carefully. That means design decisions need to support quick understanding. If key information is hidden or poorly organized, users may miss it entirely. An example of this is a navigation menu, if a website puts its menu in a random or unusual and hard place to locate then users wont be able to find what they need. A website can have tons of great and useful content but if the perception design is bad then it ruins the whole experience, users get frustrated not being able to locate something as basic as a menu.

Attention and cognitive load

Peoples attention is limited, this means designers need to be smart about what they want users to focus on. If a design has to many aspects competing for the users attention it can be overwhelming and this will drive users away. This is how to cognitive load come into play, cognitive load is how much mental effort is required to complete a task. If a design calls for a user to retain to much information tor to make to many decisions all at once then it becomes harder to use. Hicks Law shows us that the more choices a user has, the longer it takes them to make a decision (Interaction Design Foundation, n.d.) A good example of this is a website with 10 different navigation option is a lot harder to operate compared to a website with 3 organized navigation categories. Often times a more simplified design is what users find easier to work through because the navigation is clear and only askes the user to make a few simple decisions. Millers law is very similar, it suggests that people can only hold about 7 (plus or minus 2) items in their working memory at a time (Miller, 1956). Overwhelming users with to much information can cut down usability and drive away users. Designers can improve usability and fix this problem by putting information into smaller group that make the content easier to consume and process.

Empathy as a design tool

Empathy is one of the most important parts of human center design it is what lets designers understand not only what the users do, but why they do it. Design decisions would be so out of touch and disconnected from users without empathy, which is why it is so important. IDEO’s Field Guide strongly emphasizes empathy through methods like user interviews and observation (IDEO.org, 2015). These approaches help designers see what problems the user will face from the users perspective, this is extremely helpful because the problem likely would have gone unnoticed for some time until user feedback came through. For example designers might just assume that a checkout process is simple but from the user perspective and testing we can find out that entering payment information makes the user feel anxious. This is a good thing to know because then designers will now take a step to come across as more trust worthy. This can be done by adding clearer instructions, or progress indicators to make users feel more comfortable going through with the purchase. Empathy also helps designers consider accessibility, not all users have the same abilities, environments, or devices. Designing with empathy means creating systems that work for a wide range of people not just a specific group of users.

Applying UX Laws to Design Decisions

Many UX laws help to explain how perception and attention can largely influence usability, these laws are useful tools for making design decisions that align with how people naturally think and behave. Jakob’s Law states that users expect websites to work similarly to other sites they already know (Nielsen, 2000). This means that designers should be following common design patterns instead of just trying to invent or reinvent everything. For example putting a logo in the top left corner of a website that lead you back to the home page is a very common and widely known convenience. Neglecting this and trying to come up with something new but with the same goal, can easily confuse users instead of helping them. Fitts’s Law focuses on the relationship between distance, size, and interaction. It states that the time required to move to a target depends on how far away it is and how large it is (Fitts, 1954). This is why buttons should be obvious and easily accessible, small or randomly placed buttons can make navigating harder for users. Like I talked about earlier Hick’s Law emphasizes the importance of reducing choices the user has to make. Just by simplifying things like navigation, guiding users with clear paths and limiting their options a designer makes the user experience easier and more positive. Miller’s Law even reinforces the idea of taking extra steps to make sure the user does not get overwhelmed. This can be done by organizing the information into small groups, and even using bullet points. Both Hick’s Law, and Miller’s Law show that good design is not only creativity but also consideration, taking into account how the user will be perceiving and consuming the information or content, also how people think and behave.

How Early Design Choices Impact Usability

A very important take away from HCD is that even the early decisions matter, choices that are made at the beginning of the design process can either support of weaken the systems usability later on. A good example of this is when a designer doesn’t look into the users needs early on, this can not only make thing harder for the designer but also the users. As the designers might create solutions to problems that they think could effect the users but, really there was no problem to begin with. This not only wastes the designers time but it also leaves the user unsatisfied, because the real problems they face are going unnoticed. Where if the designer had researched and tested the system early on they could have prevented the real problems and saved themselves time and money. Also decisions about layout, interaction patterns and navigation all have long term effects, a badly structured interface can be hard to fix later on especially if people have already grown accustom to it. Which is why not only ISO 9241-210 but also IDEO highlight the iteration, design is not a one time process it calls for continues testing and correction. Designers need feedback so that they can improve upon what they have already begun creating to not only better it for themselves and the profit but also for the users. By including users in the design process designers are able to spot problems before they grow and become harder to fix.

Conclusion

Designing for people means more than just making something look nice it is also about creating a positive and productive experience for the user. Through understanding perception, attention and empathy, designers are able to make smarter more efficient decisions that will improve the users experience. The combination of frameworks like ISO 9241-210 and empathetic thinking like IDEO’s Field Guide, shows us the importance of both parts of designing a system, analysis and creativity are equally important. UX laws like Jakob’s, Fitts’s, Hick’s, and Miller’s show us real and effective ways to align design with human behavior. Human centered design is about recognizing that users are not just part of the system but they are the reason that the system exists. When designers prioritize the people the end results of usability is much more effective and impactful.

What Makes A Digital System Usable?

Memory and Mental Models in User Interaction

One huge interface design challenge is dealing with the fact that human memory is limited. Like Johnson states in, Designing with the mind in mind, people are much better at recognizing information than recalling it from memory, Johnson (2020). This is because a users should not have to memorize complicating instructions, find hidden commands, or navigation paths just to complete simple tasks. Instead, interfaces should provide visual cues and recognizable patterns that guide interaction. A really good example of this is a smartphones design, most apps use icons were familiar with like a magnifying glass for search, a analog clock for timers and alarms, and even an envelope for emails. Users remember patters, and recognize these symbols immediately because they have seen them repeatedly across different platforms. This makes interacting with a design easier and lessens the amount of work needed for a user to learn new app. Mental models also play a very big role in design usability, mental models are the expectations users develop about how systems should work based on previous experiences. When a design matches these expectations, users feel more comfortable and confident but when it doesn’t users get confused. For example, many users expect a websites logo to bring them back to the homepage when you click on it, when this doesn’t happen because a designer removed this feature or maybe changed its behavior then the users can get confused or even frustrated. This is because users rely on habits and patterns that influence their interaction. I personally have experienced frustration and confusion when using websites that have confusing design layouts and tabs or info menus that are hard to find. Instead of being able to normally go through tasks and find information on the site, you are forced to hunt for what you need, search through menus and try remember where information was hidden. Something that was meant to be simple is now harder then necessary for the user making them frustrated and deterring them from using this source again. This is because unnecessarily stressful experiences are turn off for consumers and when the interface does not align with user expectations, this typically creates a negative experience. Johnson (2020) explains that humans naturally seek patterns and rely on previous experiences to understand new systems. When a designers ignores interfaces patterns and habits that users are used to creates a feelings of unease for users because they are dealing with something that is difficult and unpredictable. This increases the cognitive load that users must deal with and makes users repetitively stop and think instead of being able to interact naturally with a design.

Cognitive Load and Poor Design

This leads us into or next topic which is cognitive load, cognitive load refers to the amount of mental effort that is asked of someone when completing a task. Each interaction a user makes requires the users, attention, memory, and decision-making skills. Bad or poor design increases cognitive load by forcing users to process unnecessary information and or remember too many details at once. In Designing with the Mind in Mind it talks about a principle that is similar to cognitive load it is the idea people can only focus on a limited amount of information at one time. Johnson (2020) explains that working memory is extremely limited, meaning users can become overwhelmed when interfaces present too many choices, confusing and cluttered layouts, or unclear or confusing instructions. The biggest problem that is often seen in websites that are more worried about the look of the websites design as apposed to the designs usability and function, is the user being confused and or even overwhelmed. Often times when a website is more worried about aesthetics they often have more pop up ads, crowded navigation menus, and autoplay videos. All of these things are put into place to grab the users attention and to assist them when using the website but it can actually be more confusing and overwhelming for the users. Foe example, they have popups on the side of the screen that if you click them they will take you to games, other apps or even promotions. The app has become more advertisement based whit almost every 3rd or 4th video you scroll onto is an ad for a product. Instead of keeping users attention for long as possible but instead drives the user away because of frustration. Before TikTok was an app for people to post funny little videos now it has been taken over by constant ads and people pushing you to buy their products constantly. This relates a lot Tesler’s Law, which talks about how every system has a certain amount of complexity that cannot be removed completely (Interaction Design Foundation, n.d.). A designer cannot get rid of complexity entirely, but they can decide whether the system or the user will be the one to handle it. Good designers reduce unnecessary effort for users by simplifying processes and organizing information clearly. For example, when the autofill features in web browsers reduce cognitive load by remembering addresses and payment information or other personal information. Instead of asking a user to put in their personal information each time they are filling in a important page asking for the required information fields. The users are shown visual categories, recently viewed items, and personalized recommendations that help them recognize content quickly. Interface Consistency and System Feedback

Designing for Human Limitations

Designers sometimes assume users will carefully read instructions or fully understand systems immediately. In reality, most people scan content quickly and rely on shortcuts to complete tasks efficiently. This means interfaces must be designed around human limitations rather than idealized behavior. One important idea discussed in usability design is that users prefer simplicity. A simple interface reduces stress and improves confidence for users because they make information easier to process. Simplicity does not mean removing all functionality, it means organizing features in ways that feel intuitive and manageable. For example Apple’s products are often used as examples of usability focused design because they emphasize clarity and consistency. The icons are simple, navigation structures are predictable, and actions usually produce immediate feedback. Users can often operate devices without reading manuals because the interfaces support recognition and familiar interaction patterns. A poorly designed systems often puts too much responsibility onto the users, for example password requirements. Password requirements are a good example. Many websites require users to create extremely complex passwords with different symbols, numbers, and capitalization rules. If systems do not provide password managers, autofill support, or clear instructions, users may forget credentials or resort to insecure habits such as reusing passwords. This shows the importance of designing systems that work well with human cognition instead of against it. Another very important usability strategy is grouping information into chunks information into smaller sections. Johnson (2020) explains that people process information more effectively when it is grouped into meaningful categories. Websites often use headings, spacing, and bullet points to organize content because these structures improve readability and reduce cognitive strain. A good example is an online checkout process which is usually broken into separate small stages like shipping information, payment details, and order review. By splitting up the tasks into smaller steps, the interface becomes easier to understand and complete.
