Introduction

The following blog explores the foundational concepts of systems thinking and Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) within the context of digital environments. It begins by defining what a system is and outlining its key characteristics, then introduces the concept of systems thinking. Next, it examines how systems thinking differs from traditional linear approaches and presents a model-based method for representing and analyzing complex systems. Finally, it concludes by highlighting the need for Model-Based Systems Engineering in the design and management of digital environments.

Defining a System

Before exploring the concept of systems thinking, it is important to first define what a system is in the context of this discussion. According to Jon Holt (n.d.), a system can be understood as a collection of interconnected elements that work together to achieve a specific purpose. Rather than existing in isolation, these elements interact with one another, forming a structured whole that produces outcomes through their relationships and interactions (Holt, n.d.).

Systems can take many different forms depending on their nature and application. For example, they may be physical, such as vehicles or digital devices; abstract, such as models or algorithms; or human-centered, such as organizations or social structures. In many real-world scenarios, systems often combine multiple categories, reflecting the complexity of modern environments (Holt, n.d.).

Despite their differences, most systems share several key characteristics. First, they consist of elements that are interconnected, meaning that changes in one part of the system can influence others. Second, systems exist within defined boundaries, which separate what is inside the system from what lies outside it. Third, systems involve stakeholders like individuals, organizations, or entities that have an interest in the system and may interact with it in different ways. Additionally, systems are described by attributes, which represent their properties, and are guided by needs, which define their purpose and intended outcomes. Finally, systems operate under various constraints, such as environmental, technical, or regulatory limitations, that shape how they can be designed and function (Holt, n.d.).

What is Systems Thinking?

Systems thinking is a broad concept that emphasizes understanding how each element of a system interacts within the context of the whole structure. In simpler terms, it is like assembling all the individual pieces of a system into a large mosaic, where every piece contributes to the overall picture. Instead of focusing only on isolated problems, systems thinking encourages attention to the relationships, patterns, and interdependencies among components.

Laurie Davies, a journalist and instructor at the University of Phoenix, describes systems thinking as an approach that considers the system as a whole rather than focusing solely on immediate problems. In her example of a malfunctioning shifting gear, she explains that systems thinking involves examining not just the gear itself, but also its design, operational conditions, environmental factors, and maintenance practices (Davies, 2024). By considering the various factors that contribute to a problem, systems thinking seeks to understand the relationships between the object and the broader system that may influence its behavior.

An essential aspect of systems thinking is its focus on several core principles that guide how we view and analyze systems. At its heart, it is about seeing the bigger picture and understanding how all the parts of a system work together rather than separately. Systems thinking encourages noticing patterns that emerge when elements interact, and how systems can adjust themselves through feedback to stay balanced or improve. It also reminds us to consider what is part of the system and what lies outside of it, to appreciate the complexity of real-world situations, and to include different viewpoints when making decisions (Lloyd & Smith, 2025). By applying these principles, systems thinking provides a practical framework for exploring the full context of problems, making it easier to identify root causes and anticipate the chain reactions.

The Difference Between Systems Thinking and Traditional Linear Thinking

To understand how systems thinking works, it is helpful to compare it with traditional, linear ways of thinking. Traditional thinking often follows a step-by-step approach, focusing on discrete tasks in isolation. For example, if one were tasked with building a car out of LEGO pieces, a linear approach would involve following the instructions in the manual exactly as written. By combining each component according to the guide, the builder would recreate the car as intended, with limited variation or exploration beyond the prescribed design.

In contrast, systems thinking encourages stepping beyond the guided process to explore how components can interact in alternative ways. Even with a set of identical LEGO bricks, there are countless combinations and possibilities. A systems thinking approach begins by examining each element's function, potential connections, and features, considering how each piece might serve multiple purposes or interact with other components. This approach emphasizes understanding the relationships and interdependencies between elements, rather than simply following predefined steps.

A research study conducted for the Indiana Interdisciplinary GK-12 program demonstrates that a systems thinking approach, compared to a traditional linear logic model, provides a more precise understanding of complex educational programs and their outcomes. The linear logic model, which assumes direct cause-and-effect relationships between program components, often oversimplifies complex programs and may overlook important contextual and relational factors. In contrast, a systems thinking approach using causal loop diagrams captures feedback loops, interdependencies, and emergent behaviors within the program. This allows evaluators to identify more effective solutions to problems, anticipate unintended consequences, and better understand how various program components interact to influence outcomes (Dyehouse et al., 2009).

What Is a Model-Based Approach?

Building on the ideas of systems thinking, engineers need practical ways to understand and manage complex systems. One such tool is Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE), which applies the principles of systems thinking to organize and visualize all parts of a system. A model-based approach, commonly referred to as Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE), is a modern method of systems engineering that uses models as the primary way to represent and understand a system. Instead of relying on large sets of documents, MBSE organizes all relevant system information into a structured model that serves as the central source of knowledge about the system (Holt, n.d.).

In traditional systems engineering, information about a system is typically spread across multiple documents, which can make it difficult to manage complexity and maintain consistency. In contrast, MBSE places all system knowledge within a model that acts as a simplified representation, or abstraction, of the real system. This model does not attempt to capture every detail but instead focuses on including only the most relevant information needed to successfully design and understand the system (Holt, n.d.).

Another important aspect of MBSE is that the model is organized into different “views,” each tailored to specific stakeholders. These views allow engineers, managers, and other participants to examine the system from different perspectives while still working from the same underlying model. Because of this, the model becomes a “single source of truth,” meaning that all stakeholders rely on the same consistent and structured information when making decisions (Holt, n.d.).

Overall, MBSE provides a more organized, visual, and efficient way to manage complex systems by focusing on models rather than documents. It improves communication, ensures consistency, and makes it easier to understand how different parts of a system interact.

The Need for Model-Based Systems Engineering in Digital Systems

As highlighted by Jon Holt in Systems Engineering Demystified, Model-Based Systems Engineering provides significant advantages in designing systems that are tailored to specific stakeholders. By focusing on delivering only relevant and useful information through structured models, MBSE ensures that each stakeholder can better understand the aspects of the system that matter most to them (Holt, n.d.). However, beyond these benefits, it is important to recognize why this approach is especially necessary in modern digital environments.

Holt explains that the need for modeling arises from three major challenges in systems engineering: complexity, lack of understanding, and poor communication. First, modern digital systems are inherently complex, often consisting of many interconnected components. MBSE helps manage this complexity by using visual models that make it easier to identify where complexity exists and how different parts of the system interact. This allows engineers to better control and reduce unnecessary complexity while focusing on critical system components (Holt, n.d.).

Second, MBSE improves understanding throughout the system's lifecycle. Holt suggests that when a model is easy to create, it indicates that the system is well understood. Conversely, difficulty in building a model reveals gaps, ambiguity, or missing information. In this way, modeling becomes a tool not only for representation but also for identifying weaknesses in system knowledge and design (Holt, n.d.).

Finally, MBSE enhances communication among stakeholders. Digital systems often involve diverse teams with different backgrounds and areas of expertise. By using standardized models, diagrams, and notations, MBSE provides a common language that allows stakeholders to clearly share information and collaborate more effectively. This reduces misunderstandings and ensures that all participants have a consistent view of the system (Holt, n.d.).

Overall, the need for MBSE in designing complex digital systems lies in its ability to manage complexity, improve understanding, and facilitate communication. Additionally, this approach not only provides an effective way to communicate information but also establishes a structured framework that aligns with stakeholder needs.

Conclusion

Applying systems thinking helps designers and users effectively navigate complex digital systems by focusing on the whole system, its interconnected parts, and the attributes that define it. Unlike traditional linear approaches, systems thinking emphasizes relationships, patterns, and interactions between elements to better understand how changes affect the system as a whole. To put systems thinking into practice and manage complexity, Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) provides structured, visual representations tailored to stakeholders. As a result, it helps reduce confusion and supports a clearer, more organized approach to designing professional digital systems.

Introduction

The following blog explores the concept of the system development life cycle within systems engineering. It begins by defining the idea of a life cycle and how it applies to system development. It then examines the key characteristics, advantages, and limitations of linear, iterative, and incremental life cycle models as different approaches to organizing system development. Finally, it reflects on why life cycle thinking is important for the design, sustainability, and evolution of modern digital systems.

Identifying the Systems Development Life Cycle

Defining the Life Cycle Concept

To understand the systems development life cycle, it is first necessary to define what a life cycle actually is in a systems engineering context. In Systems Engineering Demystified, Holt explains that a life cycle describes the evolution of a system (or other entity) over time, and it is a core concept used to structure and understand how systems develop, operate, and eventually retire (Holt, n.d.).

A life cycle is not just a simple sequence of steps; instead, it is made up of stages, where each stage represents a distinct period in the evolution of a system. These stages together form the structure of the life cycle. However, Holt also distinguishes between a life cycle and a life cycle model. The life cycle defines what stages exist, while the life cycle model defines how those stages are executed, including their order and relationships (Holt, n.d.).

In other words, the life cycle provides the structural view of system evolution, while the life cycle model provides the behavioral view. This distinction is important because it highlights that system development is not only about defining phases, but also about understanding how those phases interact and progress over time.

Viewing Life Cycles Through the Lens of System Development

When applied specifically to system development, the life cycle concept is commonly expressed as the System Development Life Cycle (SDLC). Holt explains that the system life cycle is closely aligned with ISO 15288 and represents the full development journey of a system from its initial conception through to its retirement (Holt, n.d.). This framing is important because it emphasizes that system development is not limited to building a solution, but includes everything from understanding needs to disposing of the system responsibly.

The system development life cycle typically begins with a conception stage, where the needs of the system are identified and defined. This includes understanding stakeholder expectations and establishing criteria that will later be used to verify and validate the system. From there, the development stage focuses on exploring possible solutions and selecting the most appropriate one based on those needs. Once a solution has been agreed upon, the production stage involves building the system and ensuring that it meets its specified requirements through verification and validation activities (Holt, n.d.).

After production, the system moves into utilization, where it is actively used in its intended operational environment. This stage is often supported by a parallel support stage, which ensures that maintenance, troubleshooting, and ongoing operational assistance are available to keep the system functioning effectively. Finally, the life cycle concludes with the retirement stage, where the system is decommissioned and disposed of in a safe and controlled manner (Holt, n.d.).

However, Holt emphasizes that these stages are not always strictly linear. Depending on the nature of the system and the chosen life cycle model, stages may overlap, repeat, or even be omitted entirely. This flexibility is essential because different systems have different levels of complexity, risk, and operational demands. As a result, the life cycle model determines how the system actually moves through these stages in practice (Holt, n.d.).

Another important insight is that this same structure can be applied beyond just systems. Similar life cycle patterns exist for projects, programs, and products, although each operates at a different level of abstraction. A project life cycle focuses on the execution of a specific initiative, while a program life cycle coordinates multiple related projects. A product life cycle, on the other hand, focuses on the evolution of a deliverable from creation through to end-of-life. While these may share similar stages, their scope and purpose differ, which reinforces the importance of clearly defining what type of life cycle is being considered in any given situation (Holt, n.d.).

Key Characteristics, Advantages, and Potential Drawbacks of Life Cycle Models

Linear Life Cycle Model

The linear life cycle model represents the most traditional and structured approach to system development. It is based on the idea that development progresses through a fixed sequence of stages, where each stage must be completed before the next one begins. Holt explains that life cycle models describe not just the stages of a system’s evolution, but also the order in which those stages are executed, with linear models enforcing a strict step-by-step flow from start to finish (Holt, n.d.).

In this approach, development typically begins with clearly defined requirements, followed by design, implementation, testing, deployment, and finally maintenance. This structure is commonly associated with the Waterfall model, which is widely recognized as one of the earliest software development approaches. According to GeeksforGeeks, the Waterfall model follows a sequential process where each phase must be completed before moving forward, making it highly structured and easy to manage (GeeksforGeeks, 2026).

The main advantage of the linear model is its simplicity. Because each phase is clearly defined and documented, it is relatively easy to understand, plan, and manage. It also provides strong predictability in terms of timelines and deliverables, which makes it suitable for small to medium-sized projects with stable and well-understood requirements. However, this strength is also its biggest limitation. Once a stage is completed, it is difficult and costly to go back and make changes. This rigidity means that the linear model struggles in environments where requirements evolve or are not fully understood at the beginning. In addition, testing occurs late in the process, which increases the risk of discovering critical issues only after significant effort has already been invested (GeeksforGeeks, 2026).

Iterative Life Cycle Model

The iterative life cycle model introduces repetition into system development by allowing the same set of stages to be repeated in cycles. Instead of completing the system in one pass, development occurs through multiple iterations, where each cycle produces a refined version of the system. Holt explains that this approach enables continuous improvement, as each iteration builds on feedback and learning from the previous cycle (Holt, n.d.).

A key characteristic of the iterative model is its focus on refinement and learning. Rather than attempting to fully define and build the system at the beginning, developers start with a basic version and progressively improve it through repeated cycles of planning, implementation, testing, and review. This allows teams to respond to uncertainty and refine requirements as understanding evolves (Holt, n.d.).

One of the main advantages of this model is flexibility. Because feedback is gathered at the end of each iteration, the system can evolve in response to stakeholder input and changing requirements. The GridFox analysis highlights that this feedback-driven approach reduces risk by identifying issues early and allowing continuous correction throughout development (Court, 2025). This makes the iterative model particularly effective in environments where requirements are not fully known at the start or are expected to change.

However, this flexibility also introduces challenges. Without strong control, iterative development can lead to scope expansion, where continuous changes extend project timelines. It also requires disciplined management to ensure that iterations remain focused and that progress is measurable. While early versions of the system may be delivered faster than in traditional models, the overall development process can become longer if refinement cycles are not carefully controlled (Court, 2025).

Incremental Life Cycle Model

The incremental life cycle model develops a system by breaking it into smaller, fully functional parts called increments. Each increment delivers a complete subset of system functionality, and new increments are added over time until the full system is complete. Unlike the iterative model, which refines the same features repeatedly, the incremental model builds additional features in stages.

A defining characteristic of this model is staged delivery of functionality. Each increment is designed to be operational on its own, allowing users to benefit from parts of the system before the entire system is finished. This makes it particularly useful in long-term projects where early delivery of value is important (Holt, n.d.).

One of the major advantages of the incremental model is early usability, as stakeholders can interact with working components of the system much earlier in the development process. This improves feedback quality and helps ensure that the system aligns more closely with real-world needs. According to Court (2025), incremental development also supports predictable delivery and reduces risk by isolating functionality into manageable components. This approach makes planning and resource allocation more structured compared to highly flexible models.

However, the incremental model also has limitations. Not all systems can be easily divided into independent functional parts, especially when components are highly interconnected. In such cases, forcing an incremental structure can lead to design inefficiencies or integration challenges. Additionally, careful architectural planning is required to ensure that each increment integrates smoothly into the overall system without creating inconsistencies across releases.

Why Is It Important to Think About a System’s Life Cycle?

Systems engineering is not just about defining systems or their life cycles, it is about understanding how systems are created, used, maintained, and improved over time. Thinking in terms of a life cycle shifts the focus from simply building a system to managing its entire lifespan, from initial concept to retirement (Holt, n.d.).

A life cycle perspective helps ensure that systems are designed with long-term use in mind. Decisions made during early stages, such as requirements and design, directly affect how well a system can be maintained, updated, and adapted later. Since systems are considered evolving entities that change over time, early design choices play a critical role in their long-term success (Holt, n.d.).

It also supports sustainability by recognizing that systems do not end at deployment. Instead, they require continuous support, monitoring, and improvement throughout their operational life. Real-world standards emphasize that maintenance is an ongoing phase that includes updates, security checks, and system support to ensure reliability and resilience over time (Northeastern University, n.d.).

Finally, thinking in terms of a life cycle allows systems to evolve alongside changing technologies and user needs. Rather than treating systems as fixed products, this perspective encourages continuous refinement and adaptation. This aligns with modern development approaches where systems are regularly updated and improved based on feedback and changing requirements (Court, 2025).

Overall, life cycle thinking encourages a more complete and forward-looking approach to system development. It ensures that systems are not only built effectively but also remain useful, secure, and adaptable throughout their entire lifespan.

Introduction

The following blog explores how human-centered design shapes usability by focusing on how people perceive, process, and interact with information. It begins by introducing the foundations of human-centered design and comparing structured and flexible approaches to understanding user needs. It then examines how perception and visual hierarchy influence the way users interpret both digital and physical interfaces. Finally, it presents key UX laws, including Jakob’s Law, Fitts’s Law, Miller’s Law, and Hick’s Law, to explain how familiarity, interaction efficiency, memory limitations, and decision making affect user experience.

Understanding Human-Centered Design

Human-centered design is a design approach focused on understanding problems through the lens of user experience. It places people at the center of the development process, ensuring that solutions are built around users’ needs, behaviors, and expectations. Rather than prioritizing technology or business goals alone, this approach emphasizes designing products and services that are meaningful and relevant to the people who use them (Landry, 2023).

At its core, human-centered design is based on the belief that the people experiencing a problem are best positioned to help solve it. Instead of imposing solutions from the outside, designers work closely with users to understand their daily experiences, identify real needs, and develop ideas that fit naturally into their lives (IDEO.org, 2015). This perspective shifts design from assumption-driven to insight-driven, resulting in solutions that are more effective and widely adopted.

A key part of this approach is adopting the right mindset. Human-centered design requires designers to embrace exploration, testing, and continuous improvement. Rather than expecting immediate success, designers are encouraged to experiment, learn from failure, and refine their ideas over time. This iterative thinking allows for deeper understanding and leads to more thoughtful and practical solutions (IDEO.org, 2015).

The process itself typically moves through three main stages: inspiration, ideation, and implementation. The inspiration phase focuses on understanding people by observing their behaviors, needs, and motivations. During ideation, designers analyze what they have learned, generate a wide range of ideas, and test possible solutions. Finally, in the implementation stage, these ideas are developed into real-world products or services that aim to create meaningful impact (IDEO.org, 2015). Although these stages provide structure, the process is not strictly linear; designers often move back and forth between them as they refine their work.

While human-centered design can sometimes feel uncertain or challenging, this ambiguity is an important part of the process. Designers must trust that by staying focused on users and continuously learning from them, they will move closer to effective solutions. IDEO emphasizes that successful design balances what is desirable for users with what is technically feasible and viable in practice (IDEO.org, 2015).

Ultimately, human-centered design is not just about methods or processes, it is about perspective. It requires empathy, curiosity, and optimism. As Emi Kolawole explains, discovering new solutions begins with understanding different people, situations, and contexts (IDEO.org, 2015). By maintaining this human focus, designers can create solutions that are not only functional, but also meaningful and impactful.

Comparing ISO 9241-210 and IDEO’s Human-Centered Design Approach

While both ISO 9241-210 and the The Field Guide to Human-Centered Design advocate for human-centered design, they approach the concept from different angles. ISO presents a structured and standardized framework, whereas IDEO emphasizes a more flexible, creative, and empathy-driven approach.

ISO 9241-210 focuses on providing a clear and systematic process for achieving usability and effective user experience. The standard outlines key stages such as understanding the context of use, defining user requirements, developing design solutions, and evaluating those solutions through user feedback. A core principle of this approach is the active and continuous involvement of users throughout the design process, ensuring that outcomes are based on real needs and measurable requirements rather than assumptions (ISO, n.d.).

In contrast, IDEO’s approach prioritizes mindset and creativity over strict structure. While it also includes phases (inspiration, ideation, and implementation) the process is presented as flexible and iterative rather than linear. Designers are encouraged to explore ideas, experiment with prototypes, and learn from failure. IDEO highlights the importance of empathy, arguing that understanding people’s experiences, emotions, and motivations is essential to creating meaningful solutions (IDEO.org, 2015).

While these two approaches may seem different, they are not meant to compete but rather to complement each other. ISO 9241-210 provides a structured and systematic process that helps designers ensure usability and consistency, while the The Field Guide to Human-Centered Design encourages creativity, empathy, and exploration. When combined, these perspectives offer a more complete understanding of what human-centered design truly represents.

A common challenge arises when human-centered design is viewed from only one perspective. Some may see it purely as a rigid, step-by-step process, while others may interpret it as an entirely open and intuitive practice driven by emotion and creativity. In reality, human-centered design exists between these extremes. It provides a structured framework that guides designers through problem-solving, while also allowing enough flexibility to adapt, experiment, and develop innovative solutions. This balance between structure and creativity is what enables designers to effectively respond to complex user needs and create meaningful experiences.

How Perception and Visual Hierarchy Affect Usability

To understand how perception and visual hierarchy influence usability, it is helpful to look at everyday digital and physical interfaces. These examples show how design choices guide attention, reduce cognitive effort, and support user understanding.

Social media platforms such as Instagram and Facebook provide a clear example of this behavior. Upon opening these apps, users naturally expect to scroll vertically to view new content. This expectation is not random but instead developed through repeated exposure and learned interface patterns. As Johnson explains, human perception is influenced by experience and context, which can “prime” users to expect certain structures while ignoring others (Johnson, 2020). If a platform suddenly required horizontal scrolling instead of vertical scrolling, it would feel confusing and inefficient because it violates established perceptual expectations. In this case, usability decreases not because the content changes, but because the visual and interaction structure no longer matches how users perceive and navigate information.

A similar principle can be seen in physical design, such as a credit card. On a typical card, the 16-digit number is divided into smaller groups rather than displayed as a single uninterrupted string. This grouping aligns with the Gestalt principle of proximity, where elements placed close together are perceived as related units (Johnson, 2020). Without this visual structure, the number would be significantly harder to read and remember. If additional information, such as the expiration date, were placed directly within the same line as the card number, it would create visual overload and make it more difficult for users to separate distinct pieces of information.

Instead, credit card design uses clear visual hierarchy to organize information. The card number is separated into grouped blocks, the expiration date is placed in its own distinct location, and the security code is often positioned separately on the back of the card with visual cues such as a signature strip or patterned background. These design decisions guide the user’s attention and help them quickly identify which information is needed in a given context. This reflects Johnson’s idea that visual structure is essential for helping users interpret and navigate complex information efficiently (Johnson, 2020).

The Role of UX Laws in Perception and Visual Hierarchy

While these examples show how perception works in practice, UX laws provide a more structured way to explain why these design decisions improve usability. The following principles—Jakob’s Law, Fitts’s Law, Miller’s Law, and Hick’s Law—build on the idea that users rely on familiarity, efficient interaction, limited memory, and simplified choices when navigating interfaces (Yablonski, 2024).

Jakob’s Law

Jakob’s Law emphasizes that users expect websites to work similarly to others they already know. This is why most websites place navigation at the top of the page and important utility icons, such as search, in familiar locations. When a design breaks these expectations, users need more time to learn how it works, which increases friction and reduces usability (Yablonski, 2024).

A clear example of this can be seen on the Pagani website. Unlike most automotive websites, its navigation is not immediately visible in a standard top bar. Instead, it is hidden on the left side and expands into a full-screen menu when clicked. This design choice creates a less familiar structure compared to typical car brand websites. In addition, the footer does not follow common patterns where users expect to find quick links, support, or secondary information. As a result, users may need more time to locate familiar functions.

Fitts’s Law

Fitts’s Law explains that the time required to interact with a target depends on its size and distance. In UX design, this means that important actions should be large enough and placed close to relevant content to reduce effort and improve speed of interaction (Yablonski, 2024).

A strong example of this principle can be seen on the Apple website. When presenting a product, Apple places primary action buttons such as “Learn more” or “Buy” directly near the product being described. This reduces the distance between the user’s focus area and the action they need to take. As a result, users can quickly move from viewing a product to engaging with it, which supports both usability and conversion.

Miller’s Law

Miller’s Law states that people can only hold a limited amount of information in working memory at one time. For this reason, information is easier to understand when it is grouped into small, meaningful chunks rather than presented as one large block (Yablonski, 2024).

A simple real-world example is credit card numbers, which are divided into groups of four digits to make them easier to read and remember. In digital design, the same principle is applied through visual grouping and sectioning. For example, on the Amazon website, content is organized into clearly defined product sections, recommendations, and categories. Each block of content is visually separated, allowing users to scan the page quickly and process information without feeling overwhelmed. This chunking of content reduces cognitive load and improves navigation efficiency.

Hick’s Law

Hick’s Law explains that the more choices a user has, the longer it takes to make a decision. When users are presented with too many options at once, decision-making becomes slower and more mentally demanding (Yablonski, 2024).

Streaming platforms demonstrate this principle well. On the Hulu website, content is organized into categories such as “Trending,” “Movies,” and “TV Shows.” Instead of showing all possible content equally, the platform groups and prioritizes options. This reduces the number of immediate decisions users must make and helps them focus on a smaller, more manageable set of choices.

Introduction

The following blog explores the concepts that support user thinking and examines what makes digital systems “usable.” It begins by discussing how memory limitations and mental models influence user interaction and shape the way people navigate digital environments. The blog then explores the importance of interface consistency and system feedback in reducing confusion and improving usability. Finally, it examines Tesler’s Law and the idea of necessary complexity, followed by practical examples of poor design choices that create unnecessary cognitive load and the lessons designers can learn from them.

Memory, Mental Models, and User Interaction

As we explored in my previous blog post, user experience is strongly shaped by the way people perceive digital systems. Those perceptions are often influenced by habits, expectations, and cognitive biases. However, perception alone does not explain why some interfaces feel natural while others feel frustrating. A major factor behind usability is human memory and the way people mentally process information while interacting with technology.

Human memory has a limited capacity, and understanding those limitations is essential for effective digital design. In Designing with the Mind in Mind, Johnson (2020) explains the concept of working memory, which refers to the small amount of information people actively focus on and remain aware of at a given moment. Unlike computer memory, working memory is connected to human attention and can only manage a limited number of items at once, typically around “five plus or minus one” pieces of information (Johnson, 2020). Because of this limitation, users can quickly become overwhelmed when interfaces require them to remember too many instructions, steps, or details simultaneously.

This limitation becomes even more important when combined with human attention. People naturally focus on one primary task while ignoring information that seems unrelated to their goal. One of the most famous examples of this is the "Invisible Gorilla" experiment created by Christopher Chabris and Daniel Simons. In the experiment, viewers are asked to count basketball passes between players wearing white shirts. While participants focus on counting, many completely fail to notice a person in a gorilla costume walking through the scene. This demonstrates a concept known as inattentional blindness: people miss obvious information when their attention is directed elsewhere. In digital environments, this means users may overlook warnings, buttons, or instructions if the interface competes for too much of their attention.

Johnson (2020) further explains that users tend to focus primarily on their goals while paying little attention to the interface itself. Once people learn a familiar way to complete a task, they often continue following the same pattern automatically. This behavior connects to the idea of mental models, which are the expectations users develop based on previous experiences with technology. For example, users expect a magnifying glass icon to represent search, a shopping cart icon to represent checkout, and a logo in the top-left corner to return them to the homepage. When interfaces follow these familiar patterns, users can rely on habit and complete tasks with less mental effort.

Because users prefer familiar interactions, designers must balance creativity with usability. Imagine designing a website for a fictional shoe company called Makeitup. While the goal may be to create a unique and visually memorable brand, the website should still include familiar interface patterns that users already understand. The navigation menu should appear in a recognizable location, such as the top of the page, and the search function should use a standard search icon. If designers ignore these common expectations simply to appear different, users may become confused and spend more energy figuring out how the website works instead of focusing on their actual goal: shopping for shoes.

Understanding memory and mental models allows designers to create interfaces that feel more user-friendly instead of mentally exhausting. Rather than forcing users to constantly think, remember, and search for information, good design supports natural habits and reduces cognitive effort. As Johnson (2020) argues, effective interfaces should work with the strengths and weaknesses of human attention and memory rather than burdening users with unnecessary complexity.

Interface Consistency, System Feedback, and Improved Usability

As we continue exploring how users think and interact with digital systems, it becomes clear that usability depends not only on how an interface looks, but also on how predictable and responsive it is. Two of the most important factors that shape usability are interface consistency and system feedback. Together, they reduce confusion, support user confidence, and lower cognitive load during interaction.

Interface consistency refers to designing systems where similar elements look, function, and behave in similar ways across a product. However, it is important to understand that consistency does not mean duplication (Figure 1). Instead, it allows users to rely on familiar patterns such as repeated navigation layouts, consistent button styles, and predictable terminology. As Diaz (2025) explains, consistency is not about making everything identical, but about creating recognizable patterns that help users feel oriented and confident. When users recognize these patterns, they can transfer their knowledge from one part of a system to another, which reduces mental effort and improves overall usability. Consistency also aligns with user expectations formed from other digital experiences, meaning users naturally rely on familiar design conventions they have already learned from other websites and applications (Diaz, 2025).

While consistency helps users understand how a system works, system feedback helps them understand what is happening in real time. Feedback refers to how a system communicates the result of a user’s action, such as confirming a submission, showing loading progress, or displaying an error message (Yale University, n.d.). Without feedback, users are left uncertain whether their action worked or whether the system is still processing. This uncertainty often leads to frustration, repeated actions, or loss of trust in the interface.

Clear system status communication is essential for usability because it keeps users informed, oriented, and in control. According to Yale University (n.d.), users should always know where they are in a system, what is happening after an action, and what to expect next. This can be achieved through progress indicators, loading states, page titles, and navigation cues such as breadcrumbs. These elements help users understand both their current position and the overall structure of the system (Yale University, n.d.).

System feedback is especially important in multi-step processes, where users need to track progress over time. For example, step indicators or progress bars help users understand how far they are in a process and what still remains. When systems also provide time estimates or clear loading states, users can better manage their expectations and decide whether to continue waiting or return later. As Yale University (n.d.) explains, this type of transparency reduces anxiety and builds trust because users are not left guessing what the system is doing.

Ultimately, both consistency and feedback work together to improve usability by reducing unnecessary cognitive effort. Consistency allows users to rely on learned patterns and habits, while feedback ensures they are continuously informed about system status and outcomes. When combined, these principles create a more predictable and reassuring experience where users can focus on their goals rather than trying to interpret the interface itself.

Tesler’s Law, Poor Design Choices, and Principles That Guide Design Decisions

Tesler’s Law and Necessary Complexity

Before discussing examples of poor design and unnecessary cognitive load, it is important to understand that not all complexity can be removed from a system. This idea is explained by Tesler’s Law, also known as the law of conservation of complexity. According to Yablonski (2024), every system contains a certain amount of complexity that cannot be completely eliminated. In other words, even the most simplified interface will still contain some necessary complexity because certain tasks naturally require information, decisions, or actions from users.

However, Tesler’s Law does not suggest that designers should ignore usability problems. Instead, it emphasizes that designers and developers should absorb as much complexity as possible so users do not carry unnecessary mental burden during interaction (Yablonski, 2024). Good design does not remove complexity entirely; rather, it organizes and manages complexity in a way that feels easier and more intuitive for users.

One example of this can be seen in modern email systems. Sending an email always requires certain information, such as the sender and recipient addresses. This complexity cannot be removed because it is essential to the task itself. However, modern email platforms reduce cognitive effort by automatically filling in the sender information and suggesting recipient addresses based on previous interactions (Yablonski, 2024). The task still contains necessary complexity, but much of the mental effort has been shifted from the user to the system.

Poor Design Examples and Lessons Learned

Although good design aims to reduce cognitive load, poor interface decisions can make even simple tasks frustrating and mentally exhausting. Many usability problems occur when designers ignore how people process information, focus attention, and make decisions.

One common issue I frequently encounter is the use of text placed over visually noisy backgrounds. Sometimes designers place white or light-colored text on top of detailed images, making the content difficult to read. As shown in Figure 2, the text blends into the mountain background, forcing users to spend additional mental effort trying to distinguish letters and words. Johnson (2020) explains that visual noise around text interrupts automatic reading processes and makes comprehension slower and more difficult. Instead of focusing on the message itself, users must first work to visually separate the text from the background.

Another frustrating design problem involves unclear loading indicators. Many users have experienced situations where a loading icon appears, but the system provides no explanation of how long the process will take or what is happening in the background. Figure 3 demonstrates a more effective approach because it includes a visible progress percentage that helps users understand the current system status. However, many systems still rely on endless loading animations without meaningful feedback, leaving users uncertain whether the system is working properly. According to Yale University (n.d.), clear system status communication reduces anxiety and helps users feel informed and in control during interaction.

A final example relates to overwhelming or inefficient selection systems. One experience I personally find frustrating is manually selecting birth dates in online forms (Figure 4). Some systems require users to navigate month-by-month through a calendar interface to reach dates many years in the past. For example, a user born in 1988 may need to repeatedly click backward through dozens of months before reaching the correct year. This creates unnecessary cognitive and physical effort for a very simple task. Yablonski (2024) connects this issue to Hick’s Law, which states that decision-making becomes slower and more difficult when users are presented with excessive or inefficient choices. In this case, the interface prioritizes visual simplicity over practical usability

These examples demonstrate how poor design can increase cognitive load by forcing users to spend unnecessary mental energy interpreting interfaces, waiting without information, or completing repetitive actions. Effective design should reduce these burdens by supporting clarity, feedback, and efficient interaction patterns.

Module 2.3 Blog Post

Introduction

The following blog explores how visual and interaction design influence the way users understand and move through digital interfaces. It begins by introducing key usability principles, focusing on learnability and understandability, and explains how clear and consistent systems help users interact more easily and with less cognitive effort. It then examines several UX laws, including the Aesthetic–Usability Effect, the Von Restorff Effect, and Postel’s Law, to show how visual design, attention, and system flexibility shape user behavior and perception. Finally, it connects these ideas to a personal experience to demonstrate how design choices impact real user interactions in practice.

Interface Layout and Control Design Impact on User Behavior

Before diving deeper into interface layout and its effect on user perception, it is important to first examine the factors that influence how users interact with digital systems. Understanding user behavior requires establishing a foundation for analyzing the decisions designers must make when creating effective user interfaces. Since interfaces are designed for people rather than machines, successful design depends heavily on how users learn, interpret, and navigate a system.

The System Needs to Be Learnable

One of the most important principles of interface design is learnability. Users are more likely to successfully navigate a website or application when the system feels intuitive and easy to understand. In Designing with the Mind in Mind, Johnson explains that people learn faster when a system is task-focused and simple because it minimizes the gap between a user’s goal and the actions required to accomplish that goal (Johnson, 2020). When users can quickly understand how to interact with an interface, they are more likely to remain engaged and complete their intended tasks successfully.

Johnson (2020) demonstrates this idea through the example of a real estate website designed for users searching for homes. Initially, the website asked users to choose between searching “by location” using a ZIP code or searching “by map.” During usability testing, however, many users became confused because they viewed both methods as the same type of search: searching by location. The only difference was how the location was entered. After observing this confusion, the designers merged the two options into a single interface that included both a map and a ZIP code field. This redesign simplified the user experience and aligned the system more closely with how users naturally think about the task (Johnson, 2020).

Consistency also plays a major role in learnability and user behavior. Johnson (2020) explains that consistent systems allow users to predict how different features will function. As users recognize familiar patterns, their interactions become faster and more automatic. Inconsistent systems, on the other hand, force users to repeatedly relearn controls and navigation methods, increasing cognitive load and reducing efficiency. Predictable layouts, navigation menus, and interaction patterns help users build confidence because they can anticipate what will happen next (Johnson, 2020).

The System Needs to Be Understandable

In addition to being learnable, digital systems must also be understandable. Users should not have to stop and interpret confusing instructions, technical terminology, or unclear interface language to complete simple tasks. As Johnson explains in Designing with the Mind in Mind, users learn faster when the vocabulary of an interface is task-focused, familiar, and consistent (Johnson, 2020). Rather than exposing users to technical jargon, designers should use language that reflects concepts users already understand from the real world. When terminology is familiar and predictable, users can navigate a system more naturally and with greater confidence.

The importance of clear UX writing is also emphasized in an article by Plerdy. Chornyy (2023) explains that UX writing plays a major role in shaping how users experience digital interfaces because it guides users through tasks and reduces uncertainty. In the article, he highlights the importance of providing clear instructions, avoiding technical language, and maintaining consistent terminology throughout a system.

For example, Chornyy (2023) discusses how ambiguous instructions can quickly confuse users. A message such as “Enter valid data” provides little guidance because users may not understand what qualifies as “valid.” In contrast, more specific instructions, such as explaining the exact format required for a phone number or password, reduce confusion and help users complete tasks more efficiently. Similarly, overly technical error messages can frustrate users because they communicate system problems in language that many people do not understand (Figure 5).

In addition, consistency in terminology is equally important for creating understandable interfaces. When websites use multiple terms for the same feature, such as “Shopping Cart” on one page and “Shopping Bag” on another, users may become uncertain about whether the two labels refer to the same function (Chornyy, 2023). This inconsistency interrupts the user’s mental flow and increases cognitive load. By using clear and consistent vocabulary, designers create interfaces that feel more predictable and user-friendly.

UX Laws and Their Impact on User Behavior

Now that the importance of designing learnable and understandable systems has been established, it is also important to examine the UX principles that shape how users perceive and interact with digital interfaces. User experience design is heavily influenced by psychological principles that explain why users respond positively or negatively to certain design choices. While I discussed several UX principles in a previous blog post, this section will focus on three additional laws that strongly influence user behavior and perception.

Aesthetic–Usability Effect

One of the influential principles in user experience design is the Aesthetic–Usability Effect. In Laws of UX, Yablonski (2024) explains that users often perceive visually attractive designs as being easier to use, even when usability problems are still present. In other words, aesthetically pleasing interfaces create a positive emotional response that influences how users judge the overall experience.

According to Yablonski (2024), visually appealing designs improve users’ perceptions because people naturally form quick first impressions when interacting with digital products. This reaction is closely connected to automatic cognitive processing, where the brain rapidly evaluates visual information before users consciously analyze the interface. As a result, users often associate clean layouts, balanced spacing, attractive colors, and organized visual hierarchy with higher quality and better functionality.

A clear example of this effect can be seen in Apple products. Apple consistently focuses on creating visually polished and minimalistic designs across both its hardware and software. While other companies may offer similar or even more advanced technical features at a lower price, Apple is often perceived as superior due to its strong emphasis on aesthetics and overall design quality. Even when small usability issues exist, users may overlook them because the overall aesthetic creates a positive emotional impression.

Von Restorff Effect

Another important UX principle that influences user behavior is the Von Restorff Effect. In Laws of UX, Yablonski (2024) explains that when multiple similar elements are presented together, the item that visually stands out is the one users are most likely to notice and remember. This principle is closely connected to selective attention, since users naturally focus on elements that appear different from their surroundings.

In interface design, this effect is commonly used to guide users toward important actions or information. Designers often apply contrast through color, size, spacing, or placement to make specific elements stand out. For example, many websites use a brightly colored “Sign Up” or “Buy Now” button to immediately draw the user’s attention (Figure 6). Notification badges, warning messages, and highlighted pricing plans also rely on this principle to help users quickly identify important content.

However, Yablonski (2024) also explains that too much visual emphasis can reduce effectiveness. If too many elements compete for attention, users may feel overwhelmed and struggle to identify what is actually important. Therefore, designers must use contrast carefully and intentionally.

Postel’s Law

Another UX principle that strongly affects user interaction is Postel’s Law. In Laws of UX, Yablonski (2024) explains this principle as: “Be conservative in what you do, be liberal in what you accept from others.” In user experience design, this means systems should provide clear and reliable output while also being flexible and forgiving when users make mistakes or provide unexpected input.

Since users do not always behave predictably, good interfaces should be designed to handle variation without creating frustration. For example, when filling out forms, users may enter information in different formats or make small typing mistakes. A well-designed system should still help users complete the task by accepting reasonable variations and providing clear feedback instead of immediately rejecting the input.

Postel’s Law is also visible in responsive and accessible design. Users interact with websites across different devices, screen sizes, and input methods, including touchscreens, keyboards, and assistive technologies. Effective interfaces adapt to these differences while still maintaining a consistent and reliable experience (Figure 7).

Personal Experience and Closing Remarks

As I move toward the final section of this blog post, I would like to share a personal experience that demonstrates how poor interface design can negatively affect users. Before discussing this example, it is important to again emphasize the value of designing for human needs and behaviors. In his article on stable user interface design, Yuri Shapochka explains that while many modern digital products rely heavily on animations, transitions, and movement to make interfaces feel interactive and “alive,” excessive motion can instead make interfaces feel unreliable and mentally exhausting (Shapochka, 2026). According to Shapochka (2026), stable interfaces allow users to build a mental map of where elements are located and how the system behaves. However, when buttons, layouts, or content unexpectedly shift, users may lose focus, hesitate, and constantly reorient themselves, ultimately creating a frustrating user experience.

Throughout my school years, I used several digital textbook platforms for accessing course materials and audiobooks. One platform I frequently used was Pearson, which included a feature that allowed students to listen to audio narration while following highlighted text on the screen. Although this feature was intended to support learning and accessibility, the interface logic often created frustration in practice. Whenever I attempted to scroll through the page to examine an image or diagram related to the reading, the audio narration would suddenly stop and jump to another section of the text. As a result, I constantly needed to pause the narration before interacting with the page, even for simple actions such as reviewing a picture or rereading a paragraph.

Coming back to the principle outlined in Postel’s Law, systems should be flexible and tolerant of natural human behavior rather than expecting perfect or rigid interaction patterns (Yablonski, 2024). In this case, the platform did not account for common actions such as scrolling, exploring visual content, or accidental touch inputs. Instead of supporting flexible interaction, the system interrupted the learning process and forced users to repeatedly adjust their behavior. Although the feature itself was useful, the lack of interface stability ultimately reduced its effectiveness and created a frustrating user experience.
