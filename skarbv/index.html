Module 1.1 Blog Post

Introduction

The following blog explores the foundational concepts of systems thinking and Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) within the context of digital environments. It begins by defining what a system is and outlining its key characteristics, then introduces the concept of systems thinking. Next, it examines how systems thinking differs from traditional linear approaches and presents a model-based method for representing and analyzing complex systems. Finally, it concludes by highlighting the need for Model-Based Systems Engineering in the design and management of digital environments.

Defining a System

Before exploring the concept of systems thinking, it is important to first define what a system is in the context of this discussion. According to Jon Holt (n.d.), a system can be understood as a collection of interconnected elements that work together to achieve a specific purpose. Rather than existing in isolation, these elements interact with one another, forming a structured whole that produces outcomes through their relationships and interactions (Holt, n.d.).

Systems can take many different forms depending on their nature and application. For example, they may be physical, such as vehicles or digital devices; abstract, such as models or algorithms; or human-centered, such as organizations or social structures. In many real-world scenarios, systems often combine multiple categories, reflecting the complexity of modern environments (Holt, n.d.).

Despite their differences, most systems share several key characteristics. First, they consist of elements that are interconnected, meaning that changes in one part of the system can influence others. Second, systems exist within defined boundaries, which separate what is inside the system from what lies outside it. Third, systems involve stakeholders like individuals, organizations, or entities that have an interest in the system and may interact with it in different ways. Additionally, systems are described by attributes, which represent their properties, and are guided by needs, which define their purpose and intended outcomes. Finally, systems operate under various constraints, such as environmental, technical, or regulatory limitations, that shape how they can be designed and function (Holt, n.d.).

What is Systems Thinking?

Systems thinking is a broad concept that emphasizes understanding how each element of a system interacts within the context of the whole structure. In simpler terms, it is like assembling all the individual pieces of a system into a large mosaic, where every piece contributes to the overall picture. Instead of focusing only on isolated problems, systems thinking encourages attention to the relationships, patterns, and interdependencies among components.

Laurie Davies, a journalist and instructor at the University of Phoenix, describes systems thinking as an approach that considers the system as a whole rather than focusing solely on immediate problems. In her example of a malfunctioning shifting gear, she explains that systems thinking involves examining not just the gear itself, but also its design, operational conditions, environmental factors, and maintenance practices (Davies, 2024). By considering the various factors that contribute to a problem, systems thinking seeks to understand the relationships between the object and the broader system that may influence its behavior.

An essential aspect of systems thinking is its focus on several core principles that guide how we view and analyze systems. At its heart, it is about seeing the bigger picture and understanding how all the parts of a system work together rather than separately. Systems thinking encourages noticing patterns that emerge when elements interact, and how systems can adjust themselves through feedback to stay balanced or improve. It also reminds us to consider what is part of the system and what lies outside of it, to appreciate the complexity of real-world situations, and to include different viewpoints when making decisions (Lloyd & Smith, 2025). By applying these principles, systems thinking provides a practical framework for exploring the full context of problems, making it easier to identify root causes and anticipate the chain reactions.

The Difference Between Systems Thinking and Traditional Linear Thinking

To understand how systems thinking works, it is helpful to compare it with traditional, linear ways of thinking. Traditional thinking often follows a step-by-step approach, focusing on discrete tasks in isolation. For example, if one were tasked with building a car out of LEGO pieces, a linear approach would involve following the instructions in the manual exactly as written. By combining each component according to the guide, the builder would recreate the car as intended, with limited variation or exploration beyond the prescribed design.

In contrast, systems thinking encourages stepping beyond the guided process to explore how components can interact in alternative ways. Even with a set of identical LEGO bricks, there are countless combinations and possibilities. A systems thinking approach begins by examining each element's function, potential connections, and features, considering how each piece might serve multiple purposes or interact with other components. This approach emphasizes understanding the relationships and interdependencies between elements, rather than simply following predefined steps.

A research study conducted for the Indiana Interdisciplinary GK-12 program demonstrates that a systems thinking approach, compared to a traditional linear logic model, provides a more precise understanding of complex educational programs and their outcomes. The linear logic model, which assumes direct cause-and-effect relationships between program components, often oversimplifies complex programs and may overlook important contextual and relational factors. In contrast, a systems thinking approach using causal loop diagrams captures feedback loops, interdependencies, and emergent behaviors within the program. This allows evaluators to identify more effective solutions to problems, anticipate unintended consequences, and better understand how various program components interact to influence outcomes (Dyehouse et al., 2009).

What Is a Model-Based Approach?

Building on the ideas of systems thinking, engineers need practical ways to understand and manage complex systems. One such tool is Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE), which applies the principles of systems thinking to organize and visualize all parts of a system. A model-based approach, commonly referred to as Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE), is a modern method of systems engineering that uses models as the primary way to represent and understand a system. Instead of relying on large sets of documents, MBSE organizes all relevant system information into a structured model that serves as the central source of knowledge about the system (Holt, n.d.).

In traditional systems engineering, information about a system is typically spread across multiple documents, which can make it difficult to manage complexity and maintain consistency. In contrast, MBSE places all system knowledge within a model that acts as a simplified representation, or abstraction, of the real system. This model does not attempt to capture every detail but instead focuses on including only the most relevant information needed to successfully design and understand the system (Holt, n.d.).

Another important aspect of MBSE is that the model is organized into different “views,” each tailored to specific stakeholders. These views allow engineers, managers, and other participants to examine the system from different perspectives while still working from the same underlying model. Because of this, the model becomes a “single source of truth,” meaning that all stakeholders rely on the same consistent and structured information when making decisions (Holt, n.d.).

Overall, MBSE provides a more organized, visual, and efficient way to manage complex systems by focusing on models rather than documents. It improves communication, ensures consistency, and makes it easier to understand how different parts of a system interact.

The Need for Model-Based Systems Engineering in Digital Systems

As highlighted by Jon Holt in Systems Engineering Demystified, Model-Based Systems Engineering provides significant advantages in designing systems that are tailored to specific stakeholders. By focusing on delivering only relevant and useful information through structured models, MBSE ensures that each stakeholder can better understand the aspects of the system that matter most to them (Holt, n.d.). However, beyond these benefits, it is important to recognize why this approach is especially necessary in modern digital environments.

Holt explains that the need for modeling arises from three major challenges in systems engineering: complexity, lack of understanding, and poor communication. First, modern digital systems are inherently complex, often consisting of many interconnected components. MBSE helps manage this complexity by using visual models that make it easier to identify where complexity exists and how different parts of the system interact. This allows engineers to better control and reduce unnecessary complexity while focusing on critical system components (Holt, n.d.).

Second, MBSE improves understanding throughout the system's lifecycle. Holt suggests that when a model is easy to create, it indicates that the system is well understood. Conversely, difficulty in building a model reveals gaps, ambiguity, or missing information. In this way, modeling becomes a tool not only for representation but also for identifying weaknesses in system knowledge and design (Holt, n.d.).

Finally, MBSE enhances communication among stakeholders. Digital systems often involve diverse teams with different backgrounds and areas of expertise. By using standardized models, diagrams, and notations, MBSE provides a common language that allows stakeholders to clearly share information and collaborate more effectively. This reduces misunderstandings and ensures that all participants have a consistent view of the system (Holt, n.d.).

Overall, the need for MBSE in designing complex digital systems lies in its ability to manage complexity, improve understanding, and facilitate communication. Additionally, this approach not only provides an effective way to communicate information but also establishes a structured framework that aligns with stakeholder needs.

Conclusion

Applying systems thinking helps designers and users effectively navigate complex digital systems by focusing on the whole system, its interconnected parts, and the attributes that define it. Unlike traditional linear approaches, systems thinking emphasizes relationships, patterns, and interactions between elements to better understand how changes affect the system as a whole. To put systems thinking into practice and manage complexity, Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) provides structured, visual representations tailored to stakeholders. As a result, it helps reduce confusion and supports a clearer, more organized approach to designing professional digital systems.

Module 1.2 Blog Post

Introduction

The following blog explores the concept of the system development life cycle within systems engineering. It begins by defining the idea of a life cycle and how it applies to system development. It then examines the key characteristics, advantages, and limitations of linear, iterative, and incremental life cycle models as different approaches to organizing system development. Finally, it reflects on why life cycle thinking is important for the design, sustainability, and evolution of modern digital systems.

Identifying the Systems Development Life Cycle

Defining the Life Cycle Concept

To understand the systems development life cycle, it is first necessary to define what a life cycle actually is in a systems engineering context. In Systems Engineering Demystified, Holt explains that a life cycle describes the evolution of a system (or other entity) over time, and it is a core concept used to structure and understand how systems develop, operate, and eventually retire (Holt, n.d.).

A life cycle is not just a simple sequence of steps; instead, it is made up of stages, where each stage represents a distinct period in the evolution of a system. These stages together form the structure of the life cycle. However, Holt also distinguishes between a life cycle and a life cycle model. The life cycle defines what stages exist, while the life cycle model defines how those stages are executed, including their order and relationships (Holt, n.d.).

In other words, the life cycle provides the structural view of system evolution, while the life cycle model provides the behavioral view. This distinction is important because it highlights that system development is not only about defining phases, but also about understanding how those phases interact and progress over time.

Viewing Life Cycles Through the Lens of System Development

When applied specifically to system development, the life cycle concept is commonly expressed as the System Development Life Cycle (SDLC). Holt explains that the system life cycle is closely aligned with ISO 15288 and represents the full development journey of a system from its initial conception through to its retirement (Holt, n.d.). This framing is important because it emphasizes that system development is not limited to building a solution, but includes everything from understanding needs to disposing of the system responsibly.

The system development life cycle typically begins with a conception stage, where the needs of the system are identified and defined. This includes understanding stakeholder expectations and establishing criteria that will later be used to verify and validate the system. From there, the development stage focuses on exploring possible solutions and selecting the most appropriate one based on those needs. Once a solution has been agreed upon, the production stage involves building the system and ensuring that it meets its specified requirements through verification and validation activities (Holt, n.d.).

After production, the system moves into utilization, where it is actively used in its intended operational environment. This stage is often supported by a parallel support stage, which ensures that maintenance, troubleshooting, and ongoing operational assistance are available to keep the system functioning effectively. Finally, the life cycle concludes with the retirement stage, where the system is decommissioned and disposed of in a safe and controlled manner (Holt, n.d.).

However, Holt emphasizes that these stages are not always strictly linear. Depending on the nature of the system and the chosen life cycle model, stages may overlap, repeat, or even be omitted entirely. This flexibility is essential because different systems have different levels of complexity, risk, and operational demands. As a result, the life cycle model determines how the system actually moves through these stages in practice (Holt, n.d.).

Another important insight is that this same structure can be applied beyond just systems. Similar life cycle patterns exist for projects, programs, and products, although each operates at a different level of abstraction. A project life cycle focuses on the execution of a specific initiative, while a program life cycle coordinates multiple related projects. A product life cycle, on the other hand, focuses on the evolution of a deliverable from creation through to end-of-life. While these may share similar stages, their scope and purpose differ, which reinforces the importance of clearly defining what type of life cycle is being considered in any given situation (Holt, n.d.).

Key Characteristics, Advantages, and Potential Drawbacks of Life Cycle Models

Linear Life Cycle Model

The linear life cycle model represents the most traditional and structured approach to system development. It is based on the idea that development progresses through a fixed sequence of stages, where each stage must be completed before the next one begins. Holt explains that life cycle models describe not just the stages of a system’s evolution, but also the order in which those stages are executed, with linear models enforcing a strict step-by-step flow from start to finish (Holt, n.d.).

In this approach, development typically begins with clearly defined requirements, followed by design, implementation, testing, deployment, and finally maintenance. This structure is commonly associated with the Waterfall model, which is widely recognized as one of the earliest software development approaches. According to GeeksforGeeks, the Waterfall model follows a sequential process where each phase must be completed before moving forward, making it highly structured and easy to manage (GeeksforGeeks, 2026).

The main advantage of the linear model is its simplicity. Because each phase is clearly defined and documented, it is relatively easy to understand, plan, and manage. It also provides strong predictability in terms of timelines and deliverables, which makes it suitable for small to medium-sized projects with stable and well-understood requirements. However, this strength is also its biggest limitation. Once a stage is completed, it is difficult and costly to go back and make changes. This rigidity means that the linear model struggles in environments where requirements evolve or are not fully understood at the beginning. In addition, testing occurs late in the process, which increases the risk of discovering critical issues only after significant effort has already been invested (GeeksforGeeks, 2026).

Iterative Life Cycle Model

The iterative life cycle model introduces repetition into system development by allowing the same set of stages to be repeated in cycles. Instead of completing the system in one pass, development occurs through multiple iterations, where each cycle produces a refined version of the system. Holt explains that this approach enables continuous improvement, as each iteration builds on feedback and learning from the previous cycle (Holt, n.d.).

A key characteristic of the iterative model is its focus on refinement and learning. Rather than attempting to fully define and build the system at the beginning, developers start with a basic version and progressively improve it through repeated cycles of planning, implementation, testing, and review. This allows teams to respond to uncertainty and refine requirements as understanding evolves (Holt, n.d.).

One of the main advantages of this model is flexibility. Because feedback is gathered at the end of each iteration, the system can evolve in response to stakeholder input and changing requirements. The GridFox analysis highlights that this feedback-driven approach reduces risk by identifying issues early and allowing continuous correction throughout development (Court, 2025). This makes the iterative model particularly effective in environments where requirements are not fully known at the start or are expected to change.

However, this flexibility also introduces challenges. Without strong control, iterative development can lead to scope expansion, where continuous changes extend project timelines. It also requires disciplined management to ensure that iterations remain focused and that progress is measurable. While early versions of the system may be delivered faster than in traditional models, the overall development process can become longer if refinement cycles are not carefully controlled (Court, 2025).

Incremental Life Cycle Model

The incremental life cycle model develops a system by breaking it into smaller, fully functional parts called increments. Each increment delivers a complete subset of system functionality, and new increments are added over time until the full system is complete. Unlike the iterative model, which refines the same features repeatedly, the incremental model builds additional features in stages.

A defining characteristic of this model is staged delivery of functionality. Each increment is designed to be operational on its own, allowing users to benefit from parts of the system before the entire system is finished. This makes it particularly useful in long-term projects where early delivery of value is important (Holt, n.d.).

One of the major advantages of the incremental model is early usability, as stakeholders can interact with working components of the system much earlier in the development process. This improves feedback quality and helps ensure that the system aligns more closely with real-world needs. According to Court (2025), incremental development also supports predictable delivery and reduces risk by isolating functionality into manageable components. This approach makes planning and resource allocation more structured compared to highly flexible models.

However, the incremental model also has limitations. Not all systems can be easily divided into independent functional parts, especially when components are highly interconnected. In such cases, forcing an incremental structure can lead to design inefficiencies or integration challenges. Additionally, careful architectural planning is required to ensure that each increment integrates smoothly into the overall system without creating inconsistencies across releases.

Why Is It Important to Think About a System’s Life Cycle?

Systems engineering is not just about defining systems or their life cycles, it is about understanding how systems are created, used, maintained, and improved over time. Thinking in terms of a life cycle shifts the focus from simply building a system to managing its entire lifespan, from initial concept to retirement (Holt, n.d.).

A life cycle perspective helps ensure that systems are designed with long-term use in mind. Decisions made during early stages, such as requirements and design, directly affect how well a system can be maintained, updated, and adapted later. Since systems are considered evolving entities that change over time, early design choices play a critical role in their long-term success (Holt, n.d.).

It also supports sustainability by recognizing that systems do not end at deployment. Instead, they require continuous support, monitoring, and improvement throughout their operational life. Real-world standards emphasize that maintenance is an ongoing phase that includes updates, security checks, and system support to ensure reliability and resilience over time (Northeastern University, n.d.).

Finally, thinking in terms of a life cycle allows systems to evolve alongside changing technologies and user needs. Rather than treating systems as fixed products, this perspective encourages continuous refinement and adaptation. This aligns with modern development approaches where systems are regularly updated and improved based on feedback and changing requirements (Court, 2025).

Overall, life cycle thinking encourages a more complete and forward-looking approach to system development. It ensures that systems are not only built effectively but also remain useful, secure, and adaptable throughout their entire lifespan.
