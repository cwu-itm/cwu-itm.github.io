Module 3.1 Blog Post

Why Interface Logic Matters More Than Ever

Hello, everyone! As I spend more time in my IT and administrative management classes, I've started thinking a lot about how information moves-not just through a network, but from a screen into a user's brain. Lately, my coursework and even my time at work have really pushed me to look past the gibberish of a complex screen to find the patterns that actually make sense to a human.

In the world of UX, those patterns are often governed by invisible laws. If you've ever felt like an app was "reading your mind" or, conversely, felt like throwing your phone across the room because you couldn't find a "Submit" button, you've experienced the power of interaction design.

Layout and Control

When we talk about how users move through an interface, we have to talk about Fitts's Law. In simple terms, the time it takes to move to a target depends on how far away it is and how big it is. Our brains are constantly calculating these distances, often subconsciously, to minimize effort (Interaction Design Foundation, 2010).

In my work as a Problem Solver at Amazon, I resolve inventory discrepancies. The software we use is data-heavy, but it works because the most frequent actions—like "Confirm" or "Scan"—are large and placed in predictable areas. If those buttons were tiny or tucked away in a corner, my productivity would tank. As Johnson points out, our motor system is remarkably precise, but it has limits. Designing for those limits means putting the most important controls where the "travel time" for our mouse or thumb is minimized (Johnson, 2020).

Recognition over Recall

One of the most important concepts from the readings is that our brains are much better at recognizing things than remembering them from scratch. This is why clear, visual icons and consistent layouts are so vital. If I have to remember that a specific "mystery" icon leads to my profile, the design has failed.

  • The Von Restorff Effect: Also known as the Isolation Effect, this predicts that when multiple similar objects are present, the one that differs from the rest is most likely to be remembered.
  • Application in Security: In cybersecurity, we use this to highlight threats. If a dashboard shows a hundred green "Healthy" status bars and one flashing red "Alert," my eye is immediately drawn to the anomaly.
  • Interaction Design: I try to apply this in my own projects. By making the primary action—like a "Start Game" button in Unreal Engine 5—visually distinct from the "Settings," I'm guiding the user's behavior.

Cognitive Load and the Human Limit

A major part of how we move through an interface depends on our working memory and cognitive limits. Johnson explains that our attention is a limited resource and we are easily distracted. When an interface is cluttered, it forces us to use up our mental energy just to figure out how to use the tool, rather than focusing on the actual task (Johnson, 2020).

We see this in any complex digital environment; if a dashboard is poorly designed, a user might miss a critical notification because it was buried under visual noise. A good interface anticipates that the user is human-meaning we make mistakes, we overthink, and we have limited patience.

Three Laws

Reflecting on my own habits, three specific UX laws stand out:

  1. Jakob's Law: This law states that users spend most of their time on other sites. They want your site to work like everything else they know. Even though I want my future game design studio to be unique, I have to stick to certain conventions. If I make the "Escape" key do anything other than open a menu, players will be frustrated (Nielsen, 2019).
  2. Hick's Law: The time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of choices. I recently went through all my digital subscriptions to cancel several because I was feeling subscription fatigue. When an interface gives me too many options, I don't feel empowered; I feel paralyzed (Yablonski, 2024).
  3. The Aesthetic-Usability Effect: We tend to perceive attractive products as more usable. I recently started using Microsoft OneNote for my notes after years of using pen and paper. Because the interface is clean and visually appealing, I'm much more patient with myself as I learn the software.

Mental Models and Logic

One concept that really stuck with me is how we build mental models of how things work. Johnson notes that our mental models are often incomplete or even incorrect, but they still guide our behavior. For example, when I'm using a new software for class, I'm constantly comparing it to tools I already know (Johnson, 2020).

If a website's interaction logic doesn't match a user's mental model, they get lost. As students, we're already balancing so much—I'm currently managing 20 credits and 20 hours of work a week. We don't have the extra brainpower to solve a puzzle just to find where to upload an assignment.

When Logic Fails

I recently had a frustrating experience that perfectly illustrates what happens when interface logic ignores the user. I was taking a quiz and was given the choice between a "Learn, Test, and Review" mode or a "Gamified Quiz." I chose the gamified version, thinking it would be more engaging.

It was a nightmare. The gamification required me to get 270 questions right three times in a row. I spent over eight hours on it, and at the end, the system just backed out to a dashboard without recording my score. This was a total failure of Postel's Law, which suggests being "liberal in what you accept." The interface should have been resilient enough to save my progress. Instead, it left me wondering if I'd have to do the whole thing over.

Visual Hierarchy and Patterns

Movement through an interface isn't just about clicking; it's about where our eyes go first. Johnson discusses how our vision is optimized for detecting structure and patterns, which is why we naturally seek out alignment and grouping in a layout. Most users follow a Z-pattern or F-pattern when scanning a page, moving their gaze along the top and then down across the content. If the most important information—like a critical security alert on a network dashboard or the primary "Connect" button on a LinkedIn profile—isn't placed along those natural paths, it might as well not exist (Johnson, 2020).

In my digital environments classes, I learned that professional formatting isn't just about looking "nice"; it's about hierarchy. Using headings, bold text, and white space guides the user's eye toward the most relevant data, preventing cognitive overload. I now plan to use these patterns across all my digital projects to make sure the user experience is clear, intuitive, and immediately scannable.

The Role of Feedback and System Status

When we interact with a digital environment, we need to know that the system heard us. Johnson explains that interactive systems must provide immediate feedback to be usable. This is something I value deeply in my Dexcom CGM. It doesn't just show a number; it shows an arrow indicating if my sugar is dropping or rising, and it alarms if I'm too low or high (Johnson, 2020).

That immediate feedback reduces my anxiety because I'm not guessing what the system (my body) is doing. Compare that to the gamified quiz experience where I finished 270 questions and received zero confirmation that my score was recorded. Without feedback, the conversation between the user and the interface is broken.

The Ethical Side of Interaction

As I look toward a career in cybersecurity, the ethics of these design choices weigh on me. Laws like the Aesthetic-Usability Effect can be a double-edged sword. While it's great that I'm more patient with beautiful software, that same beauty can sometimes mask security flaws or poor data practices.

We have a responsibility to ensure that the ways we influence user behavior are transparent. Whether it's through being honest about data collection or making privacy settings easy to find, the goal of UX should be to empower the user. I and many others in my family are concerned about privacy, which is why we don't use many IoT devices like Alexa. We should never trick a user into giving up their integrity for the sake of a smoother interface.

The Impact of Dark Patterns and Habituation

In my class on Seeing Through the Data, we discussed the impact of big data and ethical issues like greenwashing. In UX, this translates to "dark patterns"—designs meant to trick users into doing things they didn't intend, like signing up for a subscription they don't want.

Johnson notes that our brains become habituated to certain patterns. If an app always puts the "Accept" button on the right, a dark pattern might suddenly swap it with "Buy Now" to exploit our muscle memory. As someone who values being driven and principled, I find these tactics incredibly frustrating. They break the trust that Jakob's Law is built on.

Human-Centered Design

At the end of the day, all these laws point back to one central idea: human-centered design. Whether I am building a survival game in Unreal Engine 5 or troubleshooting a technical issue at an Amazon warehouse, the user is a person with specific goals and limited time. If we ignore how the mind actually works—how it perceives color, how it manages memory, or how it reacts to stress—we aren't just making "bad" software; we are creating barriers for people who are just trying to live their lives or do their jobs.

Conclusion

By respecting the laws of the mind, we can build digital environments that are not only efficient but also respectful of the people using them. Whether I'm building a video game or just organizing my own projects, I want to create systems that help people feel in control. Understanding these UX laws is a huge step in moving from being just a consumer of technology to being a creator who can truly analyze the patterns behind the interface and build something meaningful. It's a hard habit to kick—overthinking every click—but it makes me a better professional and a more conscious user.
