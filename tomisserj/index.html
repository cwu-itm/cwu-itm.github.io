Why Systems Thinking Matters in Digital Environments

Introduction

Most people who work in IT for any length of time eventually learn a hard lesson. When you fix one problem, you can create three more. A server gets patched but it might break an application in the process. A new feature is added to the ERP and a downstream workflow stops working. These situations are a result of approaching complex systems with a linear mindset. This blog post explores systems thinking as a foundational concept for anyone working in a digital environment. It defines what systems thinking is, explains how it differs from traditional linear thinking, and argues why a model-based approach is the most practical way to design (and manage) complex digital systems.

Systems Thinking

Systems thinking is a way of understanding the world by focusing on relationships rather than individual parts. It recognizes that a system is not just a collection of pieces. The pieces all interact with each other, and those interactions matter more than the pieces themselves. The Interaction Design Foundation defines systems thinking as "an approach that designers use to analyze problems in an appropriate context" that looks beyond individual symptoms to consider "a system as a whole" (IxDF, 2026). The main goal is finding root causes, not just surface level fixes. In the 1960s, Ludwig von Bertalanffy introduced "general systems theory," which proposed that parts of a system can best be understood in the context of their relationships with each other and with other systems, rather than in isolation. That principle is what is at the core of systems thinking today. Systems thinking is more circular than linear. It accounts for feedback loops, emergent behaviors, and unintended consequences. Really it focuses on what happens over time and how the parts in any system influence each other in ways.

Linear Thinking

Linear thinking is the default mode for most people and organizations. It is sequential where it isolates a problem, identifies a cause, and then applies a fix. Assuming that if you understand each part separately, you will understand the whole. This works well for simple problems that are easily defined. The problem is that most systems today are not simple. They are interconnected and dynamic with many different systems. Linear thinking does not account for feedback loops. It does not consider the ripple across the entire environment.

"The linear thinking implies the whole is equal to the sum of its parts, which is not effective from a business perspective." — Future of CIO (2015)

Systems thinking views problems as part of a wider interconnected environment. It focuses on relationships and recognizes feedback loops that can amplify effects within a system. It considers external factors and conditions. It asks not just "what caused this?" but more "what does this affect or what affects it?" Here is an example that illustrates this clearly. Imagine an IT department tasked with improving employee productivity in a company's order management process. A linear thinker might identify one lower performing step in the workflow and adds automation to speed it up. That step gets faster. But downstream, two other steps now receive data faster than they were designed to handle causing errors to increase. Creating more work to fix the issue and causing productivity to drop. Whereas a systems thinker would have mapped the entire workflow first. The relationships between steps, the people, and the data flowing between them. Then consider the external systems connected to the process. This would all be part of the analysis before any change was made and the result would have considered the whole.

The Three Evils

Holt (n.d.) identifies three root causes of systems failure in engineering projects. He calls them the "Three Evils of Systems Engineering." They are complexity, communication failure, and lack of understanding.

1. Complexity exists in every system. There are two types worth distinguishing. Essential complexity is the natural complexity that is inherent in any system. It cannot be eliminated, but can be managed. Accidental complexity is introduced by inefficiencies in the people, processes, or tools used to build the system. Accidental complexity can be reduced.

2. Communication failure happens when different stakeholders use the same words to mean different things, or when information is assumed to be shared when it is not. Successful systems engineering requires definitions so that all parties understand the language the same way.

3. Lack of understanding occurs when different points of view are not considered or assumptions are made. It essentially becomes a context problem. Where two stakeholders looking at the same system requirement can interpret it differently (sometimes depending on role). A driver and a mechanic have different ideas of what "the system must be safe" means in the context of a car. The same is true in digital environments, where developers, security teams, or end users look at the same system with different expectations.

These three evils feed into each other. Unmanaged complexity causes communication to break down. Communication breakdown leads to misunderstanding. Misunderstanding leads to more complexity. This cycle is why systems thinking is not optional and is the only approach that can address all three problems at once.

Model-Based Approach

Where systems thinking tells you how to think about a problem, Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) is the practical framework for applying that thinking in a structured way. MBSE is a complete approach to systems engineering. The main idea is that all knowledge about a system should live in a model rather than spread out across documents. That model is a simplified representation of the system and captures enough relevant information to realize the system successfully. It serves as a "single source of truth." If a stakeholder wants to understand any aspect of the system, they refer to the model. This directly addresses the main problems that drive system failures (communication and understanding). MBSE also addresses complexity by making it visible. When building a model, you will see where the complexity lives. You can see which parts of the system have the most dependencies and identify where a change is likely to create problems. This is the core argument for visual modeling. Diagrams do not just communicate information, they show structure that might have been previously invisible. There is a simple test for whether a system is well understood. If a model of the system is easy to generate, the source information is well specified and understood. If the model is difficult to generate, the system is poorly understood. Modeling makes assumptions and gaps visible.

Application in Digital Environments

Modern digital systems are not standalone applications. They are connected to other systems, users, and organizations. A change to one system can ripple across many others. The increasing complexity of modern systems is easy to illustrate. A 1970s automobile was largely mechanical, simple and well-defined. A 2026 automobile contains software across every major system and connects to satellites and cloud infrastructure. Considering things like environmental regulations, safety standards, and cybersecurity requirements. The basic need has not changed where the car still moves people from one location to another. However, the complexity of meeting that need has grown exponentially. Digital systems follow this same pattern. An ERP system in a farm operation touches accounting, inventory, payroll, and reporting. It integrates with vendors/banks and has compliance requirements. A "simple" change to how inventory is categorized can affect cost accounting, purchasing approval workflows, and financial reporting all at once. Without approaching this from a systems thinking standpoint, the downstream effects are invisible until they become problems. Systems thinking is what allows designers or engineers to trace those dependencies before they cause failures.

Conclusion

Systems thinking is not new, it has been at the foundation of organizational theory for many years. It is more relevant now than ever. Digital environments are growing more complex, more connected, and more interdependent every year. Linear thinking asks: what is wrong with this part? Systems thinking asks: how do all the parts relate, and what happens to the whole when any one part changes? A model-based approach is the most practical way to apply systems thinking to the design/management of complex digital environments. It creates shared understanding and makes complexity visible. It creates a consistent source of truth. Directing IT on a Hop Farm, I see this every day. Every change I make to our ERP or networking infrastructure or data workflows can affect people (and processes) in ways I did not anticipate. Systems thinking is not just an academic concept, it is the only approach that allows me to understand what I am changing before I change it.

Thinking in Life Cycles

Introduction

Every system has a beginning and an end. They get designed > built > tested > used > updated and then eventually retired or replaced. That is a system life cycle. With development, there is a framework for thinking about a system not just as something you build once, but as something that evolves over time. It needs to be planned at every stage. For anyone working in digital design (or even IT), understanding system life cycles is not an exercise. It is foundational for making better decisions earlier, avoiding problems later, and building things that last. This post defines the systems development life cycle, explores three major life cycle models, and reflects on why this kind of thinking matters in designing and sustaining digital systems.

Systems Development Life Cycle

The systems development life cycle is the structured process a system goes through from idea to decommissioning. It is a way of organizing the work into stages so that each phase has a clear purpose and outputs. Holt (n.d.) describes the life cycle as covering everything from the earliest concept of a system all the way through its operation and eventual retirement. The stages typically include: concept, development, production, utilization, support, and retirement. The life cycle model you choose determines how those stages are sequenced, how much flexibility exists between them, and how feedback/changes are handled. Choosing the right model for any project can be the difference between running a smooth project or a failed one.

Model 1: Linear Life Cycle

The linear or waterfall model, moves through each stage in sequence. One phase must be completed before the next begins. Requirements are gathered up front, design follows, then development, then testing, then deployment. The main advantage of this approach is simplicity. It is easy to plan, track, and works well when requirements are stable. However, they must be well understood from the start. This is one major drawback. If something changes mid-project, or if a deliverable was missed at the beginning, going back is disruptive and could be costly. For digital systems that are expected to evolve (or involve uncertain requirements), the linear model creates risk. By the time a problem is discovered in testing, large amounts of time and money have already been committed to a design that may need to change.

Model 2: Iterative Life Cycle

The iterative or flywheel model breaks the work into cycles, where each cycle produces a working version of the system that is then reviewed and improved in the next cycle. Rather than trying to define everything at the start, the iterative approach improves over time, improvement is built into the process. Early cycles focus on simplified versions of the system. Each pass adds capability and updates based on feedback. The advantage here is early visibility. Stakeholders can see a working system much sooner, and problems are fixed within the projects budget. Teaching Agile (2025) points out that the iterative model requires experienced teams and risks incomplete documentation if not managed carefully. For complex digital systems with evolving requirements, the iterative model is a strong fit. It is important to note that it requires more ongoing management attention than that of a linear approach.

Model 3: Incremental Life Cycle

The incremental model divides the full system into smaller pieces called increments and delivers each one as a functional unit that builds on what came before. The first increment delivers a working product. Each subsequent increment adds functionality until the complete system is finished. The key difference between incremental and iterative is that incremental development delivers real, usable pieces of the system to stakeholders along the way rather than just prototypes. Guru99 (2024) describes the incremental model as producing a working software version early in the life cycle, allowing customers to respond to each build. The advantage in digital environments is that users can provide real feedback based on actual use rather than how they think it should work. Similar to the linear model, you need a clear overall design vision from the start. If the full scope of the system is not understood well enough to define what the various increments will be, the model can break down quickly.

Why Life Cycle Thinking Matters

Thinking in terms of system life cycles forces a more complete view of what you are trying to build. It is easy to focus on getting a system launched and treat everything after that as someone else's problem. But digital systems do not stop needing attention when they go live. They need to be monitored, updated, potentially integrated with new systems, and eventually replaced. Holt (n.d.) describes several enabling system types that support the life cycle, including training systems, production systems, and support systems, all of which are needed long after initial development is done. If those needs are not considered early, the costs of supporting the system later could go up significantly. A system that was cheap to build but expensive to maintain is not well designed.

Working in IT on a hop farm, life cycles are my main focus. Previous systems have been deployed quickly to solve an immediate problem. Then they run for years without anyone having planned for how they would be maintained or retired. Our ERP, field management tools, and network infrastructure all have life cycles that need to be actively managed. Applying the right model at the start of a project determines how much flexibility I will have to adapt as our operation grows. A system designed without life cycle thinking baked in tends to become legacy faster than it should. Life cycles are not a formality, they are everything. It is the structure that keeps a system from becoming a problem you eventually have to work around or reactively replace.

Conclusion

The systems development life cycle is the entirety of a system's existence, from concept to retirement. The three major life cycle models are: linear, iterative, and incremental. They offer different ways to plan the system depending on how well requirements are understood, how much flexibility is needed, and how early stakeholders need to see results. No single model is perfect for every situation. What matters is intentionally choosing and understanding the trade offs. For digital systems in particular, where requirements change and technology evolves quickly, thinking in terms of a life cycle rather than just a launch forces better decisions. It helps build systems that are designed to last, adapt, and eventually be gracefully replaced.
