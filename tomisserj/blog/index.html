Why Systems Thinking Matters in Digital Environments

April 9, 2026

Introduction

Most people who work in IT for any length of time eventually learn a hard lesson. When you fix one problem, you can create three more. A server gets patched but it might break an application in the process. A new feature is added to the ERP and a downstream workflow stops working. These situations are a result of approaching complex systems with a linear mindset. This blog post explores systems thinking as a foundational concept for anyone working in a digital environment. It defines what systems thinking is, explains how it differs from traditional linear thinking, and argues why a model-based approach is the most practical way to design (and manage) complex digital systems.

Systems Thinking

Systems thinking is a way of understanding the world by focusing on relationships rather than individual parts. It recognizes that a system is not just a collection of pieces. The pieces all interact with each other, and those interactions matter more than the pieces themselves. The Interaction Design Foundation defines systems thinking as "an approach that designers use to analyze problems in an appropriate context" that looks beyond individual symptoms to consider "a system as a whole" (IxDF, 2026). The main goal is finding root causes, not just surface level fixes. In the 1960s, Ludwig von Bertalanffy introduced "general systems theory," which proposed that parts of a system can best be understood in the context of their relationships with each other and with other systems, rather than in isolation. That principle is what is at the core of systems thinking today. Systems thinking is more circular than linear. It accounts for feedback loops, emergent behaviors, and unintended consequences. Really it focuses on what happens over time and how the parts in any system influence each other in ways.

Linear Thinking

Linear thinking is the default mode for most people and organizations. It is sequential where it isolates a problem, identifies a cause, and then applies a fix. Assuming that if you understand each part separately, you will understand the whole. This works well for simple problems that are easily defined. The problem is that most systems today are not simple. They are interconnected and dynamic with many different systems. Linear thinking does not account for feedback loops. It does not consider the ripple across the entire environment.

"The linear thinking implies the whole is equal to the sum of its parts, which is not effective from a business perspective." — Future of CIO (2015)

Systems thinking views problems as part of a wider interconnected environment. It focuses on relationships and recognizes feedback loops that can amplify effects within a system. It considers external factors and conditions. It asks not just "what caused this?" but more "what does this affect or what affects it?" Here is an example that illustrates this clearly. Imagine an IT department tasked with improving employee productivity in a company's order management process. A linear thinker might identify one lower performing step in the workflow and adds automation to speed it up. That step gets faster. But downstream, two other steps now receive data faster than they were designed to handle causing errors to increase. Creating more work to fix the issue and causing productivity to drop. Whereas a systems thinker would have mapped the entire workflow first. The relationships between steps, the people, and the data flowing between them. Then consider the external systems connected to the process. This would all be part of the analysis before any change was made and the result would have considered the whole.

The Three Evils

Holt (n.d.) identifies three root causes of systems failure in engineering projects. He calls them the "Three Evils of Systems Engineering." They are complexity, communication failure, and lack of understanding.

1. Complexity exists in every system. There are two types worth distinguishing. Essential complexity is the natural complexity that is inherent in any system. It cannot be eliminated, but can be managed. Accidental complexity is introduced by inefficiencies in the people, processes, or tools used to build the system. Accidental complexity can be reduced.

2. Communication failure happens when different stakeholders use the same words to mean different things, or when information is assumed to be shared when it is not. Successful systems engineering requires definitions so that all parties understand the language the same way.

3. Lack of understanding occurs when different points of view are not considered or assumptions are made. It essentially becomes a context problem. Where two stakeholders looking at the same system requirement can interpret it differently (sometimes depending on role). A driver and a mechanic have different ideas of what "the system must be safe" means in the context of a car. The same is true in digital environments, where developers, security teams, or end users look at the same system with different expectations.

These three evils feed into each other. Unmanaged complexity causes communication to break down. Communication breakdown leads to misunderstanding. Misunderstanding leads to more complexity. This cycle is why systems thinking is not optional and is the only approach that can address all three problems at once.

Model-Based Approach

Where systems thinking tells you how to think about a problem, Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) is the practical framework for applying that thinking in a structured way. MBSE is a complete approach to systems engineering. The main idea is that all knowledge about a system should live in a model rather than spread out across documents. That model is a simplified representation of the system and captures enough relevant information to realize the system successfully. It serves as a "single source of truth." If a stakeholder wants to understand any aspect of the system, they refer to the model. This directly addresses the main problems that drive system failures (communication and understanding). MBSE also addresses complexity by making it visible. When building a model, you will see where the complexity lives. You can see which parts of the system have the most dependencies and identify where a change is likely to create problems. This is the core argument for visual modeling. Diagrams do not just communicate information, they show structure that might have been previously invisible. There is a simple test for whether a system is well understood. If a model of the system is easy to generate, the source information is well specified and understood. If the model is difficult to generate, the system is poorly understood. Modeling makes assumptions and gaps visible.

Application in Digital Environments

Modern digital systems are not standalone applications. They are connected to other systems, users, and organizations. A change to one system can ripple across many others. The increasing complexity of modern systems is easy to illustrate. A 1970s automobile was largely mechanical, simple and well-defined. A 2026 automobile contains software across every major system and connects to satellites and cloud infrastructure. Considering things like environmental regulations, safety standards, and cybersecurity requirements. The basic need has not changed where the car still moves people from one location to another. However, the complexity of meeting that need has grown exponentially. Digital systems follow this same pattern. An ERP system in a farm operation touches accounting, inventory, payroll, and reporting. It integrates with vendors/banks and has compliance requirements. A "simple" change to how inventory is categorized can affect cost accounting, purchasing approval workflows, and financial reporting all at once. Without approaching this from a systems thinking standpoint, the downstream effects are invisible until they become problems. Systems thinking is what allows designers or engineers to trace those dependencies before they cause failures.

Conclusion

Systems thinking is not new, it has been at the foundation of organizational theory for many years. It is more relevant now than ever. Digital environments are growing more complex, more connected, and more interdependent every year. Linear thinking asks: what is wrong with this part? Systems thinking asks: how do all the parts relate, and what happens to the whole when any one part changes? A model-based approach is the most practical way to apply systems thinking to the design/management of complex digital environments. It creates shared understanding and makes complexity visible. It creates a consistent source of truth. Directing IT on a Hop Farm, I see this every day. Every change I make to our ERP or networking infrastructure or data workflows can affect people (and processes) in ways I did not anticipate. Systems thinking is not just an academic concept, it is the only approach that allows me to understand what I am changing before I change it.

Thinking in Life Cycles

April 16, 2026

Introduction

Every system has a beginning and an end. They get designed > built > tested > used > updated and then eventually retired or replaced. That is a system life cycle. With development, there is a framework for thinking about a system not just as something you build once, but as something that evolves over time. It needs to be planned at every stage. For anyone working in digital design (or even IT), understanding system life cycles is not an exercise. It is foundational for making better decisions earlier, avoiding problems later, and building things that last. This post defines the systems development life cycle, explores three major life cycle models, and reflects on why this kind of thinking matters in designing and sustaining digital systems.

Systems Development Life Cycle

The systems development life cycle is the structured process a system goes through from idea to decommissioning. It is a way of organizing the work into stages so that each phase has a clear purpose and outputs. Holt (n.d.) describes the life cycle as covering everything from the earliest concept of a system all the way through its operation and eventual retirement. The stages typically include: concept, development, production, utilization, support, and retirement. The life cycle model you choose determines how those stages are sequenced, how much flexibility exists between them, and how feedback/changes are handled. Choosing the right model for any project can be the difference between running a smooth project or a failed one.

Model 1: Linear Life Cycle

The linear or waterfall model, moves through each stage in sequence. One phase must be completed before the next begins. Requirements are gathered up front, design follows, then development, then testing, then deployment. The main advantage of this approach is simplicity. It is easy to plan, track, and works well when requirements are stable. However, they must be well understood from the start. This is one major drawback. If something changes mid-project, or if a deliverable was missed at the beginning, going back is disruptive and could be costly. For digital systems that are expected to evolve (or involve uncertain requirements), the linear model creates risk. By the time a problem is discovered in testing, large amounts of time and money have already been committed to a design that may need to change.

Model 2: Iterative Life Cycle

The iterative or flywheel model breaks the work into cycles, where each cycle produces a working version of the system that is then reviewed and improved in the next cycle. Rather than trying to define everything at the start, the iterative approach improves over time, improvement is built into the process. Early cycles focus on simplified versions of the system. Each pass adds capability and updates based on feedback. The advantage here is early visibility. Stakeholders can see a working system much sooner, and problems are fixed within the projects budget. Teaching Agile (2025) points out that the iterative model requires experienced teams and risks incomplete documentation if not managed carefully. For complex digital systems with evolving requirements, the iterative model is a strong fit. It is important to note that it requires more ongoing management attention than that of a linear approach.

Model 3: Incremental Life Cycle

The incremental model divides the full system into smaller pieces called increments and delivers each one as a functional unit that builds on what came before. The first increment delivers a working product. Each subsequent increment adds functionality until the complete system is finished. The key difference between incremental and iterative is that incremental development delivers real, usable pieces of the system to stakeholders along the way rather than just prototypes. Guru99 (2024) describes the incremental model as producing a working software version early in the life cycle, allowing customers to respond to each build. The advantage in digital environments is that users can provide real feedback based on actual use rather than how they think it should work. Similar to the linear model, you need a clear overall design vision from the start. If the full scope of the system is not understood well enough to define what the various increments will be, the model can break down quickly.

Why Life Cycle Thinking Matters

Thinking in terms of system life cycles forces a more complete view of what you are trying to build. It is easy to focus on getting a system launched and treat everything after that as someone else's problem. But digital systems do not stop needing attention when they go live. They need to be monitored, updated, potentially integrated with new systems, and eventually replaced. Holt (n.d.) describes several enabling system types that support the life cycle, including training systems, production systems, and support systems, all of which are needed long after initial development is done. If those needs are not considered early, the costs of supporting the system later could go up significantly. A system that was cheap to build but expensive to maintain is not well designed.

Working in IT on a hop farm, life cycles are my main focus. Previous systems have been deployed quickly to solve an immediate problem. Then they run for years without anyone having planned for how they would be maintained or retired. Our ERP, field management tools, and network infrastructure all have life cycles that need to be actively managed. Applying the right model at the start of a project determines how much flexibility I will have to adapt as our operation grows. A system designed without life cycle thinking baked in tends to become legacy faster than it should. Life cycles are not a formality, they are everything. It is the structure that keeps a system from becoming a problem you eventually have to work around or reactively replace.

Conclusion

The systems development life cycle is the entirety of a system's existence, from concept to retirement. The three major life cycle models are: linear, iterative, and incremental. They offer different ways to plan the system depending on how well requirements are understood, how much flexibility is needed, and how early stakeholders need to see results. No single model is perfect for every situation. What matters is intentionally choosing and understanding the trade offs. For digital systems in particular, where requirements change and technology evolves quickly, thinking in terms of a life cycle rather than just a launch forces better decisions. It helps build systems that are designed to last, adapt, and eventually be gracefully replaced.

Designing for People

April 30, 2026

Introduction

Bad design is everywhere, most of it is not the result of being lazy. It is the result of designing for the system rather than the person. A button that is too small to tap easily. A menu with twenty options or no clear starting point. Screens that load slowly and give no feedback that anything is happening. Each of these is a failure to account for how people actually make decisions. Human-centered design is the methodology that pushes back. It starts with the user and stays with them through the entire design process. Success is measured by whether real people can use the result effectively. This blog post introduces that concept, compares two leading frameworks, and uses four UX laws and to show what human-centered design looks like in action.

Human-Centered Design

Human-centered design is an approach to building interactive systems that puts users and their needs/environments at the center of every decision. It is not just a philosophy, it is a process. According to the National Institute of Standards and Technology, ISO 9241-210 defines human-centered design as an approach that "aims to make systems usable and useful by focusing on the users, their needs and requirements" and by applying human factors knowledge throughout the development process (NIST, 2021). The key word in that definition is throughout. Human-centered design is not a step that happens once at the beginning of a project. It is a continuous loop of coming back to understand users, define requirements, and creating then evaluating those solutions against how they are used. It keeps design grounded in reality rather than assumption.

A deep dive into the principles and practices of human-centered design.

ISO 9241-210 vs. the IDEO Field Guide

ISO 9241-210 and the IDEO Field Guide both call themselves human-centered, but they come at the concept from different angles. ISO 9241-210 is a formal standard. It defines principles, activities, and outputs that organizations must produce at each stage of a design process. It is structured and designed to scale across large projects or teams. Placing emphasis on documentation, evaluation criteria, and evidence that the design has been tested against user needs. The IDEO approach is different in both tone and method. IDEO defines human-centered design as a creative approach to problem solving that starts with people and ends with solutions tailored to their needs, with empathy as the core tool (UserTesting, 2025). If ISO gives you a framework and a checklist, then IDEO gives you a mindset. It asks designers to observe people directly, put themselves in the user's situation, and let what they learn shape the direction of the work. Neither approach is perfect in every situation. ISO works well when you need to demonstrate compliance or manage large teams. IDEO works well when you are early in a problem and the users are hard to define in advance. Both have strengths, one values structured documentation where the other favors creative exploration.

What Users See and Understand

Design decisions that seem purely visual are actually decisions about how our brain processes information. Johnson (2020) explains that the visual system does not work like a camera recording everything in the field of view with equal clarity. Our minds are selective. The eye is drawn to things like contrast, movement, size, and color. This is before the conscious mind ever registers any of it. This means that where a designer places an element, how large they make it, and what color they assign it determines how quickly (if at all) users notice it. Visual hierarchy is the term for organizing those elements intentionally so that the most important information gets attention first. A well designed interface uses size and weight to guide the eye from the most critical action to supporting details. Where a poorly designed one places everything at the same visual level, forcing the user to figure out where to start. That extra cognitive work is not neutral. It can cause hesitation and frustration. A user would then attribute that frustration to the product rather than the design decision that caused it.

The Four UX Laws

Four UX laws make the relationship between human cognition and design concrete. Jakob's Law states that users spend most of their time on other products, which means they arrive at any new interface with expectations built from everything they have used before. Designers who ignore those expectations make users relearn familiar tasks from scratch. This creates friction before the user has accomplished anything. Fitts's Law addresses the physical act of targeting an interface element. The time it takes to click or tap a target depends on how far away it is and how large it is. Small buttons placed at the edges of a screen are harder to hit than large buttons placed near the center of interaction. Hick's Law describes what happens when users are given too many choices. Decision time increases with the number and complexity of options. A navigation menu with fifteen items does not give users more control, it slows them down and increases the chance they choose wrong. Miller's Law rounds this out by noting that people can hold roughly seven items in working memory at one time. Yablonski (2024) frames these laws not as rigid rules but as tools for understanding and advocating for users during the design process. Together they describe a user who arrives with habits, physical limitations and cognitive load, combined with a finite attention span. Designing for that user means making things simple and focused with familiarity.

What This Looks Like

Consider a mobile banking app. A designer applying Jakob's Law would structure the navigation around patterns users already know from other financial apps. Thinks like placing account balances on the home screen and keeping transfers no more than one tap away. Fitts's Law would push primary actions like "Pay" or "Transfer" to large tap targets in the lower half of the screen where thumbs naturally rest. Hick's Law would argue against showing every available feature on the main screen. Instead only show the three or four most common tasks. Miller's Law would limit any single screen to a manageable set of items. Visual hierarchy would ensure that the account balance, the most important piece of information, is the largest and most prominent element on the screen. None of these decisions are decorative and each one reduces the cognitive load placed on the user. This ends up bringing the design closer to how people actually think and behave.

Human-centered design is easy to treat as a UX concern that belongs to a specific phase of a project. In practice, it is a way of thinking that should influence every decision from requirements to retirement. The perception principles from Johnson and the UX laws from Yablonski both point to the same thing: people are not neutral users who adapt to whatever a system presents them. They come with habits, limitations, and expectations that the system either supports or fights. When the system fights them, the user loses confidence in it even if the underlying functionality is sound. When it supports them, the interface disappears and the user accomplishes everything intuitively. Getting to that second outcome requires starting with the user in mind, staying with evidence, and measuring success by the experience of the person.

Conclusion

Designing for people is not just a soft idea. It is a discipline with frameworks, laws, and measurable outcomes. ISO 9241-210 gives designers a structured process for keeping users at the center of development. The IDEO Field Guide gives designers the mindset and observation tools to understand users before anything is designed. Johnson's work on perception explains why visual choices are cognitive choices. And the four UX laws translate that understanding into practical design decisions. Used together, these tools and frameworks make it possible to build interfaces that feel intuitive because they were built around real human behavior from the start.

Supporting User Thinking

May 8, 2026

Introduction

Most people do not think about why an app feels easy or hard to use. They just know when something works or doesn't. A button that is hard to find, a settings menu buried multiple levels deep, a form that asks for the same information twice. None of these feel like design decisions from the user's perspective. They just feel like friction. However, every one of them is the result of a choice. Understanding why those choices matter starts with understanding how people actually think and remember. This post explores how memory and mental models shape the way users interact with digital systems. It also covers why interface consistency/feedback matter so much and what happens when design ignores the limits of human cognition.

How Memory Works

Human memory is not a filing cabinet. It does not store perfect copies of everything and retrieve them on demand. It is reconstructive, selective, and easily overwhelmed. Johnson (2020) explains that working memory, the part of memory used to actively process information in the moment, is extremely limited. People can hold only a small amount of information at one time. That capacity can get used up fast when an interface is cluttered or requires users to remember things between steps. For example, a checkout process that asks users to remember a discount code from one screen and enter it on another will create unnecessary strain. The user has to hold that code in working memory while navigating, any distraction in between can cause them to lose it entirely. Design that respects memory reduces how much users have to hold in their head at any given moment. It is important to require the right information at the right time.

Mental Models

A mental model is the internal picture a user builds of how a system works. People form these models based on past experience and they use them to predict what will happen when they take an action. An interface thtat behaves consistently with a user's mental model will make the interaction feel natural and requires less thought. When it doesn't the user has to rebuild their understanding, which takes cognitive effort. One example is the back button on a mobile browser. Users expect it to return them to the previous page. When an app intercepts that button and uses it to close a modal instead, the mental model breaks. The user did not get what they expected, and now they have to figure out what happened and how to actually go back. That moment of confusion is not trivial. It forces the user to question the interface and raises the cognitive cost of every subsequent action.

Tesler's Law

Tesler's Law, also known as the Law of Conservation of Complexity, states that every system has a certain amount of complexity that cannot be removed. It can only be moved from one place to another. The question designers have to answer is whether that complexity lives in the interface, where the user has to deal with it, or in the system, where the designers and developers handle it on the user's behalf (Yablonski, 2024). A good example is airline booking. Calculating fare rules, seat availability, and connection times is complex. A well designed booking system absorbs that complexity and presents the user with simple choices. A poorly designed one exposes all of that logic to the user and asks them to sort through it themselves. The complexity does not go away in either case. The design just determines who carries the burden of it.

Foundation for Usability

One of the most reliable ways to reduce cognitive load is consistency. When the same action always produces the same result and the interface elements behave the same way across the entire system, users can rely on their memory of past interactions. Johnson (2020) describes this in terms of how the brain processes familiar patterns. Repeated exposure to consistent layouts and behaviors creates strong associations that allow users to act almost automatically, without conscious thought. This is the difference between an experienced user who moves through an app quickly and a new user who has to study every screen. Consistency closes that gap faster and reduces errors. Users are less likely to take a wrong action when the interface behaves the way they expect it to.

System Feedback

Feedback is how any given system communicates with the user. It tells them that something happened, is in progress, or what went wrong. When feedback is missing or delayed, users are left to guess. They click a button and nothing changes on screen, so they will instinctively click again. If they submit a form and have no idea whether it worked. Or theyhey start a file upload and see no progress indicator, so they do not know whether to wait or try again. Nielsen Norman Group describes cognitive load as the mental resources required to operate a system, and notes that when users are forced to fill in gaps left by poor feedback, they draw on those limited resources to do the system's job (NNG, 2025). Good feedback is not just visual, it is a functional requirement. It allows users to stay oriented, confident, and in control of what they are doing.

Poor Design in Practice

Working in IT at a hop farm, I interact with enterprise software every day that was clearly not designed with the user's cognitive limits in mind. Our ERP system has a receiving workflow that requires users to navigate to three separate screens to complete what is essentially one task. Each screen has a different layout, different button placement, and different terminology for the same concept. There is no visual thread connecting them, no progress indicator showing where you are in the process, and no confirmation message when the task is complete. New employees consistently make errors in that workflow because the design gives them no mental model to follow and no feedback to orient them. The task itself is not complicated. The design makes it complicated by distributing complexity across screens instead of absorbing it. This is exactly what Tesler's Law describes, and it is a problem that better design could solve without changing any of the underlying business logic.

Reducing Cognitive Load

Reducing cognitive load does not mean removing everything until an interface is bare. It means removing everything that does not need to be there. Then organizing what remains so that users can process it without strain. Mailchimp describes this as managing cognitive overload by giving users less to process at once, using familiar patterns, and reserving mental effort for the decisions that actually matter (Mailchimp, n.d.). Progressive disclosure is one of the most practical tools for this. It presents users with only the information or options they need right now. Later revealing additional complexity only when they ask for it or are ready for it. For example, a well designed settings page shows common options up front and tucks advanced configuration behind a clearly labeled link. The advanced options are still there for users who need them. Everyone else is not forced to navigate past the more common options.

Conclusion

Usability is not a feature that gets added to a product after it is built. It is the result of design decisions made early and consistently that reflect a real understanding of how people think. Memory limits mean users cannot be expected to hold large amounts of information in their heads while navigating unfamiliar interfaces. Mental models mean consistency is not just preferred but required for users to build trust in that system. Tesler's Law means complexity has to live somewhere, and good design puts it on the system side rather than the user side. Feedback means users should never have to guess whether something worked. These principles are not simply abstract. They show up in every screen, every button, and every transition in every digital product. Understanding them is what separates design that looks good from design that actually works.

Designing for Flow

May 21, 2026

Introduction

There is a moment in almost every app where you have to stop. You are looking for a button or trying to figure out whether your last action actually worked or staring at a screen full of options with no sense of where to start. That moment is the result of an app that was not designed around how people actually think and move through digital interfaces. This post is about what it means to design for flow and how, something as simple as layout and logic can shape user behavior. I will explore what three UX laws reveal about the gap between interfaces that feel natural and ones that fight you every step of the way.

Interface Layout Shaping Behavior

The layout of an interface is not just an aesthetic decision. It is a visual set of instructions. Where elements are placed, how large they are, and grouped can tell the user what to do before they read a single word. When that structure is clear, a user can move through a screen quickly with confidence. When it is not, users slow down, can make errors, and eventually lose trust in the system. Johnson (2020) explains that systems must communicate clearly at every step so users always know what happened, what is happening, and what to do next. A well-designed interface uses layout to deliver that communication automatically. A poorly structured one will leave users guessing.

Aesthetic-Usability Effect

One of the more counterintuitive ideas in UX design is that a visually appealing interface is often perceived as easier to use. In spite of the fact that it is not objectively simpler. This is the Aesthetic-Usability Effect. Users who find an interface attractive are more tolerant of minor usability problems and more likely to persist through poor design. Nielsen Norman Group (2020) notes that this effect can actually mask usability problems during testing because users who like how something looks will report a better experience than the functionality actually delivered. This means that visual quality is not separate from usability. But instead is part of it. An interface that looks polished signals to users that care was taken, and that signal affects how they experience every interaction that follows. It is like reverse 'tainting the pot'.

Von Restorff Effect

The Von Restorff Effect, also called the Isolation Effect, states that when multiple similar elements are present, the one that looks different from the rest is the one users will notice and remember. UXtweak (2024) describes how this principle is applied in interface design to draw attention to primary calls to action, highlight important status changes, and guide users toward key decisions. The most obvious example is a "primary button". In a row of similar options, the one filled with a strong color stands out immediately. That visual difference is not decoration. It is a signal that tells the user where to direct their attention and possibly perform an action. However, there is a risk of overusing this effect, just as there is a risk to underusing it. If too many elements are visually distinct, then nothing would stand out and the effect would be lost entirely.

Postel's Law

Postel's Law states that systems should be conservative in what they send and liberal in what they accept. In UX terms, this means interfaces should accept user input in as many reasonable forms as possible while being consistent in what is output. For example, a phone number field that rejects input because the user typed dashes instead of spaces is a violation of this principle. The interface is forcing the user to conform to the system rather than the system adapting to the user. Yablonski (2024) frames this as designing with empathy, accepting that users will interact with a system in ways that were not anticipated and building interfaces that handle that gracefully rather than returning errors.

My Personal Experience With Interfaces

The ERP system I use at work is a clear example of what happens when interface logic is not designed around user behavior. The receiving workflow requires three separate screens to complete one task. Each screen uses different terminology for the same fields, buttons are placed in different corners depending on which screen you are on, and there is no progress indicator showing where you are in the process. The Von Restorff Effect works against you here because nothing is visually distinct in a meaningful way. Every screen looks equally important, which means nothing guides you toward the next action. New employees make errors in this workflow constantly, not because the task is complicated but because the interface gives them no signal about where to go or what to do next. A simple progress bar and consistent layout would solve 90% of it.

Conclusion

Interface design is not purely about making things look good. It is about making sure users can move through a system without confusion. The Aesthetic-Usability Effect shows that visual quality shapes perception and tolerance. The Von Restorff Effect shows that visual distinction directs attention and guides action. Postel's Law shows that systems should absorb the messiness of human input rather than forcing users to be precise. Each of these principles points to the same thing: good interface logic puts the burden on the system, not the user. When that works, users will flow through an interface barely noticing it. When it does not get frustrated and lose confidence in the product.

Interaction That Matters

May 28, 2026

How Timing, Feedback, and Ethics Shape the Way a User Feels

Introduction

Most users cannot explain why one app feels satisfying and another feels exhausting, they just know one feels good to use and the other does not. The difference is usually not about features. Instead, it is almost always about how the interface responds or how fast it reacts or how it handles the moments that matter most. This post explores how timing, feedback, and ethical design decisions shape the way users engage with digital systems. And, more importantly, what they remember after the interaction is over.

The Doherty Threshold

The Doherty Threshold states that system response time should be 400 milliseconds or less to keep users engaged. It is called the productive flow state. When a system responds faster than that, the interaction feels seamless. When it does not, users notice and feel lag. They start to wonder whether their tap registered, if the system even is working, and/or whether they should tap again. LogRocket (2025) describes this as a disruption in flow that leads to frustration and disengagement, even when the delay is only a second or two. This can feel like a minute or two to a user. The fix is not always making the system faster. Sometimes it is making the system feel faster by providing immediate visual feedback. For example a button that changes state or a simple progress animation. This will notify users their action was received, allowing time for the system to catch up in the background.

The Peak-End Rule

Users do not remember an interaction as a complete sequence of events. They remember how they felt and how things ended. This is the Peak-End Rule. A long and complicated checkout process can be forgiven if the confirmation screen is satisfying and clear. A smooth experience throughout can be undermined by a confusing or cold ending. Nielsen Norman Group (2024) explains that the most emotionally intense points of an experience and the final moments carry disproportionate weight in how users evaluate what they went through. For designers, this means the end of a flow is one of the most important moments in the entire experience. It deserves as much attention (if not more) as the first screen a user sees.

The Feel of Responsiveness

Motion in an interface is not decoration. When it is used well, it communicates causality and confirms that an action worked. It gives users a sense of control. When a toggle switches with a smooth animation it will communicate to the user that something changed. When a form field shakes on invalid input, the feedback is instant and clear without requiring a wall of error text. Johnson (2020) describes how users form mental models of a system based on how it behaves over time, and consistent, well-timed feedback is what allows those models to be accurate. An interface that gives no feedback or feedback that arrives too late will forces user to guess. This causes their understanding to have to rebuild with every interaction.

Ethical Design

Many of the same techniques that make interfaces feel responsive and rewarding can also be used to manipulate users into behaviors they would not otherwise choose. Things like countdown timers that create false urgency or dark patterns that make cancellation difficult to find. Yablonski (2024) argues that designers have a responsibility to use psychological principles in service of the user. Good design requires asking whether a technique is helping users accomplish their goals or simply exploiting their cognitive tendencies for business gain. Users eventually recognize manipulation even if they cannot name it, but that recognition destroys trust and is very hard to recover from.

Conclusion

How an interface responds, how fast it reacts, and how an interaction ends all shape what users feel and what they remember. The Doherty Threshold sets the standard for responsiveness. The Peak-End Rule explains why the moments that feel most intense and the final moments carry the most weight in memory. Motion and feedback build the mental model users rely on to feel in control. And ethical design is what ties all of those tools to the user's benefit. Designing interaction that matters means caring about every one of those dimensions, not just the ones that are easiest to measure.
