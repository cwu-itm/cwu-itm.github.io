Module 1.1 Blog Post

Introduction

Systems thinking is essentially an ideology that stems around looking at different components of a system and the ways they interact with one another. This is used to see how they act as a whole, and the relations found rather than looking at one section of a system. Using this way of thinking allows you to see the “big picture” so you can discover patterns within a systerm and the overall behavior of a system. This matters in today’s world, so there is a more effective decision-making process when it comes to systems. As discussed before, recognizing patterns allows us to see what trends are happening.

Systems Thinking

Systems thinking is important in so many ways as there are many core components to what makes systems thinking so significant. Teague (2024) spoke about how one key characteristic of systems thinking was holistic thinking and to addition other core components would be boundaries, feedback loops, interdependence, and open and closes systems. Boundaries in systems means limits within systems so making sure it’s not too complex and can be simplified. It’s good when you can explain the system in simple terms or when you look at the foundation, it’s all simple and not too complicated as it may lead to issues. Feedback loops are closed circles of cause-and-effect, so when there is an action, there will be an effect that follows. Interdependence is when you see how a section relies on other sections to work for functionality rather than being on its own. All these core components really shine a light on why systems thinking is so special and important to thinking about when designing digital environments.

Why Systems Thinking Matters

System thinking matters so much in general as it is able to find the root problem of issues. Using the analyzation of relationships and feedback loops, you are able to detect problems in the systems. It simplifies complex issues and minimizes the difficulty in understanding the situation at hand. This is also used in UX/UI design or just designs in general as it works with multiple components and on how they work together. Using holistic thinking in all of this will have users see the entire ecosystems and not just work on the system one by one. It creates great visuals as you see it work as one and not multiple small chunks shoved together. An example is a website and with that you need a simple wireframe where you add more elements but still make sure everything on the website works together.

Systems Thinking in Practice

A great way to see systems thinking in action is looking at it in a digital environment. We can use it to visualize the power of social media as you aren’t trying to gain the attention of one user but rather multiple. Plus, in this way, you can see users as interconnected components rather than an indicisual person behind the screen. Alassad & Hussain state that currently the social media field needs to adopt systems thinking to fully understand how to optimize how a social network works. Not using this hollistic way of thinkihng makes it difficult to fully understand everything as a whole.

Real World Applications

An example of this if you pick up your phone and open a social media application is seeing influencing. Influencers have become an occupation that essentially means selling people items or services online and using social media as a platform to reach people from just about anywhere around the world. How this connects with systems thinking is that influences see a whole scope of users where they sort of adhere to a target audience but also make their content generic to relate to many people. By using a basic template by a hook and different engagement tactics, they are able to sell their product or service to the average consumer. With this I will say to be careful as AI has become quite powerful and dangerous with many people using it to quickly make a voiceover for their videos and selling fake product. Beyond that, AI had also been a tool for others to spread misinformation. An example I've seen is AI being used to steal content and mislabel it as something else or even using other user’s videos as advertisement and making fake captions.

Systems Engineering

System engineering is another subject that relates to system thinking but in simple terms it makes sure that in the design process of managing complex systems, it makes sure all the parts work together properly. So especially if you were to take a big complex project on, it makes sure to organize it so all the components work together properly. Holt talks about how systems are essential for systems engineering and how they should be established first. One major tolic is making a system hierarchy, so you can really visualize what components go where and what is more important. You can visualize this with a beehive with the queen bee being on top and the male bees at the bottom of the hierarchy chart.

Conclusion

In conclusion, it’s important to understand systems thinking when it comes to systems engineering in digital environments as it’s the foundational idea in both. It’s different from traditional ways of thinking, like working in a situation one by one, but instead of a linear approach you see things from a holistic approach.

Introduction to Human-Centered Design

In this blog post we will be going over how good designed to truly understand their users here and not just by features. With human-centered design (HCD), you fully grasp what makes users click and interact. Understanding their needs, behaviors, and limitations is essential to make the design fully functional for anyone interacting with the site. The element of problem solving also makes its way into this process as well. Vinney explains it all too well with how each step of the design process, you should think of customers and their context first (Vinney, 2023). With this idea in mind, every step of the way to fully design your system will be mindful of users. This also focuses on how users will interact with something , so noting down what is most frequently used and how to potentially make the system easier for users to use is the process of how this goes. For an example, think of the presentation of a restaurant like the layout for example. When customers enter, the feng shui of arranging the space should be open and there should be direction. Some clear features should be an entryway, booths/tables, and a register. All should be arranged according for the flow of the customer with them entering, being seated, enjoying their meal, and then paying for the meal. If the restaurant is arranged chaotically with everything being cramped and unorganized, it makes it confusing for customers therefore losing attention and profits. Making sure to bring harmony to a layout of a building is the foundation of how users will interact with the space and the comfort it brings. HCD has 4 core elements to the idea. These ideas include beingpeople-centered, finding the right problem, thinking of everything as a system, and doing small and simple interventions. When you put everything together, it harmonizes into a beautiful system that will make your design shine. The significance that HCD has on the whole system process is that it is used worldwide and it helps with navigation on making systems easier to understand which connects users from around the world. Bates talks about how this doesn't just impact one community but every community with it’s innovative way of thinking and processing information (Bates, 2018. Knowing this, it promotes behavior like analytical thinking, creative processes, teamwork, and more.

Comparing ISO 9241-210 vs IDEO

Comparing the two approaches just means one's higher standard and the other is more practical. With ISO 9241-210 it’s esseinsal the international standard for HCD of interactive systems. It’s a more functional and structed then the IDEO field guide and emphasizes more on processes like evaluating a system and meeting the requirements. With the IDEO field guide, it’s a more practical and action-oriented design thinking method. It’s focus is more on being creative, innovative, and using empathy in this framework.

Examples that illustrate how perception and visual hierarchy affect usability

When focusing on how visual perception and visual hierarchy works and its effects on usability come down to what users see first and what grabs their attention first. Perception is based on how users interact with what they see. Visual hierarchy is basically what captures their attention first. For example, with food delivery apps, such as Grubhub highlighting their order button that draws users' attention. Other small indications in the designing process of the app is what type of font they use, the space, layout, and color and all be visual cues and how you interact with an app. Bigger font equals more attention as you can make out the text faster than smaller text. The example Johnson used comes from the textbook itself as you are scanning the page and different elements draws your eyes to them while you are focused on reading the whole page (p.29 Johnson). Another is the image used in the textbook as well on p. 29 as there is a visual picture of bigger letters and smaller ones. Essentially your eyes make out the bigger letters first even though the smaller letters are in the center. This is why websites often have their logo in the center with bigger font, so you know their aesthetic and vibe.

The 4 UX Laws

Jakobs Law

The first law is Jakob's law which is when customers' expectations on how an app with function is based on their experience with other apps. Yablonski speaks about the familiarity aspect of it all and how people yearn for comfort in different digital products or services. With this in mind, it’s important to note that apps being similar is not a bad trait at all and is favors for being effect and smooth. Knowing what users like and how the average user interacts with the application is important to note down as you begin top understand the simple function a website has. For example, making the logo a way to go to the homepage on an website is pretty common so when customers want to return to the front page, all they have to do is click or tap on the logo to go to where they want to go. Yablonski also talks about how friction within interacting with a digital system is necessarily bad but it’s better if it’s reduced as too much can be bad. It’s not all bad but keeping in mind to keep your systems familiar is the best route to go. We can also think of DCM like project management. When creating a project scope, at times there will be project creep which will effect the entirety of the project or the system so reducing that amount and stick with the original idea is more realistic.

Fitt's law

Fitt’s law entials on how bigger and closer buttons are easier to click on. With navigation systems, making these features easier for the user is essential to making the design system truly thrive. You can use look at websites on the computer screen and see if the checkout button is easy to click on. With mobile screen you have to take in account on how humans on average like to hold their hand. For example, a majority of people are right handed and hold their phone on the lower part of the phone so with checkout buttons, some apps make their button on the lower right corner of the screen making it easier for users to tap on the button. The same example that was discussed before can also be used with logos being bigger and that acting as a navigation button as it brings you back to the home screen. With this making important information the bigger buttons is always an excellent design choice.

Hick's law

With Hicks Law you need to think of the site and simplify it making it easy to navigate so leaving less options is always better. A simple design will always be effective when it comes to not overwhelming your users. Doing this also results in your users making quicker decisions since they aren't bombarded with a million options to choose from. Making sure to keep the option open and simple is always going to be better than a chaotic screen filled with too much to read and too much to decide on in the moment.

Miller's law

Miller law is keeping in mind that people on advage only keep 7 items in their memory, so this also entails that simplicity is more sought out after. Keeping in mind to keep navigation simple and stunning is a sure way to gain more attention when yhe customer doesn't have to keep track of no more than those 7 items. Anymore and it may be more overwhelming for the user.

Introduction

Designing the digital environment for flow is essential for users when interacting with it. Because they are the target audience, it’s important to design it and make decisions that influence how they interact with the result. Every digital platform has user’s hearts at its core, so every option, button, and feature is to guide users through their site. Whether it would be food, online shopping, social media, or music platforms, interface design affects how quickly and easily a user finishes tasks and efficiently gets to their goal. With good user interface design, the goal is to create a smooth experience for users because if the design is poor, users are left with confusion and frustration. An example could be a shopping website like Hollister generally has good traffic on their website due to the simplistic design, colors, buttons, layout, and overall navigation through their decision-making process. I find stress and am overwhelmed when a site has too many popups and options to choose from. One thing Hollister does is for its dropdown menu, each topic is neat with its category and when clicked on there are more specific options to choose from rather than a website having all links in one dropdown menu. This makes a world of difference due to the reduced stress and overwhelming option feature. With clothes, there are many different garments to choose from, so designing the website to make it easy to flow through is essential to get customers to use your website.

How Interface Layout and Control Design Impact User behavior

There are several ways in which layouts affect user behavior. Using different features such as navigation bars, buttons, menus, colors, search function, and aesthetics can really help your digital service make positive experience among users. Adding additional factors such as different button sizes and mapping them really make a big impact. The bigger the button, the more attention is gained as our eyes naturally dart to words or images we can input faster. Bright colors can also catch your attention, betting so seeing a bright red button while it’s surrounded with muted colors is going to stand out. As mentioned before, use popups wisely as too many can drive customers away and frustrate them. Most sites opt for an advertisement and use a deal to their advantage, so customers are more intrigued.

I read this article by Yuri Shapochka, which is about designing stable user interfaces. Another element to impacting user behavior is familiarity. Users when logging onto a platform, they sort of expect familiar features between different applications. One example can be clicking on the logo/company name to get back to the home page. Shapochka suggests that this phenomenon happens when users use relations and it being more of a conscious decision the main makes due to the fact we always are connecting patterns (Shapochka, 2026). Due to this matter, platforms often use familiar layouts and features to keep that consistency going. When a company decides to go a different route, Shpochka spoke about when the stability breaks, users are quick to move around the site cautiously and the focus narrows (Shapochka, 2026). This happens due to that familiarity being lost, so any sort of action is unpredictable at this point. There is no flow state anymore, the user is just driven by focus and hesitation.

A website example would be Hollister. I’ve navigated that website on almost every device, and they are consistent. Three UX Laws

The first law I want to discuss is Aesthetic-Usability effect. This steams from trust websites and design that are organized, neat, and feel polished. If a website has text that someone overlap or images that do, hard to read font, and a color pallet that does not go together, they will probably click away. Yablonski talks about Apple products being a clear example of how they pay attention to aesthetics which makes their reputation good and leads to successful business (Yablonski, 2024). The sleek designs of the phones make it feel premium which people opt for and with its design comes the efficiency of the phone which leaves Apple to be one of the top 3 competitors for electronics.

The second law Iwantedd to discuss was the Von Restorff effect. This one means that things that stand out are going to be remembered more. This could point to brightly colored checkout buttons or promotional offers that have a bigger sized font. Lots of limited coupon offers or sales are usually emphasized to encourage customers to make impulsive purchases. I would know because I fall for them every time.

The last law I want to touch on is Postel’s law. This means that systems should accept flexible user input while remaining clear. So, when users are typing into the search bar, if they don’t finish their input, then they should still be allowed to search up what they are looking for. Maybe the user isn't too sure what they are looking for so using the information they know, the site can hopefully help lead them in the right direction rather than the search coming to a dead end. This helps with confusion which can lead to users fizzling out with their frustration. Egger also talks about an approach to keep in mind when implementing this law which means the inputting process can handle a variety of inputs gracefully (Egger, 2024). You should also do regular secutity checks to make sure there are no potential exploits of your system.
