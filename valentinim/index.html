Systems Thinking

Systems thinking is an approach to understand how different components of a system interact with each other. It also shows how those components contribute to the behavior of a system. Systems thinking emphasizes the relationships, dependencies, and interactions that are within a system. It recognizes that systems are made up of many elements that all work together to achieve a purpose. They can be physical elements like digital processes, data structures, and human interactions. A system is not just separate parts but a network of interconnected components that influence each other. The behavior of the entire system can't always be predicted by looking at individual parts alone because of the interconnected system.

A key aspect of systems thinking is understanding that system elements rarely function independently. They rely on eachother through interfaces and information flows which allows them to communicate and perform coordinated tasks. In systems engineering, these interactions are important because they determine how well the system functions overall. "When considering any System, or System Element, it is important to understand that they will interact with other System Elements, rather than existing in isolation" (Holt, 2019). This idea shows that understanding the connections between elements is just as important as understanding the elements themselves. When designing a system, you must consider how each component will interact with the others, how information or resources will flow between them, and how these interactions affect system performance.

Another important part of systems thinking is that systems operate within a broader environment. It is influenced by stakeholders and external factors. Stakeholders could be users, organizations, regulators, or other systems that interact with the system being designed. The stakeholders will have different needs and expectations. That means that systems must be designed in a way that will address multiple perspectives. Systems thinking encourages the designer to consider these things so that systems function effectively with real-world environments. An example would be if a digital payment system has to consider the needs of customers, banks, security, and communication networks. Each of those factors influence how the system is designed and operated. Systems thinking provides a better understanding of how systems function in practice.

Systems thinking also involves analyzing systems at multiple levels. Large systems can usually be divided into smaller subsystems that contain its own components and interactions. For example, a computer system may include subsystems for data storage, processing, networking, anf user interaction. Each subsystem performs a specific funtion while working together to support the overall operation of the system. Systems thinking allows the designer to look at the subsystems individually and how they contribute to the behvaior of the whole system. This approach makes it easier to manage more complex systems and makes sure that all the parts of the system is aligned.

Differences Between Systems Thinking and Traditional and Linear Thinking

Systems thinking is different from more tradtional or linear ways of thinking by its way of thinking about problems. Linear thinking tends to view problems as sequences of events where one action leads directly to a specific result. This assumes that problems can be solved by breaking them into smaller tasks and addressing them separately. It can be effective with simple or predictable problems but becomes harder when systems contain many interacting elements. In complex systems, actions rarely have a single predictable outcome. Changes in one area may influence several other areas at the same time.

A limitation of linear thinking is that it overlooks important relationships between system componets. If you focus only on individual parts of a system, you could miss interactions that influence system performance. An example is in software development, a programmer could design a module that works well byitself but it doesn't intergrate with other modules. If the interactions are not considered early, it could lead to delays, performance issues, and system failures. Systems thinking helps this problem by examining how compoents interact before implementing design decisions.

Another difference between systems thinking and traditional thinking is how they each handle complex systems. Linear thinking will simplfy a probelm by focusing on direct cause and effect relationships. However, many modern systems have feedback loops, dependencies, and dynamic interactions that can't be easily understood using a simple cause and effect approach. Systems thinking is a broader framework for analysing the relationships and understanding how different compoents influce each other. The Systems Engineering Body of Knowledge says that systems thinking helps engineers understand how system elements interact within complex environments and how these interactions sharpen system behavior (International Council on Systems Engineering, 2025).

Traditional thinking also treats problems as separate events instead of parts of a larger systeml For example, if a system experiences performance issues, a linear approach will focus only on fixing the specific component that is causing the problem. Systems thinking will try to find if the problem is being caused by a comnination of factors based on how elements are interacting. Looking at the entire system helps provide solutions that fixes the root of the problem.

Systems thinking focuses on relationships and interactions, so its common to rely on tools that help represent complex systems. One approach is the model-based approach, also known as Model-Based Systems Engineering. A model-based approach involves creating digital models that represent system components, their relationships, and behavior. The models are structured representations of the system so that they can be analyzed, simulated, and modified throughout the system development.

Model Based Systems Engineering

Model-based approaches are applicable in the design of complex digital systems because they allow engineers to analyze interactions between components before the system is built. Digital systems often have many interacting elements and that makes it difficult to predict system behavior without analytical tools. Digital models helps to simulate system performance under different conditions and evaluate how changes affect the system. An example would be a network model that can simulate how data traffic flows through servers and routers during periods of high demand. This can show secuirty vulnerabilties and reliability issues before the system is done (IBM, 2026).

Another reason model-based approaches is helpful is because they support collaboration with stakeholders involved in the system development. Large projects involve teams with different areas of expertise. This could include software engineers, hardware designers, and project managers. Each group will view the system from a different perspective. Models share a perspective that all stakeholders can reference when discussing design decisions. The shared understanding is important for teams to remain aligned and it reduces the chances of miscommunication during the development process (IBM, 2026).

Model--based systems engineering also supports iterative design. This is important when developing complex systems. As new information is available or system requirements change, engineers can update the system model and see how this changes the systems. This means the engineers can refine the system whenever needed and test different solutions before committing to a final design (IBM, 2026).

Conclusion

Systems thinking provides a framework for understanding and designing complex digital systems. By focusing on relationships and interactions between components, systems thinking helps analyze systems as integrated structures. The systems elements have to be understood through their interactions with other elements. This is different from tradtion, linear thinking because traditional thinking relies on simple cause and effect reasoning and overlooks important relatioships. Model based systems engineering helps systems thinking by providing digital tools that represent system components and interactions in a structured and visual format. Combining both model based approaches and systems thinking, systems can be designed in a more efficient and reliable way, as well as become more capable of managing complex systems.

Module 1.2: Systems Thinking

Systems Development Life Cycle

The Systems Development Life Cycle is a structured framework used to guide how a system is planned, built, tested, deployed, used, and retired. It is a disciplined approach for developing information systems in a controlled and organized way. SDLC is a system that has phases to describe how the system evolves from an initial idea into a fully operational product. The concept of a life cycle itself is a structural concept that defines the stages an entity goes through. A Life Cycle model is a behavioral concept that describes how those stages are carried out in practice. This includes their order and repitition. The SLDC provides projects with a repeatable and measureable method for building systems. This makes sure that the development is not random but follows a consistent framework (Holt, J. 2026).

There are six stages in System Development Life Cycles. The first is the Conception Phase. This stage focuses on identifying a problem or opportunity and then defines system requirements. It involves understanding stakeholder needs and expectations. Verification and validation are established early to make sure there is clarity on the requirements. The second phase is Development. This phase is where possible soultions are explored and evaluated based on the requirements. Prototyping may be used to test these ideas before the production begins. This is the Production Phase. This is where the selected solution is developed into a fully functional system. It is also when extensive testing happens to make sure the systen is built correctly and meets user needs. Fourth there is the Utilization Phase. During this phase, the system is actively used by end users and stakeholders. Normally there is training and it operates alongside the support phase. The Support Phase is when ongoing maintenance and technical support are provided. Issues such as errors, updates, and system performance problems are managed. This makes sure the system remains reliable and effective. Lastly there is the Retirement Phase. The system is decommissioned when it is no longer needed or replaced (Holt, J. 2026).

Key Characteristics of Systems Development Life Cycle

Systems Development Life Cycles has several key characterisitcs. As mentioned before it is a structured and phased approach where development is divided into stages, all with defined objectives and outputs. Each phase produces documentation and deliverables that support accountability, tracking, and transparency. Testing and quality assurance are also key characteristics. They are integrated throughout the lifecycle rather than being left until the end. The framework also emphasizes early identifcation of risks. This allows for problems to be addressed before they become too big. SDLC is also designed to support adaptability which allows systems to evolve. It also encourages disciplined project execution, which makes sures each stage is completed before moving forward. SDLC also allows for traceability, which means that decisions, requirements, and changes can be tracked throughout the system's development. The last characterisitc is that it allows for consistency for the development teams by standardizing processes and expectations (SentinelOne, 2025).

Advantages of System Development Life Cycle

One of the major benefits of Systems Development Life Cycles is improved efficiency. Because it is a structured process, it reduces confusion and any delays in development. A defined lifecycle increases quality assurance because defects and errors can be ifentified early and corrected before they become costly issues. Risk management is stonger because potential threats are identified early instead of after deployment. Lastly, user satisfaction improves because stakeholders are actively involved in the developement process. This makes sure the final system meets their needs and expectations (SentinelOne, 2025).

There are also organizational and process advantages. It allows organizations to standardize system development for different projects as well as support strong project management control by defining structure, roles, responsibilities, and decision making authority. It improves accountability by stating who is responsible for each part of the system development process. The requirements are defined early which reduces ambiguity and ensures development aligns with the end goals. Resources are used more effectively which reduces waste and helps keep the project within budget. Lastly, cost efficiency is improved by reducing any errors, rework, and unnecessary development labor (Microsoft, 2026).

Potential Drawbacks of Linear, Iterative, and Incremental Life Cycle Models

The linear model follows a sequential structure where each phase is completed fully before moving on to the next. The development progress is a step-by-step process with no overlap between the stages. It is best suited for projects where requirements are well understood and not likely to change during development. The disadvantages of this model is it offers limited flexibility once a phase has been completed. It's not ideal for complex or large-scale systems where requirements evolve over time. A major drawback is that delivery occurs only at the end of the project, which means users don't see working versions during development (Holt, J. 2026).

The iterative model involves repeated cycles through the Systems Development Life Cycle stages rather than a single linear pass. Each interaction produces a functional version of the system that can be reviewed or tested. The feedback from each interaction is used to refine and improve future versions of the system. A drawback of this model is that stakeholder requirements may change frequently. This can lead to shifts in the development goals (Holt, J. 2026).

The incremental model develops the system in separate parts that are delivered in stages. Each part adds funtionality until the system is completely built. This allows partial development of the system before the full system is complete. The problem with this model is that not all systems can be easily divided into independent funtional parts. That's where the drawback of poor planning and coordination become more complex when managing multiple releases (Holt, J. 2026).

Importance for the Design, Sustainability, and Evolution of Digital Systems

Systems development life cycles supports effective system designs because it breaks development into structured phases which helps teams plan clearly defined milestones and deliverables. It is important for time management because it improves scheduling for project completion. It is also important for resource management by helping organizations allocate time, money, and personnel more effectively. The structured lifecycle makes sure the systems are developed within budget and avoids any unnecessary financial waste. SDLC is also important for quality, risk identification, and mitigation as mentioned before. The framework enhances sustainability by making sure the systems are reliable, maintainable, anf adaptable over time. The systems remain functional and relevant through the whole lifecycle, not just at the point of deployment (Micrsoft, 2026).

Systems Development Life Cycles increases transparency, improves efficiency, and clearly defines requirements. It also sets consistent standards across the development teams. This leads to to higher reliability and fewer erros in system outputs. SDLC helps organizations maintain long-term evolution because it supports updates and maintenance. The systems remain secure and acalable, which is important because technology and user needs change over time. Early planning and structured execution reduces both operational and long-term maintenance costs. Ultimately, SDLC allows digital systems to be built not only effectively, but also remain useful, secure, andd adaptable throughout the whole lifecycle (SentinelOne, 2025).

Module 2.1: Human First

Human-Centered Design

Human-centered design is a problem solving approach that prioritizes the needs, experiences, and perspectives of users throughout the design process. HCD creates solutions with users instead of designing products that are based on assumptions or technical possibilities. This makes sure that users real-world needs are understood and addressed. Human-centered design is rooted in the belief that the people experiencing a problem are best equipped to help develop effective solutions (IDEO.org, 2015). This approach shifts the focus from technology centered thinking to people centered innovation.

Human centered design requires designer to understand users, their tasks, and the environments that they interact with, as well as products and systems. This means there has to be direct engagement with users through observation and testing. It can't rely only theoretical research. The process is iterative, which means that the solutions are continously refined based on feedback and testing (Usability and UX Design, Chapter 4). Human-centered design i structured into three phases, which are inspiration, ideation, and implementation. The inspiration phase is when designers gather insights from users. During the ideation phase, they generate and refine ideas. Lastly, in the implementation phase, they bring solutions to life and evaluate their impact (IDEO.org, 2015). HCD's goal is to produce solutions that are not only fuctional but also efficient and meaningful to users.

ISO 9241-210 and IDEO's Field Guide

Both ISO 9241-210 and IDEO's Field Guide emphasize the importance of designing with users at the center, but they are different with their approaches and execution. The ISO 9241-210 standard is a strucutred and formal framework for human centered design. It outlines a clear sequence of steps. Those steps are planning the process, understanding the context of use, specifying user requirements, producing design solutions, evaluating designs, and iterating as needed (Usability and UX Design, Chapter 4). This method is systematic and emphasizes documentation, measurable outcomes, and evaluation to ensure usability and effectiveness.

IDEO's Field Guide is a flexible and creative approach. Rather than focusing on steps, it focuses on mindsets such as empathy, optimism, iteration, and creative confidence (IDEO.org, 2015). Designers should explore a wide range of ideaas and learn from failure. The IDEO process also follows three main phases, which are inspiration, ideation, and implementtion. During these phases, the focus is on creativity, experimentation, and innovation.

With these differences, both approaches share key similarities. They both require direct user involvement, iterative design, and continous testing and refinement. However, ISO 9241-210 is more analytical and process driven. This makes it better for structured environments, while IDEPO's approach is more exploratory and adaptable. This is ideal for innovation and creative problem-solving. Both of these approaches together can balance structure with creativity. This can lead to both reliable and innovative design solutions.

Perception and Visual Hierarchy in Usability

Understanding human perception is important for effective UX design because users don't interpret interfces objectively. Users rely on past experiences, expectations, and contextual cues to make sense of what they see. People often "see what they expect to see" which means that poorly designed interfaces can lead to misunderstandings or overlooked information (Johnson, 2020). This highlights why it is important to align design elements with user expectations to help improve usability.

Visual hierarchy helps guide users' attention and how they process information. Since users tend to scan rather than read content in detail, clear structure and oranization are essential (Johnson, 2020). Elements like size, contrast, spacing, and postion all help indicate which information is important and where users should focus their attention. For example, larger text and bold headings naturally draw attention, while properly spaced and grouped elements make interfaces easier to navigate.

Gestalt principles also explain how users naturally organize visual information. Principles like proximity, similarity, and continuity help users group related elements and understand relationships within a design (Johnson, 2020). When these are applied effectiviely, interfaces feel intuitive and easy to use. For example, a well organized form with clearly grouped fields is easier to complete than a cluttered and disorganized one. Relying only on color to convey meaning can also create accessibility issues. Users could struggle to distinguish colors under certain conditions. Combining color with text or icons improves clarity and usability. Overall, effective use of perception and visual hierarchy reduces cognitive effort and enhances the user experience (Johnson, 2020).

The Four UX Laws

The four UX laws are Jakob's, Fitt's, Hick's, and Miller's Law. They all provide insights into how users interact with digital systems and how designers can reduce user effort. Jakob's Law states that users expect interfaces to function similarly to other systems they have used, based on their exisiting mental models (Yablonski, 2024). This shows the importance of consistency and familiarity in design. When interfaces align with user expectations, they are easier to learn and use. Breaking established conventions can lead to confusion and frusteration.

Fitt's Law focuses on the physical interaction between users and interface elements. The law states that the time required to reach a target depends on its size and distance (Yablonski, 2024). Larger and closer targets are easier to interact with. For example, buttons such as "Submit" or "Buy Now" should be placed and sized appropriately. This reduces user effort and minimizes errors (Yablonski, 2024).

Hick's Law focuses on decision making by explaining that the more choices users are given, the longer it takes for them to make a decision (Yablonski, 2024). Designers should simplify interfaces by reducing the number of options and organizing information into manageable steps. This will help improve usability. For example, breaking a checkout process into multiple steps can help prevent users from feeling overwhelmed (Yablonski, 2024).

Miller's Law focuses on the limitations of human memory. It says that users can only hold a small of amount of information in their working memory at once (Yablonski, 2024). Designers should organize the information into smaller, meaningful chunks and use clear visual structures. Grouping related items and using headlines or categories can significantly improve comprehension and usability (Yablonski, 2024).

All four of these laws emphasize the importance of reducing both mental and physical effort in design. Jakob's law reduces learning effort, Fitt's Law reduces physical effort, Hick's Law reduces decision-making effort, and Miller's Law reduces cognitive load (Yablonski, 2024). When applied with the principles of human centered design and understanding perception and visual hierarchy, these laws can help create interfaces that are efficient and user friendly.

Conclusion

Human centered design provides a framework for creating effective and meaningful user experiences by focusing on real user needs and behaviors. ISO 9241-210 and IDEO's Field Guide are perspectives that ephasize structure, evaluation, creativity, and innovation. By integrating all of these approaches, designers can develop solutions that are reliable and adaptable. Understanding human perception and visual hierarchy allows designers to create interfaces that align with how users naturally process information. When combined with the four UX laws designers can reduce user effort and improve usability. Successful UX design is achieved by constantly testing, refining, and prioritizing the needs of users throughout the design process.

Module 2.2: Support User Thinking

Memory, Mental Models, Cognitive Load, and UX Design

Memory, mental models, and cognitive load are important for understanding how users interact with digital systems. Effective UX design depends on how well systems align with human cognitive abilities and limitations. Humans do not process information like computers. Humans rely on limited working memory, learned mental models, and recognition based thinking to complete tasks. Because of these limitations, poor design can overwhelm users, but well designed systems reduce effort and guide behavior. UX principles like Tesler's Law, recognition over recall, and cognitive load theory explain why some systems feel intuitve while others feel confusing.

Memory and Mental Models in User Interaction

Memory is important in how users interact with interfaces. According to cognitive psychology, working memory is extremely limited and can only hold small amounts of information for a short period of time before it is lost or overwritten (Johnson, 2020). Long-term memory stores knowledge and experiences, but retrieving it is often unreliable and requires mental effort. Because of these limitations, users depend on mental models, which are internal representations of how systems are expected to work based on prior experince.

When a system matches a user's mental model, interaction becomes easier because users can predict outcomes without needing to think deeply. For example, users expect a shopping cart icon to represent saved items or checkout functionality. If the system behaves in a familiar way, users rely on recognition instead of recall, reducing cognitive effort. However, when interfaces behave unexpectedly or inconsistently, users have to stop and think, which increases their mental workload.

A real-world example is Amazon. Amazon's interface akigns closely with user's mental models of online shopping. Users do not need to remember product categories or navigation paths because the system provides recommendations, filters, and search suggestions. This reduces the need for recall and instead supports recognition-based interaction, which is easier for users (Johnson, 2020). Amazon manages complex recommendation algorithms and inventory systems, but this complexity is hidden from the user, aligning with Tesler's Law (Yablonski, 2024).

Interface Consistency and System Feedback

Interface consistency and system feedback are important for usability because they reinforce mental models and reduce cognitive load. Consistency allows users to apply what they already know to new situations. If buttons, layouts, and navigation structures behave predictable across pages, users do not need to relearn interactions each time they move through a system. This reduces reliance on memory and increases efficiency.

System feedback also plays a role by confirming user actions and reducing uncertainty. Feedback can include visual changes, notifcations, loading indicators, or confirmation messages. Without feedback, users may feel uncertain about whether an action was successful, leading them to repeat tasks or abandon the system altogether (Johnson, 2020).

An example of system feedback in action is mobile banking apps such as Chase Mobile. When users transfer money or pay bills, the app immediately confirms the transaction with clear visual and textual feedback. This reduces uncertainty and supports user confidence. The system handles complex financial processing, but users only see simple confirmations. THis is an example of how complexity is shifted away from the user and managed by the system, aligning with Tesler's Law (Yablonski, 2024).

Cognitive Load and Poor UX Design

Cognitive load refers to the mental effort required to complete a task, including thinking, remembering, and decision-making. Humans have limited working memory, so when too much information is presented at once, users become overwhelmed. Cognitive load theory identifies three types of load, intrinsic (task difficulty), extraneous (poor design), and germane (useful learning effort). Poor UX design often increases extraneous load, which does not contribute to task completion and instead distracts the user (UserBit, 2025).

A example of poor UX design is school websites or student portals. From personal experience, these systems often contain cluttered navigation menues, unclear labels, multiple steps required to complete basic tasks like registering for classes. Users are often forced to remember course codes, prerequisites, and navigation steps across multiple pages. This increases cognitve load and leads to frustration.

Instead of supporting recognition, these systems rely heavily on recall. Students must remember information rather than selecting it from visible options. This doesn't align with the principle of recognition over recall, which states that users perform better when they can identify options visually rather than retreive them from memory (Johnson, 2020). As a result, students often open multiple tabs, take notes, or ask for help.

Attention, Mental Models, and User Behavior

Human attention is limited and goal-driven, meaning users focus only on information that helps them acheive their objectives. They tend to ignore irrelevant content and rely on cues to guide them toward their goal (Johnson, 2020). When interfaces are cluttered or poorly structured, users struggle to identify these cues, increasing cognitive load.

A strong example of attention drive design is Instagram. Instagram uses infinte scrolling and algorithm-driven content delivery to capture user attention. Users do not need to remember where they left off because the system feeds new content. While this reduces memory demands, it also removes stopping cues, making it difficult for users to regulate attention. The interface is simple to use but highly influential in shaping behavior through variable rewards such as likes, comments, and new posts.

This demonstrates how design influences behavior by controlling attention and reducing friction in interaction. While the system feels efortless, it also encourages prolonged use, showing how UX design can shape habits and emotional responses (Yablonski, 2024).

Tesler's Law and Cognitive Load Management

Tesler's Law states that every system contains a minimum level of complexity that can't be removed, only shifted between the system and the user (Yablonski, 2024). Effective UX design shifts complexity away from users and into the system itself. This is important for reducing cognitive load and improving usability.

Like the example earlier, Amazon shifts complexity through recommendation systems and autofill features. Instagram hides algorithmic complexity behind a simple scrolling feed. Banking apps handle financial calculations internally while presenting simple confirmations. Poorly designed school systems shift complexity onto the user by requiring manual navigation, memory, and interpretation of unclear instructions.

Tesler's Law also states the importance of balance. Oversimplifaction can be harmful if it removes necessary functionality or limits user control. Good design must support both beginners and advanced users by using techniques such as progressive disclosure, where advanced features are hidden until needed (Laws of UX, 2024).

Recognition Over Recall and Limited Cognitive Capacity

One of the most important principles for reducing cognitive load is recognition over recall. Users are better at identifying information than remembering it from memory. Interfaces that use menus, icons, and visible options reduce mental effort significantly compared to systems that require users to remember commands or steps (Johnson, 2020).

Humans can only process a limited amount of information at one time due to working memory constraints. When too many options or pieces of information are presented simultaneously, users become overwhelmed. This is why systems that use chunking, progressive disclosure, and simplified navigation are more effective (UserBit, 2025).

Using the same example, Amazaon uses categorized sections and recommedations instead of forcing users to search from scratch. Poorly designed school portals present too many options at once, making it difficult to focus. This demonstrates how limiting choices and organizaing information improves usability and reduces cognitive strain.

Conclusion

Memory limitations, mental models, and cognitive load shape how users interact with digital systems. Users rely on recognition rather than recall, and they depend on familiar mental models to navigate interfaces efficiently. When systems align with these cognitive patterns, usability improves significantly. Interface consistency and system feedback further support users by reducing uncertainty and reinforcing expected behaviors.

Examples like Amazon, Instagram, banking apps, and school websites demonstrate how design decisions directly ipact cognitive load and user experience. Amazon and banking apps reduce cogntive load through automation and feedback, while Instagram influences attention and behavior through simplified and engagement. School systems often increase cognitve load by focing users to rely on memory and navigate complex interfaces.

Principles such as Tesler's Law, recognition over recall, and cognitive load theory emphasize that good UX design is not about removing complexity but managing it effectively. By shifting complexity into the system and reducing unnecessary mental effort, deisgners can create more intuitive, efficient, and user-centered experiences.

Module 3.1: Designing for Flow

Introduction

Interface layout and control design play an important role in shpaing how users interact with digital systems. Every element of a website or application affects how users think, feel, and behave. These elements include colors, buttons, spacing, typography, and navigation. Good user experience (UX) design helps users complete tasks efficiently and comfortably. Poor design creates confusion, frustration, and cognitive overload. Designers have to understand how humans process information and make decisions in order to create systems that feel intuitive and supportive.

People naturally prefer systems that reduce mental effort and allow actions to become automatic through repetition and familiarity (Johnson, 2020). Similarly, Laws of UX explains that users rely on visual cues, habits, and emotional reactions when interacting with interfaces (Yablonski, 2024). Effective UX design is not just about making products functional but also about designing systems that align with human psychology and behavior.

Interface layout and control design both impact user behavior by examining cognitive load, emotional design, and visual hierarchy. Aesthetic-Usability Effect, the Von Restorff Effect, and Postel's Law are three important UX laws that help designers create more effective and user-friendly interfaces.

Cognitive Load and Human Behavior

One of the most important concepts in UX design is cognitive load. Cognitive load refers to the amount of mental effort required to use a system. Users become frusterated when interfaces require too much thinking, searching, or problem solving (Mishra, 2025). The goal of good UX design is to reduce unnecessary mental effort so users can focus on completing their tasks rather than figuring out how the interface works. Humans rely heavily on automatic thinking because it requires less energy than conscious problem solving (JOhnson, 2020). Repetition and familiarity allow actions to become habits. This means users perform better when systems are predictable and easy to understand. When interfaces contain confusing layouts, unclear navigation, and inconsistent controls, users are forced to engage in conscious thinking. This increases mental strain and slows task completion.

Cognitive load becomes especially problematic when users encounter too much information at once. Poor organization, cluttered layouts, and unpredictable workflows overwhelm users and make systems feel difficult to use (Mishra, 2025). For example, websites with crowded menus, excessive text, or unclear labels force users to search longer for information. This increases frustration and often causes users to abandon tasks altogether.

Good interface design reduces cognitive load in several ways. Designers can group related information together, create clear visual hierarchy, use familiar language, and simplify workflows. Predictable navigation structures also help users build habits and comlete actions automatically (Johnson, 2020). These design choices improve user confidence and reduce confusion.

The Aesthetic-Usability Effect

One of the most important UX laws related to interface design is the Aesthetic-Usability Effect. Users often perceive visually appealing interfaces as easier to use, even before interacting with them (Yablonski, 2024). Attractive designs create positive emotional reactions, increase trust, and encourage users to engage with the product. First impressions are extremely important in UX design. Users form opinions within seconds of viewing a website or application. Clean layouts, balanced colors, consistent typography, and organized spacing all contribute to positive perceptions. When users see an attractive interface, they are more likely to believe the product is professional, reliable, and easy to use. The emotional influence of design is also discussed by Johnson (2020), who explains that emotions strongly affect decision making and satisfaction. Positive emotional experiences encourage users to continue using a system. Frusterating experiences create negative associations. Visual appeal helps reduce stress and improves overall user comfort.

However, aesthetics alone cannout guarantee good usability. A visually pleasing interface can still frusterate users if important actions are difficult to locate or navigation is confusing. Successful UX design balances appearance with funtionality (Yablonski, 2024). Designers should use aesthetics to support usability rather than distract from it. For example, modern applications such as banking apps or shopping websites often use minimalstic layouts with clear spacing and visually distint buttons. These design choices make interfaces feel organized and approachable. Users are more willing to trust systems that appear polished and professional.

The Aesthetic-Usability Effect also connects to cognitive load. Attractive and organized layouts reduce mental effort because users can process information more easily. When visual hierarchy is clear, users know where to focus their attention. This improves efficiency and creates smoother interactions.

The Von Restorff Effect and Visual Hierarchy

Another improtant UX principle is the Von Restroff Effect, which explains that items that stand out visually are more likely to capture attention and be rememebered (Yablonski, 2024). Designers use this principle to guide users toward important actions, notifications, or information. Visual hierarchy is essential for helping users navigate interfaces efficiently. By using differences in color, size, spacing, shape, and contrast, designers can emphasize key actions. At the same time it reduces distractions. Primary buttons are often larger or more colorful than surrounding elements because they represent the most important actions users should take.

According to Yablonski (2024), if everything on a page is emphasized equally, nothing truly stands out. Overusing bright colors, animations, or bold text weakens the effectiveness of visual hierarchy and overwhelms users. Good design carefully highlights only the most important information.

Users prefer recognizable patterns and predictable layouts (Johnson, 2020). Clear visual hierarchy allows users to interact automatically without needing to think carefully about where to click or what to do next. This reduces cogntive load and improves efficiency.

The Von Restorff Effect is commonly user in online shopping websites. For example, "Add to Cart" or "Buy Now" buttons are usually brightly colored and positioned prominently on the page. These design choices immediately draw user attention and encourage action. Similarly, notifaction badges on social media platforms stand out visually to encourage users to interact with new content. Accessibility is also important when applying the Von Restorff Effect. Designers should not rely on color to communicate meaning because some users may have visual impairments or color blindness. Combining contrast with icons, labels, or shapes improves accessibility and usability for all users.

Postel's Law and Flexible Design

Postel's Law is another important UX principle that impacts user behavior and satisfaction. Systems should be flexible, forgiving, and tolerant of user interactions (Yablonski, 2024). Since people are imperfect and often make typing errors or misunderstand instructions, interfaces should adapt to users rather than punish them.

Error-tolerant systems reduce frustrtion and improve trust. Features such as autofill, autocorrect, password suggestions, and helpful error messages make systems easier to use. Johsnson (2020) explains that people avoid interfaces requiring too much conscious thinking or memorization. Flexible systems reduce mental effort and allow users to complete tasks more naturally.

Postel's Law also emphasizes accessibility. Interfaces should work across different devices, environments, and user abilities. For example, websites should adopt properly to mobile devices, tablets, and desktops. Forms should accept multiple input formats whenever possile instead of forcing users into rigid structures.

Helpful error messages are also important. Instead of displaying vague warnings such as "Invalid input," systems should clearly explain what went wrong and how users can fix the issue. This supports learning and reduces frustration. Good design often becomes "invisible" because users can complete tasks without confusion or interruption (Stevens, 2024). Flexible and forgiving systems contribute to this seamless experience. Users should feel supported rather than punsihed when mistakes occur.

Examples of Postel's Law can be seen in search engines like Google. Even when users misspell words, the system still understands the intended meaning and provides accurate results. This flexibilty creates smoother and less stressful interactions.

Person Experience with Poor Design Interface Design

A frustrating experience I have had with interface design involves the website Caring for Families, which is my doctor's office in Arizona. The website demonstrates several problems related to layout, navigation, visual hierarchy, and control design. These issues increase cognitive load and create frustration for users.

One major issue involves the "Request Appointment" feature on the homepage. The words "Request Appointment" are bold and underlined, which visually suggests they are clickable links. However, clicking the text does nothing. Instead, users must scroll farther down the page to locate a small button that actually allows appointment requests. This button does not visually stand out and barely appears interactive. This design violates the Von Restorff Effect because the wrong elements receive visual emphasis. The bold and underlined text attracts attention even though it serves no funtional purpose. Meanwhile, the actual appointment button is difficult to locate. Users naturally expect visually emphasized elements to be interactive, so this mismatch creates confusion and frustration (Yablonski, 2024).

The website also violates the Aesthetic-Usability Effect because it appears outdated and cluttered. The layout lacks consistency, spacing is poor, and important controls are difficult to identify. Users often associate modern and visually appealing interfaces with professionalism and usability. In this case, the outdates appearance immeditely creates negative expectations and lowers trust.

The patient portal creates addional usability problems. To leave a message for the doctor's office, users must access the patient portal. However, clicking the portal link displays an error message stating that "this version expired." Users are then forced to search for another location to access the correct portal. Even after reaching the correct page, the login option is hidden on the right side of the page and is a very light color that blends into the background.

This design creates unnecessary cogntive load because users have to spend extra time searching for information and interpreting unclear visual cues (Mishra, 2025). Instead of guiding users naturally the interface forces them into conscious problem solving. Humans prefer predictable systems that support automatic behavior (Johnson, 2020). Unfortunately, this website constantly interrupts automatic thinking and forces users to stop and figure out what to do next.

This website also conflicts with Postel's Law because it is not forgiving or user-centered. Instead of adapting to users, the interfave punishes the with broken links, hidden controls, and confusing navigation. Helpful error handling and clearer visual hierarchy would improve the experience. Emotionally, the website creates stress and frustration. Healthcare websites should prioritize clarity and accessibility because users may already feel anxious or overwhelmed. Emotions strongly influence satisfaction and decision making (Johnson, 2020). Negative emotional experiences reduce trust and discourage continued use of the system.

The Importance of Emotional Design

Emotional design is another important factor influencing user behavior. Interfaces affect whether users feel confident, comfortable, frustrated, or everwhelmed. Small design choices such as button labels, spacing, wording, navigation structure strongly shape emotional responses (Stevens, 2024).

Emotions are necessary for effective descion making. Positive emotional experiences improve satisfaction and increase trust in the system. Users are more likely to continue using products that make them feel comfortable and successful.

The Aesthetic-Usability Effect connects to emotional design because attractive interfaces creates positive first impressions and reduces stress. Predictable layouts and forgiving systems reduce anxiety and encourage exploration. Poor emotional design has the opposite effect. Confusing navigation, cluttered layouts, broken links, and unclear instructions create frustration and uncertainty. Users may abandon tasks or avoid returning to the product altogether. Designers must consider both funtionality and emotional impact when creating interfaces. Systems should feel approachable, supportive, and easy to understand. Emotional comfort is especially important in healthcare, banking, education, and other high-stress environments where users may already feel anxious.

Conclusion

Interface layout and control design significantly impact user behavior, emotions, and efficiency. Every design decision influences how users process information, make decisions, and complete tasks. Concepts like cognitve load, emotional design, visual hierarchy, and flexibility all contribute to the overall user experience.

The Aesthetic-Usability Effect shows how visual appeal affects trust and usability perceptions. The Von Restroff Effect shows the importance of emphasizing key actions clearly. Postel's Law emphasizes the need for flexible and forgiving systems that reduce frustration and support users naturally.

My experience with the Caring for Families websites demonstrates how poor interface design can create confusion, increase mental effort, and produce negative emotional responses. Broken links, weak visual hierarchy, and outdated layouts forced users into unnecessary problem solving and frustration. Successful UX desifn should align with human cognition and psychology. Effective interfaces reduce cogntive load, support automatic behavior, and guide users naturally through tasks. Ultimatelt, good UX design creates sytems that feel intuitive, efficient, visually organized, and emotionally supportive for users.

Module 3.2: Interaction that Matters

Introduction

Digital products have become integrated into everyday life. People interact with websites, apps, social media platforms, and software systems constantly throughout their day. User experience design has become more important because of this. UX design is not only about making interfaces look visually appealing, but it is also about shaping how users feel, behave, and interact with technology. Timing, responsiveness, motion, and error are all well designed interaction patterns. They can create meaningful and satisfying experiences for users. At the same time, these patterns can also influence user behavior that makes ethical design an essential responsibility for UX designers.

Interaction patterns affect emotions during and after digital experiences. When systems respond quickly, provide helpful feedback, and prevent errors, users will feel confident, comfortable, and satisfied. If it is poorly deisgned, systems can creare frustration, confusion, and sometimes distrust. UX laws like Peak-End Rule, Doherty Threshold, and Fitt's Law help explain why users respond emotionally to digital interactions in certain ways. These laws provide guidance for designers who want to create systems that are efficient, memorable, and ethical. Designers also have to carefully consider how persuasive techniques are used. This is because interaction patterns can either support users or manipulate them.

Feedback Timing and Responsiveness

One of the most important factors in UX design is feedback timing and responsiveness. Users expect systems to respond quickly when they click a button, submit a form, or navigate through an application. According to the Doherty Threshold, productivity and user engagement improve when systems respond within approximately 400 milliseconds (Yablonski, 2024). When feedback is immediate, users remain focused and feel in control of the interaction. Delayed responses interupt concentration and create uncertainty about whether the system is funtioning properly. For example, when a mobile banking app immediately confirms a successful transfer with a visual message and animation, users feel reassured that the action was completed correctly. On the other hand, if the app freezes or dlays feedback, users may become anxious and attempt the transaction multiple times.

Johnson (2020) explains that humans have certain time expectations when interacting with technology. Responses under one-tenth of a second feel instantaneous, while delays longer than one second become noticeable and can interrupt a user's train of thought. If delays reach ten seconds or more, users may lose focus entirely. This demonstrates how timing directly influences emotional reactions and overall satisfaction. A slow-loading website may technically function correctly, but users often perceive it as unreliable or frustrating. They may think this because of delayed responsiveness.

The Role of Motion and Animation in UX

Motion and animation are also important interation patterns that shape user experiences. Motion can help users understand relationships between interface elements, provide feedback, and create smoother transitions. For example, animations that show a menu sliding open or a card moving into place help users mentally process what is happening on the screen. Motion provides continuity and reduces cognitive load by making changes easier to follow. Well designed motion can make interfaces feel intuitive and polished.

Motion must also be used carefully. Excessive or unnecarry animations can direct users and slow interactions. Designers sometimes add motion simply because it looks modern and visually impressive. This can negatively impact usability if it delays task completion. Ethical UX design requires balancing visual engagement with functionality and accessibility. Some users may also experience motion sensitivity or cognitive overload from excessive animations. That is why motion should support usability rather than interfere with it.

The Peak-End Rule and Emotional Memory

The Peak-End Rule is another UX principle that influences emotional experiences. According to Yablonski (2024), people remember experiences based primarily on the emotional peak and the ending of the interaction. This means that users do not evaluate every moment equally. Instead, they form lasting impressions from the most emotionally intense moments and the final stages of the experience. Designers can use this principle to creare memorable and meaningful interactions.

For example, many online shopping platforms use confirmation pages with animations, order summaries, and thank-you messages after a purchase is completed. These positive ending experiences leave users feeling satisfied and reassured. Learning platforms also often celebrate course completion with certificates, badges, or congratulatory messages. This creates rewarding emotional peaks. These interactions encourage users to return because the experience ends positively.

There can also be negative endings. Negative endings can damage an otherwise successful interaction. Imagine spending twenty minutes filling out an online application only for the system to display an unclear error message at the final step. Even if the earlier portions of the process worked well, the frustrating ending may dominate the user's memory of the experience. This demonstrates how emotional responses during key moments can shape user perceptions.

Error Prevention and Fitt's Law

Error prevention is another important part of meaningful UX design. Good interfaces help users avoid mistakes before they happen instead of simply reacting to errors afterward. Designers can reduce frustration by providing clear instructions, confirmations, constraints, and visual cues. Error prevention improves confidence because users feel supported rather than punished by the system. Fitt's Law helps explain why physical interaction design matters for preventing errors. Larger and closer targets are easier and faster for users to interact (Nielsen Norman Group, 2021). Small buttons or tightly packed controls increase the likeliehood of mistakes, especially on touchscreens where finger precision is limited.

For example, mobile apps that use large tough-friendly buttons reduce accidental taps and create a more comfortable user experience. In contrast, tiny close buttons or crowded navigation menus often lead to frustration and repeated errors. Fitt's Law shows that usability is influenced by visual aesthetics and by human physical behavior. Designers must account for how people. move, tap, and navigate digital systems. By reducing the effort required to complete tasks, interfaces become more efficient and less mentally demanding. This contributes a more positive emotional experience overall.

Ethical Design and Persuasive Technology

Ethical design becomes important when persuasive techniques are involved. Modern digital products are designed to influence behavior in many ways. Social media platforms encourage engagement through notifcations. Streaming services automatically play the next episode to keep users watching. Shopping platforms use countdown timers and personalized recommendations to encourage purchases. These techniques can improve convenience and engagement. They also can become manipulative if designers prioritize profit over user well-being.

Persuasive technologies use psychological principles such as intermittent rewards and social affirmation to keep users engaged (Yablonski, 2024). Notifications, likes, and endless feeds trigger emotional responses that encourage repeated interaction. These systems are highly effective because they tap into natural human desires for social validation and novelty. However, persuasive design can become unethical when it intentionally exploits psychological vulnerabilities.

The UX Matters article on ethical design argues that designers have a responsibility to avoid harmful or manipulative experiences. Ethical design means creating systems that support users rather than deceive or pressure them (UX Matters, 2021). Dark patterns are a common example of unethical persuasive design. These patterns manipulate users into actions they may not fully intend. This can happen by subscribing to services accidentally, sharing more personal data than desired, or struggling to cancel memberships.

For example, some websites intentionally hide unsubscribe options or make cancellation processes confusing and time-consuming. Others use misleading button labels or preselected checkboxes to push users toward purchases. These techniques may temporarily increase engagement or revenue, but they damage user trust and create negative long-term experiences. Ethical UX design requires transparency and respect for user autonomy.

A good example of ethical persuasive design can be seen in fitness or wellness applications. Many fitness apps use reminders and progress tracking to encourage healthy habits. These features motivate users while still supporting their personal goals. The difference between ethical and unethical persuasion often depends on whether the design genuinely benefits the user or primarily serves the company at the user's expense.

Accessibility, Inclusivity, and User Trust

Feedback timing and motion can also influence emotional trust in ethical ways. For example, loading indicators and progress bars reassure users that a system is funtioning properly. Users become frustrated when systems fail to provide feedback during delays (Johnson, 2020). Even simple animations can reduce uncertainty and anxiety by communicating that a process is still active. This is especially important in high-stakes environments such as healthcare, banking, or transportation systems where users rely on accurate information.

Ethical design also includes accessibility and inclusivity. Designers must make sure that interaction patterns work for users with different abilities, devices, and cultural backgrounds. The UX Matters article points out examples of biased technology, like systems that fail to recognize darker skin tones because diverse testing groups were not inlcuded (UX Matters, 2021). Inclusive design helps prevent discrimination and makes sure that digital products serve broader populations fairly.

Motion design provides another example of accessibility concerns. While some users enjoy animated transitions, others might experience dizziness or sensory discomfort. Ethical designers consider these needs by allowing users to reduce or disable motion effects. This demonstrates respect for diverse user experiences rather than assuming all users interact with technology in the same way.

Balancing Business Goals with User Well-Being

Another important part of ethical interaction design is balancing business goals with user well-being. Many companies measure success using metrics like time spent on a platform, clicks, or purchases. These metrics are important but focusing exclusively on engagement can encourage manipulative practices. Designers may feel pressure to maximize attention even if it harms users' mental health or productivity.

Social media platforms provide a strong example of this tension. Infinite scrolling and autoplay features are designed to remove stopping points and keep users engaged for longer periods. While these features creates smooth interactions, they can also contribute to addictive behavior and excessive screen time. Ethical designers must question whether certain interaction patterns actually improve user experiences or if they just increase engagement metrics. At the same time, meaningful interaction patterns can create positive emotional experiences when used responsibly. Things like meditation, mental wellness apps that use calm animations, gentle feedback, and encouraging progress indicators are things to consider. These interactioin patterns are persuasive because they motivate users to continue healthy behaviors, but they do so in a supportive and transparent manner. Ethical UX design does not eliminate persuasion entirely, instead it makes sure that persuasion aligns with user interests and well-being.

Conclusion

The combination of timing, responsiveness, motion, and error prevention ultimately shapes how users feel about digital products. Smooth interactions create confidence and trust, while confusing or manipulative interactions create frustration and skepticism. UX laws like Doherty Threshold, Peak-End Rule, and Fitt's Law provide valuable insights into how people perceive and remember experiences. Designers who understand these principles can create systems that are efficient, memorable, and emotionally satisfying. Understanding psychology also comes with responsibility. Designers hold significant power because interaction patterns influence attention, emotions, habits, and behavior. Ethical UX design requires considering not only usability and business goals but also the long-term effects on users and society. Transparency, accessibility, inclusivity, and user well-being should remain central priorities throughout the design process.

Well-designed interaction patterns play a major role in shaping meaningful and ethical user experiences. Timing and responsiveness affect whether users feel confident or frustrated during interactions. Motion and feedback create clairty, continuity, and emotional engagement when used thoughtfully. Error prevention reduces cognitive stress and supports successful task completion. UX laws like Peak-End Rule, Doherty Threshold, and Fitt's law explain why these interaction patterns influence human emotions and behavior. At the same time, ethical considerations are important because persuasive techniques can easily cross the line into manipulation. Designers must use psychological principles responsibly to create experiences that support users rather than exploit them. By prioritizing transparency, inclusivity, and user well-being, UX designers can create digital experiences that are not only effective but also ethical and meaningful.
